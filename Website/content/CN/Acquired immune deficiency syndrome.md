# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a life-threatening condition caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), which impairs the immune system by targeting CD4 cells (T cells). This weakening of the immune system makes individuals susceptible to opportunistic infections and certain cancers. AIDS is the final stage of HIV infection. Transmission occurs through unprotected sex, sharing needles, and from mother to child during birth or breastfeeding. While there is no cure for HIV/AIDS, antiretroviral therapy (ART) can effectively manage the virus, allowing individuals to live longer, healthier lives by preventing the progression to AIDS.

## Highlights

- From 2015 to 2025, AIDS cases fluctuated with an increasing trend initially, peaking around late 2019, before stabilizing post-2020. <br/>
- Mortality remained high, peaking late 2019; it has since settled into a slightly lower, steady range, with higher counts often in spring and winter. <br/>
- Significant monthly variability is noted, especially early in the dataset, possibly due to differing factors including seasonal and reporting influences. <br/>
- As of June 2025, there's a slight decline in cases and deaths compared to peaks, reflecting ongoing public health efforts and possibly improved treatment access. <br/>

Monthly data shows seasonal peaks, with higher cases typically reported toward year's end and a dip at the beginning of the year. Annual increases in reported cases are apparent, particularly in November and December, where numbers frequently exceed 5,000. For instance, cases surged dramatically in December 2017 to 6,622, continuing an upward trend through to December 2018 and 2019, with cases rising above 7,000 each month.

Deaths associated with AIDS follow a similar pattern of fluctuation, aligning with case numbers. End-of-year months often report higher deaths, with December 2018 and 2019 showing numbers exceeding 2,000. This pattern persists into 2021 and 2022, where the numbers in November and December remain notably high, such as over 2,400 deaths in December 2022. In 2023, though there's variation, signs of stabilization emerge, possibly reflecting enhanced interventions or public health efforts.

These changes may be due to variations in treatment advancements, healthcare access, or new infection rates. A noticeable pattern emerged, with more deaths typically recorded towards year-end, possibly linked to healthcare service delivery or reporting cycles.

In 2019, mortality was higher, peaking in December with 2,284 deaths, suggesting factors like increased incidence or late diagnosis. The following years saw relatively stable mortality but with notable monthly variations, indicating control of the epidemic but ongoing challenges in treatment access and managing comorbidities. Data from 2023 shows persistent mortality at high levels, underscoring the need for continued prevention, treatment adherence, and public health strategies.

Entering 2025, there's a slight decline in deaths, which may suggest improvements in interventions or public health efforts in reducing AIDS-related mortality in China. Overall, while the epidemic appears managed, the data highlights continuous challenges in sustaining healthcare service delivery against evolving epidemiological factors.
