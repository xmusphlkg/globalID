# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is the advanced stage of infection caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). HIV attacks the body’s immune system, specifically the CD4 cells (T cells), reducing its ability to fight infections and certain cancers. Without treatment, HIV progresses to AIDS, where the immune system becomes severely damaged. People with AIDS are vulnerable to opportunistic infections and malignancies. HIV is primarily transmitted through sexual contact, blood transfusions, shared needles, and from mother to child during childbirth or breastfeeding. Effective antiretroviral therapy can slow disease progression and improve life expectancy.

## Highlights

- AIDS cases in mainland China have shown fluctuating monthly trends, with peaks typically in late-year months and dips in early months. <br/>
- Deaths have generally risen over the years, with significant spikes from 2018 to 2023, reflecting ongoing challenges in disease management. <br/>
- Seasonal variation is evident, with higher reports in summer and winter months, possibly linked to behavioral patterns or reporting cycles. <br/>
- As of April 2025, there were 4,534 reported cases and 1,642 deaths—slightly lower than prior months—indicating potential progress, though mortality remains high. <br/>

From 2015 to 2025, AIDS case reporting in mainland China reveals consistent seasonal trends, with noticeably fewer cases in January–February and sharp rebounds in March. This pattern reflects possible underreporting during national holidays and subsequent delayed diagnoses. Overall, both reported cases and deaths increased gradually from 2015 through 2019, peaking in late-year months—especially November and December—when monthly deaths exceeded 2,000, suggesting possible year-end data clearance or rising detection coverage.

The COVID-19 outbreak in early 2020 caused abrupt drops in January and February's figures, aligning with healthcare disruptions, followed by a rebound in subsequent months. From 2021 onward, although monthly fluctuations continued, the gap between low and high periods narrowed, indicating greater stability in data reporting. January 2023 presents an anomaly with only 1,815 reported cases but 1,777 deaths, potentially reflecting delayed reporting or diagnostic issues. In 2024 and early 2025, figures remain within a moderate range, showing slight decreases in both cases and mortality compared to peak years. This could point to earlier diagnoses and improved disease management, despite stable infection rates.

Between 2015 and 2025, reported monthly AIDS-related deaths in mainland China displayed a long-term upward trend, peaking noticeably between 2018 and 2021. The average monthly deaths rose from under 1,200 in early 2015 to over 2,000 during the late 2019–2021 period, coinciding with increasingly higher case numbers. Deaths per month demonstrated relatively consistent seasonal patterns: often lower in January and February, likely reflecting reporting lags around the Lunar New Year, and peaking again toward year-end (e.g., November–December). While total cases increased, several periods reveal deaths growing at a faster relative rate than cases (e.g., 2017–2019), suggesting delays in diagnosis, late presentation, or gaps in care linkage and retention.

After 2021, total deaths appear to plateau or slightly decline, even as new case volume stabilizes, which may reflect expanded access to antiretroviral therapy (ART), improved public health outreach, or better detection of earlier-stage infections. Notably, months like January 2023 recorded an anomalously high death-to-case ratio (nearly 1.0), suggesting either data irregularity or delayed death recording from previous infections.
