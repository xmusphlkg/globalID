# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a chronic, potentially life-threatening condition caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). By damaging the immune system, HIV interferes with the body's ability to fight infection and disease. AIDS is the most severe phase of HIV infection, characterized by a significantly weakened immune system which leads to opportunistic infections or cancers that usually do not affect healthy individuals. While there is no cure for HIV/AIDS, antiretroviral therapy (ART) can effectively manage the virus, allowing many individuals to lead relatively healthy lives and reducing the risk of transmission.

## Highlights

- From 2015 to 2025, there is a general increasing trend in the number of AIDS cases reported, with peaks observed in the latter half of several years. <br/>
- The death rate fluctuates but generally follows the trend of case increases, suggesting a strong correlation between cases and mortality. <br/>
- There was a notable peak in cases and deaths around the end of 2019 and early 2020, likely attributable to the overall healthcare strain during the pandemic period. <br/>
- Recent data from early 2025 indicates a slight decrease in both cases and deaths compared to previous peaks, suggesting possible improved management and treatment efforts. <br/>

From 2015 to 2025, AIDS case reports in mainland China show clear seasonal fluctuations, with lower counts in January–February and higher counts from mid-year to December. Peaks often occur in November and December, such as in 2018 when cases reached over 7800 monthly. This recurring pattern suggests links to behavioral trends or reporting cycles tied to calendar or institutional factors.

Overall, both reported cases and deaths increased from 2015 through 2019, peaking notably around 2018–2019. Case counts remained high through 2020–2021 despite disruptions likely caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. A temporary dip is seen in part of 2022, followed by a rise again in 2023; however, recent data from 2024 into early 2025 shows signs of slight decline or stabilization in both cases and fatalities. Deaths followed a similar uptrend, with monthly fatalities surpassing 2000 during multiple periods post-2018. Notably, while case numbers remained steady in some years, deaths fluctuated more sharply, indicating shifting case severity or possibly delayed diagnoses.

The data trend points not just to increasing case detection but also sustained transmission rates, despite long-running public health efforts. The continual rise and late plateau in deaths highlight a persistent public health burden and underline the importance of early detection, sustained treatment access, and prevention outreach targeting high-risk periods and populations.
