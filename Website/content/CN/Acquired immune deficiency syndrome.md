# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is the advanced stage of infection caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). HIV attacks the body's immune system, specifically the CD4 cells (T cells), leading to their depletion. Without treatment, HIV reduces the body's ability to fight other infections and diseases, resulting in AIDS. Symptoms of AIDS include rapid weight loss, recurring fever, night sweats, prolonged swelling of lymph glands, and opportunistic infections. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood, semen, vaginal fluids, or breast milk. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) can dramatically slow the disease's progression, allowing individuals to lead healthier lives.

## Highlights

- From 2015 to 2025, AIDS cases in mainland China show a general upward trend, peaking in 2018-2019 before gradually declining post-2023. <br/>
- Deaths also peaked in 2018-2019, with a downward trend starting in 2023, suggesting improved management or treatment. <br/>
- Seasonal variations are evident, with lower cases and deaths in January and February each year, possibly due to healthcare access or reporting delays during holidays. <br/>
- Recent months in 2025 indicate a decline in cases and deaths, suggesting potential stabilization or improved AIDS control measures. <br/>

The data on AIDS cases and deaths in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 reveals some key trends. During 2015-2019, there was a clear upward trend in both reported cases and deaths, with cases increasing from 4,268 in August 2015 to 7,366 in November 2019 and deaths rising from 1,085 to 2,245 over the same period. This rise could be attributed to various factors, including increased transmission or improved detection capabilities.

Post-2019, the pattern becomes more complex, likely reflecting the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. Early 2020 shows a decrease in cases, dropping to 2,133 in February, possibly due to pandemic-related healthcare disruptions. However, cases began to stabilize and show fluctuating patterns following the initial pandemic impact. Annual spikes in cases and deaths, particularly around November and December, suggest seasonal or reporting factors influencing these numbers.

From 2021 onwards, there emerges a fluctuating but generally declining trend in both cases and deaths, possibly indicating successful interventions or changes in public health strategies. By mid-2025, a downward trend in both metrics becomes more recognizable, suggesting improvements in treatments, prevention, or reporting practices. Overall, while the data highlights significant challenges, it also reflects potential progress in combating AIDS across the studied period.

The data on AIDS deaths in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 reveals key trends and patterns. December consistently shows elevated death counts across most years, with December 2018 marking a peak of 2444 deaths. January and February typically register lower mortality numbers, likely due to reporting lags or seasonal factors. An increase in deaths is often observed in the latter half of each year, culminating in peaks from November to January, possibly reflecting increased incidence rates or reporting delays.

From 2015 to 2025, fluctuations in mortality are evident. There is a discernible upswing in deaths around 2019 and early 2020, followed by stabilization in 2021 during the COVID-19 pandemic. This might be due to healthcare resources being directed towards COVID-19, impacting HIV/AIDS management. Despite the fluctuations, death rates show a slight upward trend, suggesting stabilizing control measures against AIDS.

From 2023 onwards, the death rate remains persistent yet slightly moderating, hinting at potential improvements in antiretroviral therapy access and public health interventions. This stabilization, however, underscores ongoing challenges in managing AIDS despite possible public health successes, highlighting the need for sustained healthcare investment, targeted strategies, and community programs to address the needs of vulnerable populations affected by AIDS in China.
