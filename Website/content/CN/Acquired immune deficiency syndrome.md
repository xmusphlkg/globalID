# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a chronic, life-threatening condition caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). HIV attacks the immune system, weakening the body's ability to fight infections and disease. Transmission occurs through contact with infected bodily fluids, such as blood, semen, or breast milk, often via unprotected sex, shared needles, or from mother to child during childbirth. Without treatment, HIV can progress to AIDS, characterized by severe immune suppression and increased vulnerability to opportunistic infections and cancers. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) can manage HIV, improving life expectancy and reducing transmission risk.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers**: From 2014 to 2024, AIDS cases showed cyclical fluctuations, peaking in the colder months, with high numbers in 2019 and a more stable trend from 2020 onwards.<br/>

- **Mortality Rates**: Deaths followed a similar cyclical trend, with notable peaks around 2018-2019. Recent years, such as 2023-2024, suggest possible stabilization but remain elevated.<br/>

- **Seasonal Trends**: Cases and deaths commonly rise towards the end of the year, suggesting potential seasonal factors influencing transmission and outcomes.<br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of September 2024, both cases and deaths have seen a slight decline from earlier months, indicating potential improvements in control measures or treatment efficacy.<br/>

The data from the Chinese mainland displays an overall increasing trend in reported Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) cases from 2014 through 2024, with periodic fluctuations in monthly figures. Notably, there are significant spikes in reported cases towards the end of each calendar year, particularly in November and December. For instance, the years 2016, 2017, and 2018 saw peaks in December, reaching upwards of 6,622, 7,897, and 7,490 cases, respectively. These peaks could potentially be attributed to heightened awareness and increased testing campaigns that typically occur towards the year's end, although other socio-environmental factors may also play a role.

The death counts generally follow a similar pattern, with a noticeable lag in reporting periods relative to new case spikes. Years such as 2017 and 2018 reveal higher mortality numbers associated with higher case counts, implying a potential link between increased disease burden and healthcare resource limitations. Furthermore, recent years like 2023 and 2024 demonstrate a varied pattern with monthly mortality fluctuations, peaking during times of surging cases, suggesting potential issues in timely treatment access or other systemic health challenges. These trends highlight the importance of continuous monitoring and strengthening healthcare infrastructure to improve case management and reduce mortality rates.

Over the subsequent years, monthly death counts generally fluctuate with minor increases and decreases, often correlating with higher case numbers. Noteworthy spikes are observed later, such as November and December 2018, where deaths rose sharply to 2,467 and 2,444 respectively, likely reflecting an increase in cases and perhaps challenges in treatment or reporting aspects during these months.

Further examination of later years, particularly 2021 to 2024, illustrates a mixed trend with fluctuations that might imply seasonal or situational factors affecting AIDS mortality.
