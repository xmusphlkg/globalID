# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a chronic, potentially life-threatening condition caused by the Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). HIV damages the immune system by destroying CD4 cells (T cells), which impairs the body's ability to fight infections and diseases. AIDS is the most advanced stage of HIV infection, characterized by the occurrence of any of more than 20 opportunistic infections or related cancers. Transmission occurs through contact with infected body fluids, primarily via unprotected sex, sharing needles, or mother-to-child during birth or breastfeeding. Effective antiretroviral therapy (ART) can control the virus and help prevent progression to AIDS.

## Highlights

- **Increase in Cases:** The number of AIDS cases in Chinese mainland has steadily increased from 2014 to May 2024, with higher peaks in recent years. <br/>
- **Monthly Peaks:** Significant case peaks occur in the latter months, especially November and December, indicating potential seasonal transmission spikes. <br/>
- **High Mortality Rates:** Deaths have also risen, frequently surpassing 1500 per month in recent years, highlighting ongoing mortality challenges. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Lower case numbers in January and February each year may suggest reporting delays or seasonal effects. <br/>

The analysis of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) cases and deaths in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals several key trends. The number of cases increased from 3,823 in May 2014 to a peak of 7,897 in December 2018, with subsequent stabilization between 5,000 and 7,000 cases per month. This general upward trend is likely due to improved diagnostic capabilities, higher transmission rates, and increased awareness. Seasonal fluctuations are evident, with case counts often dipping in early months and peaking towards the end of the year, especially in November and December.

Similarly, deaths show an upward trend, rising from 1,035 in May 2014 to peaks exceeding 2,000 in later years. This growth reflects the increasing number of cases and possibly complications related to delayed or inadequate treatment. Notably, deaths peak towards the end of the year, suggesting a seasonal pattern that may be influenced by healthcare access and other factors. The relatively high death-to-case ratios in some months indicate periods of heightened vulnerability, emphasizing the need for continuous public health efforts and improved treatment protocols.

Overall, the data highlights the necessity for sustained efforts in public health, better treatment protocols, and expanded care access to manage and reduce AIDS-related morbidity and mortality in China.

Analyzing the reported deaths from Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals notable trends and fluctuations. The number of deaths per month shows an overall upward trend, despite some periods of decrease or stabilization. For instance, deaths generally remained below 1,500 per month from 2014 to mid-2016, but gradually increased past this mark, notably surpassing 2,000 monthly deaths by December 2018. This upward trajectory seemed to continue, peaking especially during colder months and possibly reflecting compounded vulnerabilities during that period.

Noteworthy spikes in deaths occur around the end of several years, as seen in December 2016, 2017, 2018, and 2021, when deaths reached levels such as 1,535, 1,834, 2,444, and 2,486 respectively. These surges could be influenced by seasonal factors, healthcare accessibility, and other socio-economic determinants. Analysis also highlights a disturbing rise in monthly deaths from 1,758 in January 2023 to an alarming peak of 2,068 in December 2023. This clearly indicates a persistent high mortality rate, stressing the need for intensified public health interventions and resources dedicated to AIDS treatment and prevention. The trend suggests that while there may be short-term fluctuations, there is a long-term increase in mortality, possibly due to inconsistent access to effective antiretroviral therapies and lack of widespread preventive measures.
