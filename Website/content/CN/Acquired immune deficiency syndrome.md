# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a chronic, life-threatening condition caused by the Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). Once HIV enters a personâ€™s bloodstream, it attacks and destroys CD4 cells, which are crucial components of the immune system. As HIV progresses, it depletes CD4 cells, weakening the immune system and making individuals vulnerable to opportunistic infections and certain cancers. AIDS is the final stage of HIV infection, diagnosed when the CD4 cell count falls below a critical level or when specific opportunistic infections or cancers occur. Antiretroviral therapy helps manage the virus, extending life expectancy and improving quality of life.

## Highlights

- New cases of AIDS have been fluctuating but indicate a general trend of stabilization since 2009, while the incidence of new HIV infections is declining. <br/>
- AIDS-related deaths, however, show an alarming upward trend, highlighting challenges in patient care and treatment retention since 2013. <br/>
- Seasonal variations exist, with higher cases typically reported towards the end of the year, suggesting potential healthcare access or reporting delays. <br/>
- Despite concerted public health efforts, the higher infection rates among males persist, underlining the need for targeted interventions. <br/>

The data for AIDS cases and deaths in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 shows notable trends and fluctuations. Initially, from 2014 to 2016, there's a seasonal pattern with peaks in late months like November and December, and troughs in January and February. This pattern could suggest seasonal influences on transmission or reporting, or structured healthcare cycles impacting diagnosis rates. 

From 2014 to 2019, reported cases rise steadily, implying a growing epidemic, improved surveillance, increased diagnosis, or a combination of these aspects. The impact of COVID-19 is evident in early 2020 with a dip in reported cases, likely due to disruptions in healthcare systems and reporting. Despite this, the upward trend resumes post-2020, with cases and deaths showing some irregularities but generally increasing from previous years.

By 2024, case and death numbers are slightly moderated from past peaks yet remain higher compared to early years. Death rates closely track case numbers across the years, indicating possibly consistent disease severity, available treatments, or healthcare factors influencing mortality. Overall, the data reveals the dynamic nature of AIDS reporting in the region, highlighting potential areas for health policy focus, such as enhanced surveillance, timely intervention, and continuous public health education to manage and mitigate AIDS impacts effectively.
