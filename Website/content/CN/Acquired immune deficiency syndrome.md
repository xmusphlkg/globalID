# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a life-threatening condition caused by the Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). AIDS represents the most advanced stage of HIV infection, characterized by the significant weakening of the immune system, making individuals susceptible to opportunistic infections and certain cancers. The virus is primarily transmitted through unprotected sexual contact, sharing needles, or from mother to child during childbirth or breastfeeding. While there is no cure for AIDS, antiretroviral therapy (ART) can effectively manage HIV, allowing individuals to lead longer and healthier lives by suppressing viral replication and bolstering immune function.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases and Deaths**: From 2023 to October 2025, there is a notable decrease in both AIDS cases and deaths, indicating improvements in treatment or prevention strategies.<br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: There's a consistent pattern of higher cases and deaths towards the end of each year, possibly linked to increased testing or social behaviors during these months.<br/>

- **Impact of COVID-19**: The significant drop in cases and deaths in early 2020 may be attributed to the COVID-19 pandemic impacting healthcare services and reporting.<br/>

- **Stabilization Trends**: The figures suggest a stabilization in the number of cases and deaths from 2024, reflecting potential stabilization of healthcare interventions.<br/>

The analysis of AIDS data in Mainland China from October 2015 to October 2025 reveals significant trends in reported cases and deaths. Initially, the data shows a general upward trend from 2015 to 2019, peaking in December 2018 with 7,897 cases and 2,444 deaths. This increase can be associated with improved health reporting, increased testing, and rising HIV incidence. A seasonal pattern appears, with cases typically rising late each year, potentially influenced by health service factors.

Post-2020, the data fluctuates, possibly due to the COVID-19 pandemic disrupting healthcare systems, affecting HIV testing and AIDS-related services. February 2020, for instance, shows a significant dip to 2,133 cases. However, numbers rise again, indicating a recovery in testing and health services post-pandemic. Notably, after 2023, both reported cases and deaths generally decline, suggesting improved public health interventions, better access to antiretroviral treatments, and enhanced preventative measures.

These insights highlight areas for policymakers and public health authorities to consider, ensuring current interventions remain effective and exploring new strategies in AIDS prevention and treatment. This analysis demonstrates the importance of consistent health service availability and effective response to external disruptions in managing AIDS in Mainland China.

Initially, from late 2015 through to 2016, there was a pattern of fluctuation in the monthly death toll, with peaks during the winter months and dips observed during the spring. By 2017, there was an evident increase in the total number of deaths, particularly marked by a spike in December with 1,834 deaths. The trend of higher deaths during the winter months seems consistent from 2015 through to 2019, possibly indicating seasonal factors or healthcare-related challenges that might impact patient outcomes during colder months.

Over the subsequent years, AIDS-related deaths show a gradual increase until 2021 where we observe a noteworthy rise in November and December, leading up to 2,486 in December. This increase could indicate expanding challenges in prevention or treatment efforts during this time. However, starting in 2023, the monthly deaths appear to stabilize slightly, followed by a gradual decrease from 2024 onwards. Although there is a downward trend observed in monthly deaths by 2025, seasonal fluctuations remain present throughout the data set. This stabilization might suggest improvements in public health strategies, accessibility to treatments, or changes in how cases are managed, though it still indicates a significant burden of mortality associated with AIDS requiring ongoing attention.
