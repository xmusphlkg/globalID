# Acquired immune deficiency syndrome

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is the late stage of infection caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). HIV attacks and weakens the immune system, especially CD4+ T cells, making the body vulnerable to opportunistic infections and certain cancers. AIDS is diagnosed when the immune system becomes severely compromised or specific illnesses develop. HIV is transmitted through blood, sexual contact, sharing needles, and from mother to child during childbirth or breastfeeding. Although there is no cure, antiretroviral therapy (ART) can manage the virus effectively, allowing individuals to live longer, healthier lives and reducing transmission risk.

## Highlights

- **Gradual Decline in Monthly Case Reports**: Monthly AIDS cases decreased from around 6,000–7,000 in peak years (2018–2020) to below 4,300 in May 2025, indicating a slow but steady downward trend.<br/>
- **Consistent Mortality Reduction from 2022 Peak**: While deaths reached over 2,400 monthly in late 2021–2022, they have gradually declined, with 1,573 deaths reported in May 2025.<br/>
- **Reduced Seasonal Fluctuation**: Earlier years showed clear seasonality with lower reports in early months; recent data suggests more stability throughout the year.<br/>
- **Long-Term Disease Burden Remains Significant**: Despite declining trends, both incidence and mortality remain high, emphasizing the ongoing need for prevention, early diagnosis, and treatment strategies.<br/>

From May 2015 to May 2025, reported AIDS cases in mainland China display notable seasonal variation and an overall upward trend until 2019, followed by fluctuations possibly linked to public health interventions or external events such as the COVID-19 pandemic. Numbers typically dropped in early months of each year, especially January and February, which aligns with the Chinese Spring Festival period—suggesting underreporting or delayed diagnoses during holidays. After 2019, case counts and deaths show clear year-to-year variability, with 2020 showing a drop in cases likely due to pandemic-related disruptions, followed by a resumption of high monthly numbers through 2023 and a downward trend in 2024–2025.

Mortality follows a similar temporal pattern, though deaths tend to remain high even in months with fewer new cases, indicating a lag between diagnosis and mortality or a growing population of long-term undiagnosed individuals reaching advanced stages. From 2015 to 2023, death tolls increase both in absolute terms and as a proportion of cases in several months, peaking in late 2021 and 2023. However, deaths start to decline in late 2024 and into 2025, with fewer sharp monthly peaks, pointing to possible improvements in early detection, treatment accessibility, or shifts in demographic burden.

Between 2015 and mid-2025, the monthly death toll from AIDS in mainland China shows a pronounced upward trend, particularly from 2015 to 2019. Early in the period, monthly deaths generally ranged between 900 and 1500, with a gradual climb reaching over 2200 by late 2019. This steady increase occurred alongside growing case counts. In 2020 and 2021, there is a relative plateau in deaths, showing values mostly between 1500 and 2100 per month—this could reflect both stabilization in case reporting and the lingering impact of COVID-19 on public health services. Subsequently, starting in mid-2022, the death figures again rise, consistently exceeding 1700 monthly, peaking over 2000 in several months between 2023 and early 2025.

A striking characteristic is the seasonal fluctuation: deaths tend to dip in January and February, possibly due to reporting delays over the Lunar New Year holidays, with sharp rebounds in subsequent months. This pattern recurs annually but becomes less pronounced in later years as monthly deaths remain high. The overall mortality burden continues to shift upward, reflecting a residual accumulation of long-term untreated cases, aging infected populations, or delayed diagnoses. Even as monthly new case numbers plateau after 2020, consistently high fatality counts underscore persistent healthcare access gaps or late-stage diagnosis in vulnerable demographics.
