# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious viral eye infection characterized by sudden onset of painful, red eyes with subconjunctival hemorrhages, swelling, and excessive tearing. It is commonly caused by enteroviruses, particularly Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24 variant. AHC spreads rapidly through direct contact with eye secretions, contaminated surfaces, or respiratory droplets. Although symptoms are severe, the illness is generally self-limiting, resolving within 5–7 days without serious complications. Outbreaks often occur in densely populated or tropical regions, particularly in schools, workplaces, or other settings where close personal contact is frequent.

## Highlights

- **Case Surge in 2023:** A major outbreak occurred in September 2023 with 125,264 cases, far exceeding typical monthly averages. <br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Despite periodic spikes, the disease shows an extremely low fatality rate, with only 3 deaths reported since 2015. <br/>
- **Post-Outbreak Stabilization:** From late 2023 to May 2025, case numbers declined and stabilized, remaining below 3,000 per month. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends:** Higher case counts are consistently observed between June and September, indicating possible seasonal transmission patterns. <br/>

From 2015 through mid-2023, reported monthly cases of Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China followed a relatively stable seasonal trend, with modest yearly peaks typically occurring between June and September. For example, peak cases during summer months in 2015–2019 fluctuated around 3,000–5,000, with no associated deaths except for isolated incidents in 2019 and early 2020. However, the number of reported cases dropped considerably starting in 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic containment measures, such as lockdowns and improved hygiene, which likely suppressed AHC transmission. This trend persisted through 2022.

A dramatic and anomalous surge in AHC cases occurred beginning in June 2023, peaking in September 2023 at 125,264 reported cases — a scale over 25 times higher than seasonal norms — before sharply declining again by the end of the year. The cause of this spike requires further epidemiological investigation, but possibilities include reporting changes, outbreaks in new or vulnerable populations, environmental factors, or viral mutation increasing transmissibility. The absence of deaths during the 2023 surge reinforces AHC's typical clinical course as a self-limited illness. Since then, beginning in late 2023 and into 2025, monthly case counts have returned to pre-2023 levels, suggesting the spike was an acute and isolated event.

From 2015 to May 2025, Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China has shown a consistently low mortality rate, with only three deaths reported in the entire dataset: one in August 2019 and two in February 2020. The vast majority of months—over 99%—reported zero fatalities despite wide fluctuations in case numbers. Notably, some months such as September 2023 recorded extraordinarily high case volumes exceeding 125,000, yet still resulted in no deaths. This suggests that despite AHC's high transmissibility, its lethality remains extremely low across all demographics and regions tracked in the dataset.

The few anomalous death occurrences raise questions about potential coinfections, misclassification, or regional healthcare access issues during those specific periods. The death in August 2019 coincided with a seasonal peak in incidence, which may reflect a higher burden on health systems or potentially increased risk among vulnerable populations. The two deaths in February 2020 similarly occurred during a seasonal low in cases and aligned with broader national health disruptions due to the early COVID-19 outbreak, possibly affecting case management and reporting accuracy. Nonetheless, the persistent absence of deaths throughout the rest of the timeframe reinforces the clinical perception of AHC as generally self-limited and non-lethal in the Chinese context.
