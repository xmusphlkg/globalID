# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious viral infection characterized by the sudden onset of eye redness, swelling, pain, excessive tearing, and subconjunctival hemorrhages. Commonly caused by enteroviruses, particularly Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24, AHC spreads rapidly through direct contact with infected secretions or contaminated surfaces. Outbreaks frequently occur in close-contact settings such as schools and crowded communities. Symptoms typically resolve within one to two weeks without serious complications. Management focuses on symptom relief, including artificial tears and analgesics, alongside emphasizing strict personal hygiene and infection control measures to minimize transmission.

## Highlights

- A major outbreak occurred from June to September 2023, peaking at 125,264 cases in September, indicating a significant epidemiological event. <br/>
- Post-2023, a marked decline in cases was observed, with numbers stabilizing at lower levels from November 2023 through June 2025, suggesting effective control measures or changes in transmission dynamics. <br/>
- No deaths were reported since June 2023, indicating improved management or reduced severity of acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis cases. <br/>
- The steady decrease in monthly cases from June 2023 to June 2025 reflects ongoing stabilization and improvement in public health response to the disease. <br/>

The data on Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 shows a pattern of seasonal fluctuations, with case numbers typically peaking during summer months. From 2015 to 2019, cases showed moderate volatility, peaking in July 2019 with 5,265 cases and recording the first death in August 2019. This increase was notable compared to preceding years. 

In 2020, there was a significant reduction in cases, possibly influenced by public health measures due to the COVID-19 pandemic. This downward trend continued, with relatively stable and lower case numbers until mid-2023. At this point, a dramatic surge occurred, reaching an unprecedented peak in September 2023 with 125,264 cases. Despite this surge, no deaths were reported, suggesting improved management or variations in disease severity. 

This resurgence aligned with historic seasonal peaks but was overwhelmingly higher, indicating potential new epidemiological factors, such as outbreaks or changes in immunity or surveillance. By late 2024 into 2025, case numbers stabilized and returned to lower levels, possibly due to effective control efforts or natural patterns. This unusual spike in 2023 highlights the need for ongoing epidemiological analysis to understand underlying causes and to enhance future response strategies.
