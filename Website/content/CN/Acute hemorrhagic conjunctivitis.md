# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious viral eye infection characterized by sudden onset of pain, redness, swelling, and subconjunctival hemorrhages. It is caused primarily by enteroviruses, such as Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24. The disease spreads rapidly through direct contact with infected individuals or contaminated surfaces. Symptoms typically include severe eye pain, photophobia, and watery discharge. Although AHC is generally self-limiting, symptoms can be severe and may last up to two weeks. Prevention focuses on good hygiene practices to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- **Epidemiological Trends**: There has been a dramatic increase in acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis cases since mid-2023, peaking in September 2023 with 125,264 cases. <br/>
- **Recent Decline**: After the peak in 2023, cases have significantly decreased, with 2,971 cases reported in June 2024, indicating a return to pre-outbreak levels. <br/>
- **Mortality**: Despite high case numbers, mortality remains extremely low, with only three deaths reported since 2014, all occurring between 2019 and 2020. <br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns**: The disease shows seasonal peaks, typically higher in late summer and early autumn, consistent with historical data. <br/>

The reported cases of Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China show significant variability from 2014 to 2024. There is a distinct seasonal pattern with peaks typically occurring in the summer and early autumn months. The highest recorded peak was in September 2023, with an unprecedented surge to 125,264 cases, a sharp increase compared to previous years. This spike suggests a possible outbreak or an anomaly that warrants further investigation into potential causes such as viral mutations, changes in reporting practices, or environmental factors that may have facilitated transmission.

Throughout the observed period, AHC consistently exhibited a cyclical trend with lower cases in the winter months, gradually increasing towards summer. While 2014 through 2018 saw fluctuations between 2,000 to 8,500 cases per month, the period from 2019 onwards shows more consistent higher counts, particularly with noticeable peaks in mid-year months. Notably, while the number of cases increased over the years, the mortality rate remained extremely low, with only a few isolated deaths reported in 2019 and 2020. This indicates that despite its high transmission rate, AHC continues to be a largely non-fatal condition, emphasizing the need for enhanced preventive measures rather than emergency medical interventions.
