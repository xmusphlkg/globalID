# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious eye infection characterized by sudden onset of redness, swelling, and bleeding in the conjunctiva, the thin membrane covering the white part of the eye and the inner eyelids. It is primarily caused by enteroviruses, such as Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24. Symptoms include eye pain, excessive tearing, and sensitivity to light. AHC spreads rapidly through direct contact with infected individuals or contaminated surfaces. While the condition is self-limiting and typically resolves within a week, maintaining good hygiene practices is crucial to prevent outbreaks.

Interestingly, the data shows a significant spike in cases in 2023, with a dramatic increase in September, reaching an unprecedented 125,264 cases. This surge is followed by a sharp decline in the subsequent months, suggesting a possible outbreak that was eventually controlled or naturally subsided.

Throughout the dataset, the number of deaths remains remarkably low, with only three deaths reported over the entire period, indicating that while AHC may be highly contagious, it is not typically fatal. The data suggests a seasonal pattern, with higher case numbers generally occurring in the summer months, which is consistent with the behavior of many viral infections that thrive in warmer conditions. Over the years, the number of cases fluctuates, with notable peaks in the summer months, particularly from June to September, which is consistent with the seasonal nature of viral conjunctivitis outbreaks. The data indicates a significant spike in cases in 2023, with an unprecedented peak in September 2023, where cases reached 125,264. Despite this surge, the number of deaths remains remarkably low, with only three deaths reported over the entire period, specifically in August 2019 and February 2020.

This low mortality rate suggests that while AHC can be highly contagious and lead to large outbreaks, it is generally not life-threatening. The few deaths reported could be attributed to complications or co-morbidities rather than the disease itself. The data underscores the importance of public health measures to control the spread of AHC, especially during peak seasons, but also highlights the effectiveness of current medical interventions in preventing fatalities. The significant increase in cases in 2023 may warrant further investigation to understand the underlying causes, such as changes in virus virulence, population immunity, or reporting practices.
