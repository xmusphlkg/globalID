# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious eye infection characterized by the sudden onset of severe conjunctival inflammation, pain, swelling, and subconjunctival hemorrhage. It is primarily caused by enterovirus 70 and coxsackievirus A24, spreading rapidly through direct contact with infected secretions or surfaces. Symptoms typically include redness, excessive tearing, photophobia, and a gritty sensation in the eyes. While AHC is generally self-limiting and resolves within 1-2 weeks, it can cause significant discomfort and may lead to secondary bacterial infections if not managed properly. Preventive measures include good hygiene practices and avoiding contact with infected individuals.

## Highlights

- Significant surge in cases observed from June 2023, peaking in September 2023 with 125,264 cases. <br/>
- Zero deaths reported throughout the years, except in August 2019 and February 2020, with one and two deaths respectively. <br/>
- General trend shows fluctuation but recent months indicate a potential stabilization post-2023 surge. <br/>
- Current situation as of May 2024 shows modest case numbers at 2,920, a significant decrease from the 2023 peak. <br/>

Incidence sees more pronounced summer peaks from 2018, indicating possible alterations in the disease's behavior or reporting practices.

A striking upsurge occurs in 2023, with an exceptional outbreak in July (13,425 cases), August (12,742 cases), and a massive spike in September (125,264 cases). This suggests effective management and treatment of AHC within China's healthcare system.
