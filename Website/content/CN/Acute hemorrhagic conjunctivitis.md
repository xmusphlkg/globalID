# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious eye infection characterized by the sudden onset of redness, swelling, and bleeding in the conjunctiva, the thin membrane covering the white part of the eye and the inner eyelids. It is primarily caused by enteroviruses, such as Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24. Symptoms include eye pain, excessive tearing, and sensitivity to light. AHC spreads rapidly through direct contact with infected individuals or contaminated surfaces. While the condition is self-limiting and typically resolves within a week, maintaining good hygiene is crucial to prevent outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Trends**: Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis cases typically peak during the summer months, with notable increases observed from June to September each year. <br/>

- **Significant Outbreak in 2023**: A dramatic surge in cases was recorded in 2023, with a peak of 125,264 cases in September, indicating a major outbreak. <br/>

- **Mortality Rate**: The disease has maintained a low mortality rate, with only three deaths reported over the entire period, suggesting effective management and treatment. <br/>

- **Recent Decline**: As of February 2025, the number of cases has significantly decreased from the 2023 peak, indicating a return to more typical levels. <br/>

The data on Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows distinct trends. From 2015 to 2019, cases generally increased, with peaks in the summer months, aligning with the typical seasonal pattern of AHC. The highest number of cases during this period was in July 2019, with 5,265 cases, and the first recorded death occurred in August 2019, suggesting a possible increase in disease severity.

In 2020, a decline in cases is observed, likely due to COVID-19 public health measures like social distancing and improved hygiene, which reduced the transmission of AHC. However, 2023 saw a dramatic surge, with a peak in September recording 125,264 cases. This spike might be due to the relaxation of pandemic restrictions, leading to increased social interactions, or a new, more transmissible virus strain. The subsequent decline in cases towards the end of 2023 and into 2024 suggests effective public health interventions or increased population immunity.

Overall, the data highlights the need for continuous surveillance and adaptive public health strategies to manage AHC outbreaks effectively, especially in response to changing social behaviors and potential viral mutations.

Over the years, the number of cases fluctuates, with notable peaks in the summer months, which is typical for viral conjunctivitis outbreaks. The data indicates a significant spike in cases in 2023, particularly in July, August, and September, with September recording an exceptionally high number of cases at 125,264. Despite this surge, the number of deaths remains remarkably low, with only three deaths reported over the entire period, two of which occurred in February 2020.

This low mortality rate suggests that while AHC can spread rapidly and affect a large number of individuals, it is generally not life-threatening. The spike in 2023 could be attributed to various factors such as changes in virus virulence, public health measures, or reporting practices. The absence of deaths during this period, despite the high number of cases, underscores the effectiveness of medical interventions and public health strategies in managing the disease. Overall, the data highlights the importance of continuous monitoring and public health preparedness to manage such outbreaks effectively.
