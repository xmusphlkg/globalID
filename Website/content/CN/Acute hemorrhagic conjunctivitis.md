# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious eye infection caused by viruses such as enterovirus 70 and coxsackievirus A24. It presents with sudden onset of symptoms including redness, swelling, pain, tearing, and subconjunctival hemorrhages, which are small bleeds underneath the conjunctiva. AHC spreads rapidly through direct contact with eye secretions or contaminated surfaces, often leading to outbreaks in crowded areas. While the infection is typically self-limiting and resolves within one to two weeks, maintaining good hygiene and avoiding contact with infected individuals are crucial preventive measures.

- A significant spike in cases occurred in September 2023, with 125,264 cases, marking a sharp contrast to previous years, where monthly cases averaged around 3,000â€“5,000. <br/>

- Seasonal trends show peaks during summer and early fall, with cases generally tapering off towards the end of the year. <br/>

The reported cases of Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 exhibit periodic fluctuations, with noticeable peaks and declines over the years. Initially, a high number of cases were recorded in September 2014, with a noticeable decrease each month until February 2015. Afterward, a cyclical pattern is evident, with cases generally increasing towards the middle of each year and decreasing towards the year's end. This pattern suggests a seasonal effect on the transmission of AHC, potentially linked to climatic or behavioral factors typical of the warmer months that might facilitate the spread of the virus.

An anomaly appears in the data for 2023, with a sudden, dramatic spike in cases peaking in September with over 125,000 reported cases. This significant rise deviates sharply from the usual cyclical pattern observed in previous years. Such an unprecedented increase may indicate an outbreak influenced by factors such as a new viral strain, a large-scale public event, or other environmental or social changes that facilitated greater transmission. After this peak, the number of cases rapidly decreases towards the end of 2023, returning to the more typical figures seen in previous years by 2024. Despite these fluctuations, the consistent lack of fatalities across the dataset, save for three deaths in 2019 and 2020, highlights the generally non-lethal nature of AHC, although the disease's high transmissibility warrants continued monitoring and preventive measures.
