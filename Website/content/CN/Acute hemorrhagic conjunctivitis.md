# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious viral eye infection characterized by sudden onset of conjunctival redness, swelling, pain, and subconjunctival hemorrhages, which are small bleeds beneath the conjunctiva. Commonly known as "Apollo 11" disease, it spreads rapidly, especially in tropical and subtropical regions. AHC is primarily caused by enterovirus 70 or coxsackievirus A24 variant and is transmitted through direct contact with infected secretions, contaminated surfaces, or respiratory droplets. Symptoms usually appear within 24 to 48 hours of exposure. While self-limiting, AHC can cause significant discomfort and disruption but typically resolves within one to two weeks without long-term complications.

## Highlights

- A major outbreak occurred in 2023, peaking sharply in September with 125,264 reported cases, signaling an unprecedented epidemic event. <br/>
- After the 2023 surge, cases rapidly declined throughout late 2023 and continued to remain low through early 2025, suggesting effective control measures or natural epidemic resolution. <br/>
- From January to April 2025, monthly cases remained between 1,340 and 2,262, consistent with pre-2023 baseline levels, indicating no signs of resurgence. <br/>
- Zero deaths have been reported since March 2020, highlighting the low fatality rate and manageable clinical severity of the disease in the mainland. <br/>

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) exhibited strong seasonality in China, with a consistent rise in cases from late spring through summer, peaking typically between June and September each year. This pattern remained relatively stable from 2015 to 2019, with annual peak case numbers ranging from approximately 3,000 to over 5,000, and the remainder of the year exhibiting lower transmission levels. A notable anomaly occurred in July to October 2023, when cases escalated sharply, peaking exceptionally in September with 125,264 cases — over tenfold the previous peak values. This deviation suggests either a large outbreak, a change in reporting sensitivity, or a shift in pathogen dynamics or population susceptibility.

Despite the high case counts, deaths remained nearly absent throughout the observed period, with only three deaths reported from 2015 to 2025, indicating very low fatality risk. The 2020–2022 period, overlapping with national COVID-19 containment measures, showed a drop in AHC transmission compared to earlier years, potentially due to improved hygiene, reduced mobility, or school closures. After the dramatic spike in 2023, incidence returned quickly to baseline levels in late 2023 and remained low through early 2025, implying the outbreak was short-lived. These trends point to a strong influence of environmental and behavioral factors on transmission dynamics, warranting continued national surveillance and better characterization of potentially emerging serotypes.

Throughout the observed period from April 2015 to April 2025, Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) showed consistent patterns of widespread incidence but with an overwhelmingly low case fatality. Out of all recorded months, only three deaths occurred: one in August 2019 and two in February 2020. This is in stark contrast to the cumulative total of hundreds of thousands of reported cases across multiple years. The overwhelming concentration of zero deaths across nearly every month and year underscores the extremely low lethality of AHC, even during months when incidence peaked, such as the dramatic spike in September 2023 with over 125,000 reported cases and no associated fatalities.

The rare occurrences of death—particularly in 2019 and early 2020—do not appear to correspond to periods with exceptionally high case numbers compared to the subsequent 2023 outbreak. This suggests that these isolated deaths may be attributable to unusual complications, comorbidities, reporting inconsistencies, or factors independent of typical AHC progression. The acute nature of the disease and the data trend point toward AHC being a self-limiting and non-fatal condition under normal health care settings in mainland China, even during large-scale outbreaks.
