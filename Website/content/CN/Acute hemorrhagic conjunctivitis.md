# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious eye infection characterized by a sudden onset of symptoms, including redness, swelling, and bleeding under the conjunctiva, the membrane covering the white of the eye. Often caused by enteroviruses, specifically Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24, AHC is widespread in tropical regions with outbreaks frequently occurring in densely populated areas. Symptoms also include pain, photophobia, and a watery or mucous discharge. Transmission occurs through direct contact with infected secretions or contaminated surfaces. While usually self-limiting within one to two weeks, the high transmissibility often challenges public health systems.

## Highlights

- Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis peaked dramatically in September 2023 with 125,264 cases, indicating a significant outbreak. <br/>
- A notable decline in cases occurred post-October 2023, returning to pre-outbreak levels by August 2025. <br/>
- The disease showed a seasonal pattern with higher incidences usually observed from May to September each year. <br/>
- Despite the high number of cases, mortality remained extremely low, with very few deaths reported throughout the provided timeline, highlighting the disease's generally non-fatal nature. <br/>

The data on Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) in Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 highlights seasonal and annual case variations, with peaks during summer, specifically between June and August. In 2019, a significant peak occurred in July with 5,265 cases, followed by an unprecedented surge in 2023, peaking in September with 125,264 cases. These peaks suggest potential outbreaks or changes in environmental or socio-economic factors impacting transmission. Despite the high case numbers, deaths remain remarkably low, with only three recorded, indicating effective healthcare responses or the less lethal nature of the outbreaks.

Fluctuations in infection numbers across the years may result from public health interventions, mobility patterns, hygiene practices, or environmental factors. After 2023, a decline in cases is observed, suggesting successful containment or a natural resolution through population immunity or pathogen adaptation. The post-peak stabilization seen in subsequent years indicates a return to lower transmission rates comparable to pre-2023 levels. Moreover, the consistently low mortality rate highlights possible improvements in disease management or shifts in its severity. Continuous monitoring is essential for understanding how interventions, public behavior, and other factors influence AHC transmission dynamics and contribute to managing outbreak impacts effectively. This ongoing assessment is vital for informing public health strategies and mitigating future risks.
