# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious viral eye infection characterized by sudden onset of conjunctival redness, swelling, tearing, pain, and distinctive subconjunctival hemorrhages. It is primarily caused by enteroviruses, such as Enterovirus 70 and Coxsackievirus A24. AHC typically spreads rapidly in communities through direct contact with eye secretions, contaminated hands, or fomites. Though symptoms are alarming, including severe discomfort and photophobia, most cases resolve within one to two weeks without specific treatment. Complications are rare, but secondary bacterial infection may occasionally occur.

## Highlights

- Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis cases in Chinese mainland showed marked seasonality, peaking in summer and early autumn before declining in winter months.<br/>
- A dramatic surge occurred in September 2023 with over 125,000 cases, followed by a rapid return to baseline rates; such a spike may indicate an outbreak event.<br/>
- From early 2024 through December 2025, case numbers stabilized at pre-outbreak levels, with no significant outbreaks detected.<br/>
- The disease continues to have very low mortality: deaths are extremely rare, with no reported deaths since February 2020, indicating effective clinical management and low severity.<br/>

The monthly data for acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis in mainland China from late 2015 to the end of 2025 reveals a strikingly stable baseline punctuated by periods of seasonal fluctuation and an extraordinary spike in September 2023. For most years, cases peak consistently in the summer months, particularly June through September, then fall to lower levels through winter and early spring, mirroring patterns observed for other enterovirus-driven diseases. Notably, deaths are exceedingly rare, with only a handful of instances—specifically, a single death in August 2019 and two in February 2020—against a backdrop of tens of thousands of cases, underlining the generally mild prognosis of the outbreak.

Among all reported periods, September 2023 is exceptional, recording 125,264 cases—an order of magnitude above any prior or subsequent month. This surge is immediately preceded by a gradual increase starting in June and July 2023 and followed by a rapid return to baseline by November. The atypical magnitude of the September 2023 outbreak, vastly surpassing typical seasonal highs (usually in the 2,000–5,000 range), suggests an extraordinary event such as a major point-source outbreak, changes in surveillance/reporting practices, or possibly a significant shift in pathogen virulence or population susceptibility. Excluding this anomaly, trend analysis demonstrates low volatility, relatively consistent seasonality, and persistent absence of significant mortality, reflecting effective control measures and/or the relatively benign nature of acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis in this population.

Over the decade reflected in the surveillance data for acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis in mainland China, the reported mortality associated with this disease has been extremely low. Among tens of thousands of documented cases annually, only three deaths were recorded: one in August 2019 and two in February 2020. These deaths occurred in months with relatively high case counts, though no corresponding spike or unusual pattern emerged in subsequent case numbers or death registrations, suggesting these were isolated incidents rather than indicative of heightened acute severity or outbreaks with increased lethality.

The overall case fatality rate for the entire period remains negligibly close to zero. This pattern strongly aligns with the clinical understanding of acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis as a self-limiting viral illness that rarely results in serious complications. Periodic fluctuations in case numbers, including the exceptional surge observed in late 2023, do not coincide with any increase in deaths, further underscoring the low risk of mortality. This consistency provides robust epidemiological reassurance regarding the disease’s prognosis and suggests that, despite considerable endemic transmission, fatal outcomes are extraordinarily rare and possibly attributable to atypical clinical circumstances rather than inherent disease virulence.
