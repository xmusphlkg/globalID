# Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) is a highly contagious viral infection characterized by a sudden onset of symptoms including redness, swelling, pain, and subconjunctival hemorrhages in the eyes. Often caused by Enterovirus 70 or Coxsackievirus A24, AHC is transmitted through direct contact with infected individuals or contaminated surfaces. Commonly affecting communities in epidemic waves, the condition primarily resolves within one to two weeks without long-lasting effects, although supportive treatment such as artificial tears can alleviate discomfort. Preventive measures include good hygiene practices, such as regular handwashing, to curb the spread of the virus.

## Highlights

- **Steep Surge in 2023**: A dramatic spike in cases, peaking at 125,264 in September 2023, marked a significant outbreak. This indicates potential changes in virus virulence or transmission factors. <br/>

- **Decline Post-Surge**: Cases rapidly decreased to 4,940 in November 2023, followed by a gradual stabilization in numbers through 2024 and 2025. This decline suggests effective control measures may have been implemented. <br/>

- **Consistent Mortality Trend**: Despite the spikes in case numbers, mortality remains exceptionally low, with rare instances of death reported only in August 2019 and February 2020. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variation**: Historically, higher case numbers appear in warmer months, suggesting a seasonal impact on transmission rates. <br/>

The data on Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 highlights key trends. From 2015 to 2018, cases gradually increased, peaking during summer months due to warmer temperatures and higher humidity that promote viral spread. The seasonal pattern is evident, with spikes in summer and declines in winter. Notably, 2019 saw a significant increase in cases, peaking dramatically in July, and recorded the first-ever death in August, which is significant as previous years reported zero fatalities.

The trend altered in 2020, showing a decline potentially due to public health measures from the COVID-19 pandemic. However, 2023 saw an unexpected surge in cases, with a dramatic peak in September, suggesting a possible outbreak or changes in disease transmission. Despite high numbers, no deaths were reported, indicating either effective medical responses or a less virulent virus strain. This was followed by a reduction in cases in 2024 and 2025 as conditions returned to previous patterns.

This analysis underscores the cyclic nature of AHC incidence linked to seasonal changes and external public health factors. It emphasizes the need for continued public health attention and readiness to manage potential outbreaks, focusing on prevention and rapid response to reduce the disease's impact.

The data for Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis (AHC) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 demonstrates an exceptionally low mortality rate, with only a handful of deaths reported over the entire period. Notably, all recorded deaths occurred in two instances: one death in August 2019 and two deaths in February 2020. Given the large number of cases each year, this indicates that AHC is overwhelmingly non-fatal, aligning with typical clinical presentations of the disease which seldom lead to serious complications or hospitalization.

Furthermore, the data reveals significant seasonal trends, with higher case numbers occurring during the warmer months from May to September, which is consistent with the transmission patterns of enterovirus 70 and coxsackievirus A24, the primary pathogens responsible for AHC. Interestingly, a remarkable spike in cases is observed in 2023, particularly in September, yet this surge did not correlate with an increase in mortality. The overall stability in death figures suggests effective management of cases despite fluctuations in yearly incidence, illustrating that preventive measures and public health strategies implemented to control AHC outbreaks have maintained their efficacy in preventing fatalities.
