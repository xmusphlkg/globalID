# Anthrax

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Anthrax is a life-threatening infectious disease caused by Bacillus anthracis, a spore-forming bacterium. It primarily affects herbivorous animals, but humans can contract it through direct or indirect contact with infected animals or contaminated animal products. The disease manifests in three forms: cutaneous, inhalation, and gastrointestinal, each varying in severity. Cutaneous anthrax, the most common form, occurs through skin contact with spores, while inhalation anthrax, the most severe, results from breathing in spores. Gastrointestinal anthrax arises from consuming contaminated meat. Effective antibiotics and vaccines are available, but swift diagnosis and treatment are crucial for favorable outcomes.

## Highlights

- Anthrax cases in mainland China have fluctuated over the years, with a notable rise in cases during late summer months, often exceeding 100 cases, including notably 106 in August 2025.<br/>
- Mortality remains relatively low, averaging about one death per month in periods of higher incidence, demonstrating improved management and treatment strategies.<br/>
- Seasonal trends persist, with the period from July to September consistently witnessing peak case numbers likely linked to environmental or agricultural factors.<br/>
- The overall trend indicates steady case occurrence with occasional spikes, highlighting a persistent risk requiring continued surveillance and preventive measures.<br/>

Analysis of anthrax cases in the Chinese mainland from September 2015 to September 2025 reveals several trends in incidence and mortality. During this decade, there was considerable fluctuation in case numbers, with a noticeable annual increase in the summer months, particularly in July and August. This seasonal trend is likely influenced by environmental factors conducive to anthrax outbreaks, such as agricultural activities and the lifecycle of Bacillus anthracis. Peaks are observed in particular years, notably in August 2018, August 2021, August 2023, and August 2025, with cases reaching over 100 in these months. This suggests a recurring pattern, potentially linked to specific climatic conditions or socio-economic activities during the peak months such as handling of livestock, which could warrant focused preventive measures.

Mortality has been relatively low compared to the number of reported cases. Occasional deaths were reported in certain months, such as May 2016, June 2017, August 2018, July 2023, and during several months in 2024. The overall case fatality ratio appears restrained, reflecting possible improvements in healthcare response, early diagnosis, and treatment efforts. However, the sharp rise in cases during particular months, combined with sporadic fatalities, highlights the importance of robust monitoring systems and public health preparedness, especially during peak periods. Prioritizing vaccination programs for livestock, public awareness campaigns, and disrupting transmission routes remains integral to reducing both incidence and fatalities in the long term.

Anthrax-related deaths in mainland China have remained relatively low, indicating effective disease management strategies despite periodic spikes in case numbers. From the data spanning September 2015 to September 2025, there is a discernible pattern of higher anthrax cases in the summer months, specifically July and August. These months historically show increased cases—explained by factors such as environmental conditions or increased human-animal interaction during this time—but even then, anthrax deaths do not follow a strict cyclical pattern. The number of deaths per year has generally remained low, ranging from zero to three deaths annually, highlighting effective public health interventions. Quick response treatment protocols manage severe cases efficiently, minimizing fatalities.

Certain months showed isolated reports of deaths, drawing attention to the sporadic nature of anthrax fatalities. For instance, June 2017, December 2017, August 2018, and August 2021 saw two deaths in each period. These sporadic death instances could relate to several factors, including variations in strains, effectiveness of the vaccination programs in rural and high-risk areas, and possibly the availability of potent antibiotics. Moreover, the pattern does not indicate a significant upward trend in deaths over the years, which is a favorable indicator of effective disease control measures in place. The steady anthrax management combined with ongoing improvements in surveillance and rapid response initiatives exhibit the efficiency in handling potential public health threats.
