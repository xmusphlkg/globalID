# Anthrax

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by the spore-forming bacterium Bacillus anthracis. It primarily affects herbivorous animals but can also infect humans through direct or indirect contact with contaminated animal products. The disease can manifest in four forms: cutaneous, inhalational, gastrointestinal, and injectional, each with varying severity. Symptoms range from skin sores to severe respiratory issues and gastrointestinal distress. Anthrax was historically used as a biological weapon and remains a concern due to its potential in bioterrorism. Effective treatments include antibiotics and antitoxins, and a vaccine is available for at-risk populations.

## Highlights

- Anthrax cases in China show seasonal peaks, with higher incidences in July-August. <br/>
- Deaths are sporadic but have been reported almost every year since 2015, with a notable spike in 2018 and consistent occurrences thereafter. <br/>
- The trend indicates a decreasing monthly case count since the peak observed in August 2023. <br/>
- Despite some years with high case numbers, the mortality rate remains relatively low, suggesting effective management of the disease. <br/>

Anthrax cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 show clear seasonal trends, with consistent peaks during the summer, especially in August. These surges are most notable in years like 2018, 2021, 2023, and 2024, during which August cases exceeded 100. This seasonal spike suggests strong environmental or occupational links, potentially tied to livestock exposure during high-activity agricultural months. Case numbers are generally lower from January to April, indicating a pattern likely shaped by climate or farming cycles.

Despite fluctuations in case numbers, reported deaths remained low and sporadic, with most months recording zero fatalities. Occasional spikes in mortality align with high-incidence months, such as August 2018, July 2021, and August through October 2024—each with at least one death. While these figures remain modest, these periods may represent gaps in early diagnosis or delayed access to treatment. The general decline in deaths over time, despite rising or sustained case counts in some years, may reflect improved health infrastructure or targeted response efforts. Given this pattern, public health strategies should prioritize preventive education and early intervention during late summer months when risk is consistently elevated. Consistent surveillance and timely veterinary control in high-risk regions could help reduce transmission and maintain effective fatality prevention.
