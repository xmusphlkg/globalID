# Anthrax

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium Bacillus anthracis. It commonly affects domestic and wild animals, but humans can become infected through direct or indirect contact with sick animals. Anthrax spores can enter the body through skin cuts, inhalation, or ingestion, leading to different forms of the illness: cutaneous, inhalational, or gastrointestinal. Symptoms vary but can include skin sores, fever, and respiratory distress. Without prompt treatment, particularly for inhalational anthrax, the disease can be fatal. Vaccination and antibiotics can prevent infection, and anthrax is considered a potential bioterrorism threat due to its spore-forming capability.

## Highlights

- Anthrax cases show seasonal peaks, particularly in August, with an increase observed from 2014 to 2024. <br/>
- August 2023 and 2024 saw higher-than-average cases, with 123 and 91 cases, respectively. <br/>
- Deaths remain low relative to cases, with notable spikes in 2016, 2021, and 2024, each recording at least one death in August. <br/>
- Although cases decreased slightly in 2024 compared to 2023, the presence of fatalities, such as in August 2024, indicates ongoing risks. <br/>

The data on anthrax cases in mainland China from August 2014 to August 2024 reveals periodic fluctuations in the number of reported cases, with notable seasonal trends and sporadic spikes. High numbers of cases tend to occur during the summer months (July and August) in most years, with some peak years showing exceptionally elevated case counts, such as August 2018 (103 cases), August 2021 (115 cases), and August 2023 (123 cases). This seasonal pattern may reflect heightened human-animal contact, particularly in rural or agricultural regions, during these months. Deaths are generally low compared to the number of cases, with the highest count being two deaths in August 2022 and August 2023.

The annual trends suggest periods of increased anthrax transmission in particular years, with clusters of high incidence seen in 2015, 2016, 2018, 2021, 2023, and early 2024. Periods of lower transmission are also evident, such as in 2015 and 2017, where fewer than 10 cases per month were reported during the winter and early spring months. The overall trend indicates that while the disease remains present across these years, the number of deaths is relatively low, highlighting either effective treatment measures or potentially lower virulence in the reported cases. However, occasional spikes in both cases and deaths suggest that vigilance and preventive measures remain important.
