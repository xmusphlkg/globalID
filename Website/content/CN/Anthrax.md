# Anthrax

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium Bacillus anthracis. It primarily affects livestock and wild animals, but humans can become infected through contact with contaminated animals or animal products. There are three main forms of anthrax: cutaneous, inhalational, and gastrointestinal, each varying in severity. Cutaneous anthrax, the most common form, occurs through skin contact, while inhalational anthrax, the most deadly, results from inhaling spores. Vaccines are available for at-risk populations, and antibiotics can effectively treat infections if administered promptly. Anthrax is classified as a potential bioterrorism agent due to its resilience and lethality.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Anthrax cases in the Chinese mainland consistently peak during the summer months, especially in July and August, with the highest cases often in August. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** Deaths remain low compared to cases, with occasional spikes, notably in August 2018 and August 2022, each recording two deaths. <br/>

- **Recent Increase:** A noticeable rise in cases occurred from 2023 to 2024, peaking at 123 cases in August 2023, followed by high numbers in 2024. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of January 2025, cases have decreased to 21, with no reported deaths, suggesting a potential decline in the outbreak. <br/>

Over the ten-year period, the number of cases varied significantly, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months, particularly in July and August. This seasonal pattern suggests a possible link to environmental or occupational factors, such as increased agricultural activity, which is known to influence anthrax outbreaks. Despite these fluctuations, the number of deaths remained low, with only sporadic fatalities reported, indicating effective medical interventions and public health measures in place to manage the disease.

Analyzing the data further, the years with the highest number of cases, such as 2018 and 2021, did not correspond to a proportionate increase in deaths, which remained minimal. This suggests that while the incidence of anthrax can be variable, the healthcare system's ability to treat and manage the disease effectively prevents a high mortality rate. The sporadic nature of deaths, with no more than two deaths in any given month, underscores the effectiveness of current treatment protocols and possibly the availability of vaccines for at-risk populations. Overall, the data reflects a controlled public health situation regarding anthrax, with ongoing vigilance required to maintain low mortality rates.
