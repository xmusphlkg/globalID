# Anthrax

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium Bacillus anthracis. It primarily affects animals such as cattle, sheep, and goats, but can also infect humans who come into contact with contaminated animal products. The disease manifests in different forms: cutaneous, inhalational, and gastrointestinal, with inhalational anthrax being the most deadly. Symptoms vary based on the entry point, ranging from skin sores to severe respiratory distress. Anthrax spores, which are highly resilient, can persist in the environment for long periods, making them a potential bioterrorism threat. Early diagnosis and antibiotics are essential for effective treatment.

## Highlights

- Anthrax cases in mainland China have demonstrated seasonal peaks, notably in July and August, with the highest case count in August 2023 at 123 cases. <br/>
- The disease shows a recurring increase in the warmer months, followed by a decline in colder months, highlighting a seasonal trend. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations, Anthrax-related deaths remain low, with sporadic fatalities such as one death reported almost yearly during peaks. <br/>
- Recent data from 2024 indicates an ongoing trend with elevated cases in late summer to early fall, including 90 cases in September and a slight increase in associated deaths, suggesting continued vigilance and public health intervention are needed. <br/>

Analyzing the reported data on Anthrax cases in mainland China from October 2014 to October 2024 reveals several key patterns and trends. Firstly, there is a pronounced seasonal pattern, with cases generally increasing during the summer and autumn months, specifically from June to September, which may point to occupational or environmental factors associated with agricultural or farming activities prevalent during these periods. This seasonal fluctuation is evident across multiple years; for example, significant spikes in cases are observed in July and August each year, reaching a peak of 123 cases in August 2023.

Over the years, the data indicate a considerable yearly variation in the number of cases and deaths attributed to Anthrax. Notably, certain years record higher incidences without corresponding increases in mortality. Despite some years with peaks such as in August 2018, August 2021, and August 2023, the mortality rate remains relatively low, suggesting effective clinical management and public health responses. Overall, these observations suggest that while Anthrax remains a recurrent issue within these regions, public health measures possibly mitigate the lethality, keeping the death toll from rising proportionally with case numbers, indicating ongoing monitoring and adaptation of strategies are likely in place to manage outbreaks.

Analyzing anthrax-related deaths in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 highlights notable trends in morbidity and mortality. Over the decade, deaths are sporadic and infrequent, despite periodic spikes in the number of cases, suggesting that effective management strategies are likely mitigating potential fatalities. The first recorded death occurs in December 2015, introducing a pattern of occasional fatalities that do not necessarily align with the monthly peaks in reported cases, which occur primarily in the warmer months, such as July and August.

The data underline that although anthrax incidences sometimes rise significantly, they rarely translate into proportional mortality, indicating robust prevention, diagnosis, and treatment protocols. For instance, high case reports in August 2016, August 2017, and August 2021 coincide with very low death rates, pointing to successful health interventions and response strategies that have limited the disease's fatal impact effectively. This trend illustrates not only the control successes over anthrax but also points to the necessity for ongoing vigilance given the disease's potential to cause sporadic fatalities, as seen in June 2017 and August 2018.

Thus, while anthrax remains a public health challenge due to its infectious nature, the low number of deaths compared to cases suggests that mainland China's healthcare measures are effectively containing its lethality, fostering an environment that prevents widespread outbreaks from becoming overly deadly.
