# Anthrax

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Bacillus anthracis*. It primarily affects herbivorous mammals but can infect humans through contact with infected animals or contaminated animal products. The spores are highly resilient, capable of surviving in soil for decades. Human anthrax occurs in four forms: cutaneous, inhalation, gastrointestinal, and injection, with inhalation anthrax being the most severe. Symptoms vary by form but often include fever, fatigue, and severe respiratory distress in inhalation cases. Prompt diagnosis and antibiotic treatment are crucial for improved outcomes. Anthrax is also considered a potential biological weapon.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal trend**: Anthrax cases consistently peak between July and September, with August showing the highest incidence almost every year, indicating strong seasonal influence.<br/>
- **Mortality remains low**: Despite yearly fluctuations in case numbers, deaths are rare, with most months reporting zero fatalities. The highest annual death count was three, recorded in 2017 and 2024.<br/>
- **Recent stability**: From January to May 2025, case counts have remained stable (between 13–23 per month) with zero deaths, suggesting continued effective disease management.<br/>
- **Long-term pattern**: While the total number of cases varies by year, overall annual incidence has remained moderate, indicating a persistent but controlled epidemiological profile for anthrax in China.<br/>

From 2015 through mid-2025, Anthrax remained a persistently reported zoonotic disease in mainland China, exhibiting clear seasonal fluctuations with marked peaks during the summer months, particularly July and August. These surges are most notable in years such as 2016, 2018, 2021, and 2023, where August saw over 100 reported cases. This pattern aligns with increased animal handling and livestock movement during warmer periods when Bacillus anthracis spores are more likely to be aerosolized or transmitted through direct contact. Additionally, a general trend is observed where annual case totals increased unevenly into the late 2010s and early 2020s before stabilizing again post-2021, with significant year-to-year variability.

Mortality remained relatively low, with an overall case fatality rate under 1.5%, though there were infrequent spikes in deaths—such as June and December 2017, August 2018, and the latter months of 2024—that may warrant deeper investigation into outbreak circumstances or diagnostic delays. The vast majority of months reported zero deaths, indicating either timely treatment or a predominance of cutaneous anthrax presentations. The years 2021, 2023, and 2024 each recorded multiple fatalities, which contrasted the largely non-lethal patterns of preceding years, suggesting either localized outbreaks with higher virulence, delayed access to care, or reporting improvements.

Anthrax-related mortality in mainland China from 2015 through mid-2025 appears sporadic and remains low relative to incidence. While the majority of months reported zero deaths despite fluctuation in case counts, distinct surges in fatalities are observable in specific periods. The most concentrated mortality events occurred in August and December 2018, June and December 2017, and August 2022—months associated with both higher caseloads and elevated death counts. These instances suggest a potential link between delayed diagnosis or severe exposure events during peak transmission periods, often corresponding with summer months when livestock exposure increases, possibly due to agricultural practices.

A rise in annual death totals is noticed post-2021, particularly in 2024, which alone accounted for five deaths—an unusually high figure compared to previous years. Despite this, no year has shown a linear proportionality between rising case numbers and death tolls, implying effective clinical management in most instances. Evaluating the death-to-case ratio, rare months such as April 2018 (1 death in 19 cases) and September 2023 (1 death in 61 cases) reflect higher lethality proportions despite moderate case loads, pointing toward potential anomalies in strain virulence, geographic healthcare disparities, or delays in case detection. Overall, anthrax remains a low-mortality zoonosis in China, yet periodic monitoring of death clusters is essential for early identification of health system vulnerabilities or shifts in pathogen behavior.
