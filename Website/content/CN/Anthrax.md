# Anthrax

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Anthrax is an acute infectious disease caused by the bacterium Bacillus anthracis. It primarily affects herbivorous animals but can also infect humans through contact with contaminated animals, products, or spores. There are four forms: cutaneous, inhalation, gastrointestinal, and injection, each varying in severity. Cutaneous is the most common and least severe, while inhalation anthrax is more deadly. The bacteria produce toxins that cause severe illness and, if untreated, can be fatal. Vaccines and antibiotics like ciprofloxacin and doxycycline are effective for prevention and treatment. Control measures focus on animal vaccination and safe handling of potentially infected materials.

## Highlights

- Anthrax cases show seasonal peaks, particularly in August and September, with the highest cases reported in August 2018 and 2023. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations, a recurring trend of increased cases during summer months is observed, indicating possible environmental or agricultural influences. <br/>
- Mortality remains low, with only periodic deaths occurring; most notably, multiple deaths were recorded during August in recent years. <br/>
- The overall trend from 2014 to 2024 depicts occasional spikes in cases, with a notable increase in summer months, suggesting a need for heightened vigilance and preventive measures during these periods. <br/>

The anthrax case data from mainland China between 2014 and 2024 reveals a generally increasing trend in reported cases over the years, with noticeable fluctuations throughout this period. The data indicates that during the summer months, particularly from July to September, there is a substantial rise in cases, which could be attributed to environmental or occupational factors, as anthrax outbreaks are often linked with agricultural activities and animal handling. The peak in cases during August 2023 and similar high numbers in August 2018 suggest recurring seasonal outbreaks, warranting further investigation into environmental or behavioral drivers that may influence transmission dynamics.

The mortality associated with anthrax, although relatively low compared to the number of cases, shows sporadic incidences across the years. Notably, deaths were reported in December 2015, May 2016, August 2016, June 2017, December 2017, April 2018, August 2018, January 2019, August 2019, July 2021, August 2021, August 2022, July 2023, September 2023, May 2024, August 2024, and September 2024. This sporadic mortality could reflect the effectiveness of medical interventions or varying virulence of specific outbreaks. The data may also highlight areas with gaps in healthcare access, delays in treatment, or challenges in public health response and prevention measures, underpinning the need for localized intervention strategies and targeted education on the risks associated with anthrax exposure.

The data for anthrax deaths in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows that fatalities remain relatively low despite periodic increases in reported cases. Out of the total 4,143 cases recorded over this period, only 17 deaths were reported. Deaths appeared sporadically, with no deaths reported in some years and occasional spikes in specific months. For instance, while 2017 and 2018 saw a slight increase in deaths (notably in June and August), the highest number of cases generally occurred in the summer months, particularly in August, with notable peaks in 2021 (115 cases), 2023 (123 cases), and 2024 (91 cases), yet without a proportional rise in deaths.

The sporadic nature of anthrax-related deaths suggests effective case management or that many infections may be cutaneous, which typically has a lower fatality rate. However, occasional deaths, such as those in August 2022 and July 2023, emphasize the persistent threat posed by anthrax. The relatively low mortality rate, around 0.4%, indicates that while anthrax cases can increase, the disease remains largely under control with limited impact on mortality in most cases. Nonetheless, monitoring and preparedness should continue, especially during months of increased case occurrence, which tend to follow seasonal patterns.
