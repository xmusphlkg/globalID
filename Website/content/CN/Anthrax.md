# Anthrax

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Bacillus anthracis*. It primarily affects herbivorous animals, but humans can become infected through contact with infected animals or contaminated animal products. The disease exists in four forms: cutaneous, inhalation, gastrointestinal, and injection, with inhalation being the most severe. *B. anthracis* forms durable spores that can survive in the environment for decades. Though rare in industrialized nations, anthrax remains a public health concern in regions lacking effective veterinary controls. Prompt diagnosis and early antibiotic treatment are essential to improve outcomes and prevent serious complications or death.

## Highlights

- Anthrax in mainland China shows a consistent seasonal trend, peaking between July and September annually, with August typically recording the highest monthly case numbers. <br/>
- Overall incidence appears stable with modest fluctuations; cases have remained under 130 per month since 2015, and fatality rates continue to be low, indicating effective diagnosis and case management. <br/>
- The majority of deaths are isolated and rare, with no year exceeding three deaths, suggesting that while the disease persists, its lethality remains limited. <br/>
- As of April 2025, the situation appears controlled, with only 18 cases and no deaths reported, consistent with previous low transmission levels during the spring season. <br/>

Between 2015 and 2025, anthrax cases in mainland China displayed a consistent seasonal pattern, peaking primarily in the summer months—typically July and August—followed by a gradual decline through autumn and winter. For instance, in August alone, several years such as 2016 (87 cases), 2018 (103 cases), 2021 (115 cases), 2023 (123 cases), and 2024 (91 cases) recorded notable spikes. This seasonality reflects environmental and occupational factors, particularly increased human-livestock interaction during agricultural seasons in rural regions. Annual case totals also demonstrated variability but indicated a mild upward trend up to 2023.

Mortality remained low despite fluctuations in case volume, with sporadic deaths clustered in months with high exposure, such as August 2018 (2 deaths), August 2021 (1 death), and August 2022 (2 deaths). The years 2017 and 2024 were notable for having multiple months with deaths—December and June 2017 (2 deaths each) and three consecutive months in late 2024 (August to October), each reporting one death. The low case-fatality rate suggests effective clinical management and early case detection. However, the recurrence of cases and deaths in peak months underlines persistent zoonotic risk, indicating continued need for targeted surveillance and public health interventions in endemic areas.
