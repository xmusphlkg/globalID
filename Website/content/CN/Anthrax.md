# Anthrax

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Anthrax is an acute infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Bacillus anthracis*. It primarily affects herbivorous animals but can also infect humans through contact with infected animals or contaminated animal products. The disease manifests in three forms: cutaneous, inhalational, and gastrointestinal, depending on the route of exposure. Cutaneous anthrax is the most common and least lethal, while inhalational anthrax is rare but often fatal if untreated. Anthrax spores are highly resilient and can survive in soil for decades, presenting ongoing challenges for public health and biosecurity.

## Highlights

- Anthrax in the Chinese mainland shows a marked seasonal increase during July to September, with case numbers peaking consistently in August each year.<br/>
- Annual case counts have remained stable since 2019, with no dramatic outbreaks; monthly cases range from around 10 to 120, and the highest peaks are short-lived.<br/>
- Mortality remains low, with deaths primarily occurring in months of peak transmission; occasional spikes in August and September rarely exceed two deaths per month.<br/>
- As of December 2025, the situation appears under control with 23 cases and no reported deaths, indicating continued effective surveillance and case management.<br/>

Analysis of anthrax data in the Chinese mainland over the last decade shows a distinct seasonal pattern, with case numbers peaking each summer—especially in July, August, and September. Annual summer surges are evident, such as August 2018 (103 cases), August 2021 (115 cases), August 2023 (123 cases), and August 2025 (106 cases). The remainder of each year typically sees far fewer cases, often under 30 per month, emphasizing the strong link to environmental or agricultural activities during warmer months. This pattern likely reflects increased livestock exposure and heightened risk from handling contaminated animal products or soils during these periods.

Fatality numbers remain low and sporadic, mostly isolated incidents rather than concentrated during peak outbreaks, though summer months with higher cases sometimes coincide with increased deaths, such as 2022, 2024, and 2025. The low overall case fatality suggests effective detection or response but does not eliminate the risk, as annual surges still result in occasional deaths. While the total annual cases fluctuate, the pronounced seasonality persists throughout the dataset, indicating consistent underlying transmission dynamics tied to agricultural or environmental factors. These trends highlight the ongoing need for targeted surveillance and prevention in high-risk months to reduce both case incidence and mortality.

Analysis of anthrax mortality in mainland China from December 2015 to December 2025 indicates consistently low deaths relative to case numbers, with fatalities scattered irregularly across months and years. Deaths rarely exceed two per month and are not clustered over consecutive periods. The data show that even during peak notification months—such as August 2018, August 2021, and August 2023—mortality remains minimal, typically at zero or one death despite surges in cases. Overall, yearly death counts average between one and five, signaling an unstable but low mortality burden.

The bulk of deaths coincides with months of higher anthrax incidence, usually July to October, yet the pattern is not strictly proportional: some high-case months record no deaths, and fatalities can occasionally appear in low-incidence periods. This irregularity implies that case management and access to timely treatment are generally effective, though sporadic deaths suggest that outbreaks or delays in care still impact vulnerable groups in select regions. The absence of prolonged mortality spikes or substantial case-fatality rates aligns with sustained efforts in anthrax surveillance, public health response, and veterinary controls. Nonetheless, the scattered nature of deaths across a decade of data emphasizes the need for ongoing vigilance, targeted health education for high-risk populations, and continuous improvement of early detection and intervention strategies.
