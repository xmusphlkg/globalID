# Brucellosis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infectious disease caused by bacteria of the genus Brucella. It primarily affects livestock, such as cows, goats, sheep, and pigs, and can be transmitted to humans through direct contact with infected animals, consumption of contaminated animal products, or inhalation of airborne agents. In humans, brucellosis can cause flu-like symptoms, including fever, sweats, malaise, and muscle pain, and may lead to more severe complications if untreated. The disease is endemic in many regions worldwide, particularly in the Mediterranean, Middle East, and parts of Asia and Africa. Control measures focus on vaccination of animals and safe food practices.

## Highlights

- Brucellosis in mainland China exhibits a cyclical pattern with peaks in warmer months and declines towards year-end, suggesting a seasonal trend. <br/>

- From 2014 to 2024, Brucellosis cases have generally increased, with some months exceeding 9000 cases, particularly between 2021 and 2023. <br/>

- Deaths remain low over the years, indicating effective treatment and management strategies. There are occasional peaks in mortality but no significant upward trend. <br/>

- As of October 2024, a decline in cases compared to earlier months indicates potential success in interventions or natural seasonal decreases, reflecting a temporary control over the disease. <br/>

This period coincides with increased interactions between humans and livestock, potentially heightening disease transmission. Over the years, there is a notable rise in annual case numbers, with significant peaks in 2022 and 2023, which may reflect either higher exposure or improved disease reporting. Despite the increase in cases, the number of deaths remains low, indicating effective treatment and management of the disease.

Annually, there is a consistent pattern where case numbers decline at the end of the year and during early winter, as seen in December and January data. This trend could be related to reduced agricultural activities and animal interactions during colder months, impacting transmission dynamics. The cycle of Brucellosis cases rising sharply in spring and summer suggests peak exposure periods during these seasons. Continuing low fatality rates highlight successful public health responses in managing severe outcomes. However, the increasing number of cases suggests persisting transmission, likely owing to ongoing challenges in livestock management or exposure pathways that need addressing through targeted interventions.
