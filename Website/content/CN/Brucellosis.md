# Brucellosis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by bacteria of the genus Brucella, primarily affecting livestock like cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs. Humans typically contract the disease through direct contact with infected animals or by consuming unpasteurized dairy products. It is characterized by fever, malaise, night sweats, and muscle pain, with potential complications affecting the liver, heart, or nervous system if untreated. The disease is prevalent in regions where animal vaccination programs are limited, and surveillance is inadequate. Prevention includes pasteurizing milk, using protective gear when handling animals, and implementing vaccination and control measures in livestock.

## Highlights

1. **Rising Incidence:** Brucellosis cases have generally increased since 2023, with monthly counts often exceeding 7000, indicating growing transmission.<br/>

2. **Seasonal Peaks:** Infections consistently peak from May to August each year, reflecting a clear seasonal pattern linked to environmental or occupational factors.<br/>

3. **Low Mortality:** Despite high case numbers, deaths remain rare and sporadic, typically one or two per month at most.<br/>

4. **Current Status (May 2025):** Cases in May 2025 (6982) are slightly lower than previous Mays in 2023–2024, suggesting a stable but still elevated disease burden.<br/>

From 2015 to 2025, Brucellosis in mainland China exhibited clear seasonal patterns, with cases typically peaking between May and August and declining during colder months. Peaks were especially notable in mid-2021 and 2022, with June and July showing the highest monthly counts exceeding 9,000 cases in some years. This pattern suggests seasonal occupational exposure, likely linked to livestock handling and farming during warmer months. Monthly cases steadily increased over the years, with 2023 and 2024 also reporting consistently high figures, though they did not surpass 2022’s peak levels.

Despite fluctuations in case numbers, deaths per month remained low. Most months reported zero fatalities, with only isolated months — such as July 2018 and September 2023 — showing two deaths at most. This indicates a stable and low case-fatality ratio across the ten-year span, possibly reflecting effective detection and treatment practices. The rise in reported cases may reflect broader surveillance coverage or improved diagnostic efforts rather than increased disease severity. Overall, while Brucellosis remains a persistent public health concern, mortality has not risen in proportion to incidence, underscoring the importance of seasonal risk mitigation and continued veterinary-public health coordination, particularly during spring and summer months when transmission risk is highest.

The deaths appear sporadic rather than seasonal, with no clear cyclical pattern, although there are isolated months with multiple deaths—such as July 2018 (2 deaths) and September 2023 (2 deaths)—amid generally zero mortality months. The lethality of Brucellosis throughout the reported period seems more associated with discrete events rather than any sustained increase in diagnostic case load or identifiable demographic shifts.

In years with higher case counts such as 2021 and 2022, deaths did not increase proportionately, suggesting either improvements in case management or variability in reporting sensitivity for fatal outcomes.
