# Brucellosis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic bacterial infection caused by the genus Brucella, primarily affecting livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs. Humans typically contract the disease through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of raw or undercooked animal products like milk and cheese. Symptoms in humans include fever, fatigue, joint pain, and headache, often resembling the flu, potentially leading to chronic conditions if untreated. Diagnosis is usually confirmed through blood tests, and treatment involves prolonged antibiotic courses. Preventative measures include vaccinating animals, pasteurizing dairy products, and using protective equipment when handling animals.

## Highlights

- **Case Trends:** Brucellosis cases in mainland China show seasonal fluctuations, peaking from March to July annually. The highest peak was in June 2021, with 9670 cases. As of March 2025, 5575 cases indicate a moderate resurgence compared to early 2024.  
<br/>  
- **Mortality Trends:** Deaths are rare, with typically fewer than three monthly. No deaths were reported in March 2025, and the highest monthly death count was two.  
<br/>  
- **Temporal Factors:** Cases are higher during spring and summer, likely due to increased exposure during agricultural activities.  
<br/>  
- **Control Progress:** Declining cases and stable mortality rates suggest effective public health measures, although vigilance is crucial during peak periods.

The data reveals seasonal and temporal variability in reported brucellosis cases in mainland China, with an overall pattern of higher incidence during warmer months (May–September) each year. Peaks in cases are consistently observed during the late spring and summer, suggesting a possible link to increased human exposure to infected animals during this period, likely tied to agricultural or pastoral activities. A particularly pronounced surge in cases occurred from 2021 to 2023, with monthly figures in the summers of 2021 and 2022 exceeding 9,000 cases, and post-2021 case numbers remaining elevated compared to earlier years. However, annual trends suggest periodic declines in winter, with cases bottoming out between November and February.

Mortality during the period studied remained low, with reported deaths sporadic and occurring in very small numbers relative to cases. Notable death peaks included July 2021 (2 deaths), July 2023 (1 death), and sporadic fatalities scattered across other months and years, suggesting the disease has a low case fatality rate but could have a heavier impact on certain vulnerable populations. These data suggest that while brucellosis remains a public health concern due to its consistent seasonal recurrence and rising case counts in some years, it is characterized by low associated mortality. Future studies may benefit from focusing on understanding regional or behavioral risk factors driving these spikes and enforcing control measures during the high-risk months.
