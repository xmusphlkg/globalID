# Brucellosis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by bacteria of the genus Brucella, primarily affecting livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs. Humans typically acquire the disease through direct contact with infected animals or by consuming contaminated animal products, particularly unpasteurized milk and cheese. Symptoms in humans can include fever, sweats, malaise, anorexia, headache, and muscle pain, often resembling flu-like illnesses. In severe cases, it can lead to more serious complications like endocarditis or arthritis. Preventative measures include animal vaccination, pasteurizing dairy products, and using protective gear when handling animals.

## Highlights

- Brucellosis cases have shown a general upward trend, peaking during the summer months and declining during the winter. <br/>
  
- The number of deaths remains low, with occasional spikes, indicating a relatively low case-fatality rate. <br/>
  
- Recent data for May 2024 indicates 8007 cases with 1 death, showing a continued high incidence compared to earlier years. <br/>
  
- Fluctuations in case numbers suggest potential seasonal and regional variations in disease transmission. <br/>

An observable pattern is the higher incidence of cases during the spring and summer months, possibly attributable to seasonal factors affecting the livestock population and human interaction with animals. For instance, the number of cases repeatedly spikes around March to July every year, with a noticeable decline towards the end of the year. This cyclical pattern points to potential agricultural cycles or fluctuations in rural activities that heighten exposure risks during this period.

Deaths attributed to Brucellosis during the period remain sporadic and relatively low, with a maximum of only two deaths in a given month, indicating a low mortality rate for the disease. High peaks in cases, such as the approximately 10,000 cases reported in June 2022, contrast with the significantly lower figures in the following months, suggesting effective containment or seasonality impacts. Furthermore, while the fluctuations in monthly cases underline the epidemic's dynamic nature, the year-on-year increases, especially noticeable in years like 2023 and 2024, suggest other contributing factors beyond seasonality, possibly including changes in surveillance, reporting practices, or outbreaks.
