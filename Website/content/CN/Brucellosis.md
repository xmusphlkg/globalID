# Brucellosis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a contagious bacterial infection caused by the genus Brucella. It primarily affects livestock, including cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs, although it can be transmitted to humans through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of contaminated animal products. The disease causes reproductive issues in animals, such as abortions and infertility. In humans, Brucellosis presents with flu-like symptoms, including fever, sweats, malaise, and joint pain, and can lead to severe complications if untreated. It is prevalent worldwide, particularly in areas with inadequate veterinary public health measures. Control strategies include vaccination of livestock and pasteurization of dairy products.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend:** From 2015 to 2025, Brucellosis cases have increased, peaking in spring and summer, indicating potential climatic or agricultural influences. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns:** Cases consistently rise during March to July and decrease towards winter, highlighting a seasonal fluctuation likely tied to environmental factors. <br/>

- **Low Mortality:** There is a consistently low fatality rate over the years, indicating effective treatment despite the rising case numbers. <br/>

- **Recent Trends:** As of August 2025, thereâ€™s a decline from earlier peaks in the year, continuing seasonal patterns and suggesting sustained control efforts are in place. <br/>

The analysis of Brucellosis cases in mainland China from 2015 to August 2025 reveals several notable patterns. Initially, between 2015 and 2016, case numbers fluctuated but generally trended downward after a peak in August 2015. This decline during winter months likely reflects seasonal impacts on disease transmission. From mid-2016 to 2017, cases followed a cyclical pattern, with higher incidence during spring and summer, suggesting a link to livestock-related activities, as Brucellosis is often transmitted through contact with infected animals.

Between 2018 and 2023, case numbers showed an overall upward trend, with marked increases, especially in 2021. This surge might be linked to intensified agricultural activities or changes in livestock practices and surveillance systems. Although deaths remained relatively low, their consistent reporting indicates a persistent concern over disease severity, albeit with effective management limiting mortality rates. The peak in cases during the summer months highlights ongoing risk factors such as increased human-animal interactions and changing environmental conditions.

By 2024 and 2025, there is a slight decline in case numbers, suggesting that containment efforts may be taking effect. Sporadic fatalities suggest improvements in treatment and prevention strategies. This trend calls for further evaluation of livestock health practices and public health initiatives to continue mitigating the impact of Brucellosis in the region.
