# Brucellosis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by the Brucella bacteria, primarily affecting livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs. Humans can contract the disease through direct contact with infected animals or by consuming contaminated animal products, particularly unpasteurized milk and cheese. Symptoms in humans include fever, sweats, malaise, anorexia, headache, and muscle pain, often leading to chronic conditions if untreated. The disease is prevalent in regions with inadequate animal health programs. Prevention involves vaccination of livestock, pasteurization of dairy products, and protective measures for individuals working with animals.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases**: From 2014 to 2024, there is a noticeable increase in Brucellosis cases, peaking in the summer months, with the highest numbers recorded in 2021 and 2022. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: The data shows a seasonal pattern, with higher case numbers typically occurring from March to August each year. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite the rising number of cases, the mortality rate remains low, with sporadic deaths reported annually. <br/>
- **Recent Decline**: In 2024, there is a slight decline in cases towards the end of the year, suggesting potential improvements in control measures or reporting. <br/>

The data on Brucellosis cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring in the spring and early summer months. This pattern is consistent with the zoonotic nature of Brucellosis, which is often linked to livestock birthing seasons when humans are more likely to come into contact with infected animals. The data shows a general increase in the number of cases over the years, with significant peaks in 2021 and 2022, where monthly cases exceeded 9,000. This could indicate either an increase in transmission rates, improved detection and reporting, or changes in agricultural practices.

Despite the high number of cases, the mortality rate remains remarkably low, with only sporadic deaths reported throughout the years. This suggests that while Brucellosis is a significant public health concern due to its high incidence, it is not typically fatal, aligning with the disease's known characteristics of causing chronic symptoms rather than acute mortality. The data also highlights the importance of continued surveillance and control measures, particularly in high-incidence months, to manage and reduce the spread of Brucellosis in the region.
