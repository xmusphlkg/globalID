# Brucellosis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by the Brucella species of bacteria, which affects various animal hosts such as cattle, sheep, goats, and dogs. Humans typically acquire the disease through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of contaminated animal products like unpasteurized milk or cheese. It manifests with systemic symptoms including fever, joint pain, fatigue, and can lead to more serious complications like arthritis and endocarditis if untreated. Diagnosing brucellosis involves serological testing or bacterial culture, and treatment typically includes a combination of antibiotics like doxycycline and rifampin. Preventive measures focus on animal vaccination and food safety practices.

## Highlights

- Brucellosis cases in the Chinese mainland have exhibited cyclical trends with a notable increase in the warmer months, peaking annually. The number of cases in July 2025 (7301) continues this pattern. <br/>
- Although the number of reported deaths remains low, fatalities occasionally occur, emphasizing the need for ongoing monitoring and preventive measures. In July 2025, one death was recorded. <br/>
- After declining in 2022, case numbers have been gradually increasing since early 2023, reaching nearly 7301 cases by July 2025. <br/>
- Sustained high case counts and occasional deaths indicate that brucellosis remains a significant public health concern, requiring heightened surveillance and intervention strategies. <br/>

This pattern aligns with the seasonal nature of Brucellosis, often influenced by increased livestock activity in warmer months. In subsequent years, case volumes reflect cyclical peaks during mid-year months. Monthly cases generally rise from January, peaking between May and July, followed by declines towards year-end. 

From 2017 onwards, cyclical seasonal peaks continue, though there is an upward trend, particularly evident during mid-2021 through mid-2022, when monthly reports hit their highest since the dataset began. This increase could indicate higher exposure risks, enhanced reporting or detection methods, or changes in disease management practices.
