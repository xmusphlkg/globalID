# Brucellosis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a bacterial zoonotic infection caused by the genus Brucella, which primarily affects livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs, but can also infect humans. The disease is transmitted to humans through direct contact with infected animals, consumption of contaminated animal products, especially unpasteurized milk and cheese, or inhalation of airborne agents. In humans, brucellosis causes flu-like symptoms including fever, sweats, fatigue, and joint pain, and can lead to chronic conditions if untreated. Control measures include animal vaccination, proper food safety practices, and awareness to reduce transmission risks.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trends:** Brucellosis cases in Chinese mainland have shown an upward trend from 2015 to June 2025, with notable peaks in mid-2021 and mid-2022. <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks:** There is a consistent pattern of increased cases from March to June each year, indicating potential seasonal factors influencing transmission. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite fluctuating case numbers, the mortality rate remains exceedingly low, with only occasional deaths reported. <br/>
- **Recent Declines:** From mid-2024 onwards, there is a slight decrease in monthly cases, but numbers remain higher than the initial years, warranting continued public health attention. <br/>

The data on Brucellosis cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to mid-2025 shows fluctuating patterns with seasonal variations. Spring and summer months, particularly from March to August, generally exhibit higher numbers of reported cases, hinting at seasonal factors or agricultural cycles influencing transmission. From 2015 to 2019, there is a pattern of declining cases as seasons shift from summer to winter, peaking in April to July. However, anomalies appear, such as in 2021 and 2022, when cases surge beyond 9,000 in June, possibly due to intensified agricultural activities or livestock infection fluctuations.

Despite frequent fluctuations in case numbers, mortality associated with brucellosis remains minimal across the years. Death occurrences do not consistently align with peaks in case numbers, indicating other factors may influence fatalities beyond mere infection rates. For example, the highest case counts in June 2021 and 2022 did not directly correspond with higher death reports during these years. This suggests the factors affecting mortality might differ from those driving case incidence, highlighting the importance of independent monitoring of cases and deaths to better understand the disease's dynamics and healthcare responses.

This analysis underscores the need for targeted prevention strategies, optimal resource allocation, and effective therapeutic interventions to manage Brucellosis effectively in the Chinese mainland. Understanding these trends can help mitigate impacts and enhance future response efforts.

An examination of reported Brucellosis data from mainland China between 2015 and 2025 shows a generally low mortality rate despite significant fluctuations in case numbers. The Brucellosis cases reported monthly varied significantly, with peaks and troughs, yet the number of mortalities remains exceptionally low over the years. Notably, from mid-2015 to October 2023, Brucellosis-related deaths have remained minimal, with no deaths reported in many months and only isolated instances of one or two deaths in others. For example, while cases peaked in certain months such as June 2021 with 9,670 cases, no deaths were recorded. Meanwhile, months with fewer cases, like March 2017 with 4,055 cases, still experienced isolated instances of mortality.

Furthermore, though cases exceeded 9,000 in multiple months across different years, total annual mortality consistently remained low. The highest number of deaths reported in a single month occurred in July 2021 and September 2023, with two deaths respectively, indicating fatalities are quite sporadic and not directly proportionate to case numbers. This pattern might be attributed to effective treatment and management practices in China, or a possible shift in public health strategies that mitigate the fatal outcomes of Brucellosis. Additionally, given the stability in mortality rates amidst fluctuating case numbers, improving preventive measures and maintaining high standards of healthcare could be factors contributing to the low death rates associated with this bacterial infection.
