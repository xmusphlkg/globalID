# Brucellosis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a contagious zoonotic disease caused by the bacteria of the genus Brucella. It primarily affects livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs, and can be transmitted to humans through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of contaminated animal products, particularly unpasteurized milk and cheese. In humans, brucellosis is characterized by flu-like symptoms including fever, sweats, malaise, anorexia, headache, and muscle pain. Chronic infections can lead to more severe complications such as arthritis, endocarditis, or neurologic symptoms. Control measures include vaccination of livestock, pasteurization of dairy products, and educating people on safe handling practices.

## Highlights

- **Steady Increase in Cases**: Brucellosis cases have shown an overall upward trend from 2014 to 2024, with a notable spike in summer months, reaching 8,272 cases in July 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks**: The disease consistently peaks between May and July each year, highlighting a strong seasonal pattern. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite the increase in cases, the mortality rate remains very low, with most months reporting zero deaths. <br/>
- **Recent Stabilization**: In the last few years, the number of cases has stabilized, with monthly fluctuations but no drastic increases, suggesting possible improvements in control measures. <br/>

However, from 2017 onwards, case numbers show a resurgence, with peaks during late spring to mid-summer, likely linked to agricultural activities and livestock exposure, which are key factors in brucellosis transmission.

Despite fluctuations in case numbers, fatalities remain low, with 17 deaths reported over the decade, indicating effective clinical management of most cases. A notable rise in cases is observed from 2021, peaking at 9,943 cases in June 2022, although numbers slightly decreased afterward. This recent increase could indicate new challenges in controlling the disease or changes in environmental or farming practices that favor transmission. Seasonal variation is evident, with the highest cases often recorded in June and the lowest in February, underscoring the impact of agricultural cycles on infection rates. Overall, the data suggests a need for continued vigilance and adaptive control measures to address the dynamic trends in brucellosis cases.
