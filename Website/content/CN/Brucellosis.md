# Brucellosis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by the Brucella bacteria, primarily affecting livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs. Humans can contract the disease through direct contact with infected animals or by consuming contaminated animal products, particularly unpasteurized milk and cheese. Symptoms in humans include fever, sweats, malaise, anorexia, headache, and muscle pain. The disease can lead to chronic conditions if untreated, affecting organs like the liver and spleen. Brucellosis is prevalent in regions with inadequate animal health programs and poses significant public health and economic challenges. Prevention includes vaccination of animals and pasteurization of dairy products.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, Brucellosis cases have risen, peaking in summer months, especially from 2021 onwards. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variation**: Higher case numbers are typically observed from April to August, indicating a link to agricultural activities. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite rising cases, the mortality rate remains low, with few deaths reported, suggesting effective management strategies. <br/>

- **Recent Decline**: As of January 2025, there is a slight decline in cases compared to the previous year, possibly reflecting the impact of recent public health interventions. <br/>

From 2020 onwards, there is a noticeable increase in cases, possibly due to improved reporting, heightened awareness, or actual incidence rise.
