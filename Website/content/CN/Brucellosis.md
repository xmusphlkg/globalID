# Brucellosis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by the Brucella bacteria, primarily affecting livestock such as cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs. Humans can contract the disease through direct contact with infected animals or by consuming contaminated animal products, particularly unpasteurized milk and cheese. Symptoms in humans include fever, sweats, malaise, anorexia, headache, and muscle pain, often leading to chronic conditions if untreated. The disease is prevalent in regions with inadequate animal health programs. Prevention involves controlling the infection in animals through vaccination and testing, as well as promoting food safety practices.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers**: From 2015 to 2025, Brucellosis cases in mainland China have generally increased, with peaks in summer months. The highest was in June 2022 with 9,943 cases. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: There is a clear seasonal trend, with more cases from March to August, likely linked to agricultural activities. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite high case numbers, the mortality rate is low, with only sporadic deaths. The highest monthly deaths were two, in July 2018 and September 2023. <br/>

- **Recent Decline**: As of February 2025, there is a slight decline in cases compared to the previous year, suggesting improved control measures. <br/>

The data on Brucellosis in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 shows seasonal fluctuations, with case peaks typically from April to July, aligning with increased agricultural activities. Over the years, there is a general upward trend in cases, notably peaking in 2021 and 2022, where monthly cases exceeded 9,000. This rise could be due to enhanced surveillance, reporting, or actual increases in transmission linked to changes in agricultural practices or livestock handling.

Despite the increase in cases, the mortality rate remains low, with only sporadic deaths reported. This aligns with Brucellosis's nature as a chronic condition rather than an acute fatal disease. However, the occurrence of deaths underscores the disease's potential severity if not managed properly. The data suggests that while Brucellosis is not highly lethal, it poses a significant public health challenge due to its chronic nature and economic impact on affected individuals and communities.

The increase in cases in recent years highlights the need for sustained public health efforts, including education, livestock vaccination, and improved diagnostic and treatment facilities. These measures are crucial to effectively manage and control Brucellosis, minimizing its impact on public health and the economy.
