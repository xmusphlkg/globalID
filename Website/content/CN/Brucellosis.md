# Brucellosis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infection caused by bacteria of the genus Brucella. It primarily affects livestock like cattle, goats, sheep, and pigs, but humans can contract it through direct contact with infected animals or by consuming contaminated animal products. Symptoms in humans include fever, sweats, malaise, anorexia, headache, and muscle pain. Chronic infection can lead to more severe complications such as arthritis, endocarditis, and neurobrucellosis. Prevention involves controlling the disease in animals through vaccination and testing, as well as avoiding unpasteurized dairy products and practicing good hygiene around animals.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases:** Brucellosis cases in mainland China have shown a significant upward trend, particularly since 2021, peaking at 9943 cases in June 2022. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variations:** Higher case numbers are consistently observed during the summer months (May-August), indicating potential seasonal influence on disease transmission. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite the high number of cases, the mortality rate remains low, with sporadic deaths reported each year, totaling 17 deaths from June 2014 to June 2024. <br/>
- **Recent Stability:** As of June 2024, the number of cases is slightly decreasing from previous peaks, with 7711 cases reported, suggesting a possible stabilization or decline. <br/>

The data for Brucellosis in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to mid-2024 shows significant monthly and yearly fluctuations in the number of reported cases. Notably, there are seasonal trends with higher incidences typically observed from April to July, peaking during these months across several years. For instance, cases spiked to 9670 in June 2021 and to 9943 in June 2022. This seasonal pattern might suggest a correlation with agricultural activities or environmental factors prevalent during these months that facilitate the transmission of Brucellosis.

Throughout the examined period, the number of deaths remains relatively low compared to the number of cases, indicating that while the disease is widespread, it is not highly lethal. There are sporadic increases in deaths such as the two deaths reported in July 2018 and again in July 2021, highlighting occasional periods of higher mortality. The data from recent years, especially post-2020, shows higher variability in cases, which could be attributed to changes in reporting practices, diagnostic improvements, or actual fluctuations in disease prevalence due to varying control measures and public health interventions.
