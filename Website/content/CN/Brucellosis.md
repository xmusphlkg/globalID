# Brucellosis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a bacterial infection caused by *Brucella* species, primarily transmitted from animals to humans through direct contact, consumption of unpasteurized dairy products, or inhalation of aerosols. It manifests with symptoms like fever, sweats, fatigue, joint pain, and muscle aches. Commonly seen in individuals working with livestock, it can lead to chronic conditions such as arthritis, endocarditis, and neurological complications if untreated. Brucellosis is diagnosed through blood tests and treated with antibiotics. Preventive measures include vaccination of animals, proper hygiene, and avoiding unpasteurized animal products.

## Highlights

1. **Brucellosis cases remain high**: In 2024, the monthly number of reported cases fluctuates, with November 2024 seeing 3,878 cases. Brucellosis cases have remained consistently elevated since 2015. <br/>

2. **Death toll remains low**: While cases have been frequent, fatalities have been rare, with a few isolated deaths. November 2024 recorded one death. <br/>

3. **Seasonal variations**: Case numbers tend to peak in the summer months, with the highest numbers typically reported in May and June. <br/>

4. **Geographic and sectoral focus**: The disease is often linked to animal husbandry practices, with outbreaks frequently observed in rural areas with livestock-related activities. <br/>

The reported data on Brucellosis in the Chinese mainland over the past decade shows fluctuating case numbers, with notable peaks in 2015, particularly from March to June, when monthly cases surpassed 7,000. However, mortality remained low, with deaths sporadically reported in certain months, primarily in 2015, 2017, 2018, and 2021. The highest recorded deaths occurred in July and September of 2021, with two fatalities in each of those months. Despite these occasional deaths, the case count remained consistently high, with monthly cases generally exceeding 2,000, continuing well into 2024.

A seasonal pattern is evident, with higher case numbers typically seen from March to July, suggesting that agricultural activities or increased livestock exposure may contribute to these fluctuations. This pattern highlights the persistence of Brucellosis, even though fatality rates are relatively low. While there has been some variation in incidence, the disease continues to pose a public health concern, with substantial cases reported in recent years. The data underscores ongoing challenges in controlling the spread of Brucellosis despite its relatively low mortality rate.
