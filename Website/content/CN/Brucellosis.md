# Brucellosis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic bacterial infection caused by the genus Brucella, affecting various animals such as cattle, sheep, goats, and pigs. It is transmitted to humans through direct contact with infected animals, consumption of unpasteurized dairy products, or inhalation of aerosols. Symptoms in humans include fever, fatigue, muscle pain, and headaches, often referred to as undulant fever due to its relapsing nature. Diagnosis involves serological testing and blood cultures. While the disease is rarely fatal, it can lead to chronic conditions if untreated. Prevention includes animal vaccination, pasteurization, and protective measures for at-risk workers.

## Highlights

- Brucellosis cases in mainland China have fluctuated significantly, with peaks during spring and summer months, especially in May through July. September 2024 recorded 4,612 cases and 1 death.<br/>
- The highest recent case numbers were in 2021 and 2022, with peaks in June 2022 (9,943 cases) and May 2021 (8,096 cases). Cases in 2024 are slightly lower than these peaks.<br/>
- Mortality remains very low, with occasional deaths each year, mostly associated with higher case numbers.<br/>
- Overall, while cases have decreased compared to previous peak years, the seasonal rise in summer remains a notable trend in 2024.<br/>

The data for Brucellosis in mainland China between 2014 and 2024 reveals several notable patterns and trends. Initially, there's a clear seasonal trend, with case numbers peaking from March to July each year. This trend suggests a potential correlation with factors such as livestock breeding cycles, increased human-animal interaction, or environmental conditions favorable for the transmission of the causative Brucella bacteria. The cyclical pattern indicates the importance of focusing preventive measures and raising awareness particularly before these peak months to mitigate the disease burden.

Another notable aspect is the overall increase in annual cases starting around 2015, peaking in recent years with monthly cases exceeding 9,000. This escalating trend could reflect changes in surveillance, diagnosis practices, or genuinely rising incidence perhaps due to increased livestock farming activities, lack of control measures in animal populations, or insufficient human-animal interaction advisories. The death toll from Brucellosis remains low, suggesting the disease is generally non-lethal but highlights the ongoing need for effective management and control strategies, especially considering the upward trend in cases. Continuing research and adaptive public health policies will be crucial to manage and eventually reduce the incidence of Brucellosis in the region.

Throughout this period, Brucellosis cases show a distinct seasonal and annual variation, with peaks occurring in the spring and summer months. However, the death toll remains consistently low relative to the case count. This suggests a low lethality rate and potentially improved management of the disease.
