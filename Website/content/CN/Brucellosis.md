# Brucellosis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Brucellosis is a zoonotic infectious disease caused by bacteria of the genus *Brucella*, commonly affecting cattle, goats, sheep, pigs, and occasionally humans. Transmission to humans typically occurs through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of contaminated animal products, especially unpasteurized dairy. Clinical manifestations in humans range from mild, flu-like symptoms to severe complications such as arthritis, endocarditis, and organ involvement. The disease is prevalent worldwide, particularly in regions lacking effective animal vaccination and disease control measures. Diagnosis relies on clinical, serological, and microbiological methods, while treatment consists of prolonged antibiotic therapy.

## Highlights

- Brucellosis incidence in the Chinese mainland shows clear seasonality, with case numbers peaking between March and July each year and declining through winter months. <br/>
- Case counts surged in 2021 and 2022, exceeding 9,000 monthly during peak periods, but have since returned to more moderate levels in 2024–2025, with 3,000–7,000 cases per month. <br/>
- Mortality remains very low, with only 1–2 deaths reported annually, and zero deaths in December 2025, indicating effective management and generally mild disease outcomes. <br/>
- The disease persists as an endemic zoonosis, but recent trends suggest stable control and no major outbreaks by the end of 2025. <br/>

Brucellosis cases in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 to December 2025 exhibit a clear seasonal pattern, with peaks consistently observed from March to August each year and lower incidence during autumn and winter. This seasonality likely reflects agricultural cycles and increased human-livestock interaction during warmer months. While case numbers between 2016 and 2019 remain relatively stable, a noticeable uptick begins from 2020, with particularly high monthly totals in the summers of 2021, 2022, and 2023, where incidence often exceeds 9000 cases per month. These increases may indicate shifts in reporting sensitivity, surveillance, animal husbandry practices, or epidemiological drivers such as changes in livestock management or climatic factors.

Despite high case numbers, mortality remains very low throughout the decade, with most months reporting zero deaths and occasional isolated months showing only one or two fatalities. This consistent low case-fatality rate is in line with brucellosis epidemiology, given timely diagnosis and appropriate antibiotic treatment, and suggests public health interventions and medical care have remained effective at mitigating severe outcomes. The absence of correlation between peak months and elevated deaths further underscores that while brucellosis transmission persists widely in rural and agricultural regions, the risk of death is limited to vulnerable individuals rather than being directly linked to incidence surges.

Analysis of the reported brucellosis data in Chinese mainland from late 2015 through 2025 reveals a persistently low mortality rate despite fluctuating case counts. Across the 10-year period, the monthly brucellosis cases consistently range from under 2,000 to over 9,000, with seasonal and annual variations in incidence. However, reported deaths remain extremely rare, never exceeding 2 in any given month, and total deaths amount to only a handful compared to the tens of thousands of recorded cases. Most months report zero deaths; even in months with higher case counts, the number of fatalities does not increase proportionally, suggesting that brucellosis continues to pose minimal direct lethality in this context.

The ratio of deaths to cases consistently remains far below 0.05%, pointing to either effective clinical management and timely diagnosis, or that most detected cases are of milder forms. Sporadic singular deaths typically occur independently of peaks in incidence, emphasizing the lack of a strong correlation between caseload and mortality. The very low overall fatality—typically 0–1 deaths per thousands of cases—reinforces that while brucellosis is endemic with significant human morbidity, its mortality impact is minor, and outbreaks do not manifest as public health emergencies in terms of deaths. This pattern is strikingly stable over time, underscoring brucellosis as a disease demanding vigilance in case prevention and management, but with relatively minimal impact on overall population mortality rates in the Chinese mainland.
