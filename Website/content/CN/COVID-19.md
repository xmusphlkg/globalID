# COVID-19

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19 is an infectious disease caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, first identified in Wuhan, China, in December 2019. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. Symptoms can range from mild, such as fever, cough, and fatigue, to severe, including difficulty breathing and pneumonia. Some cases remain asymptomatic. The disease disproportionately affects older individuals and those with pre-existing health conditions. Global efforts to mitigate the pandemic include social distancing, mask-wearing, testing, contact tracing, and vaccination programs aimed at reducing transmission and severe outcomes.

## Highlights

- COVID-19 cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit a steep rise in 2025, peaking in May with over 440,000 cases. June continues with high numbers, signaling ongoing transmission. <br/>
- Deaths remain relatively low compared to case numbers, particularly in 2025, indicating possible effects of improved treatment or higher vaccination rates. <br/>
- After significant outbreaks in early 2022 and some recovery, 2025 marks a resurgence, surpassing previous peaks, suggesting challenges in managing newer variants or lapses in control measures.<br/>
- Despite the high case burden in 2025, the overall death toll is minor compared to initial pandemic waves, showing advancements in disease management over time. <br/>

The COVID-19 data for the Chinese mainland shows an evolving pandemic landscape from January 2020 onwards. Early 2020 witnessed a rapid increase in cases, especially in January and February, with high mortality. This aligns with the initial outbreak wave and the subsequent implementation of strict control measures. By March 2020, the cases began declining sharply, a trend that generally continued into 2021, demonstrating effective containment strategies.

However, fluctuations were noted, particularly with a surge in December 2021 and January 2022, potentially linked to seasonal factors or viral mutations. A new wave emerged in March 2022, peaking significantly in April with over 65,000 cases, indicating challenges such as the emergence of variants. Despite substantial case increases, notably low mortality suggests improved healthcare responses and vaccination impacts.

The data resumes in 2025, revealing an intense rise in cases from March to June with figures reaching the hundreds of thousands monthly. Despite the high case volumes, mortality remains low, highlighting advancements in medical management, vaccine coverage, or a shift in virus virulence. These patterns reflect the dynamic interplay of public health measures, viral evolution, and societal responses throughout the pandemic, although data gaps in December 2022 to October 2024 restrict comprehensive analysis during that interval.

The dataset illustrates the progression of COVID-19 in Mainland China from January 2020 to June 2025, emphasizing key trends in deaths relative to cases. At the pandemic's onset in early 2020, deaths were significant, with 259 reported in January and 2,611 in February, amidst high case numbers. This reflects the initial challenges in managing the virus, with high mortality indicative of early struggles with preparedness and response.

From March 2020 onward, despite ongoing cases, reported deaths dropped sharply, showing marked improvements in healthcare responses and public health strategies. By 2021, the mortality rate had substantially decreased, even as cases periodically increased. This suggests effective interventions through medical treatments, enhanced health measures, possibly vaccination rollouts and better understanding of the virus.

In analyzing the period from 2022 to 2025, case numbers occasionally spiked dramatically, yet deaths remained astonishingly low. Notably, March to May 2025 witnessed over 560,000 cases but single-digit deaths each month, reflecting advancements in pandemic management. This mismatch between case and death counts likely indicates successful control strategies, potential shifts in virus characteristics, and public health policies that reduced severity or improved immunity. The trajectory from high mortality to minimal deaths despite periodic surges signals a transition towards effective containment and mitigation of COVID-19 effects.
