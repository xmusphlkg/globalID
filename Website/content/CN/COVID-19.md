# COVID-19

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19 is an infectious disease caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, first identified in Wuhan, China, in late 2019. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and aerosols from infected individuals. Symptoms range from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia and death, particularly in older adults and those with underlying health conditions. The virus led to a global pandemic, prompting widespread public health responses, including social distancing, mask use, testing, and vaccination campaigns. Variants have emerged over time, affecting transmission rates and vaccine effectiveness, necessitating ongoing surveillance and adaptation in public health strategies.

## Highlights

- A significant surge in COVID-19 cases occurred in April 2025, recording 168,507 casesâ€”the highest monthly count since early 2020, suggesting a large-scale outbreak. <br/>
- Despite the record high in cases, the death count in April 2025 remained low at 9, indicating a reduced case fatality rate compared to earlier waves. <br/>
- After a period of minimal reporting during 2023 and 2024, data resumed in 2025 with a steady monthly rise in both cases and deaths from January to April. <br/>
- Previous large outbreaks, notably in early 2020 and April 2022, had much higher fatality rates, highlighting improved clinical management or widespread immunity in 2025. <br/>

The initial wave in early 2020 shows a rapid surge, with over 68,000 reported cases in February and a peak death toll of 2,611. This sharp initial impact quickly subsided; from April 2020 through 2021, monthly cases remained relatively low with a strikingly minimal fatality rate, suggesting effective containment, testing, and possibly strict public health interventions during this period. The first notable resurgence appears in March and April of 2022, where cases reached over 65,000 in April, echoing the scale of early 2020, accompanied by a sudden spike in deaths (422 that April). Unlike earlier waves, the resurgence in late 2022 (e.g., 62,723 cases in November) came with almost no deaths, indicating either a less virulent strain or improved clinical management.

In 2025, March and April mark a dramatic escalation, especially April with over 168,000 reported casesâ€”the highest of all recorded months. Despite this, the death toll remained disproportionately low (only 9 deaths), a stark contrast from earlier waves of similar magnitude. This disparity could imply widespread immunity (vaccine- or infection-derived), shifts in surveillance definitions, or enhancements in medical response capabilities. The pattern reflects an evolving landscape of COVID-19 dynamics, from high lethality in the outset to potentially endemic levels with lower fatality amid larger spread in recent months.

The COVID-19 mortality data for the Chinese mainland reveals distinct peaks and troughs that align with major outbreaks and subsequent containment. The initial outbreak in early 2020 saw the highest fatality concentration in February (2,611 deaths), representing a case fatality rate (CFR) of approximately 3.8%, which corresponds to diagnostic delays and overwhelmed healthcare infrastructure in the earliest phase. After March 2020, reported deaths dramatically declined to near zero for over a year and a half, suggesting strong NPIs (non-pharmaceutical interventions), effective isolation, and possibly limited transmission due to strict border and internal movement controls.

A second significant mortality surge occurred in April 2022 with 422 deaths, coinciding with a rise in cases indicative of the Omicron wave, though the CFR dropped compared to early 2020, reflecting either a less virulent variant, improved clinical management, or increased vaccination coverage. An unusual return of reported deaths emerges again in 2025, peaking modestly in April with 9 deaths out of 168,507 cases, showing a CFR of approximately 0.005%. This sharp case increase with low fatalities may suggest widespread transmission of a milder variant or strong population immunity. Overall, death data indicates concentrated mortality during major waves with long periods of quiescence, reflecting cyclical virus dynamics amid evolving public health and immunity landscapes.
