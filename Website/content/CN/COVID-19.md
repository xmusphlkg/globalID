# COVID-19

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19 is a respiratory illness caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, first identified in Wuhan, China, in late 2019. The virus spreads primarily through respiratory droplets and close contact with infected individuals. Common symptoms include fever, cough, shortness of breath, and fatigue, while severe cases can lead to pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and death. COVID-19 has resulted in a global pandemic, affecting millions of people worldwide. Preventive measures include vaccination, mask-wearing, social distancing, and frequent hand hygiene to limit transmission.

## Highlights

- COVID-19 cases in the Chinese mainland surged dramatically from January to June 2025, peaking in May 2025 with over 440,000 new cases.<br/>
- The subsequent months saw a rapid decline in case numbers, dropping to just over 15,000 in December 2025—a return to pre-surge levels.<br/>
- Despite increased transmission, mortality remained very low throughout 2025, with deaths each month in single digits.<br/>
- These trends suggest effective disease management, with containment measures significantly reducing transmission later in 2025 and very low fatality rates, likely due to vaccination and improved clinical care.<br/>

The reported COVID-19 data for Chinese mainland show an initial surge in cases in early 2020, with infections peaking in February and deaths following closely behind. This was rapidly suppressed: by March and April 2020, both cases and deaths declined sharply, coinciding with rigorous non-pharmaceutical interventions. For the rest of 2020 and throughout 2021, transmission remained low and mortality minimal, indicating effective outbreak containment and sporadic, typically well-controlled, local clusters with barely any fatal outcomes.

A notable increase appears in spring 2022, particularly in March and April, with new case counts exceeding 40,000 and 65,000 respectively—alongside a rise in deaths, particularly in April (422). This surge is suggestive of variant-driven transmission and a possible recalibration of control policies. After relative stability, a dramatic resurgence occurs in early 2025: case numbers jump to hundreds of thousands monthly, peaking at more than 440,000 in May. In contrast, deaths stay low, rarely exceeding single digits per month, indicating a significant decoupling between incidence and severity. This pattern may reflect widespread population immunity, improved clinical management, or viral attenuation. By late 2025, both cases and deaths gradually decrease, suggesting improved control or epidemic burnout. Overall, the trajectory across the years illustrates pronounced shifts linked to interventions, viral evolution, and changing population immunity.

COVID-19 mortality in mainland China was heavily concentrated in two distinct surges. The first, between January and April 2020, coincided with the initial outbreak, peaking in February 2020 (2,611 deaths) and declining sharply by May, with close to zero deaths reported monthly through the rest of 2020 and 2021. Despite ongoing cases—including sporadic monthly highs—fatalities remained extremely low, indicating strong containment, changes in clinical practice, or differences in case reporting.

A second noticeable spike appeared during the Omicron wave in spring 2022. April saw 422 deaths (about 6.4 per 1,000 cases), and May recorded 166 deaths, with mortality dropping back to low levels afterward as cases fell. From June 2022 onward, fatalities were essentially negligible despite tens of thousands of cases at times.

Large case increases recurred from January to December 2025, with several months recording over 100,000 new infections. Yet, deaths per month remained in the single digits, a stark contrast to earlier surges. This sustained decoupling of cases from deaths in the later period likely reflects accumulated immunity, effective interventions, and viral evolution reducing disease severity. Overall, the data document a transition from substantial initial mortality to consistently low fatality amid fluctuating case numbers, highlighting changes in epidemic management, population immunity, and possible reporting practices over time.
