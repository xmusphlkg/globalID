# COVID-19

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19 is a contagious disease caused by the coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, first identified in Wuhan, China, in December 2019. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and can lead to symptoms such as fever, cough, and difficulty breathing. In severe cases, it can cause pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and death, especially in the elderly and those with pre-existing health conditions. The World Health Organization declared COVID-19 a pandemic in March 2020. Efforts to control its spread include vaccination, social distancing, mask-wearing, and frequent handwashing, alongside various public health measures globally.

## Highlights

- COVID-19 cases have surged dramatically in the first half of 2025, peaking in May with over 440,000 cases, indicating a new wave of infections. <br/>

- Deaths have remained relatively low compared to case numbers, suggesting improved clinical management or less virulent variants, with July reporting just two deaths. <br/>

- Compared to earlier years, the rapid increase in cases from January to July 2025 shows significant vulnerability despite previous containment successes evident from 2020 to 2022. <br/>

- An increased case number without a corresponding rise in deaths highlights potential hospital system resilience and/or effective vaccine and treatment regimes. <br/>

Analyzing the reported COVID-19 data for the Chinese mainland, several key trends and patterns emerge. The early phase in 2020 shows a rapid escalation in cases, with peak infections in February (68,033 cases) and a significant number of deaths (2,611), indicative of the initial outbreak severity. As China implemented stringent containment measures, including lockdowns and extensive testing, a notable decline in cases followed, dropping to under 1,000 monthly cases by May 2020. Mortality reflected this trend, with nearly zero deaths from mid-2020 onwards, suggesting effective mitigation and healthcare interventions.

A resurgence of cases becomes evident in early 2022, particularly in March through May, with March seeing 41,577 cases, reaching 65,484 in April. This spike marks one of the highest since the pandemic's onset, perhaps due to variants and easing restrictions. Nevertheless, reported deaths remained relatively low. Thereafter, a significant uptick occurs from late 2024 to mid-2025, with unprecedented case numbers peaking in May 2025 at 440,662 cases but with surprisingly low mortality, suggesting increased population immunity, improved treatment protocols, or changes in virus virulence. This pattern underscores the pandemic's evolving dynamics, highlighting the complex interplay of viral mutation, human behavior, and public health responses.

The COVID-19 mortality data for the Chinese mainland reflects significant fluctuations in reported deaths across different periods. In early 2020, with the emergence of COVID-19, there was a notably high mortality rate, as evidenced by the data from January to March 2020, where 3,312 deaths were recorded amidst the initial outbreak. However, from April 2020 onward, the death figures dropped drastically, with many months reporting zero deaths despite the presence of confirmed cases. This trend could be attributed to stringent public health measures and effective containment strategies, such as lockdowns, mass testing, and contact tracing, which helped reduce fatalities significantly.

A notable resurgence of deaths is observed from March 2022, peaking in April 2022 with 422 deaths, coinciding with the Omicron variant's global impact, which likely increased transmission rates before subsequently becoming milder and more controllable. Mortality figures afterwards remained low until a slight increase reappeared from 2025, corresponding with a large surge in case numbers. Despite high infection rates, deaths remained relatively low, with single-digit mortality figures during each month from January to July 2025, possibly indicating improved healthcare response, vaccination coverage, and treatments reducing the likelihood of fatal outcomes from COVID-19 infections.
