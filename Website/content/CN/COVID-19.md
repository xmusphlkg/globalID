# COVID-19

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19 is a contagious disease caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, first identified in Wuhan, China, in late 2019. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and can cause symptoms ranging from mild illness to severe respiratory distress and death. Common symptoms include fever, cough, and shortness of breath. The virus led to a global pandemic, resulting in widespread health, economic, and social impacts. Preventive measures such as vaccination, mask-wearing, and social distancing have been crucial in controlling its spread. COVID-19 continues to evolve, requiring ongoing public health surveillance and response.

## Highlights

- A significant resurgence of COVID-19 occurred in early 2025, with cases sharply rising from 33,218 in January to 440,662 in May, indicating heightened transmission rates.<br/>
- Despite the large surge in cases, reported deaths remained comparatively low—just 28 total deaths from January to May 2025—suggesting improved clinical outcomes or lower disease severity.<br/>
- The data shows at least two major outbreak waves: the first peak in early 2020 and a second in early-to-mid 2022; current 2025 figures now exceed both in terms of case numbers.<br/>
- No case data is available between December 2022 and December 2024, leaving a gap in understanding the transition to the current epidemiological situation.<br/>

The progression of COVID-19 cases in mainland China reveals three major surges: the initial outbreak in early 2020, a resurgence in spring 2022, and a pronounced spike in 2025. The initial peak in February 2020 marked the virus’s rapid emergence, with over 68,000 cases, followed by a swift decline due to early containment strategies. For most of 2020 and 2021, monthly case numbers remained below 3,500, with near-zero deaths from mid-2020 through 2021, suggesting sustained suppression of large-scale transmission. Deaths remained minimal even during months with detectable increases in cases toward the end of 2021 and early 2022.

In 2022, the monthly case count rose sharply in March and April, aligning with the global circulation of more transmissible variants; April saw over 65,000 cases and 422 deaths, indicating a notable impact on health systems. A second significant surge is recorded across early 2025, culminating in a peak of over 440,000 reported cases in May—far exceeding previous waves, but with disproportionately low reported deaths. This divergence may reflect enhanced immunity due to vaccination or prior exposure, improved clinical management, or changes in surveillance criteria. The data after 2022 is sparse until 2025, limiting insights into the intervening trajectory, but the high volumes in 2025 indicate a substantial new epidemic wave.

The trajectory of reported COVID-19 deaths in mainland China illustrates three distinguishable mortality peaks. The initial and most severe spike occurred in the early phase of the outbreak between January and March 2020, peaking in February with 2,611 deaths. This coincided with the discovery and initial spread of the virus, prior to the implementation of aggressive suppression measures. The death toll sharply declined after March 2020, maintaining near-zero mortality levels for the better part of 2020 and 2021, even as new cases fluctuated—suggesting robust containment and mitigation efforts.

A second fatality wave reemerged in 2022 from March through May, during which 590 deaths were recorded, primarily in April amid extensive transmission. After another extended period of low reported mortality, a third surge in deaths began in January 2025, though to a comparatively lesser degree. Despite the vast increase in reported cases from March to May 2025—over 665,000 total—the death count remained relatively low (23 deaths over three months). This trend may suggest either reduced virulence, widespread immunity due to prior infection or vaccination, or improved treatment capacity, although the full interpretation depends on data quality and reporting completeness.
