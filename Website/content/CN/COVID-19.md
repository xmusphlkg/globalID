# COVID-19

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19, caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, is a respiratory illness first identified in Wuhan, China, in December 2019. It spreads primarily through respiratory droplets and close contact, with symptoms ranging from mild (fever, cough, fatigue) to severe (pneumonia, organ failure). High-risk groups include older adults and individuals with underlying health conditions. The virus led to a global pandemic declared by the World Health Organization (WHO) in March 2020, prompting widespread public health measures like social distancing, mask-wearing, and vaccination campaigns. Rapid scientific efforts have focused on understanding, treating, and mitigating its impact worldwide.

## Highlights

- COVID-19 cases in Chinese mainland saw a sharp rise in early 2022, notably in March and April, with cases reaching over 65,000 per month. <br/>
- After a period of low reported cases, COVID-19 numbers surged again in late 2022, with November reaching over 62,000 cases. <br/>
- There appears to have been a period of underreporting or significant control from December 2022 to December 2024, with data not available. <br/>
- In early 2025, cases surged again, with March recording over 56,000 cases, indicating a potential new wave of infections. The number of deaths, however, remains relatively low. <br/>

The dataset reveals significant fluctuations in COVID-19 cases in mainland China, especially at the pandemic's onset and again in late 2022 and early 2025. In early 2020, cases peaked in February with substantial deaths, but strong containment measures led to a dramatic decline by mid-2020. Subsequent months showed relatively low case numbers and zero deaths, indicating effective control measures were in place.

In 2021, cases fluctuated with minor increases but no deaths, suggesting improved healthcare responses, possibly from better treatment protocols or vaccinations. However, there was a notable rise in cases in April and again in November 2022, with a substantial increase in deaths, indicating challenges likely due to new virus variants or relaxation of control measures.

The significant spike in early 2025 suggests a resurgence, likely due to changes in virus dynamics or variations in public health policies, highlighting the persistent risk and need for ongoing vigilance. The absence of data from December 2022 to October 2024 complicates the analysis, but the 2025 surge underlines the necessity for adaptable and sustained public health strategies.

COVID-19 death patterns in the Chinese mainland show three distinct phases. The initial outbreak from January to March 2020 saw sharp mortality, with 2,611 deaths in February alone. This peak aligns with widespread transmission before effective control measures and treatments were in place. From April 2020 onward, deaths fell rapidly and remained at or near zero through most of 2021, despite fluctuating infection numbers. This period reflects the impact of strict containment, quarantines, and healthcare system adaptations.

A second moderate wave in spring 2022 brought a rise in cases and deaths—notably 422 in April and 166 in May—likely tied to outbreaks in areas like Shanghai. However, even during high transmission periods, the fatality rate remained low compared to early 2020 levels. From mid-2022 through early 2025, deaths were minimal despite recurring case surges. For example, March 2025 saw over 56,000 cases but only seven deaths. This decoupling of deaths from case counts suggests maintained control over severe outcomes, potentially due to vaccinations, improved treatments, or changes in virus severity. Overall, initial waves caused most deaths, while subsequent periods demonstrated strong suppression of mortality despite resurging cases.
