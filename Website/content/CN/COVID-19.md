# COVID-19

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19, caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus, emerged in December 2019 in Wuhan, China. It rapidly spread worldwide, leading to a global pandemic. The virus primarily spreads through respiratory droplets, and symptoms range from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia and death, especially in vulnerable populations like the elderly and those with underlying health conditions. Control measures include social distancing, mask-wearing, and lockdowns. Vaccines, developed with unprecedented speed, play a crucial role in reducing transmission and severity. The pandemic has significantly impacted healthcare systems, economies, and daily life globally, highlighting the importance of public health readiness and international cooperation.

## Highlights

- The reported cases of COVID-19 in the Chinese mainland show a significant resurgence in 2025, indicating a possible new wave, with May having the highest cases at 440,662. <br/>
- Deaths remained low despite high case numbers, suggesting improved treatment protocols and vaccination campaigns could be reducing mortality rates. <br/>
- Comparative data from 2020 to 2022 shows fluctuating case numbers; however, deaths decreased drastically, indicating enhanced healthcare responses. <br/>
- The COVID-19 situation in September 2025 shows a decline in cases to 66,915 from earlier months, potentially signaling the waning of this outbreak wave. <br/>

Analyzing COVID-19 data from the Chinese mainland reveals significant fluctuations throughout the pandemic. Early 2020 saw a rapid rise in cases and deaths, peaking in February with 68,033 cases and 2,611 deaths. This was followed by a substantial decrease in subsequent months due to strict containment measures, with cases remaining below 4,000 per month for much of 2020 and 2021, and fatalities often at zero.

However, a resurgence was witnessed from late 2021 into early 2022, notably in March and April 2022, with cases reaching 41,577 and 65,484 respectively. This spike also resulted in the highest death toll since early 2020, suggesting increased strain on healthcare systems or the impact of new variants. This resurgence reflects the potential influence of evolving virus strains and perhaps the relaxation of earlier measures.

The data for 2025 shows another dramatic surge, especially between April and June. Cases exceeded previous peaks, yet deaths remained relatively low, suggesting possible improvements in medical response, vaccines, or virus virulence. Monthly cases soared from 168,507 in April to 440,662 in May, indicating a substantial challenge for public health systems.

These trends underscore the dynamic nature of the pandemic, highlighting the ongoing need for adaptable strategies and resilience in health responses amidst emerging variants and changing transmission dynamics.

The COVID-19 data from the Chinese mainland reflects a distinct progression in virus management, particularly concerning deaths. Early in the pandemic, 2020 experienced significant fatalities with February marking the highest at 2,611 deaths. However, from June to December 2020, deaths were consistently reported as zero, indicating effective containment and treatment approaches despite ongoing cases. This trend suggests a robust healthcare response and possibly strict public health measures.

In 2022, a notable rise in deaths occurred during March and April as cases significantly increased, recording 422 deaths in April amid 65,484 infections. This spike might highlight the challenges posed by new variants or increased strain on healthcare resources. Following this, May showed a drop in fatalities, potentially reflecting adjustments in health management or successful containment efforts.

By 2025, despite a dramatic increase in cases, deaths remained low, consistently in single digits, even with monthly cases peaking at 440,662 in May. Such low fatalities suggest substantial advancements in healthcare responses, vaccination coverage, or lower virulence of circulating variants. Overall, the data underscores the impact of rapid adaptations in public health strategies in reducing mortality during sustained waves of COVID-19, demonstrating the capacity for medical systems to evolve and improve outcomes as the situation progresses.
