# COVID-19

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19, caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, emerged in Wuhan, China, in late 2019. It rapidly spread worldwide, leading to a global pandemic. The virus primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and can cause symptoms ranging from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia and death. Common symptoms include fever, cough, and difficulty breathing. The pandemic prompted widespread public health measures, including social distancing, mask-wearing, and vaccination campaigns. COVID-19 has significantly impacted global health systems, economies, and daily life, highlighting the importance of pandemic preparedness and response.

## Highlights

- **Initial Outbreak and Control (2020):** The COVID-19 outbreak in early 2020 peaked in February with over 68,000 cases and 2,611 deaths, followed by a significant decline, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Resurgence and Management (2021-2022):** A resurgence occurred in early 2022, peaking in April with over 65,000 cases and 422 deaths, suggesting improved public health responses. <br/>

- **Data Gaps and Recent Trends (2023-2025):** There is a lack of data for 2023 and 2024, but January 2025 shows a rise to 33,218 cases, indicating a potential new wave. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** The mortality rate has remained low since mid-2020, reflecting advancements in treatment and healthcare capacity. <br/>

The data for COVID-19 cases in the Chinese mainland reveals a significant initial surge in early 2020, particularly in February, when cases peaked at 68,033. This spike coincided with the early stages of the pandemic, highlighting the rapid spread of the virus and the challenges faced by health authorities in managing the outbreak. The mortality rate during this period was notably high, with 2,611 deaths reported in February alone, indicating the severity of the situation. Following this peak, there was a marked decline in cases and deaths, suggesting that containment measures and public health interventions were effective in reducing transmission.

As the data progresses into 2021 and 2022, a pattern emerges where cases remain relatively low, with occasional spikes, such as in March 2022 with 41,577 cases. The death toll during these periods remained significantly lower compared to the initial outbreak, reflecting improvements in treatment protocols and possibly the impact of vaccination efforts. The data also shows fluctuations in case numbers, particularly in late 2022, which may indicate localized outbreaks or the emergence of new variants. Overall, the trends suggest a transition from a crisis phase to a more manageable situation, although vigilance remains crucial in monitoring and responding to potential resurgences of the virus.

This period coincides with the initial outbreak and rapid spread of the virus, leading to a high mortality rate as the healthcare system grappled with the sudden influx of cases. However, from March 2020 onwards, there was a marked decline in deaths, with several months reporting zero fatalities despite ongoing cases. This suggests effective containment measures and improvements in treatment protocols, which likely contributed to reducing the mortality rate.

In 2022, there was a resurgence in deaths, particularly in April and May, with 422 and 166 deaths respectively. This increase could be attributed to new variants or changes in public health measures.
