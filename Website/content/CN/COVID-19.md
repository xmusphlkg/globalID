# COVID-19

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

COVID-19 is a respiratory disease caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, first identified in Wuhan, China, in December 2019. It rapidly spread worldwide, leading to a global pandemic declared by the World Health Organization in March 2020. The virus primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and aerosols from infected individuals. Symptoms range from mild, such as fever and cough, to severe respiratory illness and death, particularly in older adults and those with underlying health conditions. Efforts to control the spread include vaccination campaigns, mask-wearing, social distancing, and quarantine measures. COVID-19 significantly impacted global health, economies, and daily life.

## Highlights

- COVID-19 cases in Chinese mainland show a significant increase in 2025, with notably high figures peaking at 440,662 in May.<br/>
- The number of deaths remains comparatively low despite the surge in cases, suggesting improved management and perhaps effective treatment or vaccination protocols.<br/>
- The trend since 2020 indicates cyclical spikes in infection rates, pointing to potential periods of increased transmission or seasonal factors affecting case numbers.<br/>
- The large discrepancies between cases and deaths in 2025 suggest a potential decoupling, indicating either less virulent strains or robust healthcare systems mitigating severe outcomes.<br/>

The COVID-19 pandemic in mainland China has seen varying patterns of case prevalence and mortality over the different phases of the pandemic. Initially, in early 2020, during the onset of the pandemic, there was a significant surge in cases, with 68,033 reported in February 2020, which vastly contributed to early global awareness and intervention strategies. This early surge was accompanied by high mortality with deaths rising to 2,611 in the same month. After peaking in February and March 2020, there was a notable decline in cases, with substantial reductions observed through the rest of 2020 as strict containment measures were implemented, leading to lower case numbers and near-zero deaths in subsequent months.

However, the data reveals a resurgence in late 2022 and into 2023, with November 2022 documenting 62,723 cases, contrasting significantly with lower monthly figures witnessed throughout 2021. This highlights possible transmission lapses and the potential emergence of new variants or relaxation of protocols. Furthermore, 2025 saw a dramatic increase in cases with an alarming escalation to 440,662 cases in May alone, albeit with a considerably low death count, suggesting advancements in health interventions and vaccine effectiveness. The trend underscores the importance of sustained public health strategies and the potential for fluctuations in case numbers influenced by factors such as viral mutations, public compliance with health measures, and policy changes.
