# Cholera

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection with the Vibrio cholerae bacterium. It is typically contracted through the consumption of contaminated water or food. The disease is characterized by severe diarrhea and dehydration, which can lead to death if untreated. Cholera occurs in regions with inadequate water treatment, poor sanitation, and insufficient hygiene practices, mainly affecting parts of Africa, Southeast Asia, and Haiti. Vaccines and oral rehydration solutions are available for prevention and treatment, respectively. Ensuring access to clean water, improving sanitation, and education are crucial for cholera control and prevention.

## Highlights

- Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland have remained sporadic and relatively low, with a noticeable pattern of increased cases during the summer months, reflecting potential seasonal transmission dynamics. <br/>
- There have been no reported cholera-related deaths over the observation period, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease. <br/>
- The year 2023 saw a notable rise in cholera incidence during August and September, suggesting potential for outbreaks during these months, although subsequent months showed a decline. <br/>
- As of June 2025, cholera remains a manageable public health concern with ongoing monitoring essential, especially during summer months when the risk of transmission increases. <br/>

Upon examining the cholera case data in mainland China from 2015 to the projected data in mid-2025, several trends emerge. The cases per month remain relatively low but exhibit seasonal fluctuations with some periodic spikes. Notably, higher incidences recur in the months of July, August, and September almost every year, which may align with climatic or environmental factors conducive to cholera outbreaks, such as increased rainfall and warmer temperatures that can facilitate the spread of the bacteria in water sources. Years like 2016, 2018, and 2022 see noticeable spikes, particularly in the late summer months, highlighting potential vulnerabilities or lapses in preventive measures during these periods.

Despite these seasonal peaks, it is essential to note the overall containment of the disease without any recorded deaths, indicating effective healthcare and response strategies to treat or control choleral infections promptly. The subsequent declines to zero or low cases seen in the cooler months suggest effective seasonal intervention and public health education, alongside perhaps less favorable conditions for the cholera bacterium to thrive. These data insights emphasize the importance of bolstering surveillance and response measures during identified high-risk periods to maintain low cholera incidence rates in the region.
