# Cholera

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection of the intestine with the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*. It is primarily transmitted through contaminated water or food. Symptoms include profuse watery diarrhea, vomiting, and rapid dehydration, which can lead to death if untreated. Cholera is most common in regions with inadequate water treatment, poor sanitation, and insufficient hygiene. Outbreaks may occur rapidly in areas affected by disaster or conflict. Treatment mainly involves prompt rehydration with oral or intravenous fluids, and prevention relies on improved sanitation, clean water, and vaccination.

## Highlights

- Cholera incidence in the Chinese mainland remains very low, with only a handful of cases reported annually and no deaths from 2015 to December 2025.<br/>
- A seasonal trend is consistently observed, with cases peaking during summer and early autumn (June–September), suggesting increased transmission in warmer months.<br/>
- The data for 2025 shows continued sporadic cases with small peaks in July and August, mirroring previous years, and no evidence of outbreak clusters.<br/>
- The persistent absence of fatalities over the decade highlights effective case management and strong public health surveillance for cholera in the region.<br/>

Cholera case reports from mainland China over the past decade illustrate an enduring pattern of low-level endemicity interrupted by distinct seasonal surges, almost universally concentrated between June and October. These annual rises typically peak in August or September, with notable clusters such as 12 cases in August 2018 and 12 again in August 2019. Outside these months, case counts are consistently sparse or absent, underscoring a strong seasonality, most likely linked to climatic factors conducive to bacterial proliferation and transmission, such as higher temperatures and increased rainfall. The absence of reported deaths throughout the entire surveillance period suggests that timely clinical management and effective public health responses have mitigated cholera-related mortality, even during months of increased incidence.

The absence of sustained outbreaks and the presence of intermittent low-level transmission suggest that cholera is not an epidemic threat in the region but remains a disease requiring vigilance, particularly during the late summer and early autumn. Occasional years, like 2022 and 2023, display modestly higher aggregate cases, yet overall numbers stay well below epidemic thresholds, with no multi-month spikes indicative of unchecked transmission. The consistently zero-death rate throughout the dataset could signal robust reporting and case management, or possibly, underreporting of mild cases with negligible clinical impact. The cyclical case pattern and the restriction to particular months imply environmental reservoirs or seasonal amplification, rather than persistent year-round transmission chains among the population.

Analysis of the reported cholera data from the Chinese mainland over the past decade reveals a remarkable absence of mortality, with zero deaths among all recorded cases. This result persists despite intermittent case clusters, especially in the summer and early autumn months, suggesting either highly effective clinical management, low virulence strains, or superior public health interventions that prevent progression to fatal outcomes. The consistent lack of fatalities, even in months with higher case counts—most notably in July through September—points toward successful detection and treatment protocols in place, and possibly rapid access to care that mitigates the risks central to cholera mortality, such as severe dehydration.

This zero-death trend endures across years and seasons, with fluctuations in case numbers not translating into increased risk of death. This pattern suggests robust surveillance and reporting systems, as well as effective water, sanitation, and hygiene strategies that control outbreaks before they escalate. Furthermore, given the global context in which cholera can cause significant mortality, the data suggest that the population at risk may be limited, or that cases are mild or partially protected due to prior exposure or nutritional status. The absence of fatal outcomes, regardless of case count variance, sets the region apart in cholera control, indicating that the integrated public health responses are not only curbing transmission but also preventing deaths altogether.
