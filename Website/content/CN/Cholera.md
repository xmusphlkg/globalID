## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection of the intestine with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It is typically contracted through ingestion of contaminated water or food. The disease is characterized by sudden onset of profuse watery diarrhea, vomiting, and severe dehydration, which can lead to death if not treated promptly. Cholera is endemic in many parts of the world, primarily affecting regions with inadequate water and sanitation infrastructure. While effective oral rehydration solutions can treat most cases, prevention through improved sanitation, access to clean water, and vaccination are crucial in controlling outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Cholera remains under control in Chinese mainland**: The data reflects low to moderate fluctuation in cases, with a general pattern of low incidence from September 2015 to September 2025. <br/>

- **Seasonal variation apparent in case numbers**: Peaks commonly occur from June to October, with higher counts often seen in August, as in 2018, 2019, and 2023. <br/>

- **Consistently no deaths reported**: The dataset consistently shows no cholera-related deaths over the ten-year period, indicating effective clinical management. <br/>

- **Recent trends show reduced activity**: The data for 2024 and 2025 reflects declining cases compared to prior years, suggesting effective public health interventions. <br/>

The epidemiological data for cholera in mainland China from September 2015 to September 2025 highlights a low incidence of cases with a sporadic pattern, primarily observed during warmer months. This suggests a possible seasonal component related to environmental factors like increased rainfall or temperature affecting water quality and bacterial growth. Notably, cases spike in June, July, and August each year, underscoring these months as peak periods for cholera transmission. Despite recurring outbreaks, the overall number of cases remains low, and critically, no deaths have been reported, indicating effective healthcare interventions.

From 2016 to 2019, there were occasional increases in cases, especially notable with 12 cases each August, implying residual vulnerabilities despite control efforts. Subsequent years showed fluctuations in single-digit case numbers, suggesting successful ongoing public health surveillance and response mechanisms. This indicates that the healthcare system often contains outbreaks before they become severe. Furthermore, a decline in cases during colder months reflects effective preventive measures when risk is lower. The constant zero mortality rate through these years signals strong healthcare capacity to manage cholera effectively. Thus, while cholera persists sporadically, control seems effective, though vigilance, especially during warmer months, remains crucial to prevent outbreaks from escalating.
