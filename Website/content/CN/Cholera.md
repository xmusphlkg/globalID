# Cholera

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal infection caused by the ingestion of food or water contaminated with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It remains a major public health concern in many parts of the world, especially in areas with inadequate access to clean water and sanitation. Cholera is characterized by sudden onset of severe watery diarrhea, which can lead to dehydration and even death if untreated. The disease primarily spreads in conditions of poor sanitation, overcrowding, and poverty. Rapid diagnosis, rehydration therapy, improved sanitation, and access to clean water are crucial in controlling and preventing cholera outbreaks.

## Highlights

- The incidence of cholera in the Chinese mainland has shown fluctuations with sporadic outbreaks, particularly from May to September over the years. <br/>
- The most significant increase in cases was observed in 2016, 2018, and 2019, with August often experiencing a higher number of reported cases. <br/>
- Since 2020, there is a noticeable decline in cases, with 2024 having a lower and more stabilized number of reported cases, without any recorded deaths throughout the dataset period. <br/>
- Current data for 2024 suggests a decline in cholera activity, with no cases reported in October and overall reduced incidence compared to previous years. <br/>

The data on cholera in mainland China reveals periodic fluctuations in the incidence of cases over the years. Analysis begins with a relatively low and sporadic number of cases, starting in late 2014 and into 2015, where the cases began surfacing in a pattern suggesting potential seasonal influence, particularly peaking during the mid-year months. From 2016 onward, there is a noticeable rise in cases during the months of July to October, with the highest record in October 2016, demonstrating an observed pattern of increased cholera activity during warmer months, which aligns with similar global cholera outbreaks often linked to warmer climates that foster the bacteria's growth.

From 2018 through 2024, despite fluctuations, peaks within summer months remained consistent year-on-year, with significant case numbers presenting in July and August. Notably, 2018 and 2019 saw substantial increases in August, potentially indicating unique environmental or sociopolitical factors influencing transmission. By 2021, a decline in reported cases suggests either enhanced control measures or varying environmental conditions. Interestingly, through the period covered, despite the fluctuations in case numbers, there were no reported deaths, indicating effective management of cholera cases, possibly attributed to improved healthcare responses and treatment access, alongside preventive and public health interventions.

This outcome suggests that the Chinese mainland's health system has been remarkably effective in managing cholera cases, potentially offering insights into the successful treatment and containment strategies applied in the region. The absence of fatalities over such an extended timeframe likely reflects robust public health infrastructure, including timely access to medical treatment and effective preventive measures that impede the progression of cholera to life-threatening stages.

Further scrutiny of the data shows sporadic outbreaks with peaks in certain months but with no resultant deaths, underscoring the difference in outcomes compared to other regions where cholera might present a far more severe risk to life. The periodic increases in case numbers, as seen in the summers of multiple years, might correlate with seasonal factors known to exacerbate cholera spread. Despite these peaks, the consistent management suggests that public health responses were promptly activated and effectively handled. Additionally, the pattern of no deaths suggests a potentially high awareness among the public and a strong, responsive health care network capable of quickly addressing cholera outbreaks.
