# Cholera

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection of the intestine with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It is typically spread through contaminated water or food and is prevalent in areas with inadequate water treatment, poor sanitation, and insufficient hygiene. Symptoms include severe diarrhea and dehydration, which can lead to death if untreated. Cholera outbreaks can occur rapidly, especially in densely populated regions. Prevention focuses on access to clean water, proper sanitation, and hygiene practices. Treatment primarily involves rehydration, either orally or intravenously, and antibiotics in severe cases. Vaccines are also available for high-risk areas.

## Highlights



- **Seasonal Peaks:** Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit a seasonal pattern, peaking in summer months, especially from June to September. <br/>

- **Variable Case Numbers:** Case numbers fluctuate annually, with notable increases in certain years like 2016 and 2018, indicating potential outbreaks. <br/>

- **Zero Mortality Rate:** Despite case fluctuations, the mortality rate remains zero, suggesting effective treatment and management. <br/>

- **Recent Stability:** As of February 2025, no cases have been reported, indicating successful control measures or a natural decline in transmission. <br/>

The data on cholera cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a pattern of sporadic outbreaks with a notable seasonal trend. The majority of cases occur during the summer months, particularly from June to September, which aligns with the warmer and wetter climate conditions that are conducive to the spread of cholera. For instance, in 2016, there was a significant increase in cases from August to October, peaking at 11 cases in October. Similarly, in 2018 and 2019, August saw the highest number of cases, with 12 each year. This seasonal pattern suggests that environmental factors, such as temperature and rainfall, play a crucial role in the transmission dynamics of cholera in the region.

Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, it is noteworthy that there have been no reported deaths throughout the entire period. This could indicate effective public health interventions, timely medical treatment, and possibly a robust healthcare infrastructure capable of managing cholera outbreaks. The data also shows a general decline in cases from 2020 onwards, with occasional spikes, such as in July 2024. This decline might be attributed to improved sanitation, increased public awareness, and possibly the impact of broader public health measures implemented during the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have inadvertently reduced the transmission of other infectious diseases, including cholera.

The data indicates sporadic outbreaks, with some months showing higher case numbers, particularly in the summer months, which is consistent with cholera's tendency to thrive in warmer, wetter conditions. For instance, August often shows a peak in cases, such as in 2016, 2018, 2019, and 2023, which aligns with the seasonal nature of cholera outbreaks.

The consistent absence of deaths over the years could be attributed to several factors, including improved healthcare infrastructure, effective public health interventions, and possibly the availability of clean water and sanitation facilities. The data suggests that while cholera remains a public health concern, the measures in place are sufficient to prevent fatalities.
