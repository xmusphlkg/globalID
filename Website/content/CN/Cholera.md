## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal disease caused by infection of the intestine with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It is primarily spread through contaminated water or food and can lead to severe dehydration and death if untreated. Symptoms include profuse watery diarrhea, vomiting, and leg cramps. Rapid rehydration is crucial for treatment. Cholera outbreaks are often associated with inadequate sanitation and lack of clean drinking water. Vaccination, improved water quality, and hygiene practices are essential measures to control its spread. The disease remains a global health concern, particularly in regions with limited access to clean water and sanitation facilities.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Pattern:** Cholera cases in mainland China show a clear seasonal trend, with the majority of cases occurring in the summer and early autumn months (July to October).<br/>
- **Yearly Variations:** The number of cases fluctuates yearly, with notable increases in specific years such as 2016, 2018, and 2022, suggesting periodic surges.<br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, there have been no reported deaths from cholera in the data provided, indicating effective treatment or low virulence.<br/>
- **Recent Activity:** As of July 2024, there has been a slight increase in cases, but overall, the situation remains under control with no reported deaths.<br/>

The cholera cases in the Chinese mainland from mid-2014 through mid-2024 appear to demonstrate a cyclical pattern with periodic surges, predominantly during the warmer months of June through September. Initially, the data from 2014 and 2015 exhibit lower case numbers, with a noticeable rise starting in 2016, peaking in October with 11 reported cases. The subsequent years exhibit similar patterns, with cases increasing during the summer months and tapering off as the year progresses into winter, which is consistent with the seasonal nature of cholera outbreaks, often exacerbated by warmer, wetter conditions facilitating the spread of the disease.

Despite these fluctuations in case numbers, it is notable that no fatalities have been reported throughout the period, which may reflect effective clinical management and public health interventions. In several instances, peaks in particular years such as August of 2018 and 2019, with 12 cases each, suggest underlying environmental or socio-economic drivers possibly contributing to these spikes. Moreover, the data points to a subtle increase in case numbers in mid-2023, with September recording 9 cases, indicating potential shifts in factors influencing the disease's transmission dynamics. This pattern highlights the importance of continued surveillance and preparedness to mitigate potential outbreaks, particularly as climate patterns and urban dynamics evolve in the region.
