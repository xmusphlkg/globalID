## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal infection caused by the ingestion of food or water contaminated with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It manifests through severe watery diarrhea, often leading to rapid dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. The disease primarily affects areas with inadequate access to clean water and sanitation, thriving in conditions of poverty and displacement. Left untreated, cholera can be fatal within hours, but with prompt rehydration therapy – through oral rehydration solutions or intravenous fluids – recovery is typically swift. Prevention hinges on improved sanitation, access to clean water, and education on hygiene practices, supplemented by vaccines in high-risk areas.

## Highlights

- **Stable caseload and seasonal fluctuations:** Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland show a stable trend, with seasonal fluctuations, peaking typically around late summer and early fall. <br/>
- **Low mortality rate:** Despite consistent case numbers, the mortality rate remains absent, indicating effective healthcare interventions and management. <br/>
- **Recent decrease in cases:** Compared to previous years, cases in August 2025 (5 cases) are lower than the peak values noted in previous Augusts, suggesting possible improvements in prevention and control measures. <br/>
- **Decline after 2023:** A noticeable decline in cases is observed post-2023, which may reflect enhanced preventive strategies or environmental changes impacting transmission. <br/>

The cholera data from the Chinese mainland between 2015 to 2025 indicates low incidence with noticeable seasonal peaks, predominantly occurring in the summer months. Annual increases in cases typically occur around August, as seen in 2018 and 2019 when 12 cases were recorded each August, and 2023 with 8 and 9 cases in August and September, respectively. Aside from these peaks, most months, particularly during winter and spring, report few or no cases, suggesting a seasonal pattern corresponding with warmer, wetter conditions that favor cholera transmission. This pattern may be related to climatic factors or increased human activity, such as travel and agriculture, during these months.

Overall, there were no cholera-related deaths recorded during this period, which suggests effective medical management and robust public health responses in the Chinese mainland. Despite the periodic spikes, the outbreaks have been contained, reflecting a controlled epidemiological environment. Nonetheless, certain years like 2016 and 2022 saw more pronounced increases, potentially due to external influences like environmental or socio-economic changes. These observations underscore the importance of continued surveillance and responsive public health measures, especially in anticipation of peak periods, to ensure containment and prevent potential outbreaks from becoming more severe. This consistent pattern necessitates vigilance and preparedness in public health efforts to maintain control over cholera incidences.
