# Cholera

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal disease caused by the ingestion of food or water contaminated with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. The illness is characterized by rapid onset of severe watery diarrhea, which can lead to dehydration and, if untreated, death. It is primarily spread through poor sanitation and inadequate access to clean water. Cholera outbreaks can occur in both endemic regions and as epidemics in areas experiencing natural disasters or displacement crises. Prevention involves safe drinking water, proper sanitation, and hygiene practices. Treatment includes rapid rehydration with oral rehydration solutions and, in severe cases, intravenous fluids and antibiotics.

## Highlights

- Throughout 2015 to 2024, there were multiple periods of increased Cholera cases in Chinese mainland, particularly from the months of June to October, with peaks observed in both the summer and autumn months. <br/>
- From 2015 to 2025, no deaths from Cholera were reported, indicating a potential success in reducing mortality despite periodic fluctuations in case numbers. <br/>
- Significant spikes in cases were recorded in August 2016, August 2018, and August 2019, highlighting a recurring pattern of increased incidence during late summer. <br/>
- The year 2024 saw a notable reduction in cases from September to December, suggesting possible positive impacts of control measures implemented in preceding months. <br/>

Cholera incidence on the Chinese mainland has exhibited a fluctuating pattern, with occasional peaks observed within the data from 2015 to 2024. Over this period, no cholera-related deaths were reported, indicative of timely medical interventions or effective public health responses to mitigate the fatal outcomes typically associated with the disease. The number of reported cases largely remained low per month, except for certain months when notable increases were recorded, suggesting potential outbreaks or clusters.

Analysis of the data reveals a certain seasonality in cholera occurrences, with higher case numbers often emerging in the warmer months, particularly from June through September. This seasonal trend aligns with the known patterns of cholera transmission, as the pathogenic Vibrio cholerae thrive in warmer aquatic environments. The highest monthly peak occurred in August 2018, with 12 cases, and another significant peak of similar magnitude was observed in August 2019, reinforcing the seasonal aspect of the disease. Although sporadic cases are seen in other months, these peaks necessitate targeted public health interventions during the summer to effectively manage and control the spread of cholera during its likely periods of resurgence.
