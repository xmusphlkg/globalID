# Cholera

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection with the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*. It is typically spread through contaminated water and food. Symptoms include severe diarrhea and vomiting, which can lead to dehydration and, if untreated, death. Cholera is most common in areas with inadequate water treatment, poor sanitation, and inadequate hygiene. Effective prevention involves ensuring access to clean water, proper sanitation, and good hygiene practices. Treatment primarily includes rehydration, either oral or intravenous, and antibiotics in severe cases.

## Highlights

- Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland have shown seasonal peaks, particularly in the summer months (June to September), with notable increases in August and September of various years.<br/>
- Despite seasonal fluctuations, overall annual case numbers remain relatively low, with sporadic higher peaks in 2018 and 2019, and a slight increase again in 2023.<br/>
- There have been no reported deaths from cholera in the Chinese mainland from June 2014 to June 2024, indicating effective case management and treatment.<br/>
- Recent data for 2024 shows no reported cholera cases from January to June, suggesting a potential decline or improved control measures.<br/>

An analysis of the reported cholera cases in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals fluctuating trends in the incidence of the disease, with notable peaks during specific months. The data indicates that cholera cases tend to increase during the summer months, particularly in June, July, and August, which aligns with the seasonal nature of cholera outbreaks often exacerbated by warmer temperatures and increased rainfall leading to contaminated water sources. For instance, August consistently shows higher cases, with notable spikes in 2016 (5 cases), 2018 (12 cases), and 2019 (12 cases). This pattern suggests a need for heightened surveillance and preventive measures during these months to mitigate outbreaks.

Throughout the ten-year period, the number of deaths reported remained at zero, reflecting effective treatment protocols and access to medical care. Despite the seasonal increases, certain years like 2017 and 2021 showed relatively lower case numbers overall. Additionally, 2022 and 2023 exhibited a resurgence of cases, particularly in the summer, indicating potential gaps in ongoing preventive measures or environmental factors that may have contributed to increased transmission. The data underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and public health interventions, especially in peak seasons, to sustain the low mortality rate and further reduce the incidence of cholera in mainland China.
