# Cholera

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection with the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*, typically transmitted through contaminated water or food. It is characterized by rapid onset of watery diarrhea, which can lead to severe dehydration and death if untreated. Cholera thrives in areas with inadequate sanitation, poor hygiene, and limited access to clean water. Outbreaks are common in regions facing natural disasters or conflict, where health systems are disrupted. Effective treatment includes prompt rehydration, while prevention relies on improved water quality, sanitation, hygiene practices, and vaccination in endemic or high-risk areas.

## Highlights

- Cholera incidence in the Chinese mainland remains sporadic and low, with no fatalities reported from May 2015 to May 2025. <br/>
- Seasonal peaks are evident, with most cases occurring during the summer months (Juneâ€“September), particularly in years such as 2018, 2019, and 2023. <br/>
- While some years (e.g., 2021 and early 2024) saw near-zero monthly cases, there has been a slight resurgence with localized activity in late 2024 and a small increase again in May 2025. <br/>
- The long-term trend indicates effective control measures are in place, though continued surveillance is essential to manage potential summer outbreaks. <br/>

From mid-2015 through mid-2025, reported cholera cases in mainland China have remained consistently low on a monthly basis, typically in the single digits, with no recorded deaths throughout the entire period. Seasonal clustering is evident, with the majority of cases occurring between June and Septemberâ€”corresponding to summer and early fall when climatic and environmental conditions may be more conducive to Vibrio cholerae transmission. Notably, peaks occurred in August 2018 and August 2019, each recording 12 cases, and in September 2023 with 9 cases. These surges suggest sporadic localized outbreaks rather than widespread transmission, likely indicating effective outbreak containment and surveillance.

There is also a discernible pattern of inactivity during the colder months, particularly from November to April, when case numbers consistently drop to zero or near-zero across all years. This seasonality supports the association between warmer temperatures, increased rainfall or flooding, and greater cholera risk, possibly through waterborne contamination. The absence of deaths despite multiple yearly cases implies successful clinical management and early detection infrastructure. The trend overall reflects a controlled endemic presence with effective public health response mechanisms, though the repetitive seasonal spikes indicate the need for continued vigilance, particularly in known high-risk months.
