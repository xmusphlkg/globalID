# Cholera

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection with the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*, typically contracted through ingestion of contaminated water or food. It is characterized by sudden onset of profuse, watery diarrhea, which can lead to severe dehydration and death if untreated. Cholera outbreaks are closely linked to inadequate sanitation, poor hygiene, and lack of clean drinking water. While it is preventable and treatable, cholera remains a significant public health threat in areas with limited access to healthcare. Oral rehydration therapy and improved water and sanitation are essential for effective control and prevention.

## Highlights

- Cholera incidence in the Chinese mainland remains low, with most months reporting zero cases; no deaths have been recorded from 2015 through April 2025. <br/>
- A seasonal pattern is evident, with case peaks typically occurring between June and September, suggesting a summer-related transmission trend. <br/>
- Annual case numbers fluctuated, with higher counts observed in 2018, 2019, and 2022, but a general decline occurred from late 2023 into 2025. <br/>
- As of April 2025, no cholera cases have been reported in the first four months, indicating a stable and controlled public health situation. <br/>

Cholera incidence on the Chinese mainland from 2015 to early 2025 demonstrates a recurring seasonal pattern, with the majority of reported cases clustering during the warmer months between June and October. Peaks were observed in several years (e.g., 11 cases in October 2016, 12 in August 2018, and August 2019), often followed by months of zero reported cases. This cyclicality likely reflects environmental and behavioral factors favoring the transmission of *Vibrio cholerae*, such as increased seafood consumption and exposure to contaminated water sources in summer. Despite fluctuations in annual case numbers, there was a consistent absence of reported deaths, indicating effective clinical management and access to care.

Over the years, annual total cases fluctuated without a clear upward or downward trend, though with occasional increases (such as in 2022 and 2023). The data suggests local, sporadic outbreaks rather than widespread transmission, possibly linked to localized hygiene breaches or variable environmental exposures. There was a notable absence of cases through extended periods (e.g., late 2020 to early 2021), aligning with broader public health interventions during the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have also mitigated cholera spread incidentally through improved sanitation and mobility restrictions.

Across the entire recorded period from April 2015 to April 2025, there were no reported deaths from cholera in mainland China, despite intermittent case detections nearly every year. This absence of mortality, even during months with relatively elevated case counts—such as August 2018 and August 2019 where 12 cases were reported in each month—suggests that clinical outcomes were effectively managed. The persistent zero deaths may indicate successful early diagnosis, rapid medical response, effective treatment protocols such as oral rehydration and antibiotics when needed, and robust public health infrastructure. Furthermore, cholera cases occur mostly in clusters around summer months, aligning with the known seasonal patterns of the disease, yet without any associated fatal outcomes.

This consistently favorable mortality trend also reflects a potential absence of large-scale outbreaks or rural epidemics with limited access to care during the period. If the virus strains involved were of lower virulence, or if public health containment effectively limited transmission to small, localized, and medically reachable populations, it would explain these contained outcomes. Additionally, systematic reporting and surveillance seem reliable, as sporadic but traceable case data are maintained monthly, reinforcing confidence in the accuracy of the zero death count through a decade of surveillance.
