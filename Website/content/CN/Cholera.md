# Cholera

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It is primarily transmitted through contaminated water and food, leading to severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalances. Symptoms typically appear within a few hours to five days after exposure and include watery diarrhea, vomiting, and leg cramps. If untreated, cholera can lead to death within hours. Outbreaks are often associated with poor sanitation and inadequate access to clean drinking water. Effective prevention strategies include improving water quality, sanitation, and hygiene, along with vaccination in high-risk areas. Timely treatment with rehydration and antibiotics is crucial for recovery.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland consistently peak during summer months, especially from June to September, indicating a link with warmer weather. <br/>

- **Variable Case Numbers:** The number of cases fluctuates annually without a clear trend. The highest monthly cases were in August 2018 and 2019, with 12 each. <br/>

- **Zero Mortality Rate:** No deaths have been reported, highlighting effective treatment and management. <br/>

- **Recent Decline:** As of January 2025, no cases have been reported, suggesting effective control measures or a natural decline in incidence. <br/>

The data on cholera cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a pattern of sporadic outbreaks with no reported deaths. The incidence of cholera appears to fluctuate annually, with notable peaks during the summer months, particularly from June to September. This seasonal trend is consistent with the environmental conditions that favor the transmission of cholera, such as warmer temperatures and increased rainfall, which can lead to contamination of water sources.

In 2016, there was a significant increase in cases, with a peak in October (11 cases), suggesting a possible outbreak or increased transmission during that period. Similarly, in 2018 and 2019, August saw the highest number of cases (12 each), indicating a recurring pattern of heightened transmission during late summer. The data from 2022 and 2023 also show increased cases during the summer months, with a peak in September 2023 (9 cases). This pattern underscores the importance of targeted public health interventions during these months to prevent outbreaks.

Overall, the absence of reported deaths suggests effective clinical management and public health responses to cholera cases. However, the recurring seasonal peaks highlight the need for ongoing surveillance and preventive measures, particularly in regions prone to cholera outbreaks. Public health strategies could include improving water sanitation, promoting hygiene practices, and ensuring rapid response capabilities to contain potential outbreaks.
