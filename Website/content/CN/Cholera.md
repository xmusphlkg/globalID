## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal infection caused by the bacterium Vibrio cholerae, primarily spread through contaminated water and food. The disease is characterized by sudden onset of severe watery diarrhea, which can lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, posing risks of death if untreated. Endemic in regions with poor sanitation, cholera outbreaks often occur in settings of inadequate water treatment and basic hygiene infrastructure. While it can affect people of all ages, the highest risk populations are in developing countries. Prompt treatment through rehydration solutions and antibiotics can dramatically reduce mortality rates. Prevention focuses on improving water, sanitation, and hygiene.

## Highlights

- Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a seasonal trend, with peaks notably in the summer months, particularly July and August. <br/>
- From 2015 to July 2025, the number of cases fluctuated, with an observed low in 2019 but a subsequent gradual increase, especially from mid-2022 onward. <br/>
- Despite the cases detected, there have been no reported deaths attributed to cholera, indicating a likely effective healthcare response and management of cases. <br/>
- Recent data from 2025 shows a slight increase in cases, prompting potential public health actions and enhanced monitoring during peak months to mitigate further spread. <br/>

Initial observations indicate that cases predominantly occur during warmer months, notably peaking in August across several years â€“ particularly 2016, 2018, and 2019. This pattern might correlate with environmental factors conducive to cholera transmission or human activities such as increased travel or gatherings that heighten exposure to contaminated sources. The absence of reported deaths throughout the decade highlights effective clinical management and intervention strategies that prevent fatalities.

Analyzing the variability from year to year, alternating declines and increases in cases are evident. For instance, the year 2022 experienced higher annual cases than its preceding and succeeding years, with the peak in July. Such dynamic fluctuations may hinge on socio-environmental factors or shifts in surveillance intensity. Cases in 2023 and projections into 2024 again mark August with increased counts followed by drops, mirroring previous years' trends. This recurring pattern is a basis for epidemiological investigations into factors like climate, population density changes, or shifts in public health strategies. Understanding these dynamics facilitates the development of proactive containment policies and resource allocation for enhancing water sanitation systems, crucial for reducing transmission during peak periods.
