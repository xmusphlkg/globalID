# Cholera

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It is typically spread through contaminated water and food and can lead to severe dehydration and death if untreated. Symptoms include watery diarrhea, vomiting, and muscle cramps, often appearing within hours to five days after exposure. Cholera is most common in regions with inadequate sanitation and limited access to clean drinking water. Effective prevention includes ensuring safe water, proper sanitation, and good hygiene practices. Treatment primarily involves rehydration, either orally or intravenously, and in some cases, antibiotics.

## Highlights

- Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland have shown seasonal peaks, particularly in summer months, reflecting temperature-related trends. <br/>
- Recent years indicate a decline in overall cases, with 2024 showing no recorded cases as of May. <br/>
- No deaths have been reported throughout the observed period, suggesting effective clinical management of cases. <br/>
- Clustered cases in summer months, such as August 2018 and 2019 with 12 cases each, highlight the need for focused preventive measures during this period. <br/>

The reported Cholera data from the Chinese mainland displays a clear seasonal trend, with cases spiking in the summer months from May to October, indicative of the influence of environmental factors conducive to the spread of the disease such as higher temperatures and rainfall. Peaks in Cholera incidences are consistent across the years, with August being particularly significant, suggesting heightened transmission during this time. For example, the years 2016, 2018, and 2019 each saw notable increases during the summer, with August 2018 and 2019 recording 12 cases, the highest in any observed month.

Despite these trends, the overall rate of Cholera cases remains low, with no deaths reported over the decade of data. This fact reflects either the controlled virulence of the strains involved or effective medical interventions. The data also suggests that Cholera might not be a year-round issue but rather a seasonal hazard, given the many months recording zero cases, especially during the colder periods from November to April. An outlying surge in October 2016, when cases rose to 11, is a deviation from the general pattern and may require special attention to decipher unique contributing factors for such an anomaly.
