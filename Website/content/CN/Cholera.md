## Introduction

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by infection of the intestine with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. It is typically spread through contaminated water or food and is prevalent in areas with inadequate water treatment, poor sanitation, and insufficient hygiene. Symptoms include severe diarrhea and dehydration, which can lead to death if untreated. Cholera outbreaks can occur rapidly, especially in densely populated regions. Prevention focuses on access to clean water, proper sanitation, and hygiene practices. Oral rehydration solutions and antibiotics are effective treatments, significantly reducing mortality rates when administered promptly.

## Highlights

- **Cholera cases in the Chinese mainland have fluctuated, with peaks in summer months.** This aligns with cholera's seasonal nature, often increasing during warmer periods. <br/>

- **The highest case numbers were in August 2018 and 2019, with 12 cases each.** This suggests a recurring seasonal outbreak pattern, highlighting the need for targeted interventions. <br/>

- **No deaths have been reported, indicating effective clinical management.** This reflects the healthcare system's ability to manage cholera cases successfully. <br/>

- **In 2024, a slight increase in cases was noted in July and August, but overall, there is a decreasing trend compared to previous years.** This may indicate improved public health measures. <br/>

The data on cholera cases in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals a pattern of sporadic outbreaks with no reported deaths. The incidence of cholera cases appears to fluctuate annually, with notable peaks in certain months, particularly during the summer and early autumn. For instance, in 2016, there was a significant increase in cases from August to October, with a peak of 11 cases in October. Similarly, in 2018 and 2019, August saw the highest number of cases, with 12 reported each year. This seasonal pattern suggests a potential correlation with environmental factors such as temperature and rainfall, which are known to influence cholera transmission.

From 2020 onwards, the data shows a relatively stable number of cases with occasional spikes, such as in July 2022 and August 2023, where cases reached 10 and 8, respectively. The absence of deaths throughout the period indicates effective clinical management and public health interventions. However, the persistent occurrence of cases highlights the need for continuous monitoring and preventive measures, particularly in high-risk months. The data underscores the importance of maintaining robust water sanitation and hygiene practices to mitigate the risk of cholera outbreaks.
