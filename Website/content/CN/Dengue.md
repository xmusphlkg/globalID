# Dengue

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus, transmitted primarily by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes. It is common in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. Symptoms typically include high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, muscle and joint pain, nausea, vomiting, swollen glands, and skin rash. In severe cases, it can develop into dengue hemorrhagic fever or dengue shock syndrome, both potentially fatal. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but supportive care helps manage symptoms. Prevention focuses on mosquito control and avoiding bites. Vaccine options are limited and recommended only in specific populations.

## Highlights

- Dengue incidence in mainland China shows a distinct seasonal trend, with peak outbreaks occurring between August and October annually. <br/>
- Major surges were reported in 2019 and 2023, with cases exceeding 8,000 and 6,000 respectively in September alone, indicating significant outbreaks. <br/>
- Following a notable drop in cases from 2020 to 2022, 2023 and 2024 marked a sharp resurgence, with October 2024 reaching a record 11,083 cases. <br/>
- As of May 2025, dengue cases remain relatively low (126 cases) with no reported deaths, suggesting that transmission is currently low but surveillance should remain vigilant. <br/>

Dengue cases in mainland China demonstrate a strong seasonal pattern, peaking consistently between July and October across multiple years. This trend is most prominent during the high-incidence years of 2015, 2017, 2018, 2019, 2023, and especially 2024, which saw an unprecedented surge with over 11,000 cases in October alone. Historically, September has marked the apex of infections, but in 2024, this shifted slightly later, possibly indicating a change in climatic or ecological factors favorable to mosquito breeding. The data also illustrates that most years experience a sharp uptick in cases beginning in July, aligning with the monsoon season and increased mosquito activity.

The year-by-year variability suggests possible influences from vector control efficiency, climatic anomalies, or changes in surveillance and reporting practices. Notably, 2020 and 2021 saw a dramatic decline in cases concurrent with global COVID-19 control measures, hinting at collateral benefits from movement restriction and public health infrastructure reinforcement during that period. Following the low-incidence period, 2023 and 2024 saw a resurgence, with 2024 showing the highest recorded monthly and annual totals in the dataset, yet without any reported deaths, which could reflect improved clinical management or reporting inconsistencies. Deaths remain rare overall, occurring sporadically and not necessarily correlating with peak incidence months.

Similarly, during other high-incidence years such as **2023 and 2024**, where case numbers surged above 6,000 and even 11,000 in certain months, **no** deaths were recorded in 2024, and only **one** in 2023.

The overall case-fatality ratio (CFR) across the dataset is extremely low, reflecting effective clinical management and surveillance. The absence of fatalities in several years—even during moderate outbreaks—suggests improved interventions or lower virulence of circulating serotypes. The absence of deaths in recent years, particularly in 2024 despite record case numbers, highlights advancements in outbreak control, diagnosis, and patient care.
