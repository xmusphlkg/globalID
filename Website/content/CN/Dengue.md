# Dengue

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection primarily found in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. It is transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito, which is most active during early morning and before dusk. The virus has four distinct serotypes: DEN-1, DEN-2, DEN-3, and DEN-4. Infection causes flu-like symptoms such as high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. While most cases resolve with supportive care, severe dengue, previously known as dengue hemorrhagic fever, can be life-threatening and requires hospitalization. Prevention relies heavily on vector control and protective measures against mosquito bites.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Case Numbers**: The number of dengue cases in the Chinese mainland shows a consistent increase from January to June 2025, suggesting heightened transmission during warmer months. <br/>

- **Seasonal Peaks**: Past data suggest consistent peaks in dengue cases around September and October, indicating a seasonal trend likely linked with optimal transmission conditions. <br/>

- **Minimal Death Rate**: Despite high case numbers in some months, the death rate from dengue remains low, indicating effective management and treatment strategies. <br/>

- **Comparative Analysis**: As of June 2025, cases are moderately high compared to previous June figures post-2020, emphasizing the importance of sustained public health interventions and surveillance. <br/>

The Dengue case data in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to mid-2025 exhibits clear seasonal and yearly patterns. Significant peaks occur in late summer and fall months, notably from June to October. For example, in 2015, September and October witnessed sharp rises with 1,479 and 1,435 cases, respectively. These patterns persist annually, with peaks reappearing consistently in late summer from 2016 through 2019 and again in 2023 and 2024. The spikes in September 2019 and October 2024 were particularly intense, with 8,036 and 11,083 cases reported. Seasonal trends align with conditions favorable for Aedes mosquitoes, the primary vectors of Dengue, thriving in warm, humid climates during these months.

Despite variability in case numbers, Dengue mortality remained low—only sporadic deaths, typically not exceeding one per high-case month, except for October 2019, which saw two deaths. Case numbers dropped significantly during winter and spring, indicating effective disease control, reduced vector breeding, or changes in public health measures. From late 2019 to 2021, Dengue cases notably decreased, likely due to interventions and mobility restrictions during COVID-19, impacting vector control and human-vector interactions. Nevertheless, a resurgence occurred by mid-2023, highlighting the challenge of controlling Dengue amidst shifting climatic and ecological conditions, and underscoring the disease's rapid growth once favorable conditions return.

Upon reviewing the dengue data for the Chinese mainland from 2015 to early 2025, it is apparent that the mortality rate from the disease is remarkably low compared to the number of reported cases. There were no deaths recorded for substantial periods, with mortality occurring sporadically in a few instances over the years. Specifically, 2016 saw a death in February with relatively low case numbers, while 2017 had one death each in October and December alongside an uptick in cases during the latter months. In 2019, three deaths were reported, distributed between October and November during a notable surge in case numbers. Subsequently, only one death was documented in September 2023 amidst rising case counts.

Despite periodic spikes in case numbers, such as the striking increase to over 10,000 cases in October 2024, deaths remain minimal. This suggests a potentially robust health response or public health infrastructure enabling the management of severe cases and preventing fatalities on a larger scale. Seasonal fluctuations are strongly evident with higher cases during the latter parts of the summer months and early autumn, indicative of the seasonal nature of dengue transmission which favors warmer months conducive to mosquito activity. Effective disease management strategies, either via medical treatment, vector control, or public health education, seem to have contributed significantly to maintaining the remarkably low mortality despite significant caseloads in certain periods.
