# Dengue

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by four closely related viruses (DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, and DENV-4). Transmitted primarily by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes, it causes flu-like symptoms such as high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. Severe cases can develop into dengue hemorrhagic fever or dengue shock syndrome, potentially fatal conditions. Dengue is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, especially in urban and semi-urban areas. Effective vector control and preventive measures are essential in managing and reducing its transmission.

## Highlights

- Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland have seen a seasonal pattern, with peaks usually occurring between July and October each year. <br/>
- 2023 witnessed a significant surge in cases, particularly during the months of July to October, indicating an active dengue transmission season. <br/>
- As of August 2024, cases have declined compared to the peak in 2023, but numbers remain higher than the early months of 2024, suggesting a potential upward trend. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations in case numbers, the death toll remains low, indicating potential effective clinical management and control measures. <br/>

The data on dengue in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows significant seasonal surges, particularly in late summer and early fall, with peaks occurring in September and October. For example, 2014 saw a sharp rise to 28,796 cases in October, and similarly, in 2019, there were 8,036 cases in September. A recent surge occurred in 2023, with 6,494 cases reported in September, one of the highest in recent years.

Throughout the period, there were 10 recorded deaths, indicating a relatively low mortality rate despite fluctuating case numbers. The seasonal patterns suggest that warmer months, which favor mosquito breeding, are the most common periods for outbreaks, while winter and early spring consistently show lower case numbers, likely due to colder weather inhibiting mosquito activity.

The overall trend indicates that dengue remains a recurring public health challenge in China, though outbreaks follow a predictable seasonal cycle, and mortality remains low relative to the number of cases. The data also highlights the importance of continued surveillance and control measures during peak transmission periods.

This pattern, where high case counts do not correspond to proportionally high death counts, continues throughout the period. For example, 2019 saw another sharp rise in cases in September (8,036 cases), yet only two deaths were reported in October and one in November despite thousands of cases.

Deaths from Dengue in the Chinese mainland have remained sporadic and relatively low. From 2014 to 2024, despite several spikes in Dengue cases—such as in 2023, when cases peaked at 6,494 in September—deaths have remained rare, with only a single fatality recorded that month. The low mortality despite high infection rates may reflect effective public health measures, improved medical interventions, and disease management strategies.
