# Dengue

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus, predominantly transmitted by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, including Southeast Asia, the Pacific Islands, and the Americas. The illness is characterized by sudden onset of high fever, severe headaches, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding manifestations. Severe forms, such as dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome, can be life-threatening. There is no specific treatment, but early detection and supportive care significantly reduce the risk of complications and mortality.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Trends:** Dengue cases in Chinese mainland peak notably from July to October, with significant spikes in 2019 and 2024. <br/>
- **Yearly Fluctuations:** A sharp decline in cases occurred during 2020, likely due to pandemic measures, but cases increased thereafter. <br/>
- **Mortality Rates:** Deaths from dengue are infrequent, occurring in specific years such as 2016, 2017, 2018, 2019, and 2023, with none recently. <br/>
- **2025 Case Analysis:** By October 2025, cases have surged to 3,727, reflecting continued high incidence similar to 2023 and 2024 trends, with no fatalities reported. <br/>

The dengue outbreak in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 exhibits seasonal fluctuation with spikes in cases primarily in late summer and early autumn. Peaks occur in September and October, likely due to favorable mosquito breeding conditions like increased temperatures and rainfall. A notable surge happened in 2019, with 8,036 cases in September, suggesting severe transmission possibly influenced by climatic factors. High numbers reappeared in 2023 and 2024, with 2024 setting a record at 11,083 cases in October.

The pattern reveals cycles of increased transmission during peak months followed by significant reductions, bottoming out from February to April. Despite large case numbers, deaths have remained minimal, indicating effective healthcare and public health measures. Consistent high case volumes, however, stress healthcare and highlight the need for continued vigilance. Sporadic increases may result from climate changes, virus dynamics, or shifts in health policies. It's crucial for authorities to consider these seasonal patterns in planning control strategies and interventions to reduce the burden of dengue in affected regions. 

Managing dengue requires tackling urban mosquito breeding sites, ensuring public awareness, and strengthening health systems to handle peak case periods effectively. Observing these trends helps anticipate and mitigate risks, ensuring better preparedness for future outbreaks.

The analysis of dengue-related deaths in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 indicates a low fatality rate despite occasional surges in cases. Within this decade, there were only six reported dengue-related deaths amidst a substantial number of cases, highlighting the effective control and clinical management strategies in place. Notably, higher case numbers, particularly post-monsoon, did not consistently lead to increased deaths. For example, September 2019 recorded over 8,000 cases, but October 2019 had only two deaths, even as high incidence continued. This suggests that public health measures effectively mitigate mortality.

Deaths were sporadic, occurring in 2016, 2017, 2019, and 2023, without forming a consistent mortality pattern. The years 2020 to 2025 recorded no deaths, despite case resurgences in months like September and October. This absence of a direct correlation between cases and deaths implies improvements in healthcare responses, public awareness, or possibly lower strain virulence. Effective vector control and healthcare interventions seem to maintain low mortality rates despite annual case fluctuations.

The consistent management of case surges underscores the importance of sustained surveillance and preventive measures to manage potential future outbreaks. The data highlights the critical role of healthcare systems and public health policies in containing fatal outcomes during dengue outbreaks, maintaining a focus on reducing cases and related complications.
