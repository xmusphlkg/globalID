# Dengue

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, primarily transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito. The disease is caused by four distinct but closely related serotypes of the dengue virus (DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, and DENV-4). Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe conditions such as dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome. Dengue fever is characterized by sudden onset of fever, severe headache, muscle and joint pains, and rash. There is no specific treatment for dengue, and management focuses on supportive care with vigilant monitoring of symptoms.

## Highlights

- Dengue incidences have fluctuated over the years, with notable peaks in the months of July to November every year since 2015. The highest peaks occurred between August and October.<br/>
- There was a sharp increase in cases in 2023 and 2024, with 2024 October registering 11,083 cases, the highest recorded in the data set.<br/>
- Seasonal trends show a persistent rise in cases after each summer, indicating potential environmental and vector-specific factors affecting transmission.<br/>
- Mortality due to Dengue remains minimal with only a few deaths recorded. In recent years, there have been zero deaths, suggesting effective clinical management and interventions.<br/>

Dengue cases in mainland China exhibit distinct seasonality, with sharp increases during late summer and early autumn, aligning with favorable conditions for Aedes mosquitoes, the primary vectors. Spikes in Dengue cases consistently appear in September and October, notably peaking in October 2024 at 11,083 cases. This pattern underscores the need for intensified vector control and public health measures during peak transmission periods. Moreover, dramatic year-to-year fluctuations are evident, such as September 2019’s 8,036 cases and August 2023’s 4,198 cases. These intense surges contrast starkly with the low numbers during the early winter and spring months, echoing a natural decrease in mosquito activity when temperatures drop.

Examining fatalities, Dengue-related deaths are sparse and scattered, primarily noted in 2016, 2017, and 2019, despite varying case counts. This suggests improvements in clinical management and health responses that could minimize deaths despite high infection rates. The data reflects a healthcare system that manages to keep fatalities low, although increased attention may be required to handle the heightened caseloads during peak months effectively. Understanding these trends is crucial for crafting timely interventions, bolstering healthcare response capabilities, and ensuring resources are strategically allocated, particularly during the months when Dengue presents the greatest threat to public health in China.

The dataset on dengue mortality in the Chinese mainland exhibits a relatively low death toll compared to the number of reported cases over the years. Notably, the majority of months from July 2015 through June 2025 show zero recorded deaths, indicating an effective public health response in preventing fatalities even as the disease incidence fluctuates. The few instances where deaths were reported occurred sporadically, with only one death recorded in months such as February 2016, October 2017, December 2017, December 2018, October 2019, and November 2019. The data suggests an improved ability to manage cases and mitigate mortality.

Years with particularly high case counts, such as 2015, 2019, 2023, and 2024, did not correspond to similar peaks in fatality, pointing towards a commendable healthcare response. It could be attributed to factors such as timely medical intervention, improved vector control, and heightened public health awareness campaigns. Moreover, the sustained periods without any fatalities, even in the face of sizable outbreaks, attest to robust healthcare infrastructure and successful implementation of dengue management protocols. The spike in cases in 2024 without consequent deaths underscores the critical role of preparedness and healthcare measures in place to protect lives despite increased disease pressure.
