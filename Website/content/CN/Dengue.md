# Dengue

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Dengue is a viral infection transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes, primarily Aedes aegypti. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. Symptoms include high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding, such as nose or gum bleeding. Severe cases can lead to Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever or Dengue Shock Syndrome, potentially fatal conditions. There is no specific antiviral treatment; management focuses on supportive care to maintain fluid balance and alleviate symptoms. Prevention relies on mosquito control and personal protection measures, along with the development and use of vaccines.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Dengue cases peak in late summer to early autumn, with significant surges in September and October, especially in 2014, 2019, and 2023. <br/>

- **Recent Increase:** September 2024 reported 4,845 cases, indicating a sharp rise compared to 2023, highlighting the need for immediate public health action. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite high case numbers, mortality remains low, with sporadic deaths, reflecting effective medical treatment. <br/>

- **Potential Resurgence:** The rising trend in 2024 suggests a possible resurgence, requiring continued vector control and community awareness efforts. <br/>

The data shows a significant pattern of seasonal fluctuation in Dengue cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024. Dengue cases consistently peak during late summer and early autumn, particularly in September and October, with the highest spike occurring in October 2014 (28,796 cases), followed by a similar trend in subsequent years. These peaks are characteristic of Dengue transmission, which is heavily influenced by environmental factors such as temperature and rainfall, which facilitate mosquito breeding. A sharp decline is observed in the winter months, from December to February, where cases are nearly negligible, indicating the seasonality of the disease.

Additionally, the number of deaths remains relatively low despite fluctuations in case numbers, with 2014 and 2019 showing a slight increase in fatalities. Notably, the cases surged again in 2023 and 2024, after a period of lower incidence from 2020-2022, likely due to a combination of public health measures, reduced travel, and possibly climatic changes affecting mosquito populations during the COVID-19 pandemic. However, a resurgence in cases in 2023-2024 suggests a return to pre-pandemic transmission dynamics.

Dengue-related deaths in mainland China appear to be relatively rare despite occasional surges in reported cases. For instance, in 2014, the peak number of cases in October (28,796) was accompanied by only two deaths, and during other high-case months in the same year, such as September, the death toll was similarly low. This pattern suggests that while Dengue outbreaks can cause significant morbidity, fatal outcomes are less common, potentially due to robust healthcare responses or the nature of the disease's impact in the region. The highest death toll observed over the entire dataset was in 2019, where a total of three deaths occurred alongside a substantial rise in cases, particularly in September (8,036 cases). Even in subsequent years with fluctuations in cases, deaths remained minimal.

Further analysis shows that periods with large outbreaks—such as August 2023 with 4,198 cases or September 2023 with 6,494 cases—resulted in very few deaths, highlighting a pattern where deaths are not proportionally aligned with case surges. The overall death rate across the years is consistently low, with some isolated spikes, indicating that although Dengue remains a public health concern in terms of infection rates, fatalities are rare. This could reflect improvements in disease management, early detection, and treatment protocols.
