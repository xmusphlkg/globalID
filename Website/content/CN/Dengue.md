# Dengue

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus, transmitted primarily by the Aedes aegypti mosquito. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. The disease has four distinct serotypes (DENV-1 to DENV-4), and infection with one provides lifelong immunity to that type but not to the others. Symptoms include high fever, severe headache, joint and muscle pain, skin rash, and mild bleeding. Severe forms, such as dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome, can be life-threatening. There is no specific treatment, but supportive care can help manage symptoms effectively.

## Highlights

- Dengue incidence in mainland China shows a clear seasonal pattern, peaking from July to October each year, with major outbreaks observed in 2019, 2023, and significantly in 2024. <br/>
- The 2024 season experienced the highest recorded monthly case count (11,083 in October), surpassing the 2019 peak and highlighting a strong dengue resurgence. <br/>
- Fatality rates remain remarkably low across the 10-year period, with very few deaths (0–2 annually), indicating improved clinical management and surveillance. <br/>
- As of April 2025, dengue activity is relatively low (74 cases) with no deaths, suggesting the typical post-peak decline and a return to baseline transmission levels. <br/>

Over the ten-year span from 2015 to early 2025, Dengue case trends in the Chinese mainland reveal clear seasonal peaks primarily concentrated between July and November. The most notable surges occurred in 2019 and 2024, both exhibiting dramatic escalations with totals exceeding 17,000 and 23,000 cases annually, respectively. September and October consistently emerge as the peak months. For instance, in 2019, September accounted for 8,036 cases, while 2024 shows an even more pronounced October spike with 11,083 cases. These patterns correlate with known climatic and ecological conditions conducive to increased mosquito activity during late summer and early fall in subtropical regions.

From 2020 through 2022, a sharp drop is observed, including a near cessation of cases in 2021, potentially reflecting the effect of COVID-19 containment measures such as reduced travel, improved vector control, or underreporting. The rebound in 2023 and 2024 suggests renewed vulnerability or resurgence of vector populations. Fatalities remained extremely low across the time series, totaling only six deaths—demonstrating a low case-fatality rate. This suggests effective clinical management of cases despite periodic spikes in incidence. However, the sudden post-pandemic resurgence signals the need for renewed public health focus on vector surveillance and environmental control.

Between 2015 and early 2025, Dengue mortality on the Chinese mainland was remarkably low relative to case counts, with only **seven deaths** recorded across the entire data set. These deaths were confined to isolated years: one in February 2016, one in October and another in December 2017, one in December 2018, three in the peak epidemic year 2019 (two in October and one in November), and one in September 2023. This demonstrates a fatality rate far below 0.01% across the dataset, emphasizing the remarkably high survival rate even during outbreak surges. Notably, no deaths were reported during the largest surge in 2024, when October alone saw over 11,000 cases.

Yearly patterns show that increases in deaths aligned only partially with epidemics. The largest yearly death toll occurred in 2019, coinciding with a peak of over 33,000 cases from July to October. Other high-incidence years, such as 2017 and 2023, recorded thousands of cases with just one death per year, concentrated in outbreak peaks. The data suggest that factors beyond case volume — possibly geographic distribution, healthcare access, virus strain, or co-morbidities — contributed to the rare occurrence of deaths. The overall profile reflects effective case management despite seasonal spikes, consistent with sustained public health interventions.
