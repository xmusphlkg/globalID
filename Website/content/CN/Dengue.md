# Dengue

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. It is transmitted primarily by the Aedes aegypti mosquito. The disease manifests with flu-like symptoms, including high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. Severe cases can develop into dengue hemorrhagic fever or dengue shock syndrome, which can be fatal. There is no specific treatment for dengue, but early detection and access to proper medical care lower fatality rates. Prevention focuses on controlling mosquito populations and avoiding bites through protective measures.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland show a clear seasonal pattern, with significant peaks from July to October each year. <br/>

- **Recent Surge:** In 2024, there was a dramatic increase in cases, especially in October, with over 11,000 cases, marking the highest monthly count in the dataset. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** Despite high case numbers, deaths remain low, suggesting effective clinical management or a less virulent strain. <br/>

- **Long-term Trends:** After a decline from 2020 to 2022, dengue cases resurged in 2023 and 2024, indicating a potential cyclical pattern or changes in vector control effectiveness. <br/>

The Dengue data from mainland China between 2014 and 2024 shows significant fluctuations, with notable peaks in cases. In 2019, cases surged dramatically from 1,260 in July to 8,036 in September, highlighting the seasonal nature of Dengue, which often peaks in warmer months due to increased mosquito activity. This pattern is consistent with Dengue's transmission dynamics, which are influenced by climatic conditions.

A significant drop in cases was observed in 2020, likely due to COVID-19-related public health measures that inadvertently reduced Dengue transmission through increased awareness and reduced human movement. However, by 2023, there was another surge, with cases peaking at 6,494 in September. This resurgence could be linked to changes in vector control, climatic conditions, or increased post-pandemic mobility.

In 2024, the data indicates an even higher peak in October, with 11,083 cases, suggesting a need for enhanced surveillance and control measures. Despite these fluctuations, the number of deaths remains low, indicating effective clinical management. The recurring nature of these outbreaks underscores the importance of sustained vector control efforts and public health interventions to mitigate Dengue's impact in the region. This analysis highlights the need for continuous monitoring and adaptive strategies to address the challenges posed by Dengue in mainland China.

The data on Dengue cases and deaths in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals several key trends. Notably, the number of cases fluctuates significantly, with peaks typically occurring in the late summer and early autumn months, which aligns with the seasonal nature of Dengue transmission. For instance, there are notable spikes in cases during September and October across multiple years, such as in 2015, 2017, 2019, and 2023. These peaks are likely influenced by climatic conditions favorable to the Aedes mosquito, the primary vector for Dengue, which thrives in warm and humid environments.

Despite the high number of cases, the mortality rate remains remarkably low, with only a few deaths reported over the entire period. This suggests effective clinical management and public health interventions in place to prevent fatalities. The data shows isolated instances of deaths in February 2016, October 2017, December 2017, October 2019, November 2019, December 2018, and September 2023. The low death toll, despite the high incidence of cases, indicates that while Dengue is a significant public health concern in terms of morbidity, the healthcare system's capacity to manage and treat the disease effectively mitigates its potential lethality. This analysis underscores the importance of continued surveillance, vector control, and public health education to maintain low mortality rates amidst fluctuating case numbers.
