# Dengue

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a viral infection caused by the Dengue virus, transmitted primarily by Aedes mosquitoes, notably Aedes aegypti. Common in tropical and subtropical regions, the disease manifests as dengue fever, characterized by high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. A more severe form, dengue hemorrhagic fever, can lead to bleeding, blood plasma leakage, and potentially fatal consequences. Preventive measures focus on mosquito control and personal protection against bites, as no specific antiviral treatment exists. Vaccination is available but varies in efficacy and suitability based on serotype exposure.

## Highlights

- **Case Surge**: Dengue cases in Chinese mainland have risen sharply from mid-2023, peaking at 11,083 cases in October 2024. This upward trend continued in recent months with high case figures.
<br/>
- **Seasonal Pattern**: Cases show a clear seasonal spike from July to October, associated with favorable conditions for mosquito breeding, such as warmer and wetter weather.
<br/>
- **Low Mortality**: Despite high case counts, mortality remains low, last reported in September 2023, highlighting effective clinical management.
<br/>
- **Current Situation**: As of September 2025, 2,321 cases have been reported, emphasizing ongoing transmission risk and the need for robust surveillance and control measures.
<br/>

The dengue case data for China from 2015 to 2025 showcases recurring seasonal peaks, primarily observed from July to October. These periods show significant spikes in case numbers, with September often having the highest incidence, such as in 2019 where cases soared to 8,036, indicating a seasonal pattern likely driven by conducive climatic conditions for mosquito breeding. The data highlights the cyclical nature of dengue transmission, largely influenced by the climate during late summer to early autumn, which supports substantial mosquito activity and human-mosquito interaction.

Between 2020 and 2021, the data reflects a marked reduction in cases, suggesting effective public health measures or environmental factors impacting mosquito populations or transmission capacity. The dramatic decline in reported cases during these years could signal successful vector control or other interventions limiting outbreak potential. Conversely, the upward trend from 2022 to 2025, peaking significantly in October 2024 with 11,083 cases, underscores the challenges of dengue control in the face of changing conditions such as increased urbanization, climate change, or altered disease management approaches. 

These observations point to the necessity of sustained vector control efforts and adaptability in public health strategies, particularly during periods of high transmission risk, to effectively mitigate widespread outbreaks and minimize dengue's impact on public health in China.
