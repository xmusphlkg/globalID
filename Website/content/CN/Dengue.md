# Dengue

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus, transmitted primarily by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes. The disease manifests in flu-like symptoms, including high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. There are four distinct serotypes of the virus (DEN-1, DEN-2, DEN-3, and DEN-4), and infection with one serotype provides lifelong immunity to that type but not to the others. Severe forms include dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome, which can be fatal. Prevention focuses on vector control and public health measures.

## Highlights

- **Resurgence in Cases**: Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a notable resurgence since mid-2023, peaking at 6,494 cases in September 2023. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends**: Data from 2014 to 2024 indicate a seasonal pattern, with cases typically increasing around July to October each year. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate**: Despite the high number of cases, the death rate remains low, with sporadic deaths reported, the latest being one in September 2023. <br/>
- **Current Situation**: As of May 2024, cases are gradually increasing with 109 reported in May 2024, suggesting a possible early onset of the seasonal peak. <br/>

The analysis of Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals significant seasonal outbreaks, especially in late summer and early autumn. For instance, in 2014, cases sharply increased from 23 in May to a peak of 28,796 in October, with the highest mortality in September and October (4 and 2 deaths, respectively). Similar patterns occurred in 2017, 2019, and 2023. Notably, 2017 saw a rise to 1,904 cases in September, while 2019 peaked in October with 6,120 cases and two deaths.

Years like 2020, which had unusually low cases peaking at 287 in October, likely reflect the impact of global movement restrictions and COVID-19 measures. A marked spike was seen again in 2023, with cases surging to 6,494 in September. Mortality rates varied, with the highest fatalities occurring in 2014, 2017, and 2019, though some years saw sporadic deaths, such as one in February 2016 despite only 45 cases.

The data highlight the importance of continued public health vigilance and vector control, especially before the monsoon months, to mitigate peaks during high transmission seasons. Sustained efforts are crucial to managing and preventing significant outbreaks, as evidenced by the varying yearly trends and seasonal surges in Dengue cases.

Despite such surges, the mortality rate remains low, indicating effective health interventions. Specifically, deaths were sparse, with six in 2014, one in February 2016, two in October and December 2017, and three in 2019, particularly concentrated during peak outbreak periods.

In instances of high caseloads, such as July and August 2023, where the case numbers reached 1,604 and 4,198 respectively, only one death was recorded in September. This consistent trend of low mortality relative to the number of cases is indicative of robust clinical management and timely healthcare responses in mitigating fatalities. The data underscores the critical need for ongoing prevention, rapid diagnosis, and effective treatment strategies to maintain the minimal mortality rates amid periodic dengue surges.

Thus, while case numbers vary significantly across years and exhibit seasonal patterns, the low and sporadic number of deaths suggests that existing healthcare measures are significantly limiting the lethality of dengue outbreaks in the Chinese mainland.
