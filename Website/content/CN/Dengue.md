# Dengue

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. It is caused by the dengue virus, which has four distinct serotypes (DEN-1, DEN-2, DEN-3, and DEN-4). The virus is transmitted primarily by Aedes mosquitoes, particularly Aedes aegypti. Symptoms typically include high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. Severe cases can develop into dengue hemorrhagic fever or dengue shock syndrome, which can be fatal. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but supportive care can significantly improve patient outcomes.

## Highlights

- Dengue cases show significant seasonal peaks each year, typically from July to October, with a notable rise in cases during this period. <br/>
- There is a marked increase in cases in 2024, peaking in October with 11,083 cases, indicating a surge compared to previous years. <br/>
- The number of deaths remains relatively low despite the large number of cases, demonstrating a relatively effective healthcare response. <br/>
- The first quarter of 2025 shows a decrease in cases, following the seasonal trend of lower dengue activity during cooler months. <br/>

Dengue data from 2015 to early 2025 in the Chinese mainland reveals strong seasonality, with pronounced peaks from August to October each year. Cases rose gradually from spring, peaking in autumn, and dropping sharply by winter. Notably, 2019 had the highest September count at over 8000 cases, followed by significant outbreaks in 2023 and 2024, culminating in a peak of over 11,000 cases in October 2024—a record high in the dataset.

From 2015 to 2018, annual outbreak peaks ranged between 1400 and 1900 cases. A sharp increase occurred in 2019, which was then followed by an abrupt decline in 2020 through 2022. This drop coincides with nationwide COVID-19 control efforts that may have indirectly reduced dengue transmission through travel restrictions and enhanced public health measures.

Dengue-related deaths were rare, with only a few isolated incidents (2016, 2017, 2018, 2019, and 2023), despite some years experiencing higher case loads. Overall, the persistently seasonal pattern, post-2022 resurgence, and minimal mortality underscore the need for sustained vector control and early response strategies, especially during the late summer and early autumn period when transmission risk is highest.

The fatality data for dengue in the Chinese mainland from March 2015 to March 2025 illustrates a low overall death rate when juxtaposed with the number of reported cases, indicating effective clinical management and public health interventions over the years. Notably, higher mortality figures appear sporadically and predominantly in certain months where dengue cases surged such as October 2017, October 2019 and September 2023. However, these instances of mortality remain isolated, with a maximum of two deaths reported in a single month, despite the thousands of cases documented during peak outbreak months.

In evaluating the temporal dynamics of dengue mortality, it can be observed that the deaths rarely exceed one case per month, and are absent in many instances even during traditionally high-transmission months such as August and September. The sporadic nature of these deaths hints at possible focal outbreaks in regions potentially experiencing higher virulence or inadequate immediate medical response, warranting further investigation. The efficacy of interventions, possibly enhanced clinical treatment protocols or vector control strategies, seems broadly effective in maintaining low mortality rates across the region, notwithstanding the increasing case numbers witnessed in the latter years, emphasizing the resilience of health systems in adapting to evolving outbreak landscapes and possibly pointing towards incremental improvements in case management and public awareness.
