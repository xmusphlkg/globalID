# Dengue

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. It is caused by the dengue virus, which has four distinct serotypes (DENV-1 to DENV-4). The primary vectors are Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes. Symptoms range from mild fever to severe forms like dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome, characterized by high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, rash, and bleeding tendencies. There is no specific treatment, but early detection and supportive care can significantly reduce mortality. Prevention focuses on mosquito control and personal protection measures.

## Highlights



- **Seasonal Peaks:** Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit seasonal peaks, notably from July to October each year, consistent from 2015 to 2024. <br/>

- **Recent Surge:** In 2024, cases surged dramatically, with October reaching 11,083 cases, the highest recorded, indicating a potential outbreak or increased transmission. <br/>

- **Low Mortality:** Despite high case numbers, mortality remains low, with few deaths reported, suggesting effective management or less virulent strains. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of February 2025, cases are low at 51, reflecting a typical seasonal decline after the peak, consistent with historical patterns. <br/>

The data on Dengue cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals significant fluctuations in the number of reported cases, with notable peaks and troughs. The most substantial increase in cases occurred in 2019, with a dramatic rise from 1,260 cases in July to 8,036 cases in September, followed by a decline towards the end of the year. This pattern suggests a seasonal trend, likely influenced by climatic conditions favorable to the Aedes mosquito, the primary vector for Dengue. The data also shows a consistent pattern of increased cases during the late summer and early autumn months, which aligns with the mosquito breeding season.

In contrast, the years 2020 and 2021 saw a significant reduction in reported cases, with numbers dropping to single digits in many months. This decline could be attributed to public health interventions, changes in reporting practices, or external factors such as the COVID-19 pandemic, which might have affected both the transmission dynamics and the healthcare system's capacity to report cases. However, the resurgence of cases in 2023 and 2024, with peaks in September and October, indicates a potential relaxation of control measures or changes in environmental conditions that favor mosquito proliferation. The data underscores the importance of sustained vector control efforts and public health surveillance to manage Dengue outbreaks effectively.

The data on Dengue cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several key trends. Notably, the number of reported cases fluctuates significantly, with peaks typically occurring in the late summer and early autumn months, which aligns with the seasonal nature of Dengue transmission. For instance, in 2019, there was a dramatic increase in cases from August to October, with September alone reporting 8,036 cases. This pattern is consistent with the lifecycle of the Aedes mosquito, the primary vector for Dengue, which thrives in warmer, wetter conditions.

Despite the high number of cases, the mortality rate remains remarkably low. Over the entire period, only a handful of deaths are reported, with a total of six deaths from 2015 to 2025. This low mortality rate could be attributed to several factors, including effective public health interventions, improved clinical management of Dengue cases, and possibly the reporting practices. The data suggests that while Dengue remains a significant public health concern in terms of morbidity, the risk of mortality is relatively low, indicating successful management and treatment strategies in place.
