# Dengue

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Dengue is a viral infection transmitted primarily by Aedes mosquitoes, particularly Aedes aegypti. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, affecting millions globally each year. Symptoms typically include high fever, severe headaches, joint and muscle pain, rash, and mild bleeding. In some cases, dengue can progress to severe dengue, which can lead to shock, organ failure, and death. There is no specific antiviral treatment for dengue; management focuses on supportive care. Preventive measures include mosquito control and personal protection against bites. Vaccines are available in some countries for individuals with a history of dengue infection.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit a clear seasonal pattern, with significant peaks from July to October each year, indicating strong seasonal transmission. <br/>

- **Recent Surge:** In 2024, there was an unprecedented increase in cases, with October reaching over 11,000 cases, the highest monthly count in the dataset, suggesting a potential outbreak. <br/>

- **Low Mortality:** Despite high case numbers, the mortality rate remains low, with only a few deaths reported sporadically, indicating effective healthcare response. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of January 2025, cases have decreased significantly from the October 2024 peak, suggesting a return to typical lower winter transmission levels. <br/>

The data on Dengue cases in the Chinese mainland reveals a significant fluctuation in reported cases over the years, particularly highlighting a dramatic increase in 2019. The year began with relatively low case numbers, but by September, the cases surged to 8,036, peaking at 6,120 in October. This spike coincided with the onset of the rainy season, which is known to facilitate the breeding of Aedes mosquitoes, the primary vectors for Dengue transmission. The increase in cases during this period suggests a correlation between environmental factors and the incidence of the disease, emphasizing the need for effective vector control measures during high-risk months.

Following the peak in 2019, there was a notable decline in cases in 2020, with numbers dropping significantly to single digits in several months. This decline could be attributed to enhanced public health interventions, including increased awareness, community engagement in vector control, and possibly the impact of COVID-19 measures that limited movement and gatherings. However, the resurgence of cases in 2023, particularly in July and August, indicates that Dengue remains a persistent public health challenge. The data underscores the importance of continuous surveillance and proactive measures to mitigate outbreaks, especially in the face of changing climatic conditions that may influence mosquito populations and disease transmission dynamics.

The analysis of Dengue-related deaths in the Chinese mainland reveals a concerning trend despite the significant number of reported cases over the years. From 2015 to 2025, the data indicates that while cases surged, particularly in 2017 and 2019, the mortality rate remained alarmingly low. Notably, the only recorded deaths occurred in 2016, 2017, 2019, and 2023, with a total of just five deaths across these years. This suggests that while Dengue fever is prevalent, the case fatality rate is exceptionally low, which could be attributed to improved healthcare responses, early detection, and effective management of the disease.

However, the sporadic nature of deaths raises questions about the underlying factors contributing to severe cases. The data shows that the majority of cases were reported during the warmer months, particularly from July to October, which aligns with the typical transmission patterns of the Aedes mosquitoes. The lack of deaths in years with high case counts, such as 2019, where over 8,000 cases were reported in September alone, indicates that while the disease is widespread, the healthcare system may be effectively mitigating severe outcomes. Continuous monitoring and targeted public health interventions are essential to maintain this trend and address any potential spikes in mortality associated with future outbreaks.
