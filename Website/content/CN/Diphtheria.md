# Diphtheria

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae, primarily affecting the mucous membranes of the throat and nose. It is characterized by sore throat, low-grade fever, and an adherent membrane on the tonsils, pharynx, or nasal cavity, potentially leading to breathing difficulties. Left untreated, toxins produced by the bacteria can cause severe complications, including myocarditis and peripheral neuropathy. Diphtheria is preventable through vaccination, specifically with the diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis (DTP) vaccine. While widespread immunization has significantly reduced its incidence, diphtheria remains a concern in regions with low vaccination coverage. Prompt medical treatment with antitoxin and antibiotics is crucial.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland have remained exceptionally low from 2014 to July 2024, with sporadic occurrences reported. <br/>
- The highest number of cases recorded in a single year was in 2020, with a total of two cases, indicating a successful containment or prevention strategy. <br/>
- There have been no reported deaths from diphtheria throughout this period, highlighting effective medical responses when cases arise. <br/>
- The data suggests a stable situation with an ongoing need for vigilance in maintaining current public health measures to prevent potential outbreaks. <br/>

From the reported data for diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland, it is evident that the occurrence of the disease has been remarkably low over the entire period from July 2014 to July 2024. Throughout this time, only a handful of cases have been detected, and fortunately, no deaths have been attributed to the disease. The sporadic case identification points to a situation of effective control, with incidents few and far between: a single case each was reported in October 2016, June 2018, August 2020, September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. This isolated presence of diphtheria cases suggests that the disease is not prevalent or widely spreading in the population.

The limited incidence can be attributed to several factors, possibly including high vaccination coverage, public health interventions, and effective surveillance systems that either prevent outbreaks or quickly contain cases upon detection. The absence of any increase in fatalities further emphasizes the success of medical interventions and treatment protocols available for managing the handful of cases. The consistent periods with zero reported cases, interspersed with singular instances, reflect a situation that is likely being well-managed by public health authorities, preventing the bacterium from taking hold and propagating within the community. This controlled pattern indicates a steady-state scenario where sporadic cases are expected but do not indicate any systemic failure or widespread public health threat.

Over this decade-long span, there were a total of five reported cases, with no concentration of cases in any particular year. The sporadic reporting of individual cases—specifically noted in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023—indicates very limited transmission or possibly imported cases. This lack of reported fatalities could reflect effective clinical management and treatment, particularly the timely administration of diphtheria antitoxin and antibiotics, which are critical in reducing mortality.

The absence of deaths in the context of these reported cases may also indicate a robust public health infrastructure that successfully manages and controls outbreaks, preventing larger clusters and ensuring early diagnosis and treatment. Another factor contributing to this outcome is the likely high vaccine coverage against diphtheria in China, which limits the susceptible population and averts widespread transmission. This vaccination coverage could explain the sporadic cases as isolated incidents rather than endemic occurrences. Robust surveillance systems may aid in promptly identifying and addressing singular cases, preventing severe outcomes as reflected in the zero-death toll over this ten-year period.
