# Diphtheria

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is an acute bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae, primarily affecting the upper respiratory tract. The disease spreads through respiratory droplets or direct contact with infected individuals. Characterized by sore throat, fever, and swollen glands, diphtheria can lead to the formation of a thick, gray pseudomembrane in the throat or nose, potentially causing airway obstruction. Complications include myocarditis and neuritis. While rare in regions with effective vaccination programs, outbreaks occur in areas with low immunization coverage. Prevention relies on the DTaP and Tdap vaccines, and treatment includes antitoxin administration and antibiotics.

## Highlights

- Extremely low incidence of diphtheria: From March 2015 to March 2025, only seven cases were reported in mainland China, highlighting effective control measures. <br/>
- Absence of diphtheria-related deaths: Across all cases reported, no deaths occurred, suggesting timely medical intervention or mild disease presentation. <br/>
- Sporadic occurrence: Cases were scattered across different years, with no clustering or outbreak pattern, indicating isolated incidents rather than sustained transmission. <br/>
- Stability in recent years: Since June 2023, no new cases have been documented up to March 2025, showcasing continued successful public health strategies and vaccination programs. <br/>

Over this ten-year period, diphtheria cases were rarely reported, with specific instances primarily isolated to individual months. For instance, a single case was reported in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. These isolated reports suggest that diphtheria is not endemic but rather emerges sporadically, possibly due to gaps in vaccination coverage or imported cases.

Zero deaths during this timeframe emphasize the effectiveness of the health system in managing reported cases and preventing fatal outcomes. This could be attributed to the established healthcare infrastructure and widespread vaccination programs, which are crucial in controlling such infectious diseases. The absence of clusters or outbreaks despite these sporadic cases also indicates effective case management and quarantine measures. Overall, while occasional cases of diphtheria arise, the lack of sustained transmission or significant mortality aligns with a well-controlled public health scenario.
