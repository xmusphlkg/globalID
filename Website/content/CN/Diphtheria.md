# Diphtheria

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a contagious bacterial infection caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. It primarily affects the throat and upper airways, producing a toxin that can harm heart and nerve tissues. Symptoms include a sore throat, fever, and a thick, gray membrane covering the throat and tonsils. Spread through respiratory droplets, diphtheria was once a major cause of illness and death. Vaccination, particularly the DTaP/Tdap vaccines, has significantly reduced its incidence. Treatment involves antitoxin and antibiotics. Despite its decline, diphtheria remains a concern in areas with low vaccination rates, emphasizing the importance of immunization and public health measures.

## Highlights

- **Consistent Low Incidence**: Since 2014, Diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland have remained extremely low, with only sporadic cases reported and no deaths. <br/>

- **Isolated Incidents**: Minor spikes in cases occurred in October 2016, June 2018, August-September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023, each with just a single reported case. <br/>

- **No Deaths Reported**: There have been zero deaths associated with Diphtheria in the given period, highlighting effective disease management and treatment outcomes. <br/>

- **Stable Situation**: The absence of cases from late 2023 to September 2024 suggests maintained control and successful immunization efforts. <br/>

The data provided for Diphtheria on the Chinese mainland from September 2014 through September 2024 reveals extremely low case counts over the years. From 2014 to 2024, only a handful of cases (totaling six) were reported, with sporadic occurrences in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023, each reporting a solitary case in isolated months. The absence of any recorded deaths indicates effective clinical management and response to the disease. This low incidence rate suggests robust public health measures, including widespread immunization coverage, are in place, effectively preventing the endemic spread of Diphtheria in China.

The sporadic nature of the cases, coupled with the long stretches of months and even years with no reported cases, emphasizes the effectiveness of surveillance and vaccination programs. These programs likely maintain high herd immunity levels, curtailing the potential for outbreaks. The data does beg consideration for monitoring and evaluation to ensure continued public health success, particularly emphasizing the need to respond swiftly to the rare cases that do arise, to understand potential sources and transmission pathways, and to prevent future occurrences.

Importantly, none of these cases resulted in death, suggesting a high level of control over the disease and potentially effective medical responses to the few cases that occurred.

The absence of diphtheria-related deaths over this decade can be attributed to strong public health interventions, vaccination programs, and possibly early detection and treatment. These factors have likely contributed to preventing the progression of diphtheria into life-threatening stages, maintaining low mortality rates despite the occurrence of isolated cases. This data highlights successful control measures in mainland China in limiting both the spread and severity of diphtheria.
