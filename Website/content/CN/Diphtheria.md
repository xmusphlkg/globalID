# Diphtheria

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a contagious bacterial infection caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*, primarily affecting the mucous membranes of the respiratory tract. It spreads through respiratory droplets or contact with contaminated objects. Symptoms include sore throat, fever, weakness, and a thick gray or white coating in the throat, which can cause breathing difficulties. Severe cases may lead to myocarditis or nerve damage. Diphtheria can be fatal if untreated but is preventable through vaccination, specifically the DTaP or Tdap vaccines. Prompt administration of diphtheria antitoxin and antibiotics is crucial for effective treatment.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria incidence in mainland China has remained extremely low from April 2015 to April 2025, with only seven confirmed cases and zero deaths reported over the ten-year span. <br/>
- Sporadic cases occurred in isolated months (2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023), with no evident seasonal or geographic clustering. <br/>
- No diphtheria cases have been reported since June 2023, reflecting nearly two years of complete absence by April 2025. <br/>
- The sustained low incidence and absence of fatalities suggest effective immunization coverage and robust public health surveillance systems. <br/>

From April 2015 to April 2025, reported diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland remain extremely rare, with only six isolated incidents recorded: October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. This translates to single-case events occurring approximately once every one to two years over the decade, with no clustering outside of the two consecutive months in 2020. Importantly, no deaths have been reported among these cases, suggesting successful clinical management, early detection, or low pathogenicity of the strain involved. The sporadic and geographically separated nature of these cases is consistent with a nation sustaining high immunization coverage and effective surveillance systems.

The temporal pattern lacks seasonality and does not indicate any sustained transmission. Given the extremely low incidence and absence of secondary cases, imported infections or isolated lapses in immunization coverage may be responsible. The maintenance of diphtheria-free status despite vast population size and regional disparities reflects the robustness of national vaccination programs. Nonetheless, the emergence of even a single case serves as a sentinel event, warranting vigilance in both clinical suspicion and immunization policy enforcement, especially in border regions and transient populations.
