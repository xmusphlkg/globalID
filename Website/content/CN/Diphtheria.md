# Diphtheria

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a serious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae, affecting the mucous membranes of the throat and nose. It spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Symptoms include a sore throat, fever, and swollen glands, with a thick, gray coating on the throat or tonsils. This coating can obstruct breathing and swallowing. Complications may include myocarditis, nerve damage, and respiratory failure. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, typically administered in childhood as part of the DTaP vaccine. Prompt treatment with antitoxins and antibiotics is crucial to manage the infection and prevent complications.

## Highlights

- **Low Incidence:** Diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland have remained extremely low from 2015 to January 2025, with only sporadic cases reported. <br/>
- **No Deaths Reported:** Throughout the entire period, there have been no deaths attributed to diphtheria, indicating effective management and treatment of the few cases that occurred. <br/>
- **Sporadic Cases:** The few cases that did occur were isolated, with no more than one case reported in any given month, suggesting no significant outbreaks. <br/>
- **Effective Control Measures:** The data suggests that public health measures and vaccination programs have been effective in controlling diphtheria in the region. <br/>

The data on diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals a concerning trend of sporadic cases with no reported deaths. Throughout the majority of the observed years, particularly from 2015 to 2019, there were no cases reported, indicating a significant control over the disease during this period. However, the emergence of isolated cases in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023 suggests that while the disease is not widespread, it has not been completely eradicated. The cases appear to be infrequent and isolated, with only one case reported in several months across different years.

The absence of fatalities associated with these cases is a positive indicator, suggesting effective management and treatment protocols in place. The sporadic nature of the cases could be attributed to various factors, including vaccination coverage, public health initiatives, and possibly the population's immunity levels. Continued surveillance and vaccination efforts will be crucial in maintaining the low incidence of diphtheria and preventing potential outbreaks in the future. The data underscores the importance of ongoing public health vigilance, even in the absence of widespread disease activity.
