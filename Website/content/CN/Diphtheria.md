# Diphtheria

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is an acute bacterial infection caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. It primarily affects the respiratory tract, but can also infect the skin. The bacteria produce a toxin that can lead to severe respiratory issues, heart failure, and nerve damage. Symptoms include sore throat, fever, swollen glands, and a thick, gray coating on the throat and tonsils. Diphtheria spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Vaccination with the diphtheria toxoid vaccine is the most effective prevention method, significantly reducing incidence rates globally.

## Highlights

- **Stable Incidence**: The incidence of diphtheria in mainland China has remained extremely low, with only sporadic cases reported since 2014. <br/>
- **No Deaths Reported**: There have been no reported deaths due to diphtheria during the entire period from June 2014 to June 2024, indicating effective clinical management. <br/>
- **Isolated Case Peaks**: Minor spikes in case numbers were observed in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023, with each year recording only 1 case. <br/>
- **Recent Stability**: From July 2023 to June 2024, only one case was reported in June 2023, with no further cases afterward, indicating a return to stability. <br/>

The data for diphtheria in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals a generally stable situation with very few reported cases and no deaths. Over this decade, only a handful of diphtheria cases were recorded: one in October 2016, one in June 2018, two in August and September 2020, and one each in July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. This indicates a sporadic occurrence of the disease rather than a sustained or widespread outbreak.

Throughout the ten-year period, the consistently low number of cases, coupled with the absence of deaths, suggests effective control measures and a robust public health infrastructure capable of managing and containing diphtheria. The isolated nature of the cases implies that when infections do occur, they are swiftly addressed, preventing further transmission. The data does not exhibit any alarming trends or sudden increases, reaffirming the success of ongoing vaccination programs and surveillance efforts in maintaining low incidence rates of diphtheria.
