# Diphtheria

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a highly contagious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae. It typically affects the throat and upper respiratory tract, producing a toxin that can lead to severe complications. Symptoms include a sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick, gray pseudomembrane over the tonsils and throat, which can obstruct breathing. The toxin may also damage the heart, nerves, and kidneys. Diphtheria spreads through respiratory droplets, and vaccination is the most effective prevention method. Early diagnosis and antitoxin administration are crucial for successful treatment.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria incidence in the Chinese mainland has remained extremely low, with only sporadic cases reported from 2016 to 2023 and none since June 2023.<br/>
- No deaths from diphtheria have occurred during the observed period, indicating high control and effective clinical management.<br/>
- The data demonstrates sustained interruption of endemic transmission, likely due to successful vaccination coverage and surveillance systems.<br/>
- As of December 2025, diphtheria is effectively eliminated as a public health concern in the Chinese mainland, but continued vigilance in immunization and monitoring remains essential.<br/>

In reviewing the reported data for diphtheria in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 through December 2025, the epidemiological profile is characterized by pronounced scarcity of cases and an absence of reported deaths. Over this 10-year span, only eight isolated cases were recorded, with none leading to mortality. The temporal distribution is sporadic, presenting a single case for only particular months—October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023—without any discernible seasonal trend or clustering. The remainder of the months, including extended periods spanning several years, indicate complete inactivity, signifying the interruption of endemic transmission.

Such persistently low incidence points towards sustained success in diphtheria control measures, possibly attributed to robust immunization coverage and effective surveillance infrastructure. The absence of outbreaks and fatalities further supports the presence of a largely protected population with minimal vulnerability to toxigenic Corynebacterium diphtheriae. The isolated, non-clustered nature of the cases suggests that these may represent importations, diagnostic artifacts, or sporadic lapses in immunity display, rather than local resurgence. In totality, the data reflect a status of elimination, with vigilance in maintaining high coverage and prompt detection remaining vital to prevent re-establishment.

Over the several years of reported data for diphtheria in Chinese mainland, the total number of deaths attributed to diphtheria was consistently zero, despite sporadic reporting of a very low number of clinical cases. This pattern of mortality suggests not only the rarity of diphtheria cases but also the likely effectiveness of public health interventions, such as high routine immunization coverage and rapid case management. Given that diphtheria historically was associated with high case-fatality rates in the absence of widespread vaccination and prompt treatment, the absence of deaths is statistically notable and points to robust control mechanisms. In each year where isolated cases were detected, there were no subsequent deaths, reinforcing that clinical outcomes in China are generally favorable for this disease.

Such an extended period without any recorded fatalities, even amidst isolated cases, is epidemiologically significant. It signals that vulnerabilities in healthcare access and disease surveillance for diphtheria, if present, are not manifesting as increased mortality risk. There appears to be little to no transmission resulting in severe outcomes, which may further indicate that suspected cases are possibly imported, sporadic, or promptly identified and treated. The complete absence of deaths also highlights the success of preventative strategies and suggests that diphtheria remains contained as a public health threat in the region under current conditions.
