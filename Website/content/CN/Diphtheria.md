# Diphtheria

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a highly contagious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the throat and nose, leading to a sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick, gray pseudomembrane across the throat and tonsils. This membrane can obstruct breathing and swallowing. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or close contact with an infected person. Without timely treatment, the disease can lead to severe complications, such as myocarditis or peripheral neuropathy. Vaccination, particularly with the DTaP or Tdap vaccines, is the most effective prevention against diphtheria.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland have remained extremely low over the past decade, with isolated cases reported sporadically from 2016 to 2023. <br/>
- The most recent detected cases occurred in June 2023, marking the end of reported incidences up to the present, as of October 2025. <br/>
- There have been zero recorded deaths from diphtheria throughout the entire data period, indicating effective healthcare management and vaccination efforts. <br/>
- The trend suggests sustained control over diphtheria transmission, highlighting success in public health interventions, including vaccination and surveillance measures, since no cases have been reported since mid-2023. <br/>

Throughout this period, cases of diphtheria were recorded intermittently, with singular instances reported sporadically and no detectable pattern or seasonal variation in the occurrence of the cases. Specifically, isolated cases were reported in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023, while the remaining periods recorded zero cases. Importantly, all these cases were non-fatal, indicating a possibly high level of healthcare intervention and effective treatment post-diagnosis.

Considering the vast population size and geographic diversity of the Chinese mainland, the sporadic and rare occurrence of diphtheria, reflected in the data, suggests potentially effective vaccination coverage and public health measures in place to control the disease. The consistent assurance of zero deaths over the decade-long timeline further implies that the health system provides adequate response capabilities to manage isolated cases effectively, minimizing mortality risks. This trend aligns with global efforts to maintain diphtheria at low endemic levels through widespread immunization strategies, underscoring the critical role of vaccines in curtailing both the spread and impact of this previously more prevalent disease.
