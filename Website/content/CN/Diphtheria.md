# Diphtheria

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a serious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae, affecting the mucous membranes of the throat and nose. It spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Early symptoms include a sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick, gray membrane covering the throat and tonsils, leading to difficulty breathing and swallowing. Complications can include myocarditis, nerve damage, or airway obstruction. Vaccination, particularly DTaP for children and Tdap for adults, is crucial for prevention. Prompt administration of antitoxin and antibiotics such as erythromycin or penicillin is essential for treatment and reducing transmission risks.

## Highlights

- Sporadic cases of diphtheria have been reported intermittently from 2016 to 2023, with isolated incidence every one to two years, indicating a very low transmission rate. <br/>
- No diphtheria-related deaths have been recorded in the Chinese mainland over the entire period of observation, reflecting effective treatment and control measures. <br/>
- The last reported case occurred in June 2023, with no new cases identified as of June 2025, suggesting a possible halt in transmission. <br/>
- The consistent absence of cases since mid-2023 could indicate successful public health interventions, potentially augmented by robust vaccination strategies. <br/>

The analysis of the reported data for Diphtheria cases in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2023 indicates a remarkably low disease occurrence. Throughout this period, most months recorded zero cases, with sporadic, isolated instances appearing intermittently. Specifically, these isolated cases were documented in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023, totaling only 7 cases across nearly eight years. Despite the occurrence of these isolated cases, no diphtheria-related deaths were reported, underscoring the effective management of the condition in those few instances where it arose.

The data reflects a scenario of successful diphtheria control, likely attributable to robust vaccination programs and public health initiatives in place in mainland China. It is likely that routine childhood immunization programs, which include the diphtheria toxoid, have sustained population immunity, thereby preventing a resurgence of the disease. The absence of diphtheria-related deaths can also be indicative of the efficient healthcare systems available to manage and treat the rare occurrences effectively, demonstrating the preparedness and capability of medical infrastructure to respond to this infectious disease. Overall, the data showcases a favorable public health outcome wherein diphtheria is practically non-existent as a public health threat within the region.
