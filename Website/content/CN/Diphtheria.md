# Diphtheria

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a serious bacterial infection caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the throat and nose. The disease is characterized by a thick, gray coating on the throat and tonsils, which can lead to difficulty breathing, heart failure, paralysis, or even death if untreated. Diphtheria spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, significantly reducing incidence rates. Early diagnosis and treatment with antitoxins and antibiotics are crucial for managing the infection and preventing complications.

## Highlights

- **Low Incidence:** Diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland have remained extremely low, with only sporadic cases reported from 2016 to 2023. <br/>
- **No Deaths Reported:** Throughout the entire period from 2014 to 2024, there have been no deaths attributed to diphtheria, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease. <br/>
- **Isolated Cases:** The few cases that have occurred are isolated, with no significant outbreaks, suggesting effective public health measures and vaccination coverage. <br/>
- **Stable Situation:** As of December 2024, the situation remains stable with no new cases reported in the last year, reflecting continued control over the disease. <br/>

The data for diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a remarkably low incidence of the disease, with only a few isolated cases reported over the years. Specifically, there were single cases reported in October 2016, June 2018, August 2020, September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. Notably, there were no deaths reported in any of these instances, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease when it did occur.

This pattern suggests that diphtheria is well-controlled in the region, likely due to effective public health measures such as widespread vaccination and robust healthcare infrastructure. The sporadic nature of the cases could be attributed to isolated incidents of exposure or lapses in vaccination coverage in specific areas. The absence of deaths further underscores the effectiveness of the healthcare response in managing the disease promptly and effectively. Overall, the data reflects a successful public health strategy in maintaining low diphtheria incidence and preventing mortality.
