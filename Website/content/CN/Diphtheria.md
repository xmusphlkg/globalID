# Diphtheria

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is an acute, highly contagious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae. It primarily affects the respiratory tract, including the nasopharynx and larynx, but can also cause cutaneous infections. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or close contact with infected individuals. Symptoms include sore throat, fever, swollen glands, and the formation of a thick pseudomembrane over the throat, which can obstruct breathing. Complications may involve myocarditis and peripheral neuropathy. Vaccination, typically part of the DTaP combined immunization, is the most effective prevention method. Timely administration of antitoxin and antibiotics is critical for treatment.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria cases remain sporadic, with only a few isolated incidences between 2016 and 2024. Most months report zero cases. <br/>
- No deaths from Diphtheria have been recorded since 2014, suggesting effective treatment and control measures. <br/>
- Peaks in case numbers occurred in isolated months, including 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023, but these events were not sustained over time. <br/>
- As of August 2024, there have been no reported cases of Diphtheria, indicating ongoing disease control and low transmission in the Chinese mainland. <br/>

An analysis of the diphtheria data from mainland China reveals an overall low incidence of reported cases between 2014 and 2024. During this period, there were only six isolated cases of diphtheria, with no recorded deaths. Notably, the first case was recorded in October 2016, followed by a second case in June 2018, and a gradual increase in sporadic cases with one reported in August 2020, another in September 2020, and single cases in July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. These sporadic cases appear to be infrequent and widely spaced across the 10-year period, suggesting effective disease control, likely attributed to public health interventions such as vaccination.

Despite these isolated cases, there is no indication of an outbreak or sustained transmission, which aligns with the absence of mortality. This data pattern may suggest that while diphtheria remains a risk, it is largely under control within the population, with occasional cases likely linked to gaps in immunization coverage or imported infections. Regular monitoring and maintaining high vaccination rates would be key to preventing future cases and sustaining this low incidence trend.
