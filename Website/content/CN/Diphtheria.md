# Diphtheria

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae. It primarily affects the nose and throat and can lead to breathing difficulties, heart failure, paralysis, or even death if untreated. The hallmark sign is a thick, gray coating on the throat and tonsils. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or contact with contaminated surfaces. Due to vaccination efforts, diphtheria is rare in developed countries but remains a concern in areas with low immunization coverage. The diphtheria toxoid vaccine, often combined with tetanus and pertussis vaccines, has significantly reduced its incidence globally.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria cases have remained sporadic and extremely low from 2015 to 2025, with only seven cases reported over a decade. <br/>
- There have been no deaths associated with Diphtheria in the Chinese mainland during this period, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease. <br/>
- The last reported case was in June 2023, followed by no new cases up to August 2025, suggesting possible interruption of transmission. <br/>
- The consistent absence of cases since mid-2023 highlights effective public health measures and vaccination programs that have likely contributed to controlling the disease. <br/>

The reported data for diphtheria cases in the Chinese mainland from August 2015 to August 2025 demonstrates a remarkably low incidence of the disease. Over this ten-year period, only seven cases were recorded, with no associated deaths. Notably, these isolated cases are distributed sporadically over the years, with single cases appearing in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. This distribution suggests that diphtheria is exceedingly rare and possibly sporadic, with no clear seasonal or temporal trends apparent in the data. Each reported year with cases consistently records only one incident, emphasizing the isolated nature of these occurrences.

The absence of any reported deaths across all these cases indicates effective treatment and management of the disease when it occurs. This low incidence could be attributed to successful public health initiatives such as widespread immunization programs, which are known to be critical in preventing diphtheria outbreaks. The vaccination coverage rates in China are traditionally high, which likely contributes to the containment of the disease. Additionally, the absence of any sustained outbreak patterns or death tolls highlights the efficacy of national surveillance and rapid response to infections. Overall, this data reflects a positive public health landscape for diphtheria control in the Chinese mainland.
