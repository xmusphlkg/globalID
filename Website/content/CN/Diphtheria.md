# Diphtheria

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a contagious bacterial infection caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. It primarily affects the throat and nose, leading to sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick grayish membrane that can obstruct breathing. The bacteria produce a toxin that can damage tissues, potentially leading to complications such as myocarditis and nerve damage. Diphtheria spreads through respiratory droplets and close contact. Vaccination with the DTP vaccine has significantly reduced the incidence of the disease, but outbreaks can still occur in areas with low immunization rates. Early treatment with antibiotics and diphtheria antitoxin is essential.

## Highlights

- **Low incidence of diphtheria:** There have been very few reported cases of diphtheria in the Chinese mainland over the past decade, with only sporadic cases observed in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023. <br/>
- **No deaths recorded:** Despite occasional cases, no fatalities from diphtheria have been reported in recent years. <br/>
- **Continued absence of cases in 2024:** As of November 2024, there have been no new cases or deaths of diphtheria reported in the country. <br/>
- **Effective prevention:** The low number of cases suggests strong vaccination coverage and effective public health measures against diphtheria. <br/>

The rare occurrence of diphtheria cases could be attributed to high vaccine coverage and robust healthcare responses, with cases likely being identified and treated early, preventing severe outcomes. 

The absence of any deaths during the entire period further highlights the success of China’s healthcare infrastructure in managing diphtheria. The country appears to have effectively controlled the disease through surveillance and immunization, as evidenced by the long periods without any reported cases. However, these isolated incidents underscore the ongoing need for vigilance in maintaining high vaccination rates and being prepared for any potential outbreaks. While diphtheria remains rare, the data from 2014 to 2024 emphasizes the importance of continued public health efforts to prevent resurgence, especially in the context of international travel and potential gaps in immunity.

This suggests that while diphtheria was still being detected sporadically, it had not resulted in any significant mortality.
