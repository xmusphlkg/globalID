# Diphtheria

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a serious bacterial infection caused by the bacterium Corynebacterium diphtheriae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the respiratory tract and occasionally the skin. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing or through direct contact with infected lesions. Symptoms include a sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick, gray membrane covering the throat and tonsils, which can obstruct breathing. Without treatment, diphtheria can lead to severe complications like myocarditis and neuropathy. Vaccination, specifically the DTaP and Tdap vaccines, is highly effective in preventing diphtheria, making it rare in developed countries.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria incidence in the Chinese mainland has been extremely low over the past decade, with sporadic cases reported. <br/>
- The highest occurrence since 2014 is a single monthly case, reflecting effective public health measures and vaccination efforts. <br/>
- There have been no reported deaths from diphtheria in the Chinese mainland, highlighting successful treatment outcomes and health interventions. <br/>
- The recent year, 2024, reports zero cases, indicating sustained control and prevention of the disease as of October, with vigilance needed to maintain this status. <br/>

Over the ten-year period, there were few reported cases, specifically in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023, each time affecting only one individual. This suggests a low prevalence of the disease, indicating effective control measures and possibly high vaccination coverage rates. Notably, there were no reported deaths associated with these cases, emphasizing the effectiveness of timely medical interventions and the availability of healthcare resources to manage such infections promptly.

This pattern reflects the success of public health campaigns and routine immunization programs aimed at maintaining high immunity levels within the population. The sporadic nature of Diphtheria cases might also point to effective surveillance systems able to quickly identify and contain isolated incidents before they can spread further. This level of control over a potentially fatal infectious disease underscores the importance of sustained public health efforts and demonstrates a resilient response system capable of preventing outbreaks and maintaining disease elimination status.
