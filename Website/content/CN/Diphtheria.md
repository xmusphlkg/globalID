# Diphtheria

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a highly contagious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae, primarily affecting the throat and upper respiratory tract. It spreads through respiratory droplets and direct contact with infected individuals. Symptoms include a sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick gray or white pseudomembrane on the throat and tonsils, which can obstruct breathing. In severe cases, it can lead to heart and nerve damage. The disease is preventable through vaccination with the diphtheria toxoid, usually administered in combination with tetanus and pertussis vaccines (DTaP or Tdap). Prompt antibiotic treatment is crucial for those infected.

## Highlights

- **Low Incidence:** Diphtheria cases remain rare in Chinese mainland, with only intermittent single cases reported from 2016 to mid-2023. No cases have been reported from late 2023 through September 2025.<br/>
- **Zero Mortality:** There have been no deaths recorded from Diphtheria throughout the entire dataset duration, indicating effective case management and treatment.<br/>
- **Control Success:** The consistent absence of cases and deaths from 2024 to 2025 suggests successful disease control efforts, likely due to vaccination and public health interventions.<br/>
- **Stable Situation:** The trend demonstrates stability in controlling Diphtheria, with no new outbreaks and a successful containment of isolated cases over the years.<br/>

The diphtheria case data from the Chinese mainland, spanning from September 2015 to September 2025, reflects sporadic occurrences of the disease over the years. The dataset indicates a total of seven reported cases distributed across isolated months: one in October 2016, one in June 2018, followed by minor increases in 2020 (with two cases in August and September), and single occurrences in July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. Importantly, there were no recorded deaths associated with any of these cases. This suggests that diphtheria has been largely controlled or remains a rare occurrence, with adequate healthcare interventions preventing fatalities.

Analyzing this pattern, it appears that diphtheria cases occur intermittently rather than seasonally or annually, which could point to isolated outbreaks or imported cases rather than endemic transmission within the population. The absence of deaths further indicates successful containment and treatment strategies during these isolated incidents. This sporadic incidence might be attributed to strong vaccination efforts and public health surveillance in China, effectively reducing the risk of local transmission. However, continued vigilance and sustained immunization practices are crucial to prevent any potential resurgence of the disease, especially since a single case, if unchecked, can lead to wider community spread.

The data on diphtheria cases in mainland China from September 2015 to September 2025 highlights an extremely low incidence of the disease. Over this ten-year period, only a handful of diphtheria cases were recorded, with none of these resulting in fatalities. Specifically, cases were sporadic and spread across years, with only one occurrence each noted in October 2016, June 2018, August 2020, September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. This sporadic pattern indicates effective public health measures and vaccination efforts, given the potential for diphtheria to cause serious outbreaks if left unchecked.

The lack of reported fatalities over the entire period is even more informative. It underscores the likely efficacy of medical interventions and the healthcare system's ability to manage the few cases that did arise. Furthermore, the consistent zero-mortality rate over the years suggests that while diphtheria has not been eradicated, the rare instances of occurrence do not escalate to critical outcomes, thanks, in part, to prompt identification and treatment. This effectively illustrates a commendable level of control over an infectious disease that historically posed a significant threat to public health.
