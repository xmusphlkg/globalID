# Diphtheria

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Diphtheria is a serious bacterial infection caused by *Corynebacterium diphtheriae*. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the throat and nose, producing a toxin that can lead to breathing difficulties, heart rhythm problems, or even death. It spreads through respiratory droplets or contact with contaminated objects. Common symptoms include sore throat, fever, swollen glands, and a thick gray coating in the throat. Vaccination with the diphtheria toxoid is the most effective prevention method. Despite being rare in countries with high vaccination coverage, diphtheria remains a threat in areas with limited immunization access.

## Highlights

- Diphtheria incidence in mainland China remains extremely low, with only seven reported cases from May 2015 to May 2025 and no associated deaths.<br/>
- Isolated cases occurred sporadically in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023, mostly during summer months, suggesting no sustained transmission.<br/>
- No cases have been reported for a consecutive 12-month period since June 2023, indicating disease inactivity over the last year.<br/>
- The data supports the conclusion that diphtheria is well-controlled in China, likely due to effective immunization programs and robust surveillance systems.<br/>

From May 2015 through May 2025, reported diphtheria cases in mainland China remained extremely low, with a total of only seven confirmed cases and no associated deaths. These isolated cases appeared sporadically across multiple yearsâ€”specifically, one case each in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023. There were no clusters or sustained periods of transmission, suggesting that these events were isolated and possibly related to imported cases or localized breakdowns in immunity or vaccine coverage.

The pattern of annual single-case appearances in the summer months from 2020 to 2023 may indicate seasonal vulnerability or reporting variation, but with such low incidence, determining periodicity or seasonality is statistically unreliable. The complete absence of fatalities underscores effective clinical management capacity in detected cases and possible early identification. The extended months and years with zero incidence further reinforce the impact of strong routine immunization, surveillance, and public health infrastructure in preventing re-establishment of endemic transmission.
