# Diphtheria

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Diphtheria is an acute bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae, primarily affecting the respiratory tract, including the tonsils, throat, and nasal cavity. The disease spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Symptoms include sore throat, fever, and the formation of a thick, gray pseudomembrane on the throat and tonsils, which can obstruct breathing. Severe complications may include myocarditis and neuropathy. Vaccination with the diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis (DTP) vaccine remains the most effective preventive measure. Early administration of diphtheria antitoxin and antibiotics is crucial for treatment.

## Highlights

- Overall, Diphtheria cases in Chinese mainland remain extremely low, with sporadic occurrences since October 2016. <br/>
- A total of six cases have been reported over the past decade, with no associated deaths, indicating effective early detection and treatment. <br/>
- The latest reported case occurred in June 2023, emphasizing the importance of continuous surveillance and public health measures. <br/>
- The absence of cases since June 2023 suggests successful containment and disease control efforts up to May 2024. <br/>

Analysis of diphtheria cases in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 indicates an overall low incidence, with a few isolated cases reported over the observed period. The data reveals a notable absence of cases and deaths over extended periods, interrupted by singular occurrences primarily in 2016, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023. This pattern suggests successful ongoing control and prevention measures, with only occasional lapses resulting in a small number of reported cases. Specifically, there was one case in October 2016, June 2018, August and September 2020, July 2021, July 2022, and June 2023.

Given the sporadic nature of the recorded cases and the lack of reported deaths, it is evident that China has maintained effective healthcare strategies to manage diphtheria. The absence of continuous case clusters further signifies that the few cases that appeared did not lead to secondary transmission, underscoring the efficiency of the public health response. The consistent data void of deaths also indicates not only the containment of the disease but also the overall robustness of medical interventions in preventing fatal outcomes when diphtheria does occur.
