# Dysentery

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Dysentery is an infection of the intestines, characterized by inflammation, causing diarrhea with blood and mucus. It is typically caused by bacteria (such as *Shigella* or *Salmonella*) or parasites (*Entamoeba histolytica*). Transmission occurs through contaminated food, water, or person-to-person contact. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Dysentery can range from mild to severe, with the risk of complications like electrolyte imbalance or organ failure in extreme cases. Prevention includes good hygiene, proper sanitation, and safe drinking water practices. Treatment typically involves antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, depending on the cause.

## Highlights

1. **Overall Decline in Cases:** Dysentery cases have significantly decreased from over 17,000 per month in 2015 to around 2,000 cases per month in 2024, indicating a steady decline in incidence. <br/>

2. **Low Mortality:** Deaths remain rare, with occasional peaks, such as in 2020 and 2021, but the case-fatality rate remains consistently low. <br/>

3. **Seasonal Variation:** Dysentery cases are generally higher in the summer months (May to August), suggesting a seasonal pattern linked to temperature and hygiene factors. <br/>

4. **Current Situation (November 2024):** The most recent data (November 2024) shows a low number of cases (2,128), maintaining the trend of minimal mortality and low incidence. <br/>

The data on dysentery cases in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 shows notable seasonal fluctuations, with peaks typically occurring from May to July each year. For instance, in 2015, cases surged to 13,563 in May and 19,481 in July. These spikes suggest environmental and behavioral factors, such as warmer weather and increased public gatherings, play a role in disease transmission. Despite these outbreaks, mortality remained low, with many months reporting zero deaths.

From 2017 onwards, a gradual decline in cases is evident, particularly in the latter years of the dataset. By 2024, the number of monthly cases had largely stabilized below 10,000, indicating improvements in public health measures, sanitation, and access to medical treatment. The decrease in case numbers, along with consistently low death rates, may reflect enhanced disease control strategies. Additionally, certain months, like February and November, tend to report fewer cases, possibly due to reduced transmission in colder weather or less mobility during these months. Overall, the data suggests that while dysentery remains a concern, its burden has lessened over time, likely due to better health interventions and public awareness.

For instance, in 2015, despite case numbers peaking at over 17,000, there were only one to three deaths. The highest recorded number of deaths occurred in January 2020, when three fatalities were reported despite relatively low case numbers (3,309). These occasional deaths, typically ranging from one to three per month, suggest that while dysentery is a persistent public health issue, its lethality has generally remained low, possibly due to effective treatment and public health measures.

Overall, there has been a significant decline in deaths over the years. From 2014 to 2019, many months recorded no fatalities, and this trend continued into 2024. This reduction may reflect improvements in healthcare access, sanitation, and disease management. The relatively low death toll, even with fluctuating case numbers, suggests that the population may be better equipped to handle the disease, or the pathogens themselves may have become less virulent. However, occasional deaths, such as those seen in 2020 and 2021, highlight the ongoing risks and the need for continued vigilance in controlling dysentery outbreaks.
