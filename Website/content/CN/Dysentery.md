# Dysentery

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an infectious disease characterized by inflammation of the intestines, primarily the colon, resulting in severe diarrhea with blood and mucus. It is commonly caused by bacteria (such as *Shigella*) or parasites (such as *Entamoeba histolytica*). Transmission typically occurs through contaminated food, water, or direct person-to-person contact in areas with inadequate sanitation. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and frequent, painful stools. Dysentery is more prevalent in developing regions and poses a significant public health concern, especially among children and vulnerable populations. Prompt diagnosis and treatment are essential.

## Highlights

- Dysentery cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a consistent decline from 2015 through December 2025, dropping from over 6,000 cases per month in winter 2015 to approximately 1,500 in December 2025. <br/>
- Seasonal peaks remain evident, with higher numbers typically recorded from May to October, but these peaks have become less pronounced over time, suggesting improved disease control. <br/>
- Reported deaths have been rare throughout the period, with zero or one death per month and several consecutive months with no deaths, indicating low disease mortality. <br/>
- Overall, dysentery surveillance indicates substantial progress toward containment, with historically low case numbers in 2025 and stable, minimal mortality. <br/>

Analysis of monthly dysentery data in Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals pronounced seasonality and a substantial long-term decline in reported cases. Early in the period, peak summer months (June to September) routinely exceeded 10,000 cases, with the highest incidence surpassing 18,000 in 2016 and 2017. In contrast, winter months typically showed much lower case counts—often below 5,000. Starting in 2020, there is a sharp decrease in cases that persists in subsequent years. By 2023–2025, even the summer peaks seldom exceed 4,500 cases, and winter numbers have commonly dropped below 2,000, indicating a significant shift in overall burden.

Throughout the decade, monthly deaths remained low, with zero deaths reported in the majority of months and only sporadic single fatalities. Such patterns suggest effective medical management and disease control, as well as improvements in public health infrastructure reducing severity and transmission. Key environmental factors—like higher transmission in warmer months—persist, but the decline after 2019 likely reflects layered interventions, changes in health behaviors, and possible shifts in reporting or diagnostic practices, especially amid the COVID-19 pandemic. This epidemiological profile highlights the impact of targeted health measures and the importance of maintaining surveillance and preventive strategies, particularly in anticipation of seasonal surges.

Analysis of the reported dysentery data indicates an extremely low case-fatality ratio across the ten-year period in mainland China. Despite seasonal fluctuations yielding tens of thousands of annual cases—especially in summer months—the number of monthly deaths remains predominantly at zero, with only sporadic single deaths between 2016 and 2025. Notably, years with higher case counts such as 2016-2019 did not show a corresponding rise in deaths, reflecting robust prevention, timely medical care, or improvements in surveillance and reporting. For most years, deaths are limited to one or two per annum, with 2020 notably seeing a brief spike (three deaths in January), possibly related to unique circumstances in that period.

The persistence of such a low mortality rate, despite sizable reported case numbers, suggests dysentery in China is rarely fatal under current conditions. This outcome is likely influenced by improvements in both access to medical resources and general public health infrastructure. As case numbers decline in later years, deaths remain rare and uncorrelated, indicating further gains in control and management. The scattered nature of deaths, with no long-term upward or seasonal clustering, points to isolated occurrences rather than systemic failures. Overall, the data support the conclusion that dysentery has transitioned from a significant health threat to one largely mitigated in terms of mortality, with continued vigilance needed to maintain these gains.
