# Dysentery

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Dysentery is an acute infectious disease characterized by inflammation of the intestines, particularly the colon, resulting in severe diarrhea with blood and mucus. It is predominantly caused by two types of pathogens: the bacterium Shigella, leading to bacillary dysentery, and the parasite Entamoeba histolytica, causing amoebic dysentery. Common symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and vomiting. Dysentery is often spread through contaminated water, food, or direct contact with infected individuals. The disease is prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and hygiene. Treatment typically includes rehydration, antibiotics for bacterial cases, and antiparasitic medications for amoebic instances.

## Highlights

1. **Declining Cases**: From 2014 to 2024, dysentery cases in China show a notable decline, with October 2024 reporting 2,680 cases, reflecting significant improvement over the years. <br/>

2. **Low Mortality**: The mortality rate remains consistently low, with recent months, including October 2024, reporting zero deaths, indicating effective management of the disease. <br/>

3. **Seasonal Variations**: Cases generally peak between June and August, but these peaks have decreased yearly, showcasing strengthened public health measures. <br/>

4. **Current Status (October 2024)**: Dysentery incidence is at its lowest in a decade, highlighting successful interventions and control strategies across the Chinese mainland. <br/>

An analysis of dysentery cases reported in mainland China from late 2014 to October 2024 reveals several key patterns within the data. Initially, there is a marked seasonality in the case numbers, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months of June, July, and August. For example, in 2015, the highest number of cases was reported in July with 19,481 cases. This pattern repeats itself in subsequent years, with consistently higher reported cases during the warmer months which are possibly linked to increased transmission rates during the summer when sanitary conditions might be compromised.

Over the years, there is an observable downward trend in the annual number of cases. Starting from a high in 2014 and 2015, the number of cases generally declines each year. By 2024, the cases reported in October reduced significantly compared to the same month in earlier years, reflecting potentially successful public health interventions or improvements in sanitation and healthcare access. Another notable aspect of the data is the relatively low number of deaths compared to the high volume of cases, suggesting that while the condition is prevalent, it tends largely to be non-fatal, possibly due to effective treatment protocols. Over the decade, despite the high incidence of cases, the number of deaths annually never surpasses three, which indicates advancements in clinical management and supportive care.

The analysis of the dysentery-related deaths in mainland China over the span from October 2014 to October 2024 reveals a low mortality rate relative to the number of reported cases. The data shows that most months recorded zero deaths, with sporadic occurrences of one or two deaths, and only a couple of months reporting a death toll higher than two. This suggests a combination of effective healthcare interventions and possibly the mild pathogenic strains prevalent in the region, which may contribute to minimal fatalities despite noticeable case counts. During this period, the highest number of deaths in any given month was three, indicating that even at its peak, dysentery did not cause significant mortality.

Analyzing the trends further, there are instances when higher numbers of cases did not correspond with higher deaths, suggesting effective medical responses or perhaps non-lethal strains dominant during those periods. For example, during peak months like July 2015 and August 2016, although the case numbers were high, deaths remained remarkably low. In contrast, in January 2020, while case numbers were relatively low compared to mid-year averages, there were three deaths, the highest recorded in any month surveyed, which might be attributed to an anomaly in strain virulence or healthcare limitation during that time. The persistent and relatively consistent low death figures over this timeframe highlight a successful public health framework addressing dysentery outbreaks in mainland China.
