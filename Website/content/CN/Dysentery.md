# Dysentery

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an infectious disease characterized by inflammation of the intestines, primarily affecting the colon, and causing severe diarrhea with blood or mucus. It is commonly caused by either bacterial infections, such as Shigella, or amoebic infections, primarily Entamoeba histolytica. Transmission occurs through ingestion of contaminated food or water, often in areas with poor sanitation. Symptoms typically include abdominal pain, fever, and frequent, painful bowel movements. Prompt diagnosis and treatment, usually involving antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, are crucial to prevent dehydration and complications. Maintaining proper hygiene and sanitation is essential for prevention.

## Highlights

- Overall incidence of dysentery in mainland China has shown a consistent decline from 2015 to April 2025, with monthly cases dropping from over 9,000 in April 2015 to 2,235 in April 2025. <br/>
- A clear seasonality pattern persists, with cases peaking during summer (June–August) each year, though the amplitude of seasonal peaks has significantly diminished over time. <br/>
- Annual deaths have remained very low throughout the decade, with several years reporting zero fatalities; in 2025 (Jan–Apr), only one death was recorded. <br/>
- The data suggests effective public health interventions, improved sanitation, and healthcare access have contributed to long-term disease control and reduced case fatality. <br/>

Reported dysentery cases in mainland China from April 2015 through April 2025 demonstrate a marked seasonal pattern with consistently higher incidence during the summer months (June–August) each year. Peaks were most apparent from 2015 to 2017, especially in July and August, with monthly counts exceeding 16,000 and reaching a high of nearly 20,000 in July 2015. Following 2017, however, case numbers began a slow but consistent decline, possibly indicating improved public health interventions or changing environmental exposure patterns. By 2020 and more dramatically post-2021, totals during peak months dropped below 8,000 and continued decreasing annually, with 2024 showing midsummer counts under 5,000—an over 70% decline from 2015 highs.

Looking at year-round patterns, while the seasonality persisted, the overall base level of cases during low-incidence months (typically December to February) also declined significantly. From over 6,000 cases in winter 2015–2016, winter months post-2022 rarely exceeded 2,000. Mortality remained extremely low throughout the period, with many months registering zero deaths and occasional isolated fatality events. An outlier is January 2020, with 3 deaths amid the early COVID-19 period, perhaps indicating overlapping burdens on the healthcare system. Overall, the data reflect long-term epidemiological decline in disease incidence, possibly due to continually improving sanitation, health infrastructure, and disease surveillance.

From April 2015 through April 2025, the reported dysentery mortality in mainland China was low and sporadic, with a total of 24 deaths across 120 months despite over 900,000 cumulative cases. Most months recorded zero fatalities, and no year exceeded a total of four deaths. The yearly distribution reveals occasional peaks—2020 January saw an anomalous spike of three deaths despite reduced case numbers due to COVID-19 disruptions. No discernible seasonal mortality trend is evident, although case counts consistently peak between June and August, suggesting decoupling between incidence and fatal severity.

The data points to a sustained decline in both overall case counts and mortality from 2015 onward, especially from 2019 to 2025, with only isolated deaths in low-incidence settings (e.g., February 2021 and November 2023). This decoupling between high incidence and low fatality implies improvements in disease management, access to care, and perhaps pathogen strain dynamics. The sharp reduction in deaths after 2021, despite continued albeit lower case levels, underscores the effectiveness of public health handling and early treatment strategies in reducing complications associated with dysentery.
