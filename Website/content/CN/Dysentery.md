# Dysentery

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disease of the intestine, particularly the colon, resulting in severe diarrhea with blood and mucus in the feces. It is primarily caused by infections from bacteria such as Shigella or parasites like Entamoeba histolytica. Transmission occurs through contaminated food or water, or person-to-person contact. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Treatment typically involves rehydration and, in bacterial cases, antibiotics. Preventive measures include proper sanitation, hygiene, and access to clean water. Dysentery is more prevalent in developing regions with inadequate sanitation facilities.

## Highlights



- **Decreasing Trend**: Dysentery cases in the Chinese mainland have significantly declined from 19,481 in July 2015 to 1,706 in February 2025. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Higher case numbers are typically observed during summer months (June to August), indicating a seasonal transmission pattern. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: The mortality rate remains low, with sporadic deaths over the years. The highest monthly deaths were three in January 2020. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: Since 2023, case numbers have stabilized at lower levels, suggesting effective control measures and improved public health interventions. <br/>

The data on dysentery cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months (June to August) and troughs in the winter months (December to February). This pattern is consistent with the behavior of dysentery, which often sees increased transmission during warmer months due to factors such as increased outdoor activities and potential contamination of water sources. The highest number of cases was recorded in July 2015, with 19,481 cases, while the lowest was in December 2024, with 1,915 cases. 

Over the years, there is a general downward trend in the number of reported cases, particularly noticeable from 2018 onwards. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, better sanitation, and increased awareness and education about hygiene practices. The number of deaths associated with dysentery remains relatively low throughout the period, with occasional spikes, such as in January 2020, where three deaths were reported. This low mortality rate suggests effective medical interventions and access to healthcare. However, the persistence of cases indicates that dysentery remains a public health concern, necessitating ongoing efforts in prevention and control.

This aligns with the typical epidemiology of dysentery, where warmer temperatures facilitate bacterial growth and increase the likelihood of contact with contaminated water. Despite these seasonal increases in cases, the number of deaths remains low, indicating a low fatality rate. This suggests that while dysentery is a persistent public health issue, it is not often fatal, likely due to effective medical treatments and public health interventions.

Throughout the years, the number of deaths does not consistently correlate with the number of cases. For example, in July 2015, there were 19,481 cases but only three deaths. This pattern continues, with occasional deaths not necessarily occurring during months with the highest case counts. The low mortality rate may be due to improvements in healthcare, sanitation, and treatment protocols. However, the occurrence of any deaths highlights the importance of ongoing public health efforts to prevent and manage dysentery outbreaks. Continued vigilance is necessary to maintain low mortality rates and to address the factors contributing to the spread of dysentery, particularly during peak transmission periods.
