# Dysentery

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disorder of the intestine, primarily affecting the colon, characterized by severe diarrhea with blood and mucus. It is commonly caused by bacterial infections, such as Shigella or Entamoeba histolytica, and can also result from viral or parasitic infections. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Dysentery is often transmitted through contaminated food and water, making it prevalent in areas with poor sanitation. Treatment typically involves rehydration and, in some cases, antibiotics. Preventive measures focus on improving hygiene and sanitation practices to reduce the risk of infection.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend in Cases**: Dysentery cases in the Chinese mainland have significantly decreased from over 6,000 in January 2015 to 1,659 in January 2025, indicating effective public health interventions. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variation**: Historically, higher case numbers peaked in summer months, particularly July and August, but this trend has diminished, suggesting improved control measures. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: The mortality rate has remained low, with sporadic deaths reported, indicating that dysentery is not a major cause of mortality. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: The number of cases has stabilized at lower levels in recent years, reflecting sustained control efforts and improved hygiene practices. <br/>

The data on dysentery cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals significant fluctuations in reported cases and deaths over the years. Initially, in 2015, there was a notable peak in cases during the summer months, particularly in July, with 19,481 cases reported. This spike could be attributed to various factors, including seasonal variations, increased transmission rates, or environmental conditions conducive to the spread of the disease. The mortality rate during this period remained relatively low, with only a few deaths reported, indicating that while the disease was prevalent, it was not leading to high fatality rates.

As the years progressed, a general decline in cases was observed, particularly from 2019 onwards, where the numbers dropped significantly, with many months reporting fewer than 5,000 cases. This decline could suggest improvements in public health measures, increased awareness, and better sanitation practices. However, sporadic increases in cases were still noted, such as in July 2019 and May 2021, indicating that while the overall trend was downward, dysentery remained a public health concern that required ongoing monitoring and intervention. The data also highlights the importance of continuous surveillance and response strategies to manage outbreaks effectively and reduce the incidence of dysentery in the population.
