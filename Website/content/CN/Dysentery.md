# Dysentery

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disorder of the intestine, particularly the colon, that results in severe diarrhea with the presence of blood and mucus in the feces. It is commonly caused by infections from bacteria such as Shigella or by parasitic organisms like Entamoeba histolytica. Dysentery is transmitted through contaminated food or water and is prevalent in areas with poor sanitation. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Immediate treatment is crucial to prevent complications, and it often includes rehydration and antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, depending on the cause. Preventive measures focus on improving hygiene and sanitation.

## Highlights

- **Significant decline in cases since 2014:** Dysentery cases have steadily decreased over time, from a peak of over 20,000 cases in August 2014 to approximately 4,374 cases in August 2024.<br/>
- **Low mortality rate:** Deaths have remained consistently low, with only sporadic fatalities recorded, such as 1-3 deaths per year in recent years, despite the high number of cases.<br/>
- **Seasonal trends observed:** Cases tend to peak during the summer months, typically from June to August, reflecting a seasonal pattern.<br/>
- **Continued control efforts needed:** Although cases have decreased, sustained public health interventions are essential to further reduce dysentery's incidence. <br/>

The data on dysentery cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals a fluctuating but generally declining trend in reported cases, especially from 2020 onwards. The highest case counts were observed during the summer months, particularly from May to August, with peaks in July across most years. For example, the peak number of cases was recorded in July 2015 with 19,481 cases, gradually decreasing to 4,512 cases in July 2024. This seasonal pattern suggests a possible correlation with environmental factors such as temperature and hygiene conditions that may influence the spread of the disease. 

Regarding mortality, deaths from dysentery were relatively rare, with most months reporting zero fatalities. However, there were sporadic increases in deaths, such as in 2015 and 2020, where mortality was slightly higher, with three deaths reported in both July 2015 and January 2020. The reduction in both cases and fatalities in the later years, particularly post-2020, could be attributed to improvements in public health measures, sanitation, and possibly increased awareness or prevention efforts. Despite some fluctuations, the overall trajectory points toward a more controlled incidence of dysentery, with both cases and deaths remaining significantly lower in the recent years leading up to 2024.
