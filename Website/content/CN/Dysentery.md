# Dysentery

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an intestinal infection resulting in severe diarrhea with blood, accompanied by abdominal pain and fever. Caused primarily by bacteria like Shigella or parasites such as Entamoeba histolytica, dysentery spreads through contaminated food, water, or contact with infected feces. It is prevalent in areas with inadequate sanitation and hygiene. Symptoms include dehydration, stomach cramps, and frequent, painful bowel movements. Diagnosing dysentery involves stool tests to identify the pathogen. Treatment typically includes rehydration and antibiotics or antiparasitic medications for bacterial or parasitic dysentery respectively. Preventative measures emphasize sanitation, hygiene, and access to safe drinking water.

## Highlights

- Dysentery incidence in the Chinese mainland has declined markedly from over 13,000 cases in May 2015 to just 2,978 cases in May 2025, reflecting sustained improvements in disease control. <br/>
- There is a consistent seasonal trend with peaks in summer (June–August) and lows in winter, suggesting environmental or behavioral factors influencing transmission. <br/>
- Death rates have remained extremely low throughout the decade, with total monthly deaths rarely exceeding one; notably, no deaths were reported in May 2025. <br/>
- Since 2020, the annual case totals have progressively decreased, indicating strengthened public health measures and possibly improved sanitation and hygiene practices. <br/>

The epidemiological trend of dysentery in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals a clear seasonal fluctuation with consistent annual peaks in summer months (typically June to August) and lows during winter (December to February). This cyclical pattern indicates a probable environmental or behavioral component that facilitates transmission during warmer, more humid periods, consistent with known dysentery transmission routes. A long-term declining trend in total reported cases is observed, decreasing from monthly highs above 19,000 in 2015 to below 5,000 even during peak months in 2023–2025. This suggests notable public health progress, potentially through improved sanitation, health education, or medical access.

Mortality remained low throughout the decade, with only sporadic deaths reported and no clustering across time or geography evident from the data alone. Notably, even as cases dropped steadily post-2020, occasional single-month fatalities persisted, typically isolated and not correlating with case surges. The abrupt decline in early 2020 aligns temporally with widescale public health interventions due to COVID-19, including lockdowns and heightened hygiene, which likely contributed to breaking transmission chains. The persistent downward slope and containment of deadly outcomes imply sustained and successful disease control efforts, though continual surveillance remains essential to prevent resurgence.
