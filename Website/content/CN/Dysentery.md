# Dysentery

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disorder of the intestine, particularly the colon, resulting in severe diarrhea with blood. It is caused by infections with bacteria, such as Shigella or certain types of Escherichia coli, or protozoa like Entamoeba histolytica. Transmission predominantly occurs through the fecal-oral route, often due to contaminated food and water. Common symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and urgent, painful bowel movements. Dysentery is more prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and limited access to clean water. Treatment typically involves rehydration and may require antibiotics or antiparasitic medication, depending on the cause.

## Highlights

- Dysentery cases display a significant decrease from 2015 to 2025, with cases in August dropping from 18,181 in 2015 to 3,686 in 2025, indicating improved control and prevention measures. <br/>
- Deaths have remained remarkably low throughout the period, with slight fluctuations, indicating better healthcare access and management of severe cases. <br/>
- A seasonal pattern is observed, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months, suggesting environmental factors may influence transmission rates. <br/>
- The consistent reduction in cases over the years signifies enhanced public health strategies and possibly improved water sanitation infrastructure in the region. <br/>

The reported dysentery cases in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 display a significant fluctuation, with a general declining trend over the years. Initially, the number of cases per month was markedly higher, with peaks typically observed during the summer months. In August 2015, the reported cases were at their highest at 18,181, which gradually decreased over subsequent years, indicating a successful intervention and control strategy. Each year's pattern shows an increase in cases during the warmer months (from May to September), possibly due to factors like seasonal weather conditions conducive to the transmission of dysentery-causing pathogens.

The data on fatalities remains relatively low compared to the number of cases, suggesting effective management and treatment strategies. However, some years, such as 2020, experienced a slight spike in deaths relative to previous and subsequent years, which could imply issues such as healthcare access or strain on healthcare systems potentially exacerbated by concomitant crises like the COVID-19 pandemic at the time. Despite the evident reduction in the overall number of cases, continuous monitoring and public health efforts are essential to address potential epidemics or localized outbreaks, particularly during periods of increased transmission conducive to seasonal activities and environmental conditions.

This decline is further noticeable during the typically observed peak months of June through August, where a significant annual decrease is seen. For instance, cases decreased from highs of over 18,000 in August 2015 to fewer than 4,000 cases by August 2025. This downward trajectory is accompanied by a relatively low number of deaths over the years, with monthly deaths mostly in the range of 0 to 1, signifying exceptionally low mortality rates relative to the number of cases. Significant increases in deaths, such as the three deaths reported in January 2020, are anomalies rather than trends.

Mortality data reveals no apparent seasonal pattern consistent with case numbers. There are months with higher case counts but little to no deaths recorded, indicating successful disease management and treatment practices. The sporadic nature of deaths and consistently low numbers suggest effective public health interventions and access to health services that mitigate fatalities. Although specific months like November 2022 and February 2025 reported a death despite lower case counts, these instances do not significantly detract from the overall trend of low lethality.
