# Dysentery

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disorder of the intestine, particularly the colon, that results in severe diarrhea containing blood and mucus. It usually stems from infections, predominantly bacterial (e.g., Shigella, Campylobacter) or parasitic (e.g., Entamoeba histolytica). Dysentery is typically spread via contaminated food or water and is more common in areas with poor sanitation. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and frequent, painful bowel movements. It may lead to dehydration and requires prompt medical attention. Preventative measures encompass improving sanitation, ensuring access to clean water, and practicing good hygiene. Treatment often involves antibiotics or antiparasitic medications.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend**: Dysentery cases in mainland China have generally declined from October 2015 to October 2025, with peaks in 2016 and reductions thereafter. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variability**: Cases show seasonal patterns, usually peaking during summer (June to August) and declining in winter (November to February). <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite case fluctuations, mortality rates have remained low, with many months recording no deaths and only sporadic fatalities. <br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of October 2025, dysentery cases continue to decline, with recent figures lower than past peaks, reflecting successful control efforts. <br/>

After this peak, a gradual decline in both cases and seasonality is evident, particularly after 2020, possibly due to increased hygiene and public health measures from the COVID-19 pandemic.

Mortality rates during this period remained low, with sporadic deaths, including three deaths in January 2020, signaling an unusual peak in mortality. This may suggest an anomaly or response-related factor. From 2023 to early 2025, the data shows reduced monthly cases, especially during cooler months (October to February), maintaining a count generally below 2,000. This decline could indicate effective public health strategies and heightened awareness, contributing to lower transmission rates.

Overall, the data reflects an encouraging trend of managing dysentery, with reduced cases and control over seasonal peaks. Sustained public health efforts, focusing on hygiene and sanitation, seem to have played a crucial role in this positive trajectory. As the years progress, continuing these efforts could further diminish dysentery's impact, keeping incidence rates low and preventing seasonal surges.

The data from 2015 to 2025 on dysentery in the Chinese mainland indicates a fluctuating number of cases with minimal mortality. The peak periods in terms of cases were generally observed during the mid-year months, notably from May to August. Despite the high incidence rates, deaths remained remarkably low, with sporadic occurrences of one or two deaths in some months. This pattern suggests that while dysentery infections are relatively common, the mortality rate is contained. The containment could be attributed to effective public health interventions, access to healthcare, or the general resilience of the population to this disease.

There are certain months where specific spikes in fatalities are observable, such as in December 2016, January 2020, and May 2021, where multiple deaths were reported. These instances could reflect particular outbreaks or strains of dysentery that warranted concern or perhaps reflect weakened healthcare responses or vulnerabilities within specific demographics during those times. However, the specific conditions leading to these mortalities are not detailed in the dataset. Overall, the data implies that while the occurrence of dysentery is persistent, it has not translated into an equally significant burden of mortality, thereby highlighting the relative efficacy of management strategies in place to mitigate fatal outcomes.
