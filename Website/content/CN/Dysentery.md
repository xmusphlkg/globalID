# Dysentery

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an intestinal infection characterized by severe diarrhea with blood and mucus, caused by bacteria or parasites. The primary pathogens responsible include Shigella and Entamoeba histolytica. It commonly spreads through contaminated food, water, or direct contact with fecal matter, making it prevalent in areas with inadequate sanitation. Symptoms often include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration, requiring prompt treatment to prevent complications. Treatment usually involves antibiotics for bacterial dysentery and antiparasitic medications for amoebic dysentery. Maintaining proper hygiene and ensuring access to clean water are critical preventive measures against dysentery outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Declining Pattern:** Dysentery cases in mainland China have decreased significantly from 19,481 in July 2015 to 3,899 in July 2025, showing a continuous declining trend. <br/>

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Cases peak during warmer months, typically from April to September each year, indicating seasonal factors in transmission. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Mortality rates remained consistently low, with isolated deaths, suggesting effective treatment measures throughout the years. <br/>

- **Current Status:** As of July 2025, dysentery cases are slightly increasing compared to the previous months, yet remain much lower than earlier years, reflecting sustained public health efforts. <br/>

This seasonal pattern repeats annually, with elevated cases typically in the summer months of July and August, and reduced numbers in the cooler months of January and February. Such seasonality aligns with the behavior of dysentery, which tends to thrive in warm, wet conditions.

Over the years, mortality remains consistently low, with occasional deaths reported. This reflects improvements in healthcare and effective treatment protocols within China. A notable decrease in cases begins in 2020, aligning with the global COVID-19 pandemic, suggesting impacts from enhanced public health measures like sanitation and hygiene practices, reducing transmission not just of COVID-19, but other diseases, including dysentery.

By 2025, there’s a sustained decline, indicating ongoing public health improvements and effective disease management.
