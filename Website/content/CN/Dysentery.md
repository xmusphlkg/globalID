# Dysentery

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disorder of the intestine, particularly the colon, which results in severe diarrhea containing mucus and blood. It is primarily caused by infections from bacteria such as Shigella or parasites like Entamoeba histolytica. The condition is prevalent in settings with inadequate sanitation and limited access to clean water. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Dysentery spreads through contaminated food and water or person-to-person contact. Proper hygiene, safe drinking water, and effective sanitation are crucial for prevention. Treatment often involves rehydration, either oral or intravenous, and antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, depending on the cause.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases**: From 2015 to March 2025, dysentery cases dropped significantly, from a peak of over 19,000 to below 5,000, indicating successful control efforts. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite case fluctuations, deaths have remained minimal, reflecting effective treatment and healthcare management. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variation**: Cases typically rise during warmer months, with peaks in July and August, indicating seasonal influences. <br/>
- **Recent Trends in 2025**: Cases have stabilized at lower numbers, suggesting sustained control measures continue to have a positive impact. Continued vigilance is necessary to maintain progress. <br/>

Analysis of dysentery data in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals a distinct seasonal trend, with case counts peaking between May and August annually. For example, July 2015 saw 19,481 cases, with consistent surges during summer months across most years. This seasonal pattern likely reflects environmental factors such as higher temperatures and rainfall, which can increase transmission risk through contaminated water and food sources. Behavioral changes in summer, including more frequent outdoor activities and higher food exposure, may also contribute.

Over time, a clear downward trend is visible in overall case counts. Figures have decreased markedly from monthly highs above 15,000 in 2015–2017 to fewer than 5,000 cases per month after 2020, reaching lows of around 1,700–2,000 in early 2025. This suggests long-term improvements in sanitation, water quality, and public health systems. In contrast, death counts remain very low—typically zero to one per month—indicating effective treatment and case management. Notably, occasional spikes in mortality, such as in January 2020 and May 2021, suggest isolated lapses or high-risk cases. The consistent pattern of reduced disease burden over the decade signals successful control efforts, although vigilance remains essential, particularly during seasonal peaks.
