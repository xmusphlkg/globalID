# Dysentery

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disease of the intestine, particularly the colon, which results in severe diarrhea containing mucus and/or blood. It is typically caused by infection with bacteria, such as Shigella or Escherichia coli, or amoebae like Entamoeba histolytica. The disease often spreads through contaminated food or water and is more prevalent in areas with poor sanitation. Symptoms may include abdominal pain, fever, and frequent, painful bowel movements. Treatment usually involves rehydration therapies, and sometimes antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, depending on the causative agent.

## Highlights

- Dysentery cases have shown a steady decline from May 2014 (14,200 cases) to May 2024 (3,408 cases).<br/>
- Mortality rates have remained consistently low, with occasional spikes, highlighting effective interventions but also sporadic vulnerabilities.<br/>
- Seasonal trends indicate higher case numbers during summer months (June to August), suggesting environmental or behavioral factors.<br/>
- Recent data (May 2024) suggests a slight uptick in cases compared to the previous months, indicating a need for continued vigilance and potential seasonal preparations.<br/>

An analysis of the dysentery cases in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 shows clear seasonality, with cases spiking each year during the warmer, wetter months of May to August. For example, in 2014 cases rose to 22,311 in July before decreasing towards the year's end. This seasonal pattern remains consistent across the data set, although the annual case count demonstrates a notable decline from 2016 onward, culminating in significantly lower figures by 2024, suggesting improved public health interventions and a possible enhancement in sanitation and hygiene practices over time.

The mortality associated with dysentery across these years is consistently low despite the variability in case numbers, with most months recording zero to one death. Notable exceptions include July 2015 with three deaths and January 2020 with the same number, indicating that while dysentery infections are common, they seldom result in fatalities, likely due to effective medical treatment. Occasional months with higher mortality rates may point to specific outbreaks or variations in healthcare delivery. The overall decreasing trend in cases and the low mortality rates reflect the potential impact of public health initiatives targeting the prevention and treatment of dysentery.

Notably, the number of deaths per month varied from 0 to 3, with higher death counts typically not correlating directly with the months of highest case numbers. When analyzing the temporal patterns, it is apparent that there is no clear seasonal trend in the dysentery deaths. Many months with high case counts, such as June 2014 (19,689 cases), August 2015 (18,181 cases), July 2016 (16,701 cases), and July 2017 (16,316 cases), resulted in zero or only one death. This highlights that while the prevalence of dysentery shows periodic increases, the mortality rate does not appear to follow the same pattern. Consequently, improvements in healthcare management or other factors could be influencing the mortality independent of the case incidence fluctuations.
