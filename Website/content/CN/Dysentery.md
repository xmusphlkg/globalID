# Dysentery

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disease of the intestine, particularly the colon, resulting in severe diarrhea with blood and mucus. It is often caused by infections from bacteria such as Shigella or protozoa like Entamoeba histolytica. The condition is most prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and is spread through contaminated food and water. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and watery diarrhea. It can lead to significant dehydration and, if untreated, can be life-threatening, especially in vulnerable populations. Preventive measures include improving sanitation, handwashing, and access to clean drinking water, while treatment typically involves rehydration and antibiotics.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases**: Dysentery cases have significantly dropped from over 22,000 in July 2014 to around 4,500 in July 2024, showing improved control measures. <br/>
- **Seasonal Pattern**: Higher cases occur consistently during summer months (June-August), indicating a clear seasonal trend in transmission. <br/>
- **Low Mortality**: Despite fluctuations in cases, the death rate remains very low, with only a few deaths recorded over the years, reflecting better healthcare management. <br/>
- **Recent Stabilization**: Case numbers have stabilized in recent years, averaging between 1,500 and 5,000 cases monthly, suggesting sustained control over the disease. <br/>

The reported data for dysentery in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a clear trend of seasonal variation with peaks typically occurring in the summer months, particularly in June, July, and August. This seasonal pattern is consistent throughout the years, suggesting that environmental factors such as temperature and humidity, which are higher in these months, may play a significant role in the transmission and outbreak of dysentery. The case numbers generally decrease in the cooler months, from November to February, reflecting a lower transmission rate during these periods.

Over the years, there is a noticeable decline in the number of reported dysentery cases. The highest number of cases reported was in July 2014, with 22,311 cases, which gradually declined to 4,512 cases by July 2024. The mortality rate associated with dysentery remains low, with a very small number of deaths reported each year, often ranging between 0 to 3 deaths per month. This indicates that while dysentery remains a significant public health issue, the case fatality rate is relatively low, suggesting effective clinical management and possibly improved public health interventions over the years.
