# Dysentery

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory gastrointestinal disorder primarily affecting the large intestine, characterized by severe diarrhea containing blood and mucus. Typically caused by bacterial infections such as Shigella or amoebic infections like Entamoeba histolytica, dysentery spreads through contaminated food and water, poor sanitation, and close human contact. Symptoms include frequent loose stools, abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Prompt diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent severe complications. Management includes rehydration, antibiotics for bacterial causes, and antiparasitic medication for amoebic dysentery. Preventive measures focus on improving hygiene, sanitation, and access to clean drinking water, particularly in developing regions.

## Highlights

- Dysentery cases in mainland China show a significant seasonal trend, with higher numbers during summer months, peaking in June to August annually. Recent data for 2025 indicates cases in June rose to 3,670, maintaining the summer peak pattern. <br/>
- There is a clear declining trend in annual dysentery cases from 2015 to 2025. This suggests improved public health measures and sanitation, with cases dropping from over 11,000 in 2015 to around 3,700 in 2025. <br/>
- Mortality remains low, with sporadic deaths recorded each year. Most months observe zero deaths, indicating effective treatment and disease management strategies over the years.<br/>

The dysentery cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 show a clear seasonal trend with higher incidence during warmer months, particularly June to August, and fewer cases in colder months like January and February. This pattern aligns with other gastrointestinal diseases that proliferate in summer due to increased humidity and temperature, facilitating pathogen transmission via contaminated water and food. Notably, there is a gradual decline in case numbers over these years, suggesting improved public health measures or higher efficacy in disease control. For instance, cases in July 2015 hit 19,481, while in February 2016 they fell to 5,258.

Mortality remains consistently low, with annual deaths in single digits and a peak of three deaths in January 2020, potentially linked to simultaneous health crises during that period. The most significant decrease in dysentery cases is seen in early 2020 when public health measures initiated due to COVID-19 may have inadvertently reduced transmission rates. This decline continued into late 2022, illustrating the impact of sustained health initiatives. The combination of low mortality and decreasing annual case numbers underscores effective public health strategies in combating dysentery. Nonetheless, ongoing vigilance and sanitation improvements remain essential to maintain control over dysentery incidence in China, ensuring continued public health safety.
