# Dysentery

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Dysentery is an inflammatory disease of the intestine, particularly the colon, resulting in severe diarrhea with blood and mucus in the feces. It is primarily caused by infections from bacteria such as Shigella or parasites like Entamoeba histolytica. The condition is often spread through contaminated food or water, poor sanitation, and inadequate hygiene practices. Symptoms include abdominal pain, fever, and dehydration. Treatment typically involves rehydration and, in bacterial cases, antibiotics. Preventive measures include ensuring access to clean water, proper sanitation, and good personal hygiene to reduce transmission risk.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend in Cases:** Dysentery cases in the Chinese mainland have significantly declined from over 7,000 in December 2014 to under 2,000 by December 2024. <br/>

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Higher case numbers are observed during summer months (June to August) each year, indicating a consistent seasonal pattern. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate is low, with sporadic deaths over the years. The highest monthly deaths were three, recorded in January 2020. <br/>

- **Recent Stability:** In 2024, cases have stabilized at lower levels compared to previous years, suggesting effective control measures and public health interventions. <br/>

The data on dysentery cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring during the summer months (June to August) and troughs during the winter months (December to February). This pattern is consistent with the behavior of dysentery, which often sees increased transmission during warmer months due to factors such as increased outdoor activities and potential contamination of water sources. The highest number of cases was recorded in July 2015, with 19,481 cases, while the lowest was in December 2022, with 1,215 cases. 

Over the years, there is a general downward trend in the number of reported cases, particularly noticeable from 2018 onwards. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, better sanitation, and increased awareness and education about hygiene practices. Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, the death toll remains relatively low throughout the period, with most months reporting zero deaths. This suggests that while dysentery remains a public health concern, the fatality rate is low, possibly due to effective medical interventions and access to healthcare. The data indicates a need for continued public health efforts to maintain and further reduce the incidence of dysentery, especially during peak transmission periods.
