# Echinococcosis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic disease caused by the larvae of tapeworms from the genus Echinococcus. The two primary forms affecting humans are cystic echinococcosis (CE), caused by Echinococcus granulosus, and alveolar echinococcosis (AE), caused by Echinococcus multilocularis. CE typically involves the liver and can form large cysts, while AE resembles malignant tumors and can spread to other organs. Transmission occurs through ingestion of eggs in contaminated food, water, or soil, often linked to dogs or wild canids. Diagnosis involves imaging techniques, serology, and, in some cases, biopsy. Control efforts focus on deworming dogs and improving sanitation.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Echinococcosis shows a fluctuating but overall stable trend from 2015 to 2025, with occasionally higher occurrences, indicating localized outbreaks. <br/>
- The year 2017 marked a peak in cases, notably in March (1077 cases), suggesting potential seasonal or environmental factors influencing disease outbreaks. <br/>
- Mortality remains low throughout the years, often with zero monthly deaths, but notable spikes occurred in July 2024 with 5 deaths. <br/>
- Since mid-2024, a slight increase in deaths is observable; this trend requires monitoring and might imply changes in disease severity or health infrastructure strains. <br/>

The data on Echinococcosis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveal patterns and challenges in managing the disease. Monthly cases fluctuate significantly, ranging from as low as 91 in February 2020 to over 1,000 in March 2017. Notably, an increase in cases is often seen towards the end of the year, in November and December, potentially due to seasonal factors or changes in public health efforts. These variations suggest the disease's persistent endemic presence, possible reintroductions, or ongoing challenges in implementing effective control measures.

Deaths related to Echinococcosis are low relative to the number of reported cases, with sporadic occurrences and few pronounced peaks, such as July 2024. This low mortality rate, despite high case numbers, may indicate effective treatment or underreporting of fatalities. The periodic nature of deaths could hint at local outbreaks, demographic factors, or differences in healthcare access and quality. Peaks in deaths may require investigation into healthcare policies, environmental changes, and genetic shifts in Echinococcosis strains. Overall, the low mortality rate is promising, but the persistent and variable case numbers indicate ongoing challenges. This highlights the need for continued surveillance, public health interventions, and management strategies to effectively address Echinococcosis in the region.
