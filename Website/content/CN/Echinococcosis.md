# Echinococcosis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by infection with the larval stages of tapeworms belonging to the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects the liver, lungs, and other organs, leading to the formation of cysts. These tapeworms are typically transmitted to humans via ingestion of eggs, often through contact with infected animals such as dogs or through contaminated food and water. The two main forms of the disease are cystic echinococcosis (hydatid disease) and alveolar echinococcosis, each varying in severity and treatment approach. Diagnosis involves imaging and serological tests, while treatment includes antiparasitic medications and sometimes surgery.

## Highlights

- Case numbers peaked between 2016 and 2017, with a high of over 1,000 cases in March 2017, followed by a gradual decline through 2022. <br/>
- From 2023 to April 2025, the number of reported Echinococcosis cases stabilized between 300â€“500 per month, indicating consistent endemic transmission. <br/>
- Mortality remained low over the 10-year period, with sporadic deaths; a notable spike occurred in July 2024 with five deaths, followed by isolated fatalities in late 2024 and early 2025. <br/>
- The disease burden persists primarily in western regions, necessitating sustained surveillance, intersectoral control efforts, and public health education in endemic areas. <br/>

Between 2015 and 2025, the reported Echinococcosis cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit pronounced annual seasonality, with case counts typically peaking in the winter and early spring months, especially from November to March. The highest monthly incidence occurred in March 2017 with 1,077 cases. Post-2017, there was a gradual downward shift in the annual peak values, suggesting either improved control measures or a shift in reporting or exposure patterns. For example, from late 2021 to late 2022, case numbers dropped sharply, with November 2022 recording just 97 casesâ€”a record low in the dataset. Variability also tends to stabilize in later years, which may reflect maturation of surveillance systems or genuine shifts in transmission dynamics.

Mortality associated with Echinococcosis remains consistently low and sporadic across the decade. Out of hundreds of monthly data points, only rare months reflect associated fatalities, almost all of which report just one to two deaths, with the exception of July 2024 where five deaths were recordedâ€”a potential outlier warranting further investigation. These sporadic events may relate to access to care, late-stage detection, or atypical severe presentations. Despite relatively stable case counts, the low fatality rate underscores a chronic disease profile with typically long latency and manageable outcomes when appropriately diagnosed and treated.
