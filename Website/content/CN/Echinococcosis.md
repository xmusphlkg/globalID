# Echinococcosis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic parasitic disease caused by the larvae of Echinococcus tapeworms, primarily affecting mammals such as dogs and sheep. Humans, as incidental hosts, acquire the disease by ingesting eggs through contaminated food, water, or soil. It has two main forms: cystic echinococcosis, stemming from Echinococcus granulosus, and alveolar echinococcosis, caused by Echinococcus multilocularis. These forms can cause liver and lung damage. Diagnosis typically involves imaging and serological tests. Treatment options include monitoring, surgical intervention, and antiparasitic medications. Preventative strategies focus on controlling host populations and implementing public health education to minimize transmission risk.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases**: Echinococcosis cases show a general upward trend over the years, peaking at various points, such as in March 2017 (1077 cases) and March 2024 (534 cases). <br/>
- **Low Mortality**: While the number of deaths has remained low overall, there were a few isolated spikes in mortality, including 5 deaths in July 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variability**: There is some seasonality in cases, with noticeable fluctuations across different months, often peaking in early spring (March-April) and summer (July-August). <br/>
- **Recent Stability**: In 2024, the disease incidence appears relatively stable, though there was a slight spike in July, coinciding with an increase in fatalities. <br/>

The data on Echinococcosis cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals several patterns. While the number of cases fluctuates monthly, there is a general upward trend observed during certain periods. For instance, case numbers notably spiked in late 2016, reaching 825 in December, and similarly in March 2017 with 1,077 cases. Another significant peak was observed in March 2024 with 534 cases. However, certain months consistently show lower cases, particularly around February of each year, possibly related to seasonal or reporting variations.

Deaths associated with Echinococcosis remain extremely low, with only sporadic occurrences. Between 2014 and 2024, there were a total of 12 reported deaths, mostly isolated in individual months such as July 2015 and 2024. The case-fatality ratio, therefore, remains remarkably low. However, the recent jump to five deaths in July 2024 suggests a potential anomaly or shift that could require further investigation, especially considering the preceding and subsequent months had zero deaths. This trend of low mortality, despite periodic rises in case numbers, indicates that while Echinococcosis is prevalent, it is rarely fatal in most cases across mainland China.
