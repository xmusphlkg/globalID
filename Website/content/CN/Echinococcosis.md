# Echinococcosis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects humans and livestock, with two main forms: cystic echinococcosis (caused by E. granulosus) and alveolar echinococcosis (caused by E. multilocularis). Humans contract the disease via ingestion of eggs shed in feces of infected animals, especially dogs. The larvae form cysts in organs, notably the liver and lungs, leading to severe health issues. Diagnosis includes imaging techniques and serological tests. Treatment may involve surgery, medication, or a combination of approaches. Prevention focuses on controlling transmission in animal hosts.

## Highlights

- **Steady caseload with fluctuation**: Echinococcosis cases in mainland China show regular fluctuations, ranging from a low of 97 cases in November 2022 to peaks exceeding 1,000 cases in early 2017 and March 2024.<br/>
- **Low mortality but recent spikes**: While deaths were rare, the last few years (2023-2024) have seen sporadic increases, notably 5 deaths in July 2024 and 2 in September 2024.<br/>
- **Declining trend in 2022**: A sharp decline in cases occurred from mid-2022, with some months seeing as few as 97 to 140 cases, before cases rose again in 2023 and 2024.<br/>

Echinococcosis mortality rates in mainland China remain relatively low despite fluctuations in case numbers. Over a span of 10 years (2014 to 2024), a total of 76 deaths were reported, with the highest concentration of fatalities occurring in recent months (2024 July and September). For most of the period, deaths were sporadic and isolated, with no clear pattern linked to case surges, such as the large case spikes seen in March 2017 (1,077 cases) or December 2016 (825 cases), both of which had no reported deaths. This suggests effective management and treatment efforts for most cases of echinococcosis.

However, the rise in deaths in 2019, 2020, and 2024, coinciding with relatively moderate case numbers, might indicate challenges in particular regions or populations. The significant spike in deaths in July 2024 (5 deaths out of 461 cases) raises questions about potential changes in disease virulence, healthcare accessibility, or co-factors such as comorbidities. Further investigation into these isolated events may help explain why mortality increased in these particular periods, despite the generally low mortality trends over the years.
