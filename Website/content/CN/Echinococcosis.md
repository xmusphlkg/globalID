# Echinococcosis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus *Echinococcus*. The two main forms affecting humans are cystic echinococcosis (CE), caused by *E. granulosus*, and alveolar echinococcosis (AE), caused by *E. multilocularis*. Humans become accidental intermediate hosts through ingestion of eggs excreted in the feces of infected canines. CE leads to fluid-filled cysts in organs like the liver and lungs, while AE involves invasive tumor-like growths, primarily in the liver. The disease occurs worldwide, particularly in rural and pastoral regions, and presents serious health burdens without timely diagnosis and treatment.

## Highlights

- Echinococcosis case numbers have gradually declined from a peak in 2017, with 2025 May reporting 292 cases, reflecting strengthened control measures. <br/>
- Mortality remains low overall, yet notable spikes were observed in July 2024 (5 deaths) and smaller increases in late 2024 to early 2025, indicating possible local outbreaks or reporting improvements. <br/>
- Seasonal trends are apparent, with case surges often occurring in winter and spring months (e.g., March), suggesting a need for targeted interventions during these periods. <br/>
- From 2022 onward, stability in low monthly deaths demonstrates progress in case management and early detection across endemic regions. <br/>

Echinococcosis case reports in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 show notable temporal and annual variability. A marked seasonal pattern is apparent, particularly with increased cases between November and March, possibly linked to delayed clinical presentations or a reporting lag following peak transmission periods. For instance, March 2017 recorded the highest monthly count at 1077 cases, with similarly elevated figures in adjacent months. While annual totals fluctuate, there’s a general peak period from 2016 through 2019, followed by a noticeable dip in 2020, likely influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic-related disruptions to healthcare access or surveillance systems. Post-2021, the data suggests a modest rebound and stabilization in annual case numbers, albeit at lower levels than the 2016–2019 peak.

Despite the chronic and often prolonged nature of echinococcosis, associated mortality appears very limited, with sporadic deaths (notably 2015 July, 2016 March–April, 2019 February/May/November, and 2020–2025 sporadic months) totaling under 20 over the entire ten-year span. However, anomalies such as July 2024—with 5 deaths reported—raise concerns for localized outbreaks, reporting corrections, or severe complications in that period. Such spikes warrant close epidemiologic follow-up to identify potential failures in early detection, access to surgical care, or rise in highly invasive disease forms. Overall, while case volume remains substantial in endemic regions, effective clinical management and low fatality rates suggest broad health system resilience, but challenges remain in control, especially in early diagnosis and prevention.

The case fatality rate (CFR) over the entire period is approximately 0.08%, indicating that while echinococcosis remains endemic, its lethality is limited under current healthcare and diagnostic conditions. Peaks in mortality were isolated, such as in July 2024 (5 deaths) and February 2019 (2 deaths), without a clear seasonal or cyclical pattern. These anomalous spikes suggest potential delays in diagnosis, variations in healthcare access within local outbreaks, or changes in reporting accuracy.

The consistently low mortality despite fluctuations in reported case numbers, particularly during high-incidence months like March 2017 (1,077 cases) or December 2016 (825 cases), implies sustained effectiveness in disease management and possibly earlier detection and intervention. It is notable that deaths are typically uncoupled from overall case surges, reinforcing that severe outcomes may be more dependent on localized healthcare disparities or individual-level factors (e.g., comorbidities, age, disease burden) than broader epidemiological trends. The clustering of deaths in more recent years such as 2019–2025, after an extended death-free interval (2017–2018), warrants targeted investigation into potential shifts in parasite virulence, population migration, or lapses in surveillance and control programs.
