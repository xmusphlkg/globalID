# Echinococcosis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus Echinococcus. The most common forms are cystic echinococcosis (E. granulosus) and alveolar echinococcosis (E. multilocularis). Humans become infected through ingestion of eggs shed in the feces of definitive hosts, typically dogs or wild canids. The disease is characterized by the formation of hydatid cysts, primarily in the liver and lungs, leading to symptoms ranging from asymptomatic to severe organ dysfunction. Echinococcosis is prevalent in regions with close contact between humans, livestock, and dogs.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Echinococcosis in mainland China has remained stable since 2021, with monthly cases typically ranging from 200–450 and a modest seasonal fluctuation.<br/>
- Mortality is generally low, with most months reporting zero deaths, although occasional mortality spikes occurred (e.g., July 2024: five deaths; September 2025: two deaths).<br/>
- Sustained case reporting and sporadic deaths indicate persistence and ongoing transmission of Echinococcosis within endemic regions, but severe outcomes remain infrequent.<br/>
- As of December 2025, disease control efforts have maintained relatively low case and death rates, yet continued surveillance and targeted interventions are necessary to prevent outbreaks and reduce transmission.<br/>

Reported cases of echinococcosis in mainland China from late 2015 through 2025 exhibit marked interannual and seasonal fluctuations, with peak values commonly observed in the winter and early spring months of several years. Notably, the highest monthly case counts, such as March 2017 (1077) and December–January across multiple years, coincide with periods when healthcare-seeking behavior and reporting may increase, possibly reflecting diagnosis clustering after annual medical examinations or targeted screening campaigns in endemic regions. After 2017, there is a gradual reduction in peak case numbers, with years such as 2022 showing notably diminished counts, which may reflect either real declines in incidence, changes in surveillance system sensitivity, or external disruptions to healthcare access and surveillance activities.

Throughout the decade, annual total case numbers suggest a sustained burden, yet the sporadic nature of deaths—almost universally registering as zero with occasional spikes—signals that mortality remains low relative to morbidity. The occasional months with multiple deaths, particularly in July 2024, could reflect local outbreaks, delayed diagnoses, or changes in reporting standards; however, these events remain exceptional. The general trend points to echinococcosis being a persistent public health challenge in mainland China, with seasonal and possibly region-specific factors influencing variations in notification rates. Despite ongoing transmission, improvements in case management likely contribute to the low case fatality rates evidenced over time.

Analysis of deaths from echinococcosis in mainland China demonstrates persistently low mortality in the face of ongoing endemic transmission. Across a decade of monthly data, the majority of months record zero deaths, despite hundreds of reported cases during many periods. Deaths appear sporadically—typically as isolated events, with rare months showing two or more, such as February and September 2019, July and September 2024, and a small cluster in late 2020. Notably, July 2024 recorded five deaths, the largest single-month total, but this spike is not part of a sustained trend across the dataset.

The consistently low fatality rate, even during periods of elevated case numbers, suggests effective case management, access to healthcare, or predominance of less severe disease presentations. Clusters of deaths, although infrequent, may be associated with localized outbreaks, delays in diagnosis, or particular at-risk populations. Overall, echinococcosis remains a persistent public health issue on the Chinese mainland but rarely results in death, supporting the efficacy of current healthcare responses while underscoring the importance of surveillance and control to prevent isolated mortality events. The pattern emphasizes that while morbidity persists, mortality is largely controlled, and vigilance against lapses in management must continue.
