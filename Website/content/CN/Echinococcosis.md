# Echinococcosis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects humans and livestock, with two main forms: cystic echinococcosis (CE) and alveolar echinococcosis (AE). CE, also known as hydatid disease, is caused by Echinococcus granulosus and is characterized by cyst formation in organs like the liver and lungs. AE, caused by Echinococcus multilocularis, is more severe and can resemble cancer due to its invasive growth. Transmission occurs through ingestion of eggs from contaminated food, water, or soil, often linked to contact with infected animals.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers:** Echinococcosis cases in the Chinese mainland have fluctuated, with peaks in March 2017 (1,077 cases) and December 2016 (825 cases). Recent data from 2024 shows a resurgence with 534 cases in March. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** Deaths have been low, but July 2024 saw 5 deaths, the highest in the dataset, indicating a potential rise in severity or reporting. <br/>

- **Recent Decline and Stability:** After early 2024 peaks, case numbers have stabilized, with a slight decline towards year-end, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** Higher cases are often reported in the first quarter, indicating environmental or behavioral factors in transmission. <br/>

The data on Echinococcosis cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 shows fluctuating trends. Initially, the number of cases varied moderately, with a significant increase in late 2016 and early 2017, peaking at 1,077 cases in March 2017. This peak might indicate seasonal or environmental factors affecting transmission or improved detection and reporting. After this peak, cases generally decreased, with fluctuations, reaching a low of 97 in November 2022. This decline could suggest effective public health interventions or environmental changes reducing transmission.

Mortality data shows sporadic deaths, totaling 13 over the ten-year period. The highest number of deaths in a single month was five, recorded in July 2024. The low mortality rate compared to the number of cases suggests that while Echinococcosis is prevalent, it may not be highly lethal, or medical interventions are effective. The increase in deaths in 2024 could indicate a need for further investigation into potential changes in disease severity, healthcare access, or reporting accuracy. 

Overall, the data underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and targeted public health strategies to manage and reduce Echinococcosis incidence in the region. Understanding these trends can help in developing effective interventions and policies to control the disease's spread and impact.
