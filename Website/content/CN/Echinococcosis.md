# Echinococcosis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic disease caused by the tapeworms of the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects the liver and lungs, with two main forms: cystic echinococcosis (caused by Echinococcus granulosus) and alveolar echinococcosis (caused by Echinococcus multilocularis). Humans become accidental hosts through the ingestion of eggs shed in the feces of infected definitive hosts, such as dogs and wild carnivores. This disease can lead to significant health issues, including organ dysfunction and even death if untreated. Diagnosis is typically via imaging and serological tests, and management often involves a combination of surgery and antiparasitic medication.

## Highlights

- Echinococcosis cases in Chinese mainland showed cyclical trends, peaking typically in winter months. <br/>
- Annual case counts fluctuated, reaching a high of 1077 cases in March 2017 and low periods in 2022. <br/>
- Mortality remained low overall, with occasional spikes such as in July 2024, which recorded 5 deaths. <br/>
- Recent data as of March 2025 indicate a moderate number of cases (445) and zero deaths, suggesting controlled mortality. <br/>

An examination of echinococcosis cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals periodic fluctuations in reported cases. Peaks occur frequently around December 2016 and March 2017, with 825 and 1077 cases respectively, suggesting seasonal factors or variable public health responses. In 2024, a noticeable spike in deaths, especially in July with 5 fatalities, contrasts with previous years, indicating potential changes in the disease's severity, healthcare challenges, or reporting practices.

While fatalities remain low relative to cases, periodic clusters of deaths—such as in July and September 2024 with 5 and 2 deaths respectively—highlight possible challenges in medical interventions or disease complications during those times. This necessitates further investigation into the underlying causes. An interesting trend between 2020 and 2022 shows a significant decline in cases, reaching a low by late 2022. This drop might align with broader public health measures or impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic affecting health systems and reporting accuracy.

Overall, the variations in echinococcosis incidence and mortality emphasize the need for understanding seasonal patterns, healthcare system responsiveness, and the factors that contribute to spikes in both cases and deaths. Addressing these could improve future epidemiological responses and outcomes for Echinococcosis management on the mainland.
