# Echinococcosis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus *Echinococcus*. It primarily affects the liver and lungs but can involve other organs. The disease is transmitted through ingestion of eggs excreted by infected dogs, which can contaminate food, water, or soil. There are two main forms: cystic echinococcosis, often involving fluid-filled cysts, and alveolar echinococcosis, which resembles a malignant tumor. The disease can cause severe health issues and requires surgical intervention or long-term medication. Preventive measures include controlling dog populations and avoiding contaminated food and water.

## Highlights

- **Increase in Cases (2014-2024)**: Echinococcosis cases in mainland China fluctuated, peaking in March 2017 with 1,077 cases. Recent months show stabilization around 300-500 cases. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: The disease has maintained a low mortality rate, with a few deaths over the years, totaling around seven deaths. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variations**: No consistent seasonal pattern in case numbers, indicating factors other than seasonality might influence incidence. <br/>
- **Recent Trends**: In the first half of 2024, case numbers remain steady with a slight decline from the previous year, suggesting better control measures or reporting accuracy. <br/>

The analysis of Echinococcosis cases in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals a fluctuating trend. Significant spikes in cases occurred in December 2016 (825 cases), March 2017 (1077 cases), and December 2017 (569 cases). These peaks may indicate seasonal patterns or outbreaks. Periods of lower incidence, such as February 2020 (91 cases) and October 2022 (115 cases), contrast these peaks. Overall, the trend shows periodic increases and decreases, suggesting the need for continuous monitoring and investigation into potential causes, such as environmental factors or changes in surveillance and reporting practices.

The data also show that while the number of cases varied significantly, the mortality rate remained consistently low, with only a few deaths reported sporadically. Notable months with recorded deaths include July 2015, March and April 2016, February and May 2019, November and December 2020, and July 2021. The low mortality rate relative to the number of cases suggests that while Echinococcosis is prevalent, it is effectively managed with medical intervention. However, the sporadic deaths indicate the necessity for improved health interventions and access to healthcare in affected areas to prevent fatal outcomes. The continuous variation in case numbers underscores the importance of persistent public health efforts and research to understand the dynamics of Echinococcosis transmission and to develop targeted control strategies.

However, the deaths remained remarkably low, totaling only 9 over the entire period, with isolated instances in July 2015, March 2016, April 2016, February 2019, May 2019, November 2019, November 2020, December 2020, and July 2021. This suggests that while Echinococcosis is relatively prevalent, it is not highly lethal.

The consistent reporting of zero deaths in most months highlights the effectiveness of medical interventions and management strategies in place. Notably, the months with fatalities do not exhibit significantly higher case numbers than months without fatalities, suggesting that factors other than the sheer number of cases influence mortality. These factors could include the timing of diagnosis, the quality of medical care, and patient-specific characteristics. Overall, this data underscores the importance of continued surveillance and the need for targeted medical support to further reduce the already low mortality rate of Echinococcosis in China.
