# Echinococcosis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic disease caused by the larval stages of tapeworms belonging to the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects humans, livestock, and wildlife. The disease manifests in two major forms: cystic echinococcosis (hydatid disease) caused by E. granulosus, and alveolar echinococcosis caused by E. multilocularis. Transmission occurs through ingestion of eggs from contaminated food, water, or direct contact with infected animals. In humans, it can lead to severe health complications and even fatality if left untreated. Echinococcosis poses significant public health challenges, particularly in rural communities with close human-animal interactions.

## Highlights

- Decreasing Trend: From 2015 to 2022, echinococcosis cases in mainland China decreased steadily, with a significant drop in cases post-2018. <br/>
- Recent Increase: From 2023, a resurgence is evident, with cases rising annually, peaking in early 2025 with a notable increase in January. <br/>
- Mortality Spike: Deaths are sporadic but notable, peaking in 2024 July, with multiple months in recent years reporting one or more deaths. <br/>
- Current Status: As of June 2025, the reported cases are slightly down from early 2025, indicating recent control measures might be effective; vigilance remains essential. <br/>

Years 2016 and 2017 saw elevated numbers, followed by a general decline from 2018, which might indicate changes in control measures or data collection methods.

Though echinococcosis typically has low mortality, some deaths were occasionally recorded. These instances are sporadic, usually with one or two deaths each year, indicating a disconnect between case surges and fatality rates. However, July 2024 stands out with an exceptionally high death toll of five, which could point to heightened disease severity, healthcare challenges, or improved reporting accuracy during that time. Despite the disease's persistence as a public health issue, the overall low fatality rates highlight effective management and prevention efforts.

Overall, the data suggests that while case numbers have generally decreased since their peak in 2017, occasional surges occur, underlining the need for ongoing vigilance.
