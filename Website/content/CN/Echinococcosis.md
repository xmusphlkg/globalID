# Echinococcosis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic parasitic disease caused by the larvae of Echinococcus tapeworms. It primarily affects the liver and lungs of humans and is transmitted through the ingestion of eggs shed in the feces of definitive hosts, commonly dogs and wild canids. The two main forms are cystic echinococcosis (CE), caused by Echinococcus granulosus, and alveolar echinococcosis (AE), caused by Echinococcus multilocularis. Symptoms vary depending on the location and size of the cysts, but may include abdominal pain, nausea, and coughing. Diagnosis typically involves imaging methods and serological tests, while treatment options include surgery and anti-parasitic medications.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Case Trend:** Echinococcosis cases have shown a general upward trend since 2014, with notable spikes in March 2017 and July 2024. <br/>
- **Rising Mortality in 2024:** The most significant rise in deaths occurred in July 2024, with five deaths reported, the highest in the observed period. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** There are consistent seasonal fluctuations, with higher case numbers typically occurring in spring and early summer months. <br/>
- **Recent Surge in Cases:** The first half of 2024 witnessed a resurgence in cases, peaking at 534 in March and remaining elevated through July, indicating a potential outbreak or worsening of the disease. <br/>

The reported data for Echinococcosis in mainland China from 2014 to mid-2024 shows significant fluctuations in the number of cases and a low but notable mortality rate. From 2014 to 2016, there was an overall upward trend in the number of cases, peaking in March 2017 with 1,077 cases. This increase could suggest a potential rise in transmission or improved detection and reporting of the disease. However, subsequent years saw a gradual decline in the number of cases, with some periodic spikes, such as in December 2018 (538 cases) and March 2019 (467 cases). A particularly noticeable decrease occurred in 2020, likely influenced by the global COVID-19 pandemic, which might have affected both the reporting and management of Echinococcosis cases.

The mortality rate remained low throughout the period, with sporadic deaths reported almost every year, peaking in July 2024 with 5 deaths. This could indicate the presence of more severe cases or potential outbreaks. However, the data also reflects that despite the number of reported cases, the fatality rate of Echinococcosis remains minimal, likely due to effective medical management and timely interventions. The variation in monthly case numbers suggests possible seasonality or changes in exposure risk, necessitating further investigation into environmental, socio-economic, and healthcare factors influencing Echinococcosis transmission in different regions of China.
