# Echinococcosis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by the larval stages of tapeworms belonging to the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects the liver and lungs, but can also impact other organs. The disease is transmitted to humans through ingestion of eggs found in the feces of infected animals, typically dogs. There are two main forms: cystic echinococcosis, caused by Echinococcus granulosus, and alveolar echinococcosis, caused by Echinococcus multilocularis. Symptoms vary depending on the location and size of the cysts, and may include abdominal pain, nausea, and organ dysfunction. Treatment often involves surgery and antiparasitic medications.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers:** From 2015 to 2025, Echinococcosis cases show fluctuations, with peaks in 2017 and 2025, suggesting outbreaks or improved detection. <br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Deaths remain low, with occasional spikes, such as in July 2024, indicating effective treatment or reporting issues. <br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns:** Higher cases are often reported in the first quarter of each year, possibly linked to environmental or socio-economic factors. <br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of February 2025, cases are high, but deaths are minimal, highlighting ongoing challenges in controlling disease spread despite low mortality. <br/>
