# Echinococcosis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus Echinococcus. It primarily affects animals but can be transmitted to humans through contact with infected animal feces or consumption of contaminated food and water. The disease manifests in two main forms: cystic echinococcosis, caused by Echinococcus granulosus, and alveolar echinococcosis, caused by Echinococcus multilocularis. Symptoms vary based on cyst location and size, often affecting the liver and lungs. Diagnosis involves imaging techniques and serological tests, while treatment includes antiparasitic drugs and, in some cases, surgical intervention. Prevention focuses on hygiene and controlling animal hosts.

## Highlights

- Echinococcosis cases in mainland China have shown fluctuations, with a noticeable peak occurring in March 2017, registering 1077 cases. <br/>
- Mortality rates have been consistently low, often recording zero deaths, although occasional spikes are noted, such as July 2024 with five deaths. <br/>
- The case counts have generally decreased slightly from early peaks, with minor variations, averaging below 500 monthly cases in recent years. <br/>
- No significant epidemic trends suggestive of escalating mortality or significant case surges have been observed, implying a stable disease situation as of August 2025. <br/>

Echinococcosis in mainland China exhibits notable fluctuations in case numbers and sporadic fatalities over the years reported. The data indicates a pronounced peak in cases during the colder months, notably in December 2016 with 825 cases and January 2017 with 804 cases, before reaching a zenith in March 2017 with 1077 cases. This trend of increased infections during winter months suggests a potential seasonal influence or heightened case detection during these periods. However, subsequent years did not reach the same peak levels, indicating a fluctuation in incidence. 

Despite various fluctuations, fatalities remained relatively low across the timeline with sporadic occurrences, suggesting likely improvements in medical interventions or disease management strategies. The period from January 2018 to December 2025 saw a decreasing trend in overall cases year on year, except for certain isolated spikes such as March 2024 with 534 cases, indicating possibly localized outbreaks or improved disease reporting during specific times. It is also noteworthy that from 2018 onwards, the number of deaths does not significantly rise, pointing towards effective control measures or healthcare responses to the disease. Overall, the data reflects an active effort in monitoring and controlling Echinococcosis, while implying a need to understand underlying factors driving case spikes.
