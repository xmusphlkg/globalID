# Echinococcosis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Echinococcosis is a zoonotic parasitic disease caused by tapeworms of the genus Echinococcus, primarily Echinococcus granulosus and Echinococcus multilocularis. The disease manifests in two main forms: cystic echinococcosis (CE), commonly known as hydatid disease, and alveolar echinococcosis (AE). Humans can become accidental intermediate hosts by ingesting eggs shed in the feces of infected definitive hosts, such as dogs. CE often affects the liver and lungs, forming cysts, while AE is more aggressive and can be fatal if left untreated. Diagnosis involves imaging techniques and serological tests, and treatment may involve surgery or antiparasitic medications.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend of Cases**: From 2016 onwards, there was a noticeable increase in the number of Echinococcosis cases, peaking multiple times with over 500 cases in several months. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate**: Despite the high number of cases, the mortality rate remains low, with sporadic deaths reported primarily between 2015 and 2020, and rarely thereafter. <br/>
- **Recent Decline and Stability**: Cases have slightly decreased and stabilized starting from 2021, with less variability and lower peaks compared to previous years. <br/>
- **Current Situation**: As of May 2024, new cases reported were 380, indicating a continuation of the trend of lower and more predictable case numbers. <br/>

The data on Echinococcosis cases and deaths from the Chinese mainland from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals notable trends. Initially, from 2014 to mid-2016, case numbers fluctuated between 200 to 500 monthly, peaking at 544 in March 2016 and 825 in December 2016. Deaths during this period were exceedingly rare, with only single deaths recorded sporadically.

From mid-2016 to the end of 2017, there was a pronounced rise in cases, reaching a high of 1,077 in March 2017. Post-2017, cases largely stabilized but saw minor peaks, such as 504 in November 2018 and 438 in January 2024. Despite these fluctuations, deaths remained sparse, though slightly more frequent in 2019 and beyond, with an average of one death in selected months.

In summary, while the early phase saw moderate fluctuations, the period from mid-2016 exhibited significant rises in case numbers, stabilizing again after 2017. Throughout this decade, fatalities due to Echinococcosis were significantly low, highlighting effective management and treatment despite variations in infection rates. The most recent reports indicate a slight resurgence in early 2024. This analysis underscores the importance of regular surveillance and tailored interventions.
