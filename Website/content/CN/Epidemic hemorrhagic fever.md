# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever refers to a group of illnesses caused by viral infections leading to fever and bleeding disorders. These are primarily transmitted by arthropods such as ticks and mosquitoes or through contact with infected hosts. Common types include Yellow Fever, Dengue, Ebola, and Lassa Fever. Symptoms typically range from mild fever to severe hemorrhage, shock, and organ failure. The diseases affect multiple organ systems and can be fatal if untreated. Combatting these epidemics involves vector control, surveillance, and vaccination, underscoring the importance of global health initiatives to reduce their impact.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in Cases**: From 2018 to 2025, the number of hemorrhagic fever cases consistently decreased, with cases dropping from over 2,000 monthly at peak to 354 by June 2025.<br/>
- **Decreasing Mortality**: The mortality rate has significantly reduced, with monthly deaths decreasing from double digits in earlier years to mostly zero or one in 2025.<br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns**: Cases tend to increase in late autumn and early winter, with November consistently having higher case counts across years.<br/>
- **Current Outlook**: As of June 2025, the disease appears to be under better control, reflected by lower case numbers and minimal deaths, suggesting effective public health interventions.<br/>

The analysis of epidemic hemorrhagic fever cases in mainland China showcases fluctuating patterns from June 2015 through June 2025. The data reveals an annual trend where cases typically increase toward the year's end, peaking around November and December, and then decrease in the early months of the next year. For example, November 2017 recorded 2021 cases with 12 deaths, and this pattern repeats annually, indicating potential seasonal influences, possibly linked to climate conditions or rodent activity increasing human exposure.

Post-2018, while monthly cases and fatalities show overall reductions, year-end peaks persist, albeit at lower magnitudes than in 2017 and 2018. By 2024 and early 2025, cases and deaths significantly decrease, hinting at successful public health interventions or enhanced population immunity. This downward trend after 2023, accompanied by fewer fatalities, suggests improved epidemic management. The marked decline in severe outcomes could result from improved healthcare strategies, vaccination campaigns, or changes in population exposure.

Despite these gains, end-of-year spikes still occur, indicating ongoing challenges in eradicating the disease entirely. The persistence of these peaks emphasizes the need for sustained healthcare vigilance and adaptive strategies to fully control the epidemic. Effective monitoring and continued public health measures remain crucial to managing future outbreaks and reducing the impact of this seasonal trend.
