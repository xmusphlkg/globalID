# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever (EHF) refers to a group of viral diseases characterized by fever, bleeding, and organ damage. The most common viruses responsible include Ebola, Marburg, Lassa, and Crimean-Congo hemorrhagic fever. EHF is typically transmitted through contact with infected bodily fluids or vectors like rodents or ticks. Symptoms include sudden fever, fatigue, dizziness, bleeding, and sometimes shock. Early diagnosis and supportive care are crucial for improving survival rates, as there are no specific antiviral treatments for most EHF viruses. Preventive measures include quarantine, protective gear, and vector control.

## Highlights

1. **Declining Incidence and Mortality**: From 2014 to 2024, the number of cases and deaths due to epidemic hemorrhagic fever has generally decreased, with notable declines in monthly cases and deaths since 2018.  
<br/>  
2. **Seasonal Peaks**: Epidemic hemorrhagic fever shows a clear seasonal pattern, with higher case counts during the warmer months, particularly in November.  
<br/>  
3. **Geographic Spread**: The disease continues to be prevalent across various regions, with notable peaks in November each year, suggesting ongoing regional transmission risks.  
<br/>  
4. **Low Mortality in Recent Years**: Despite fluctuations in case numbers, recent years (2023-2024) have seen lower mortality rates, with deaths remaining relatively few.  
<br/>

The data on Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever (EHF) in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows notable fluctuations in both case numbers and mortality. Between 2014 and 2017, cases peaked during the winter months, with the highest reported in November and December 2017 (over 2000 cases), accompanied by higher mortality rates, such as 22 deaths in December 2017. However, after this peak, both cases and deaths began to stabilize and decrease, with periodic increases in 2018 and 2019, particularly in colder months. The case-fatality rate remained relatively low after 2017, with many months in 2019 and 2020 showing fewer than 10 deaths.

From 2021 onwards, cases and fatalities continued to decline. Monthly case numbers often stayed below 500, and the number of deaths dropped significantly, with many months reporting zero or only one fatality. This suggests effective public health measures, including improved surveillance and healthcare responses, may have contributed to this decline. Although there were occasional upticks, particularly in November (e.g., 1776 cases in November 2020), the overall trend indicates a successful reduction in the disease’s impact. Despite this, some seasonal increases remain, particularly in late autumn and winter, with slight rises in November 2023 and 2024, reflecting the ongoing risk of EHF.
