# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever refers to a group of illnesses caused by distinct viruses, characterized by fever and bleeding disorders. Common examples include Dengue, Yellow Fever, and Ebola Virus Disease. These infections are often transmitted through arthropod vectors like mosquitoes or through direct contact with infected individuals or animals. Symptoms typically begin with high fever, fatigue, and muscle aches, progressing to mild bleeding manifestations, such as petechiae or, in severe cases, organ failure and hemorrhagic shock. Rapid identification and isolation of cases, supportive treatment, and vector control are essential for outbreak management and prevention.

The analysis of Epidemic hemorrhagic fever data in the Chinese mainland reveals key trends over several years. From 2015 to 2018, cases and deaths show a seasonal pattern with notable spikes from October to December. November and December consistently record the highest incidences, such as in 2017, where cases were 2021 and 2105, with 12 and 22 deaths. This trend continues into 2018, highlighting a seasonal factor, possibly related to climatic conditions promoting disease transmission during these months.

From 2019 to 2022, there is a general decline in cases and deaths, except for occasional increases again from October to December. These months account for higher incidences, suggesting targeted interventions might be crucial for effective management. Persistent seasonal peaks imply interventions during these months could significantly reduce the epidemic's impact.

By 2023 through 2025, there's a marked reduction in cases and deaths, indicating that control measures have improved. This period, despite occasional spikes such as in November and December 2023, shows a promising trend toward declining severity and spread. The decreasing trend suggests progress in public health strategies or natural epidemiological shifts. Continuous monitoring and targeted preventive actions in these peak months could further aid in mitigating the impacts of Epidemic hemorrhagic fever on the mainland.
