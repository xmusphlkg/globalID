# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever, also known as viral hemorrhagic fever (VHF), encompasses a group of illnesses caused by distinct families of viruses, including arenaviruses, filoviruses, bunyaviruses, and flaviviruses. These diseases are characterized by fever and bleeding disorders, and can progress to shock, multi-organ failure, and high mortality rates. Transmission often occurs through direct contact with infected animals or humans, or via vectors such as mosquitoes and ticks. Examples include Ebola, Marburg, Dengue, and Lassa fever. Diagnosis requires laboratory testing, and treatment is primarily supportive, with some conditions benefitting from specific antiviral therapies.

## Highlights

- The number of cases and deaths from Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in Chinese mainland is subject to seasonal peaks in November.<br/>
- A decreasing trend in both cases and deaths is observed from November 2021 to May 2024, suggesting improved disease control.<br/>
- Mortality rates have declined, with several months recording no deaths, indicating advancements in treatment and healthcare responses.<br/>
- May generally shows a rise in cases, yet figures for May 2024 (359 cases, 2 deaths) are significantly lower compared to peak years, reflecting a potentially more contained situation.<br/>

The provided data for Epidemic hemorrhagic fever cases in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals recurring seasonal peaks and variations in cases and death rates. Generally, the data shows higher incidence rates during the later months of each year, particularly from October to December, with notable spikes in both 2017 and 2018. These periods coincide with the peak activity of rodents, which are known vectors for hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS) commonly seen in China.

A closer examination reveals a downward trend in the number of cases reported annually post-2018. Despite the persistently high incidence periods in late autumn and early winter, there's a reduction in the amplitude of these peaks over the years. The mortality rates, although fluctuating, do not show a clear decreasing trend, suggesting that while the number of cases may be decreasing, the lethality or severity of the disease might remain a significant public health challenge. This pattern necessitates continued targeted public health interventions, particularly rodent control and vaccination during high-risk periods, to mitigate both the incidence and severity of the disease.

Notably, there are consistent peaks in late autumn and early winter. For instance, in November and December of 2017, 2018, and 2021, death tolls reached double digits, underscoring a seasonal component that influences the epidemic's dynamics.

Anomalies in death rates were also observed despite relatively stable or declining case numbers. Particularly notable are the December 2017 figures, where deaths reached 22 from 2105 cases, and November and December of 2018, with deaths of 33 and 17 from 2213 and 1700 cases, respectively. These spikes suggest periods of increased virulence or compromised healthcare responses.

A gradual decrease in cases from 2019 onward aligns with a reduction in mortality rates, indicating improvements in healthcare responses and public health interventions. However, isolated spikes, like those in November 2023 and December 2021, recorded higher death rates that require further investigation into potential causes, such as environmental factors or variations in pathogen virulence.

Overall, while the seasonality of cases is evident, the data also points to periods requiring additional scrutiny to understand and mitigate the factors contributing to higher mortality during these months.
