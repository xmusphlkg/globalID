# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever (EHF) refers to a group of illnesses caused by several distinct families of viruses, including hantaviruses, arenaviruses, filoviruses, and flaviviruses. Characterized by fever and bleeding disorders, EHF can progress to shock, organ failure, and death if untreated. Transmission varies by virus type; common vectors include rodents and arthropods, with human-to-human transmission possible in certain cases. Regions affected span Asia, Africa, and the Americas. Early detection, supportive care, and preventive measures, such as vector control and vaccination, where available, are crucial to manage and mitigate outbreaks effectively.

## Highlights

- **Recent Trends:** Cases of epidemic hemorrhagic fever have significantly decreased since 2017, with a gradual decline in case numbers from 2021 onwards. <br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of October 2024, the number of cases stands at 374, marking a decrease from previous monthly averages. Mortality rates have also remained low. <br/>
- **Seasonality Observed:** The data shows seasonal spikes, particularly in the autumn and winter months each year, aligning with historical observations of the disease's behavior. <br/>
- **Public Health Implications:** Continued monitoring and rodent control efforts are crucial, as the disease shows high correlation with rodent population index, although current incidence indicates effective management. <br/>

The data on Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China from October 2014 to October 2024 shows a seasonal pattern, with cases peaking in the winter months of October to December, suggesting a possible connection between colder weather and increased transmission or case reporting. For example, case numbers exceeded 2000 during the winter of 2017 and 2018, indicating higher transmission rates or reporting during colder seasons. Conversely, there is a consistent decrease in case numbers during the summer months, potentially due to environmental factors or changes in social behavior during warmer weather that may affect transmission.

While mortality rates fluctuate, they tend to rise with case numbers, though not as sharply. A noteworthy observation is the overall decline in both cases and deaths from 2021 onward, despite occasional spikes like those in November 2023 and December 2021. This decline may point to more effective public health interventions, better medical care, or increased public awareness about disease prevention. Although recent years show lower numbers compared to earlier periods, external factors such as changes in reporting practices could also influence these trends. Overall, the data suggests some success in controlling the spread of the disease, as seen in the general decrease in both morbidity and mortality over time.

Analyzing overall trends, there appears to be a general downward trend in the number of both cases and deaths from around 2020 onwards.
