# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever, also known as hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS), is an acute viral illness caused by hantaviruses. It is primarily transmitted to humans through contact with the urine, droppings, or saliva of infected rodents. The disease is characterized by sudden onset of fever, hemorrhagic manifestations, and renal dysfunction, which can range from mild to severe. The incubation period is typically 2 to 4 weeks. Common symptoms include high fever, headache, abdominal pain, and bleeding. Severe cases may lead to shock, acute kidney injury, and require hospitalization for supportive treatment.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trends**: There is a noticeable decline in both cases and deaths from Epidemic hemorrhagic fever from 2018 onwards, with particularly steep reductions observed from 2021. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variation**: The data shows a recurring pattern with spikes in cases and deaths typically occurring in the months of November to January. However, this pattern has weakened post-2021. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: As of March 2025, the case and death counts are at relatively low levels, showcasing successful control measures and possibly increased public health interventions. <br/>

- **Mortality Rate**: Despite fluctuations in cases, the number of deaths has remained minimal in recent years, indicating effective treatment protocols or lower virulence. <br/>

The data for Epidemic hemorrhagic fever (EHF) in mainland China from March 2015 to February 2025 shows marked seasonal fluctuations, with case peaks usually occurring towards the end of the year, particularly from October to December. November and December frequently record higher case numbers, with November 2017 reaching a notable peak of 2,021 cases. This recurring pattern implies heightened disease transmission during these months, potentially due to ecological or climatic factors affecting vectors or hosts.

Fatality data present more erratic patterns, not consistently linked with case numbers. Deaths fluctuate month-to-month and year-to-year, suggesting complexities beyond case volume, such as healthcare accessibility or response effectiveness, influencing mortality. For instance, November 2017 had high case numbers but fewer deaths compared to December, which recorded more fatalities despite similar cases. This disparity indicates factors like medical intervention timing and pathogen virulence significantly impact death rates.

Despite a reported downtrend in recent years, indicating potential improvements in disease control or reporting strategies, November and December remain critical. They require increased vigilance and preparedness to mitigate EHF's impact, emphasizing consistent monitoring and strategic health policies to manage and prevent seasonal surges effectively. An examination of the reported data for Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China from March 2015 to March 2025 reveals both fluctuations and trends in the number of cases and correlating deaths. In the earlier years, particularly from 2015 to 2018, we observe relatively higher case numbers and correspondingly higher fatalities. For instance, November and December consistently display elevated figures, such as in 2017 with 2,021 cases and 12 deaths in November, followed swiftly by 2,105 cases and 22 deaths in December. This highlights a seasonal pattern linked with higher incidences and mortalities during colder months.

More recent data from 2019 to early 2025 show a general decrease in both cases and deaths. The year 2023, for example, exhibits significantly reduced figures, with a noticeable decline in both variables, such as only three deaths in October despite 439 cases, and another drop in November with 1,320 cases but just three deaths. Similarly, January 2025, with 301 cases resulting in just one death, signifies an improvement in management or response strategies. This overall downward trend points to potential advancements in medical interventions, public health measures, and better awareness and preparedness, suggesting a progressively successful containment and treatment of the epidemic hemorrhagic fever within the region.
