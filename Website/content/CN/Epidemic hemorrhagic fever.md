# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever is a group of illnesses caused by several distinct families of viruses, including Arenaviridae, Filoviridae, Bunyaviridae, and Flaviviridae. These viral infections are characterized by fever and bleeding disorders and can progress to shock, organ failure, and high mortality rates. Transmission often occurs through contact with infected animals, ticks, or mosquitoes, and in some cases, person-to-person via body fluids. Notable examples include Dengue, Ebola, and Hantavirus infections. Control measures focus on vector management, protective clothing, and hygiene practices, while vaccines and antiviral treatments are limited to specific types.

## Highlights

- **Overall Decline:** Over nine years, cases of Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in China show a marked decline from thousands in 2014 to under 200 by September 2024. The number of deaths has similarly decreased. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns:** Higher case numbers consistently occur in late autumn and winter, particularly notable in November and December each year. <br/>

- **Recent Stability:** In recent years, especially post-2022, cases and fatalities have stabilized at lower levels, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Current Situation (September 2024):** As of September 2024, there are 192 cases with 1 death, suggesting low transmission and effective public health interventions. <br/>

The data for epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows seasonal and annual fluctuations in case counts and deaths. Notably, peaks in cases tend to occur during the last quarter of each year, particularly in November and December, which often coincide with an increase in mortality. For instance, November 2017 and 2018 saw some of the highest reported case counts (2021 and 2213, respectively) and were accompanied by relatively high death tolls (12 and 33). This pattern of heightened cases toward the year-end suggests a possible seasonal component, potentially linked to environmental factors or human behavior that affect the transmission dynamics.

Over time, there is a notable decline in the number of cases reported per month, especially post-2018, which might reflect improved public health interventions or natural epidemiological shifts. For example, in 2018, monthly case counts ranged between 512 and 2213, while in 2023, the highest was 1320 (November), and by September 2024, cases had dropped to 192. The mortality rate generally mirrors case counts, though there are instances of higher fatality during spikes, such as in December 2018 (33 deaths) and December 2021 (26 deaths), indicating that these peaks may pose greater health risks and stress on medical infrastructure.
