# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever refers to a group of viral diseases characterized by fever and bleeding disorders. These diseases include Dengue, Ebola, Marburg, Yellow Fever, and Lassa fever, among others. Typically triggered by RNA viruses, they are transmitted via vectors like mosquitoes or through contact with infected animals or humans. Symptoms often start with high fever, fatigue, muscle pain, and can progress to severe bleeding and organ failure. Outbreaks are more common in tropical and subtropical regions. Prompt diagnosis and supportive care are critical, as some forms have high mortality rates without timely treatment.

## Highlights

- **Declining trend in cases**: From 2014 to August 2024, there has been a clear reduction in cases, with August 2024 reporting 202 cases, compared to over 500 in previous years. <br/>
- **Lower mortality rates**: Mortality rates have also significantly decreased, with zero deaths reported in August 2024, reflecting better management and treatment strategies. <br/>
- **Seasonal fluctuations**: The disease shows seasonal spikes, typically peaking during the fall and early winter, notably November and December, with high case numbers in those months. <br/>
- **Recent stabilization**: In the past few years, cases have stabilized at lower levels, particularly in the summer months, suggesting better control and prevention efforts. <br/>

The data on epidemic hemorrhagic fever cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows significant variability in both the number of cases and deaths throughout the years. The data reveals distinct seasonal patterns, with a noticeable rise in cases typically starting around October, peaking in November and December, and gradually decreasing by the spring months. This seasonal increase is likely related to environmental and behavioral factors that facilitate the transmission of the disease, which is often associated with rodent populations. For example, November 2017 saw a sharp peak of 2,021 cases, followed by 2,105 cases in December of the same year. Similar peaks are evident in 2018 and 2021, suggesting a cyclical nature of outbreaks during colder months.

In terms of fatalities, the number of deaths does not consistently correlate with the total number of cases. While some months with high case counts, such as November and December 2017, report higher fatalities (12 and 22, respectively), other periods, such as November 2020, show a notable number of deaths (14) despite fewer cases compared to other peaks. This discrepancy might reflect differences in healthcare responses, the virulence of the pathogen, or the demographics of those infected. Additionally, in recent years, especially from 2023 onwards, there is a general decline in both cases and deaths, potentially due to improved disease management, public health measures, or natural changes in transmission dynamics.

An analysis of the deaths associated with Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever (EHF) on the Chinese mainland reveals some key patterns across the years from 2014 to 2024. The death toll fluctuates, with noticeable peaks occurring in November and December of multiple years. For example, in November 2017 and November 2018, deaths surged to 12 and 33, respectively. Similarly, December 2017 and December 2018 saw death counts of 22 and 17, highlighting that the latter months of each year tend to experience higher mortality. This may indicate a seasonal or environmental factor exacerbating the severity of cases, possibly linked to the cooler months or increased transmission.

Additionally, while the number of cases does not always correlate directly with deaths, the years with higher case counts generally exhibit more fatalities. For instance, in November 2020 and 2021, deaths reached 14 and 12 respectively, during periods of high case numbers. However, there are notable instances where lower case counts still resulted in deaths, such as in May 2021, where 8 deaths occurred despite only 685 reported cases, suggesting that the lethality of the disease can vary independently of the number of reported cases, possibly due to differences in healthcare access, response times, or viral strains.
