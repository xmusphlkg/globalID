# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever refers to a group of illnesses caused by distinct viruses characterized by fever and bleeding disorders. These illnesses typically affect the vascular system and can lead to hemorrhage, often resulting in severe illness or death. Predominantly spread by mosquitoes or rodents, notable types include Dengue, Yellow fever, Crimean-Congo hemorrhagic fever, and Ebola. The viruses belong to families such as Arenaviridae, Filoviridae, and Flaviviridae. Initial symptoms often include high fever, fatigue, and muscle pain, progressing to bleeding manifestations. Timely diagnosis and supportive care are crucial for effective management and control.

## Highlights

- A clear long-term declining trend is evident, with monthly cases falling from over 2000 in late 2018 to fewer than 300 by May 2025.<br/>
- Mortality has markedly decreased; no deaths were reported in the last four months, suggesting improved disease management or lower virulence.<br/>
- Seasonal peaks are still noted annually in Novemberâ€“December, but their intensity is significantly reduced compared to earlier years.<br/>
- The current situation as of May 2025 reflects low transmission levels, with 268 reported cases and zero deaths, indicating sustained epidemic control.<br/>

The temporal distribution of epidemic hemorrhagic fever cases in mainland China from May 2015 to May 2025 shows marked seasonality, with a consistent pattern of increased incidence during the colder months, particularly between October and January. This seasonal rise is most prominent in the years 2017 through 2021, where monthly case peaks often surpassed 2,000, notably in November and December. This pattern aligns with the ecology of rodent hosts and increased human exposure during harvest and early winter periods, which are known to elevate transmission risk. Conversely, the summer months, especially from June to September, consistently represent periods of lower incidence across all observed years, sometimes dropping below 200 reported cases.

From 2015 onward, there was a general trend toward increasing case totals until a peak around 2021, followed by a noticeable decline through 2023 and into 2025. Not only do post-2021 figures suggest a reduction in total reported cases, but fatalities have also shown a downward shift, with several recent months reporting no deaths. This could reflect improvements in surveillance, early diagnosis, vaccination efforts in high-risk populations, public health education, or possible shifts in environmental or rodent population dynamics. However, despite the overall decline, the persistence of late-year spikes indicates that core transmission mechanisms remain active and seasonally reinforced.
