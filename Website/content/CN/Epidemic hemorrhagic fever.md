# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever, often referred to as viral hemorrhagic fevers (VHFs), is a group of illnesses caused by several distinct families of viruses, including Arenaviridae, Filoviridae, Bunyaviridae, and Flaviviridae. These diseases are characterized by fever and bleeding disorders and can lead to high mortality rates. Common VHFs include Ebola, Marburg, Lassa fever, and dengue. Transmission often occurs through contact with infected animals, humans, or vectors such as mosquitoes and ticks. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe, life-threatening hemorrhaging. Control measures focus on outbreak containment, vector control, and supportive medical care.

## Highlights

- Epidemic hemorrhagic fever cases have significantly decreased from 2015 to 2025, indicating effective control measures. Cases dropped from over 2000 in late 2017 to less than 300 in 2025. <br/>
- Deaths have declined consistently, with monthly fatalities reducing from double digits in earlier years to zero in many months of 2025, signifying improved treatment or prevention strategies. <br/>
- Seasonal trends show higher case counts during late autumn and early winter, suggesting climatic or behavioral influences on transmission. <br/>
- As of October 2025, low endemic levels and negligible mortality rates indicate a stable and successfully managed epidemiological situation in the Chinese mainland. <br/>

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in the Chinese mainland presents a distinct seasonal pattern, with cases typically increasing in the cooler months. From 2015 to 2025, a notable spike occurs each November and December, peaking in November 2018 with 2,213 cases. This suggests that cooler temperatures and related human behavior may heighten transmission risks. However, from 2023 onwards, there is a significant decline in cases, indicating effective public health measures or increased population immunity.

Mortality rates show some concerning peaks, such as in December 2021 with 26 deaths. Overall, the mortality rate per case remains stable, pointing to consistent treatment protocols. Although higher cases occur in colder months, the magnitude of these peaks has decreased over recent years, possibly due to successful control measures or changes in environmental or social factors impacting the disease's spread. The overall decline in cases since 2023 is promising, suggesting improvements in disease management and possibly changes in environmental conditions or prevention strategies.

The lower case numbers and deaths observed in 2023 through 2025 reflect a positive trajectory. Continued vigilance and public health efforts are crucial to maintain and further this progress. The data underscores the importance of adaptable health strategies to mitigate seasonal surges and highlight the ongoing need for effective monitoring and intervention strategies.
