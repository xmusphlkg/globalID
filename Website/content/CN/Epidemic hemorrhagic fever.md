# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever is a collective term for viral diseases characterized by fever and bleeding disorders, primarily affecting the vascular system. It includes a group of illnesses like Dengue, Ebola, Lassa, and Hantavirus infections. These diseases are caused by diverse families of viruses, often transmitted by vectors such as mosquitoes, ticks, or rodents. The symptoms typically include high fever, internal bleeding, and organ impairment, which can lead to severe complications or death without prompt treatment. Outbreaks are often associated with ecological disruptions, human encroachment on wildlife habitats, or inadequate health infrastructure in affected regions.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Epidemic hemorrhagic fever has shown a substantial decline since 2015, with a noticeable reduction in both cases and deaths over time. <br/>  
- Peak incidence was observed traditionally during colder months, particularly between October and December, but recent data suggests a consistent low case count year-round without significant peaks. <br/>  
- By September 2025, cases have dwindled to their lowest levels, with only 102 reported cases and no fatalities, indicating effective control measures are in place. <br/>  
- The trend reflects improvements in prevention, treatment, and possibly vaccination programs, contributing to reduced disease burden and mortality. <br/>

The data on Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever in the Chinese mainland reveals a seasonal pattern with case peaks in winter, especially November and December, from 2015 to 2025. This pattern likely results from climatic influences on rodent vectors and changes in human behavior during colder months, which may facilitate virus transmission. The correlation between higher case counts and increased fatalities is evident during peak months; notable spikes occur in 2017, 2018, and 2021, challenging public health efforts and necessitating targeted interventions.

From 2022 onwards, there is a marked decline in both cases and deaths, suggesting improvements in disease control, increased public awareness, and better management protocols developed over the years. This sustained decrease indicates effective strategies in controlling viral spread, highlighted by lower mortality rates and fewer cases. The downward trend from 2022 through 2025 reflects advancements in healthcare responses and greater adherence to public health measures, minimizing public health threats from the epidemic.

The consistency of the seasonal trend over the years underscores the importance of focusing preventive measures and public health resources during high-risk months. It also highlights the need for continuous monitoring and adaptive strategies to maintain control and enhance community resilience against this endemic threat, contributing to long-term public health stability.
