# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fevers are a group of illnesses caused by several distinct families of viruses, including Arenaviridae, Bunyaviridae, Filoviridae, and Flaviviridae. These fevers are characterized by fever, fatigue, dizziness, muscle aches, and bleeding under the skin and internal organs. The diseases often have animal hosts and are transmitted to humans via arthropods or direct contact with infected animals or their secretions. Outbreaks are commonly seen in Africa, South America, and Asia. Controlling these diseases involves preventing transmission through vector control, improving sanitation, and adhering to strict quarantine measures during outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks and Trends**: Epidemic hemorrhagic fever shows seasonal peaks typically in the late fall and winter months, particularly noticeable in November and December. <br/>

- **Declining Cases and Deaths**: There is a noticeable decline in both cases and deaths from 2018 onwards, reaching low levels in 2022 to mid-2024, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Recent Year Analysis**: As of July 2024, cases remain low compared to the winter months, with minimal fatalities, indicating effective ongoing disease management. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers**: The resurgence in cases in late 2023, especially in November, underscores the importance of sustained surveillance and interventions. <br/>

The reported data for epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China across the years reveals a distinct cyclical pattern, with cases peaking annually between November and December. Conversely, the lowest number of cases typically occurs from February to March. For instance, in November and December, the years 2017 and 2018 saw significant spikes in reported cases, with 2021 and 2023 following a similar trend. This suggests that climatic or environmental factors, and possibly increased human activities or interactions during these months, might be influencing the transmission of the disease. It's notable that death counts don't consistently mirror the peaks in cases, which might indicate improved medical interventions or changes in the virus's virulence.

In recent years, a general decline in both cases and deaths is observed, particularly from 2022 onward. The lower numbers in cases and deaths from 2022 through 2024 suggest potential improvements in disease management, vaccination efforts, or public health strategies. However, the data for November and December 2023 show an uptick, raising questions about potential new strains or lapses in public health vigilance. It's crucial to delve into these anomalies to understand if they represent natural variances or if they're due to other factors such as reporting changes or epidemiological shifts. Consistent monitoring and research into environmental, biological, and societal factors are vital for comprehending these patterns and enhancing disease control efforts.
