# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever refers to a group of illnesses caused by several distinct families of viruses, including Arenaviridae, Filoviridae, Bunyaviridae, and Flaviviridae. These diseases are characterized by fever and bleeding disorders and can progress to high fever, shock, and death in severe cases. Transmission often occurs through contact with infected animals or humans, with some viruses spread by arthropod vectors like mosquitoes or ticks. Notable examples include Ebola, Dengue, and Hantavirus. Prevention focuses on controlling vectors, avoiding contact with infected hosts, and implementing protective measures in healthcare settings.

## Highlights



- **Decreasing Trend:** From 2015 to 2025, cases have significantly declined, peaking in November 2018 (2,213 cases) and dropping to 172 cases by February 2025.<br/>
- **Mortality Rate:** Deaths peaked in December 2018 (33 deaths) but have decreased, with zero deaths in February 2025.<br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns:** Higher cases occur in late autumn and early winter, indicating a seasonal trend.<br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of February 2025, the disease is under control with the lowest cases and no deaths, reflecting effective public health measures.<br/>

The data on Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring in the late autumn and early winter months, particularly in November and December. This pattern is consistent across multiple years, suggesting a potential link to environmental or social factors that may influence the transmission of the disease during these months. For instance, November 2017 and 2018 saw significant spikes in cases, with 2021 and 2023 also showing elevated numbers during the same period. The data indicates a general decline in cases from 2019 onwards, with a few exceptions, such as the notable increase in November 2023.

The mortality rate, while relatively low compared to the number of cases, also shows some seasonal variation, with higher death counts often coinciding with the peaks in case numbers. However, the data from recent years, particularly from 2020 onwards, suggests a gradual decrease in both cases and deaths, which could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, or advancements in medical treatment and prevention strategies. The consistent reduction in cases and deaths in the latter years of the dataset, especially from 2022 to 2025, may reflect successful interventions and a better understanding of the disease's transmission dynamics.
