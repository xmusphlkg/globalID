# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever refers to a group of viral illnesses characterized by fever, bleeding tendencies, and increased vascular permeability. These diseases are typically caused by RNA viruses, such as hantaviruses, arenaviruses, filoviruses, and flaviviruses. Transmission occurs through contact with infected animals, arthropod vectors, or human-to-human spread in some cases. Clinical manifestations range from mild symptoms to severe, life-threatening hemorrhagic shock and organ failure. Epidemic outbreaks often occur in specific regions, with notable examples including Ebola, Dengue, and Crimean-Congo hemorrhagic fevers. Early detection and supportive care are crucial for reducing mortality rates.

## Highlights

- Epidemic hemorrhagic fever cases in mainland China have declined significantly since 2018, with monthly cases in late 2025 less than one-third of peak periods.<br/>
- A clear annual seasonality persists, with higher case counts from November to January and low incidence during summer months.<br/>
- The case fatality rate has also dropped, with most months in 2025 registering zero deaths, except for November (5 deaths) and December (6 deaths).<br/>
- The current situation suggests effective disease control, with persistent but low-level transmission and improved outcomes compared to previous years.<br/>

Analysis of Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever data in mainland China from late 2015 to 2025 reveals marked seasonal and annual fluctuations in reported cases and deaths. Typically, incidence rises sharply around late autumn and winter months, peaking in November or December, while summer and early autumn present notably lower case counts. Within each year, this pattern is consistent, with a steep increase in cases from October, peaking in November or December, and declining through the spring and summer. This cyclical trend coincides with ecological factors affecting rodent populations and human exposure patterns, given that epidemic hemorrhagic fever in China is predominantly associated with hantavirus infections transmitted from rodents.

A notable long-term trend is the gradual decrease in both total annual cases and deaths after 2021, with counts in 2023–2025 significantly lower than pre-pandemic years such as 2017–2019. The data suggest improvements in public health interventions, rodent control, vaccine coverage, or healthcare access may have contributed to this reduction in disease burden. Additionally, periodic surges in the late autumn and winter months, particularly prominent in 2017, 2018, and 2021, coincide with higher mortality, perhaps reflecting delayed healthcare-seeking or heightened transmission. In contrast, recent years show both a dampening of overall cases and a substantial reduction in deaths, indicating possible success in disease management and improved early detection, though close monitoring remains essential for outbreak prevention and response.

From December 2015 to December 2025, deaths due to epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China display clear seasonal and annual variation, with a tendency for mortality to peak in late autumn and winter (notably November to January). The highest monthly deaths are seen in November and December of several years—such as 22 deaths in December 2017 and 33 in November 2018—correlating closely with large increases in case numbers. The apparent cyclical nature indicates that mortality burden rises when overall transmission is highest, pointing to a predictable seasonality in severe outcomes.

Over time, both cases and deaths gradually decline, particularly from 2023 onward, reflecting much lower mortality—a trend likely influenced by intensified disease control, improved healthcare access, or broader shifts such as those witnessed during the COVID-19 pandemic. The proportion of deaths per case remains relatively stable during periods of high transmission, while months with low incidence almost always show minimal or zero deaths, suggesting no significant increase in case fatality even as total cases decrease. This pattern supports that epidemic hemorrhagic fever lethality is closely tied to incidence rather than any change in the virus's severity. Recent lower numbers point to successful interventions or changing epidemiology, but the periodic spikes indicate continued vigilance is needed.
