# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever, often referred to as hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS), is caused by hantaviruses. This zoonotic disease primarily affects the kidneys and is characterized by fever, hemorrhagic manifestations, and renal failure. Rodents are the natural hosts, with humans contracting the virus through inhalation of aerosolized rodent excreta. Symptoms typically include fever, headache, back pain, abdominal pain, and renal dysfunction. Regions affected include Asia, Europe, and parts of Russia, with outbreaks often linked to increased rodent populations. Early diagnosis and supportive care are crucial for management and recovery.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases and Deaths:** Significant decline from 2014 to 2024, with recent years showing less than 500 cases per month. <br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns:** Higher cases typically observed in late autumn and early winter (November-December). <br/>
- **Recent Trends:** As of June 2024, cases are at 433 with no deaths, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate has decreased, with fewer deaths per number of cases, especially in recent years. <br/>

The data on Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China from mid-2014 to mid-2024 reveals significant fluctuations in the number of cases and deaths, highlighting seasonal and possibly cyclical patterns. Notably, the highest number of cases was reported in November 2017 (2,021) and November 2018 (2,213), while the lowest numbers are observed towards the middle of each year, particularly in months like February and August. The seasonal peaks generally occur towards the end of the year, particularly in November and December, indicating potential seasonal factors influencing the transmission dynamics.

Death rates exhibit similar temporal patterns but with less pronounced peaks, suggesting variability in case fatality rates across different periods. For example, November 2018 recorded the highest number of deaths (33), while many months, particularly in recent years, show fewer deaths even when cases are relatively high, possibly reflecting improvements in medical responses or changes in the virulence of the disease. Overall, while the number of cases has seen periodic spikes, the more recent data indicates a general decline in both cases and deaths, which could be attributed to improved public health measures and disease management strategies.

The peak periods generally occur in late autumn to early winter, as evidenced by consistently high case and death counts during these months across multiple years. For example, November and December often show the highest case and death counts, such as in 2017 and 2018, where deaths peaked at 22 and 33, respectively. This pattern suggests a seasonal factor influencing the spread and severity of the disease, potentially linked to environmental or socio-behavioral factors prevalent during these months.

Throughout the observed years, there is also an indication of fluctuating mortality rates, with some years like 2018 and 2021 showing particularly high death rates compared to other years. The data also reveals instances of low-case months with relatively high mortality, indicating that the disease may have a sporadic yet severe impact on certain populations or under specific conditions. Notably, the months with lower case numbers, such as February across multiple years, generally show lower death counts, but the absolute death numbers still suggest a persistent public health challenge. This detailed analysis underscores the need for targeted public health interventions during peak periods and further research into the seasonal dynamics of EHF transmission and mortality.
