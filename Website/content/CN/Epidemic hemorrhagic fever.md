# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever, also known as hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS), is a zoonotic viral disease primarily caused by hantaviruses. It is transmitted to humans through contact with infected rodent excreta, saliva, or urine. The disease is characterized by sudden onset of fever, chills, headache, abdominal pain, and in severe cases, hemorrhage and renal dysfunction. Endemic in parts of Asia and Europe, particularly China, Korea, and Russia, it poses significant public health concerns. Severity ranges from mild to fatal, and supportive therapy is the mainstay of treatment due to the lack of a specific antiviral cure.

## Highlights

- There has been a significant decrease in the number of reported cases and deaths of epidemic hemorrhagic fever since 2019, with cases reducing from highs of over 1400-2000 to fewer than 400 per month by April 2025. <br/>
- Mortality rates have also decreased significantly, with zero deaths reported from February to April 2025, reflecting improved disease management and control measures. <br/>
- Seasonal spikes in cases observed historically in November and December have reduced considerably in the latest years, indicating better preventive measures. <br/>
- The consistent decrease in cases and deaths over the years highlights an overall positive trend in controlling the epidemic hemorrhagic fever within the Chinese mainland. <br/>

Analysis of the reported Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever (EHF) data from the Chinese mainland over a ten-year period reveals a consistent seasonal trend, with case numbers peaking between October and January each year. This pattern suggests a possible link to environmental or behavioral factors that vary seasonally, such as rodent activity or human exposure related to agricultural cycles or holiday travel. Notably high peaks occurred in November-December between 2017 and 2021, with December 2018 reaching 2,213 cases and a significant mortality spike of 33 deaths. While the fatality rate fluctuated month-to-month, higher case counts in these peaks were often accompanied by increased deaths, indicating a proportional relationship during surges.

From 2022 onward, a substantial decline is observed both in case count and associated deaths, suggesting a shift in either disease dynamics or external interventions such as improved surveillance, public health response, or environmental control measures. The trend continues through 2024 and early 2025 with record low incidence compared to previous years. By contrast, earlier years (2015–2020) exhibited more pronounced seasonality and larger fluctuations, likely reflecting different stages of intervention efficacy or changes in reporting accuracy. Overall, the data paints a picture of gradual containment, though periodic late-year surges still warrant attention.

Between 2015 and 2025, deaths from epidemic hemorrhagic fever in mainland China exhibited significant seasonal and interannual variation that often mirrored case surges, particularly peaking in the winter months of November through January. Notably, the period from late 2017 to 2018 recorded the highest mortality in the dataset, with November and December 2018 accounting for 33 and 17 deaths respectively, aligning with a surge exceeding 2,200 cases per month. Winters consistently had higher fatality counts, suggesting a potential correlation with environmental or behavioral factors influencing exposure risk or access to timely care.

Despite periodic spikes, deaths steadily declined from 2021 onward. By 2023–2025, the monthly death count decreased markedly, with most months reporting one or no fatalities despite continued case incidence, indicating improvements in disease management, early diagnosis, or public health interventions. This downward trend, especially coupled with stable or declining case burdens in recent years, reflects improved control and perhaps enhanced public health responses across high-incidence provinces.
