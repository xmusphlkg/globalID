# Epidemic hemorrhagic fever

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Epidemic hemorrhagic fever (EHF) refers to a group of viral infections characterized by fever, bleeding, and multi-organ dysfunction. It is primarily caused by viruses such as Ebola, Marburg, and Lassa fever. These viruses are typically transmitted to humans through contact with infected animals or bodily fluids of infected individuals. EHF outbreaks can lead to high mortality rates and pose significant public health challenges. Symptoms often include fever, fatigue, dizziness, and bleeding from various body parts. Early diagnosis and supportive care are crucial for improving survival rates during outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend in Cases:** From 2015 to 2025, there is a decline in cases, peaking in 2018 and reducing significantly by 2025, indicating improved control measures. <br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Higher cases occur in colder months (November to January), suggesting a link to environmental factors or human behavior. <br/>

- **Stable Mortality Rate:** Despite case fluctuations, the mortality rate remains low and stable, indicating effective clinical management. <br/>

- **Current Stability:** As of January 2025, cases and deaths are at one of the lowest points, reflecting a stable and controlled situation. <br/>

The data on Epidemic Hemorrhagic Fever (EHF) in the Chinese mainland reveals significant fluctuations in both reported cases and deaths over the years. In 2015, the year began with a high of 1,016 cases in January, followed by a gradual decline until August, where cases dropped to 438. However, a notable resurgence occurred in November with 1,614 cases, indicating potential seasonal patterns or outbreaks. The mortality rate during this year was relatively low, with a total of 66 deaths across the months, suggesting that while the disease was prevalent, the case fatality rate remained manageable.

As the years progressed, particularly from 2017 to 2021, there was a marked increase in both cases and deaths, peaking in December 2017 with 2,105 cases and 22 deaths. This spike may reflect a combination of factors, including environmental conditions, public health responses, and changes in reporting practices. The subsequent years showed a decline in cases, particularly in 2022 and 2023, where the numbers dropped significantly, with some months reporting fewer than 300 cases. This decline could indicate improved public health measures, increased awareness, and better management of the disease, although the sporadic increases in certain months suggest that EHF remains a concern that requires ongoing surveillance and response strategies.

The data on Epidemic hemorrhagic fever in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends in both cases and deaths. Initially, the number of cases and deaths fluctuated significantly, with notable peaks in November and December of each year. For instance, in November 2017, there were 2,021 cases with 12 deaths, and in December 2018, the cases peaked at 2,213 with 33 deaths, indicating a seasonal pattern where the disease tends to surge in the colder months. This pattern suggests that environmental factors or human behaviors during these months might contribute to the increased transmission of the disease.

Over the years, there is a general trend of decreasing cases and deaths, particularly noticeable from 2020 onwards. By 2023 and 2024, the number of cases and deaths has significantly reduced, with some months reporting zero deaths. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, and possibly the development of better treatment protocols or vaccines. The data also shows a reduction in the severity of outbreaks, as seen in the lower death rates relative to the number of cases in the later years. This trend is promising and suggests that the efforts to control the disease are having a positive impact.
