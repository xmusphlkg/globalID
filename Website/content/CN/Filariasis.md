# Filariasis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like nematode worms, primarily transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. The most common forms are lymphatic filariasis, which can lead to severe swelling and disfigurement (elephantiasis), and subcutaneous filariasis, affecting the skin and eyes. The disease is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, affecting millions of people worldwide. Symptoms may include fever, lymphedema, and skin rashes. Prevention focuses on mosquito control and mass drug administration to reduce transmission. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to managing symptoms and preventing long-term complications.

## Highlights

- **Stable Elimination**: Filariasis has been effectively eliminated in the Chinese mainland, with no reported cases or deaths from January 2015 to January 2025, except for a single case in August 2021. <br/>

- **Sporadic Occurrence**: The isolated case in August 2021 suggests a sporadic occurrence, possibly due to importation or a lapse in preventive measures, but it did not lead to further transmission. <br/>

- **Sustained Control Measures**: The absence of cases over a decade indicates successful and sustained control measures, including vector control and public health interventions. <br/>

- **Continued Vigilance Required**: Despite the success, continued surveillance and preventive strategies are essential to prevent re-emergence and maintain the filariasis-free status. <br/>
