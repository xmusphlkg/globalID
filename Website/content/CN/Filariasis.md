# Filariasis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Filariasis is a tropical disease caused by thread-like parasitic worms, primarily transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. The most common forms are lymphatic filariasis, which can lead to severe swelling of the limbs (elephantiasis), and subcutaneous filariasis, affecting the skin and eyes. The disease is prevalent in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific Islands. Symptoms may include fever, lymphedema, and skin rashes. Diagnosis is typically through blood tests, and treatment involves antiparasitic medications like diethylcarbamazine. Preventive measures include mosquito control and mass drug administration in endemic areas to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- **Consistent Zero Cases:** From February 2015 to February 2025, the Chinese mainland reported zero cases of Filariasis, except for a single case in August 2021. This indicates effective control and possible elimination efforts. <br/>
- **No Reported Deaths:** Throughout the entire period, there were no deaths attributed to Filariasis, suggesting successful management of the disease and its complications. <br/>
- **Sporadic Case in 2021:** The isolated case in August 2021 did not lead to further transmission, highlighting the robustness of the surveillance and response systems in place. <br/>
- **Sustained Elimination Status:** The data supports the conclusion that Filariasis remains under control in the region, with no resurgence observed over the decade. <br/>
