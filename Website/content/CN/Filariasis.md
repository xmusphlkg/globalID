# Filariasis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Filariasis is a neglected tropical disease caused by thread-like parasitic worms, primarily transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. The disease predominantly affects people in tropical and subtropical regions, including parts of Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. Lymphatic filariasis is the most common form, characterized by severe swelling and deformities, particularly in the limbs and genitals, a condition known as elephantiasis. Symptoms can also include fever and chronic pain. Diagnosis typically involves microscopic examination of blood samples. Control efforts focus on mass drug administration to interrupt transmission, improved sanitation, and vector control to reduce the incidence of mosquito bites.

## Highlights

- **Stable Elimination:** The data indicates a successful elimination of Filariasis in the Chinese mainland, with zero cases reported consistently from 2014 through July 2024, except for two isolated cases in 2014 and 2021. <br/>
- **Sporadic Incidents:** Only two sporadic cases were recorded over a decade from November 2014 and August 2021, without any associated deaths, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>
- **Sustained Control Measures:** Continuous absence of cases from August 2021 onwards highlights the sustained effectiveness of public health interventions and monitoring systems. <br/>
- **Public Health Assurance:** The data underscores the robust health strategies in place, which have maintained the elimination status of Filariasis for over a decade. <br/>

The data on reported filariasis cases on the Chinese mainland reveals a near-complete elimination of the disease in recent years, with only two recorded cases over the ten-year period from July 2014 to July 2024. The first case occurred in November 2014, and the second case was reported in August 2021. Both instances resulted in zero deaths. Between and after these cases, no further incidences were recorded, showcasing a substantial success in maintaining control over the spread of filariasis within the region.

This trend is an indicator of effective disease management practices, strong public health initiatives, and comprehensive vector control strategies that have been implemented over the years. The significant reduction in case numbers also aligns with global efforts to eliminate filariasis as part of the World Health Organization's (WHO) public health policies. The isolated occurrence of cases suggests continuous surveillance and prompt public health responses might have efficiently addressed these outliers, ensuring that filariasis does not re-establish itself in the Chinese mainland.
