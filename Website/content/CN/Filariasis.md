# Filariasis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like filarial worms, primarily Wuchereria bancrofti, Brugia malayi, and Brugia timori. Transmitted to humans through mosquito bites, filariasis commonly affects lymphatic vessels, leading to lymphatic filariasis. Chronic infection may result in severe swelling (lymphedema), most notably of the limbs, and can progress to elephantiasis. The disease is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, especially in Africa and Asia. Preventive measures include mosquito control and administration of anti-filarial drugs to reduce transmission and morbidity in affected populations.

## Highlights

- Filariasis incidence in Chinese mainland remains extremely low, with only one reported case (August 2021) over the past decade and zero deaths from 2015 to December 2025.<br/>
- Sustained absence of new cases since 2021 suggests effective control measures and minimal transmission of the disease.<br/>
- The data demonstrates continued success in Filariasis elimination efforts and strong public health infrastructure.<br/>
- As of December 2025, Filariasis is not considered a significant public health concern in Chinese mainland, although ongoing surveillance is essential to prevent re-emergence and promptly detect any imported or sporadic cases.<br/>

Over the course of a decade, monthly surveillance data for filariasis reported from mainland China revealed an absence of incident cases, except for a single case recorded in August 2021. This unprecedented consistency over approximately 120 months, encompassing both pre-pandemic and intra-pandemic periods, suggests the sustained interruption of transmission and near-total elimination of the disease in the population. The solitary case in 2021 did not result in mortality and did not trigger subsequent case clusters, indicating that it may represent an imported case or a rare sporadic reactivation, rather than reestablishment of endemic transmission.

The stability of the data across several years, including periods of heightened surveillance and changing health system priorities, demonstrates the robustness of control and detection measures. No seasonal pattern, outbreak signals, or mortality burden is observed, and the data is compatible with successful prior elimination strategies that have been implemented and rigorously monitored. The absence of further incidences post-2021 further supports the maintenance of elimination status, with minimal risk of resurgence under current conditions.
