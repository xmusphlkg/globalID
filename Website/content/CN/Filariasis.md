# Filariasis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like worms, primarily *Wuchereria bancrofti*, *Brugia malayi*, and *Brugia timori*. The disease is transmitted through the bite of infected mosquitoes. Infected individuals may experience symptoms like fever, lymphatic swelling, and in severe cases, elephantiasis, where the limbs or genitals become enlarged and deformed. Filariasis is endemic in tropical and subtropical regions, with the highest prevalence in parts of Africa, Asia, and the Pacific Islands. Preventive measures include mosquito control and mass drug administration to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- **Filariasis cases have sharply declined**: After reporting one case in November 2014, the incidence of Filariasis has been zero for over nine years, from 2015 to November 2024. <br/>
- **No deaths reported**: There have been no recorded deaths due to Filariasis in the entire period from 2014 to November 2024. <br/>
- **Effective control measures**: The consistent zero cases since 2015 suggest successful public health interventions, including vector control and mass drug administration. <br/>
- **Ongoing vigilance needed**: Although no cases are reported, continued monitoring is essential to prevent re-emergence and ensure sustained disease elimination efforts. <br/>

The reported data for filariasis in mainland China shows a notable pattern of minimal case incidence over the past decade. From 2014 to 2024, the only significant event was the single case recorded in November 2014. This data suggests a major reduction in the disease burden, likely due to successful public health interventions, including mass drug administration (MDA), vector control efforts, and improved sanitation and health education. Following 2014, there were no further reported cases or deaths, with the disease remaining absent from the records in subsequent years up until the present.

The absence of cases for such an extended period may indicate that the country has effectively suppressed the transmission of filariasis in its endemic regions. However, the occurrence of even a single case in 2014 warrants attention, as it could reflect isolated outbreaks or potential reintroduction through migration or environmental changes. Nonetheless, the data suggests that the public health system in China has managed to control filariasis transmission effectively, and the current zero-case trend supports the possibility that the country may be nearing the goal of eliminating filariasis as a public health problem. The absence of deaths further reinforces the success of management and surveillance programs in minimizing both morbidity and mortality associated with the disease.
