# Filariasis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like nematode worms, primarily Wuchereria bancrofti, Brugia malayi, and Brugia timori, spread by mosquitoes. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, affecting millions worldwide. The infection is often asymptomatic, but chronic cases can lead to severe lymphatic damage, causing lymphedema and elephantiasis, characterized by swollen limbs and genitals. Filariasis is a major cause of permanent disability and social stigma. Control efforts focus on mass drug administration, vector control, and promoting hygiene to disrupt transmission and prevent new infections. Global health initiatives aim to eliminate filariasis as a public health problem.

## Highlights

- Filariasis cases have been virtually absent on the Chinese mainland, with only one recorded case in August 2021. This indicates successful elimination of the disease over the decade. <br/>

- There has been no reported mortality related to Filariasis from 2015 until September 2025, highlighting effective control measures and healthcare responses.<br/>

- Sustained zero-case reports from September 2015 to September 2025 suggest stable disease-free status, likely due to continued vector control initiatives and mass drug administration strategies.<br/>

- The absence of cases since 2021 aligns with China's health targets in eradicating neglected tropical diseases, reflecting comprehensive public health policies and monitoring systems.<br/>

The data for filariasis cases in mainland China from September 2015 to September 2025 indicates a remarkable absence of reports throughout almost the entirety of this timeframe, with a singular exception in August 2021. This suggests that filariasis has been effectively controlled or eradicated as a public health concern during these years. The zero reported deaths across this span further corroborates the idea that filariasis has not posed a significant threat to mortality in these regions during this period. The reported case in August 2021 could potentially represent an isolated or imported incident rather than indicative of local endemic transmission, given the subsequent months show no further cases or deaths.

This sustained period of zero cases and deaths might reflect successful public health initiatives and preventative measures implemented in previous years, leading to the interruption of transmission cycles within human populations. Public health strategies, including vector control, mass drug administration, and ongoing surveillance, could have played pivotal roles in achieving this level of control. Moreover, the data might underscore the importance of maintaining ongoing monitoring and response plans to prevent any resurgence, especially through vigilance concerning importation risks or emerging vector populations that could revive transmission cycles. Overall, these data paints an optimistic picture of filariasis management effectiveness in the Chinese mainland over these years.
