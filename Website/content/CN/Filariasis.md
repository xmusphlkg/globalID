# Filariasis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like filarial worms transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. The primary species causing human infection are Wuchereria bancrofti, Brugia malayi, and Brugia timori. These worms inhabit the lymphatic system, leading to a condition known as lymphatic filariasis, which can cause severe swelling, commonly known as elephantiasis. It primarily affects populations in tropical and subtropical regions. Diagnosis is made through blood tests, while treatment involves antiparasitic medications like diethylcarbamazine. Prevention focuses on controlling mosquito populations and implementing mass drug administration programs to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- Filariasis cases have been consistently low in mainland China since 2014, with only two reported cases in the past decade (November 2014 and August 2021).<br/>
- No deaths from Filariasis have been reported throughout this period, indicating effective control measures.<br/>
- Since August 2021, there have been no new cases of Filariasis, reflecting ongoing efforts to maintain the elimination of this parasitic disease.<br/>
- The trend suggests that mainland China has sustained Filariasis elimination, with no significant resurgence as of September 2024.<br/>

The data on Filariasis cases in mainland China from September 2014 to September 2024 shows a remarkably low incidence, with only two cases reported over the 10-year span. These occurred in November 2014 and August 2021, with no associated deaths. The data reveals no sustained transmission, as there were no consecutive months with cases, suggesting that Filariasis has largely been controlled or eliminated in the region. The absence of deaths further supports effective case management, with no reported fatalities related to the disease during this period.

Given the long intervals between the two isolated cases, it can be inferred that any remaining cases are sporadic, likely imported rather than domestically transmitted. The sustained absence of new cases over multiple years is a strong indicator of the success of public health interventions, possibly stemming from vector control programs, disease surveillance, and targeted treatment strategies. This highlights China’s capacity to maintain control over Filariasis, with no apparent resurgence of the disease.
