# Filariasis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Filariasis is a tropical, parasitic disease caused by thread-like filarial worms transmitted to humans through the bite of an infected mosquito. The disease primarily occurs in Africa, Asia, the Western Pacific, and parts of the Americas. Lymphatic filariasis, the most common form, affects the lymphatic system, leading to severe swelling, disability, and stigma. Symptoms include lymphedema, elephantiasis, and hydrocele. Control strategies focus on mass drug administration, vector control, and patient care. Preventative measures include mosquito nets and vector management. Global efforts aim to eliminate it as a public health problem through coordinated mass treatment campaigns.

## Highlights

- Filariasis cases in the Chinese mainland remain extremely low, with no new reported cases from January 2022 to August 2024. <br/>
- The last recorded cases occurred in November 2014 and August 2021, each with only one case and no deaths, indicating effective disease control measures. <br/>
- Over the past decade, the pattern shows sporadic and isolated cases rather than an enduring outbreak, highlighting successful management and possible elimination efforts. <br/>
- The absence of deaths throughout this period emphasizes the effectiveness of treatment strategies and public health interventions against Filariasis in the region. <br/>
