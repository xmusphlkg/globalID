# Filariasis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Filariasis is a tropical parasitic disease caused by thread-like filarial worms transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. Predominantly occurring in tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, Africa, and the Pacific Islands, the disease is characterized by chronic symptoms, most notably lymphatic filariasis, which can lead to lymphedema and elephantiasis. The adult worms inhabit the human lymphatic system, disrupting its function and causing significant morbidity. Diagnosis typically involves the detection of microfilariae in blood samples, and treatment includes antiparasitic drugs like diethylcarbamazine and ivermectin. Prevention strategies focus on vector control and mass drug administration campaigns to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- Filariasis cases in the Chinese mainland have been consistently negligible, with no recorded cases or deaths from 2015 to October 2025, except for a single case in August 2021. <br/>
- The disease appears to be effectively controlled or eradicated, reflecting successful public health interventions over the years. <br/>
- Surveillance and preventive measures remain crucial to maintain this status and prevent potential re-emergence, particularly given global travel dynamics. <br/>
- Continuous monitoring and public health awareness are essential to sustain the current situation, ensuring quick response to any new potential cases. <br/>
