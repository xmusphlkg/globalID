# Filariasis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Filariasis is a tropical disease caused by thread-like parasitic worms, primarily transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. The most common forms are lymphatic filariasis, which can lead to severe swelling of the limbs and genitals (elephantiasis), and subcutaneous filariasis, affecting the skin and eyes. The disease is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, Africa, the Western Pacific, and parts of the Americas. Symptoms may include fever, lymphedema, and skin rashes. Prevention focuses on mosquito control and mass drug administration to reduce transmission and alleviate symptoms in affected populations.

## Highlights

- **Consistent Zero Cases:** From December 2014 to December 2024, the Chinese mainland reported zero cases of Filariasis, except for a single case in August 2021. This indicates effective control and possible elimination of the disease. <br/>
- **No Reported Deaths:** Throughout the entire period, there were no deaths attributed to Filariasis, suggesting successful management of the disease and its complications. <br/>
- **Sporadic Occurrence:** The isolated case in August 2021 suggests that while the disease is largely controlled, vigilance is necessary to prevent re-emergence. <br/>
- **Sustained Elimination Efforts:** The data reflects sustained public health efforts and possibly effective interventions that have kept Filariasis at bay in the region. <br/>
