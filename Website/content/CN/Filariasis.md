# Filariasis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like nematode worms transmitted to humans through the bites of infected mosquitoes. The most common forms are lymphatic filariasis, caused by *Wuchereria bancrofti*, *Brugia malayi*, or *Brugia timori*. These parasites target the lymphatic system, potentially leading to severe swelling, commonly known as elephantiasis. Symptoms may include fever, lymph node enlargement, and chronic lymphedema. The disease is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in parts of Asia, Africa, the Pacific, and the Americas. Mass drug administration and vector control are key strategies for prevention and elimination.

## Highlights

1. Filariasis cases in mainland China have remained remarkably low, with only one reported case in August 2021, indicating successful control measures over the years. <br/>
2. There have been zero reported deaths due to Filariasis from 2015 through to April 2025, demonstrating effective treatment and management practices. <br/>
3. Consistent zero-case reports since 2021 suggest that transmission rates are well-contained, potentially due to effective vector control and surveillance strategies. <br/>
4. As of April 2025, the Filariasis situation in mainland China reflects successful eradication efforts, with no new cases or deaths reported, signifying a stable and controlled epidemiological environment.<br/>

The data on filariasis cases in mainland China from April 2015 to April 2025 reflects an exceptionally low incidence, with only a single case reported, in August 2021, and no associated deaths noted throughout this entire period. This sustained absence of new cases over an extensive timeframe suggests a significant level of control over the transmission of filariasis within the region. Such outcomes might be attributed to effective public health interventions, consistent monitoring, and management efforts, including vector control strategies and potential mass drug administration programs, which have historically been utilized in the region to combat filariasis.

This near-zero incidence aligns with China's historical efforts towards eliminating the disease, as reflected by WHO verification of filariasis elimination in the country in 2007. The sporadic case reported in 2021 could represent an isolated incident potentially linked to either imported infection or failure in disease surveillance. Nonetheless, the isolated nature of this case and the lack of subsequent cases indicate robust public health infrastructure capable of preventing any further transmission. This comprehensive and sustained control of filariasis highlights the success of past initiatives and illustrates the importance of maintaining vigilant surveillance systems and continuous health education to mitigate risks of re-emergence.
