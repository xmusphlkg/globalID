# Filariasis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Filariasis is a parasitic disease caused by thread-like filarial worms, transmitted to humans through mosquito bites. The disease is primarily prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions. The worms target the lymphatic system, causing lymphedema, elephantiasis, and hydrocele. Symptoms can include severe swelling of limbs, genitals, and other body parts. The disease progresses slowly, often over several years, leading to chronic and debilitating conditions. Control efforts focus on mass drug administration (MDA) programs, vector control, and improved hygiene practices to reduce transmission and infection rates.

## Highlights

- Filariasis cases in the Chinese mainland have been extremely rare, with only two reported cases between June 2014 and June 2024. <br/>
- The last recorded cases were in November 2014 and August 2021, indicating sporadic occurrences. <br/>
- No deaths have been reported due to Filariasis during this period, suggesting effective management of the disease when it occurs. <br/>
- The data indicates that Filariasis is not currently a significant public health concern in the Chinese mainland as of June 2024. <br/>

The reported data for Filariasis in the Chinese mainland from June 2014 to June 2024 shows a strikingly low incidence of cases, with only two recorded cases throughout the entire period. These cases were reported in November 2014 and August 2021, respectively. The data highlights that there were no deaths attributed to Filariasis during this time frame. This trend suggests an effective control and possibly near-elimination of Filariasis in the region, given the rarity of reported cases and absence of fatalities.

The consistent absence of new cases across multiple years, especially between the years 2015 to 2020 and again from September 2021 to June 2024, underscores the sustained success in managing the disease. The isolated nature of the two cases, separated by nearly seven years, indicates that any outbreaks were promptly contained, preventing further transmission. This pattern reflects the effectiveness of public health measures and surveillance systems in place, which have evidently maintained low transmission rates and protected the population from this parasitic disease.
