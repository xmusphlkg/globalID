# Gonorrhea

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It commonly infects the mucous membranes of the reproductive tract, including the cervix, uterus, and fallopian tubes in women, and the urethra in both men and women. It can also affect the throat, eyes, and rectum. Symptoms may include painful urination, abnormal discharge, and genital pain, although some individuals may be asymptomatic. If left untreated, gonorrhea can lead to severe health issues such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women and epididymitis in men, both potentially causing infertility.

## Highlights

- There is a notable increase in gonorrhea cases from 2014 to 2017, peaking at 13,803 cases in August 2017, indicating rising transmission rates.<br/>
- After 2017, case counts fluctuate, with a dip in early 2023, showing a decrease in cases but a tendency to rise again toward late 2023 and early 2024.<br/>
- Deaths remain rare, with periodic instances throughout the data yet consistently staying low, suggesting effective treatment options.<br/>
- By August 2024, the number of cases is lower than the peak years, indicating potential improvements in disease control or variability in reporting and transmission dynamics.<br/>

The data on gonorrhea cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals several trends in case numbers, showing fluctuations over time with peaks in certain months and years. In general, the number of cases saw a steady increase from 2014 to 2017, peaking in August 2017 with 13,803 cases. This upward trend in gonorrhea incidence can be observed again in August 2020 with 11,643 cases, and more consistently from 2021 onward. The highest annual peaks tend to occur in the summer months, especially July and August, suggesting a seasonal pattern to case reporting or transmission.

Deaths related to gonorrhea have been minimal, with a majority of months reporting zero fatalities. However, there were isolated instances where deaths were recorded, including in December 2014, December 2015, April 2016, and sporadically across subsequent years, with a notable spike in January 2020 (four deaths). These rare occurrences of death suggest that while the disease is typically non-lethal, complications or comorbidities may have contributed to these outcomes in specific cases.
