# Gonorrhea

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the urethra, cervix, rectum, throat, and eyes. Common symptoms include painful urination, abnormal discharge from the genitals, and, in women, pelvic pain. However, many infected individuals may be asymptomatic. Untreated gonorrhea can lead to serious health complications, including infertility and increased risk of HIV transmission. Diagnosis is typically confirmed through laboratory testing of bodily fluids. Treatment involves antibiotics, though rising antibiotic resistance is a growing concern in managing the infection.

## Highlights

- **Steady Increase in Cases:** From 2014 to 2017, there was a noticeable increase in gonorrhea cases, peaking in 2017 with monthly cases often exceeding 12,000. <br/>
- **Fluctuations and Decline:** After 2017, the number of cases fluctuated but showed a general decline, particularly noticeable from 2022 onwards, with a significant drop in 2023. <br/>
- **Mortality Trends:** Deaths remained low throughout the period, with occasional spikes, such as in January 2020 and 2021, indicating sporadic increases in severity or reporting. <br/>
- **Recent Stabilization:** As of December 2024, the number of cases appears to have stabilized around 9,000 to 10,000 per month, suggesting a potential plateau in the disease's spread. <br/>

This rise could be attributed to various factors, including increased transmission rates, improved reporting and diagnosis, or changes in public health policies and awareness.

From 2019 onwards, the data shows a slight decline in cases, particularly noticeable in 2020, which could be influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic and its impact on healthcare systems and public behavior. The number of deaths reported is minimal, with only sporadic occurrences, indicating that while gonorrhea remains a significant public health concern, it is not a major cause of mortality. The data on gonorrhea cases and deaths in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals a consistent pattern of low mortality despite fluctuating case numbers. However, the number of deaths remained remarkably low, with only sporadic occurrences. This suggests that while gonorrhea is prevalent, it is not a significant cause of mortality, likely due to effective treatment options and public health interventions.

The sporadic nature of the deaths, with only a few months reporting any fatalities, indicates that these may be outliers or related to specific complications or comorbidities rather than the infection itself. The data underscores the importance of continued surveillance and public health efforts to manage gonorrhea, focusing on prevention, early detection, and treatment to maintain low mortality rates. The low death rate also highlights the effectiveness of current medical treatments and the healthcare system's ability to manage this infection.
