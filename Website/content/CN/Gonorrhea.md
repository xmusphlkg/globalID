# Gonorrhea

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the urogenital tract, but can also infect the throat, eyes, and rectum. Transmission occurs through unprotected sexual contact with an infected person. Symptoms in men may include painful urination and discharge from the penis, whereas women often experience mild or no symptoms, posing challenges for prompt diagnosis. Untreated gonorrhea can lead to severe health complications, such as infertility and increased risk of acquiring or transmitting HIV. Antibiotic resistance poses a growing challenge in treatment.

## Highlights

- **Overall Decline in Cases**: Monthly cases of gonorrhea have generally decreased since 2022, with the lowest in early 2023. This suggests some control measures may be effective. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variation**: Consistently lower case numbers are observed in the early months of each year, likely due to seasonal or behavioral factors. <br/>

- **Stable Mortality Rates**: The number of deaths from gonorrhea remains extremely low, indicating successful management of complications. <br/>

- **Recent Fluctuations**: 2024 shows minor fluctuations in cases, with slight increases from mid-year but still low compared to pre-2020 levels. <br/>

The data for Gonorrhea cases in mainland China from September 2014 to September 2024 reveals several key trends and fluctuations. Beginning in 2014, the cases showed a steady pattern with minor fluctuations, notably dropping in February each year, likely due to reporting or seasonal factors such as the Chinese New Year. From 2016 onward, there was an observable upward trend, with cases peaking in 2017 and 2018, indicating a potential growth in transmission or detection rates. Several years exhibited monthly surges, with August 2017 peaking at 13,803 cases, one of the highest during the period.

The year 2020 displayed a notable decrease in reported cases during the initial months, corresponding with the COVID-19 pandemic’s onset, which could have impacted healthcare access or reporting practices. Cases continued to rise again in the following months, maintaining higher numbers through to 2021. However, 2022 showed a downward trend, which continued into 2023 and 2024, potentially reflecting increased public health interventions or changes in population behaviors. Deaths remained strikingly low throughout the period, with scattered appearances mainly in December months, suggesting that while transmission was common, mortality was rare or underreported, possibly due to effective treatment.
