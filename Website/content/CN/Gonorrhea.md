# Gonorrhea

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It primarily affects warm, moist areas of the body such as the urethra, throat, eyes, and female reproductive tract. Common symptoms include a burning sensation during urination and discharge, though it can be asymptomatic, especially in women. Untreated gonorrhea can lead to severe health complications, including infertility and increased susceptibility to HIV. Diagnosis is typically confirmed through nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs). Treatment involves antibiotics, but rising antibiotic resistance poses a significant public health challenge, necessitating ongoing research and surveillance efforts.

## Highlights

- The incidence of gonorrhea cases in mainland China has shown a fluctuating trend from 2015 to 2025, with a notable decrease in cases from 2019 to early 2023, before rebounding through 2025.<br/> 
- The highest recorded incidence was in 2017 and early 2018, with monthly cases exceeding 13,000, while the lowest occurred in early 2023 with cases dropping to 4,762 in January.<br/>
- Deaths related to gonorrhea remain sparse over the years, with most months recording zero deaths; occasional deaths appear sporadically across the dataset.<br/>
- Recent trends indicate a resurgence in monthly cases nearing 11,000 by mid-2025, pointing to a possible need for further public health interventions.<br/>

The reported cases of gonorrhea in mainland China exhibit periodic fluctuations over the years, with a noticeable increase from 2015 to 2017. The number of cases rose from 8,621 in October 2015 to a peak of 13,803 in August 2017, reflecting a significant upward trend in gonorrhea incidence. Post this period, the data reveals a mixed pattern with cases generally fluctuating between 10,000 to 13,000 per month until around early 2020. Notably, there was a sharp decline during the early months of 2020, likely reflecting the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic and the related public health measures, which may have restricted access to healthcare services or altered reporting practices.

Post-2020, the case counts began to recover to pre-2020 levels, stabilizing around 9,000 to 12,000 in recent months. While the data suggests some reduction and stabilization in later years, a distinct seasonal trend with lower case numbers in the early months of the year can be observed, potentially tied to New Year celebrations and subsequent disruptions in data reporting or healthcare access. Deaths attributed to gonorrhea remain minimal throughout the period, with only sporadic occurrences reported, indicating improvements in the management of the disease and its complications.

The reported data on gonorrhea cases and deaths in mainland China from October 2015 to October 2025 reveals certain trends that shed light on the disease's impact and severity in terms of mortality over a decade. Throughout the reported time span, the number of gonorrhea cases has seen fluctuations with a general increasing trend peaking in the summer months. Notably, this data reveals a discernible seasonal pattern, as case numbers tend to be lower in the early months of each year, potentially influenced by factors such as climate, social activities, or healthcare accessibility during winter periods.

Despite the upward trend in reported cases, the occurrence of deaths related to gonorrhea remains consistently low. With deaths being intermittent and rarely exceeding one per month, it indicates that while gonorrhea is prevalent, it seldom reaches a fatal severity in mainland China. These sporadic deaths could be attributed to complications arising from untreated infections or co-morbidity with other health conditions. Furthermore, the relatively low death count suggests that the healthcare system might be effective in managing the disease's complications, or it reflects the disease's inherent low lethality when treated appropriately. The notable spikes in deaths, such as in January 2020 with four deaths, prompt further investigation into possible external factors or healthcare constraints impacting mortality rates during specific periods.
