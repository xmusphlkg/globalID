# Gonorrhea

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the urogenital tract, but can also infect the throat, eyes, and rectum. Transmitted through unprotected sexual contact, symptoms in men include painful urination and pus-like discharge, while women may experience increased vaginal discharge and pelvic pain, though many remain asymptomatic. If untreated, gonorrhea can lead to severe health complications, including infertility and increased risk of HIV infection. Diagnosis typically involves nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs), and treatment usually involves antibiotics, although antibiotic resistance is of growing concern.

## Highlights

- Over the years, gonorrhea cases have shown a general upward trend, peaking in 2017 with a monthly average surpassing 12,000 cases, before declining slightly. <br/>
- As of July 2024, the monthly cases have stabilized around 8,000-10,000, indicating a controlled yet persistent presence of the disease. <br/>
- Mortality associated with gonorrhea remains extremely low, with sporadic cases, highlighting the disease's non-lethal nature. <br/>
- Notable seasonal variations exist, with higher cases during summer months, potentially linked to increased social activities. <br/>

The data on gonorrhea cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 show a rising trend with seasonal fluctuations. Initially, from mid-2014, cases ranged from roughly 5,912 in February 2015 to 10,046 by December 2015. February consistently reports lower cases, likely due to reduced reporting during the Chinese New Year. The upward trend becomes evident from 2016, with cases peaking during mid-year months like July and August, reaching over 13,000 by 2017. This increase appears to stabilize by 2019, with monthly cases around 10,000, still reflecting a consistent February dip.

With the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020, a significant drop is noted, reaching the lowest in February 2020, possibly due to disruptions in healthcare access and reporting. However, by mid to late 2020, cases rebounded, peaking in late summer and early autumn. Throughout, deaths related to gonorrhea remain minimal, usually zero or isolated incidents, indicating effective management despite rising cases. By 2023-2024, case numbers stabilize near 10,000, though the seasonal pattern persists. This data suggests an overall increase in reported cases with temporary pandemic-related disruptions but generally effective clinical handling given the low death rate.
