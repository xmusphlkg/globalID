# Gonorrhea

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*. It primarily affects mucous membranes of the reproductive tract, but can also infect the rectum, throat, and eyes. Transmission occurs through vaginal, anal, or oral sex with an infected person. Many individuals may be asymptomatic, particularly women, which can delay diagnosis and increase the risk of complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease or infertility. When symptoms do occur, they may include painful urination, abnormal discharge, or genital discomfort. Gonorrhea is treatable with antibiotics, though resistance is an increasing concern.

## Highlights

- Gonorrhea cases in mainland China increased steadily from 2015 to 2018, peaking at over 13,000 monthly cases in 2017, before showing gradual fluctuations through 2025.<br/>
- A notable drop occurred in early 2020, likely linked to COVID-19 disruptions, with cases falling to 3,524 in February 2020 before rebounding later that year.<br/>
- Since 2022, case counts have stabilized between 6,000 and 11,000 monthly, with April 2025 reporting 8,975 cases and no deaths.<br/>
- Deaths from gonorrhea remain extremely rare, with only sporadic fatal cases (mostly single monthly deaths) seen during the ten-year period.<br/>

Between April 2015 and April 2025, reported gonorrhea cases in mainland China showed a consistent upward trend from 2015 through 2018, peaking around mid-2018, followed by stabilization and mild fluctuations in subsequent years. From the initial monthly cases around 7,600–8,500 in 2015, the figures increased steadily to over 13,800 by August 2017. This period of acceleration suggests rising transmission or enhanced detection owing to improved diagnostics, increased screening, or both. Post-2018, monthly counts hovered mostly between 10,000 and 12,500 cases, suggesting a possible plateau. Seasonality remained evident across years, with recurring dips during February, coinciding with the Spring Festival and healthcare access disruption patterns.

The most pronounced deviation came in early 2020, where a significant drop began in February (3,524 cases), coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 outbreak and strict public health controls across China. This disruption continued through much of 2020 but was followed by gradual recovery to prior levels by late 2020 and onward. Total deaths remained minimal throughout the entire period—sporadic and mostly isolated (never exceeding four in a single month). The persistently low mortality aligns with the disease's clinical profile but underscores ongoing transmission risks. The long-term epidemiological profile suggests that while fatality remains negligible, gonorrhea continues to pose a notable and persistent burden of morbidity across the population.

From 2015 to April 2025, Gonorrhea deaths in mainland China have remained extraordinarily rare despite steadily rising case numbers during the mid-to-late 2010s. Out of over 1.1 million reported cases, only 17 deaths occurred, reflecting an extremely low case fatality rate. Deaths did not correlate strongly with incidence peaks; for instance, 2017–2019 saw monthly cases rise above 12,000 yet registered only occasional single-month deaths. The year 2020 saw the highest annual death toll (6), notably during the early pandemic months, which may suggest indirect effects such as delayed access to care or reporting disruptions rather than increased virulence.

Most deaths appear sporadic with no seasonal clustering, though isolated years (like 2022) exhibited deaths across multiple non-contiguous months. From 2023 onward through early 2025, there have been virtually no reported gonorrhea-related fatalities, even with relatively high case volumes. This pattern points to improvements in diagnosis, timely treatment, and possibly the increased use of newer, more effective antibiotic regimens within national STD control programs. The consistency and persistence of near-zero mortality underscore gonorrhea’s low lethality when access to appropriate clinical management is maintained.
