# Gonorrhea

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*. It commonly affects the urethra, rectum, throat, and cervix. Transmission occurs through vaginal, anal, or oral sex with an infected person. Many cases are asymptomatic, but symptoms can include painful urination, genital discharge, and pelvic pain. If left untreated, gonorrhea can lead to serious health complications such as infertility, pelvic inflammatory disease, and increased susceptibility to HIV. It is diagnosed through laboratory testing and typically treated with antibiotics. Rising antimicrobial resistance is a growing concern in managing gonorrhea effectively.

## Highlights

- **Rising and fluctuating trend:** Gonorrhea cases in Chinese mainland rose steadily from 2015 to 2019, peaking in summer months, then declined after 2020 with seasonal variability. <br/>

- **Low mortality:** Deaths remain rare throughout the decade, with most months reporting zero fatalities; occasional deaths occurred sporadically. <br/>

- **Recent situation:** As of May 2025, 9,533 cases were reported, marking a modest increase from earlier 2025 months. <br/>

- **Public health outlook:** Though overall incidence has declined since the 2017–2019 peak, the recent uptick warrants continued surveillance and preventive efforts. <br/>

The reported gonorrhea data from mainland China (May 2015 to May 2025) show fluctuations with an overall increasing trend in case numbers up to 2019. A seasonal pattern emerges with February typically showing reduced cases, likely due to decreased healthcare access during the Lunar New Year, such as 6399 cases in February 2016 compared to higher counts in following months like October's 10791. Over time, annual peaks generally occur during warmer months like July and August, with August 2017 recording 13803 cases.

Deaths are infrequent but sporadically reported, mostly at year-end or early months, such as December 2015 and April 2016. The inconsistency between spikes in case numbers and deaths suggest improved clinical management or varying health system capacities. Post-2020, the pandemic's onset sees a decline in cases, reflecting possible impacts of health interventions or behavior changes. This is followed by stabilization and a gradual decline towards 2025, possibly due to public health measures or changes in reporting.

The data indicate a complex interplay between seasonal trends, health system responses, and external factors like COVID-19, highlighting the ongoing challenges in controlling gonorrhea amidst varying societal behaviors and healthcare access in China. This underscores the importance of adaptive health strategies to efficiently manage fluctuations in disease incidence.

From May 2015 to May 2025, national surveillance data for gonorrhea in mainland China recorded a total of **15 deaths** amidst over **1.17 million reported cases**. The distribution of gonorrhea-related deaths was sporadic and displayed no consistent seasonal or annual pattern, appearing as isolated events in otherwise low-fatality periods. Notably, the year 2020 showed an aberrant spike with **6 deaths**—the highest single-year count across the decade—with a concentration in January, suggesting an unusual circumstance that may merit further investigation for diagnostic or reporting shifts, concurrent health burdens, or data misclassification. All other years exhibited between 0 to 2 deaths, with long stretches of zero mortality even during periods of high incidence.

The overall case-fatality rate remained extremely low—approximately **0.0013%**, indicating that while gonorrhea was widely transmitted, it rarely resulted in fatal outcomes in this population. Interpretatively, these outcomes align with global understanding of gonorrhea as a typically non-lethal, though clinically significant, sexually transmitted infection; however, under-reporting or misattribution cannot be ruled out. The occurrence of deaths may reflect severe complications such as disseminated infections, co-infections, untreated disease, or compromised host factors, rather than the inherent virulence of *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*.
