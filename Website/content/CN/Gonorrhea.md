# Gonorrhea

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Gonorrhea is a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the urethra, cervix, rectum, throat, and eyes. Transmission occurs through sexual contact with an infected person. Symptoms may include painful urination, abnormal discharge, and, in women, pelvic pain. However, many individuals may be asymptomatic. Untreated gonorrhea can lead to serious health complications, including infertility and increased risk of HIV. Diagnosis is typically made through laboratory testing, and treatment involves antibiotics, though antibiotic resistance is an increasing concern.

## Highlights

- **Steady Increase in Cases (2015-2019):** Gonorrhea cases in the Chinese mainland showed a steady increase from 2015 to 2019, peaking in 2017 with monthly cases often exceeding 12,000. <br/>

- **Fluctuations and Decline (2020-2023):** The number of cases fluctuated significantly from 2020 onwards, with a notable decline in 2022 and 2023, where monthly cases dropped below 10,000. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite the high number of cases, the mortality rate remains extremely low, with only sporadic deaths reported over the years. <br/>

- **Recent Trends (2024-2025):** As of January 2025, the number of cases has slightly decreased compared to previous years, indicating a potential stabilization or decline in the disease's prevalence. <br/>

The data indicates a significant rise in cases from 2015 to 2017, with a peak of 13,803 in August 2017. However, the subsequent years saw a decline in cases, particularly in 2020, where the numbers dropped sharply, with January reporting only 8,254 cases.

The mortality data remains consistently low, with only a few reported deaths throughout the years, suggesting that while Gonorrhea cases are prevalent, they are not leading to significant mortality. The sporadic spikes in cases could be attributed to various factors, including increased testing, changes in sexual behavior, or public health interventions.
