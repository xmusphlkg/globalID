# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral illness primarily affecting children aged under 5 years, though it can also occur in adults. Caused by enteroviruses, most notably the coxsackievirus, the disease spreads through close personal contact, respiratory droplets, and contaminated surfaces. HFMD is characterized by fever, sore throat, and a rash involving red spots and sometimes blisters on the hands, feet, and inside the mouth. While usually mild and self-limiting, lasting about 7 to 10 days, complications can arise in rare cases. Good hygiene practices are crucial for prevention and control.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Hand, foot, and mouth disease exhibits seasonal peaks from April to October, with significant highs in 2023 and 2025, indicating persistent annual resurgence. <br/>

- **Zero Deaths Since Late 2020:** Since late 2020, no deaths have been reported, suggesting effective health interventions or improved healthcare responses, reducing severe outcomes despite fluctuating cases. <br/>

- **Post-Pandemic Resurgence:** Following a case dip in 2020, likely due to COVID-19 measures, there was a resurgence in cases, peaking in summer months annually. <br/>

- **2025 October Surge:** October 2025 saw a sharp spike in cases, aligning with historical seasonal trends but underscoring the need to assess new or returning factors. <br/>

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals significant trends in the number of cases and fatalities. From 2015 to 2019, cases show a clear seasonal pattern, peaking between May and July and then declining towards the end of each year. Deaths remain low relative to the number of cases, with the highest fatalities occurring during these peak months, suggesting a potential link between increased transmission and severe cases.

In 2020, there is a sharp decline in reported cases, coinciding with the global COVID-19 pandemic. This drop likely results from heightened public health measures like improved hygiene, social distancing, and restrictions that inadvertently reduced HFMD transmission. As conditions stabilized post-2020, HFMD cases began to rise again, peaking significantly in 2023. The resurgence likely follows the relaxation of pandemic measures, leading to increased human interaction.

Interestingly, fatalities drastically decreased after 2019 and remain close to zero by 2025, indicating possible advancements in clinical management or a reduction in the severity of viral infections. From 2024 onward, the data suggests case stabilization, with less pronounced seasonal peaks compared to earlier years. The continued absence of deaths might reflect ongoing improvements in healthcare responses and preventive strategies.
