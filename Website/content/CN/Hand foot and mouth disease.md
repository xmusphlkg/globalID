# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a common, contagious viral infection primarily affecting young children. It is caused by various enteroviruses, most notably the Coxsackievirus A16 and Enterovirus 71. HFMD is characterized by fever, sore throat, and a distinct rash with painful sores typically found in the mouth, and small blisters on the hands and feet. The disease is usually mild, and symptoms resolve within seven to ten days. Transmission occurs through close contact with nose and throat secretions, blister fluid, and feces. Good hygiene practices are crucial in preventing the spread of HFMD.

## Highlights

- **Recent Surge in Cases**: Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) saw a significant resurgence in 2023, peaking in July with 457,212 cases, the highest since 2016. This resurgence continued into 2024, albeit at a lower rate, with 170,723 cases in July. <br/>
- **Notable Decline in Mortality**: Despite the increase in cases, there have been no reported deaths since March 2023, indicating potential improvements in healthcare or disease management. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends**: HFMD exhibits strong seasonal variation, with case numbers consistently peaking between May and July each year, followed by a gradual decline towards the end of the year. <br/>

Analyzing the monthly data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from July 2014 to July 2024 reveals notable seasonal patterns, with cases peaking consistently between April and July. From 2014 to 2019, cases frequently exceeded 300,000 during peak months, highlighting high transmission rates in warmer months, likely due to conducive climatic conditions and increased contact among children. However, deaths steadily decreased over this period, reflecting improvements in diagnosis and clinical management.

A significant drop in cases is observed in 2020, coinciding with COVID-19-related public health measures such as lockdowns and social distancing, which likely impeded HFMD transmission. Despite the lower case numbers in 2020, data from late 2020 onwards show a gradual return to pre-pandemic levels, but with consistently low mortality rates, suggesting advancements in treatment or changes in disease reporting. By mid-2023, the cases began to reach peaks similar to those seen before the pandemic. However, a decline in cases is noted from late 2023 into 2024, possibly due to ongoing public health measures, increased awareness, or changes in population immunity. Overall, the trends indicate a seasonal and cyclical nature of HFMD, with public health interventions and improved medical responses playing a critical role in reducing the disease's impact.

The reported data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in the Chinese mainland from July 2014 to July 2024 reveals a fluctuating pattern in both case numbers and associated deaths. The highest mortality periods occurred during the months with significant case surges, such as May and June of 2016, when deaths reached 43 and 45, respectively, correlating with the peak case numbers. However, overall, the number of deaths shows a downward trend from 2014 to 2024, even though the total case counts continued to fluctuate. This decline in mortality could indicate improvements in clinical management, public health interventions, or disease surveillance efforts over the years.

From 2020 onwards, the data reveals a sharp decline in both cases and deaths, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic period, where months like February to May 2020 saw very few cases and no reported deaths. This trend may reflect the effects of widespread public health measures like social distancing and enhanced hygiene practices, which inadvertently reduced HFMD transmission. In the more recent years, especially from mid-2022 to mid-2024, despite a resurgence in case numbers, the death toll remained very low or zero, suggesting a continued effective management strategy and possibly an increase in public awareness or changes in population immunity levels.
