# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting young children, although it can also occur in adults. Caused by enteroviruses, particularly the Coxsackievirus, HFMD presents with symptoms such as fever, sore throat, and a characteristic rash with blisters on the hands, feet, and mouth. The disease is typically mild and resolves within 7 to 10 days. It spreads through close personal contact, respiratory droplets, and contact with contaminated surfaces. Preventative measures include good hygiene practices, such as regular handwashing, to limit transmission. Complications are rare, but severe cases may require medical attention.

## Highlights

- **Case Trends:** HFMD in China showed significant fluctuations from 2015 to 2025, with peaks notably in 2016, 2018, and 2023. A resurgence occurred from May to July 2023, surpassing 450,000 cases in June and July. <br/>

- **Seasonal Pattern:** A consistent seasonal trend is evident, with higher cases during warmer months (May-July), decreasing towards year-end. <br/>

- **Mortality Rates:** Despite case fluctuations, deaths have remained low, with none reported in 2023, 2024, or 2025, highlighting better disease management. <br/>

- **Current Situation (September 2025):** As of September 2025, 159,828 cases were reported, showing a seasonal decrease from summer with no deaths, indicating controlled transmission. <br/>

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth disease (HFMD) in mainland China from 2015 shows seasonal variation and disease patterns over the years. Case numbers peak in warmer months, especially from May to July, highlighting the seasonal transmission likely tied to increased social interaction among children, the primary affected group. During 2015-2019, while deaths occurred, especially peaking in June 2016 with 45 fatalities, they remained minor compared to case counts, indicating HFMD is highly contagious but rarely severe if managed properly.

A significant drop in cases in 2020 correlates with COVID-19 containment measures, where public interactions, and consequently HFMD transmission, were minimized. This period showcases how public health interventions for one disease can impact others. Post-2020, cases rebounded as restrictions eased, aligning once more with pre-pandemic trends. The absence of reported deaths in recent years suggests improved healthcare responses, possibly due to enhanced treatments or changes in virus virulence.

Analyzing the trends from 2015 to 2025, it is evident that HFMD incidence often peaks during the late spring and early summer months, particularly between May and July. The disease's fatalities exhibit a notable decline over the years, with the number of deaths gradually decreasing despite fluctuations in case counts. This suggests an enhancement in healthcare management or medical intervention strategies, which effectively mitigated mortality rates even when the disease's spread remained substantial during peak months.

Particularly in the years leading to 2018, there is an observable drop in fatalities, with the mortality rate in recent years from 2019 onwards consistently staying at or below one fatal case per month, despite substantial fluctuations in case numbers. In the initial years observed, the mortality was higher with counts occasionally reaching double digits, especially in months of high incidence. For instance, years 2016 and 2015 exhibit numerous deaths concomitant with large case numbers. As the years progress, particularly from 2020 onwards, effective control measures, enhanced healthcare infrastructure, or possibly improved preventive measures and public awareness might have played a pivotal role in substantially reducing death rates, culminating in zero reported deaths from 2023 onwards.
