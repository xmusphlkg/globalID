# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) is a common viral illness affecting mainly children under 5 years old. It's caused by enteroviruses, particularly the coxsackievirus. Symptoms include fever, mouth sores, and a rash on hands, feet, and sometimes buttocks. HFMD spreads through direct contact with an infected person's secretions, such as saliva, blister fluid, or stool. It's usually mild, with most children recovering in 7-10 days without medical treatment. Good hygiene, including regular hand washing, is essential to prevent the spread of the disease.

## Highlights

- Peak HFMD cases are consistently observed in summer, especially June and July, with June 2023 recording 433,084 cases and July 2023 at 457,212 cases. <br/>

- Deaths have significantly decreased in recent years, with no deaths from June 2023 onwards, showing improved outcomes. <br/>

- Cases dropped notably during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, with April 2020 seeing only 3,001 cases compared to over 150,000 in previous years. <br/>

- In 2024, cases are gradually increasing again, with 275,966 reported in June, but no deaths, indicating better disease management. <br/>

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 illustrates significant seasonal fluctuations in case numbers, typically peaking in the summer months. The most notable spikes in cases occurred in June of each year, with 484,724 cases in June 2014 and a peak again in June 2023 with 433,084 cases, followed by a slight decrease in June 2024 to 275,966 cases. This pattern underscores the cyclical nature of HFMD, which tends to rise in warmer months due to increased viral activity and greater human contact.

Mortality rates from HFMD have seen a decline over the years, particularly notable in the later part of the dataset. While deaths were relatively higher in 2014 with 106 deaths in June alone, the figures show a marked decrease, with many months in recent years reporting zero deaths despite high case numbers. For instance, despite a substantial number of cases in 2023, the death toll remained consistently low, with several months reporting zero fatalities. This decline in mortality could be attributed to improved healthcare responses, public health interventions, and perhaps greater community awareness and preventive measures. The significant reduction in death rates, even with fluctuating case numbers, highlights the progress made in managing HFMD more effectively.
