# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting young children. It's caused mainly by the coxsackievirus and enterovirus, manifesting as fever, sores in the mouth, and a rash on the hands and feet. Transmission occurs through contact with an infected person's respiratory droplets, saliva, fluid from blisters, or feces. Symptoms typically resolve within 7â€“10 days without medical intervention. Preventative measures include good hygiene practices like frequent hand-washing and avoiding close contact with infected individuals. Rarely, complications can occur, necessitating medical attention.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Hand, foot, and mouth disease spikes during May to July, with a high of 457,212 cases in July 2023. The trend continues with 226,183 cases in May 2024. <br/>
- **Declining Fatalities:** Notably, no deaths have been reported from May 2023 through May 2024, reflecting improved disease control and case management. <br/>
- **Post-COVID-19 Recovery:** Cases dipped dramatically during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 but began to climb again in 2021, suggesting a resurgence following eased restrictions. <br/>
- **Yearly Fluctuations:** The data reveals significant yearly variations, with 2023 showing a considerable increase compared to prior years. <br/>

The analysis of Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) data in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows pronounced seasonal trends, with peaks typically in late spring and early summer. The highest number of cases is consistently observed in May and June. For instance, cases peaked at 528,777 in May 2014 and 457,212 in July 2023. Notably, 2020 marked an anomalously low year for cases, likely influenced by COVID-19 public health measures that reduced HFMD transmission.

Interestingly, a significant increase in HFMD cases is noted from 2023 onward, yet this rise does not correlate with higher mortality. Deaths dropped to zero during this period, suggesting improvements in healthcare responses or changes in viral virulence. Mortality rates have generally decreased over the years, indicating enhanced clinical management and public health strategies.

The annual cyclical nature of HFMD, with fluctuations in both cases and fatalities, underscores the importance of sustained public health vigilance and effective management practices. The data also highlight the impact of external factors, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, on disease reporting and transmission dynamics. Overall, while HFMD cases show marked seasonality and annual variability, advancements in medical response appear to be mitigating the disease's fatal impact.

Analyzing the Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) data from mainland China from 2014 through early 2024 reveals a seasonal pattern in cases with peaks during May to August annually, which correlates with an initial high mortality rate that sees a declining trend over the years. The year 2014 marked some of the highest fatalities, with May recording 114 deaths against 528,777 cases. However, a significant decrease in mortality is observable from 2018 onwards, despite continuing high case numbers, for instance, December 2018 saw 153,086 cases but only 1 death. This decline becomes more pronounced post-2019, with many months recording zero deaths, indicative of improved disease management and possibly the impact of COVID-19 public health measures.

The COVID-19 pandemic years (2020 onwards) demonstrate a stark reduction in both cases and deaths, suggesting that interventions to curtail the pandemic may have also impacted the transmission of HFMD. Post-pandemic, as case numbers bounce back, the mortality rate remains remarkably low, suggesting enhanced healthcare responses, public health awareness, and possibly greater immunity within the population. By 2024, high case numbers do not translate into fatalities, showcasing a considerable leap in managing the disease effectively and preventing deaths.
