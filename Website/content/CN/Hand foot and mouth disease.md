# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting children under five years old. It is caused by enteroviruses, most commonly the coxsackievirus. Symptoms include fever, sore throat, and a rash with blisters on the hands, feet, and inside the mouth. The disease spreads through direct contact with an infected person's secretions, such as saliva or nasal mucus. While HFMD is generally mild and resolves within a week, maintaining good hygiene practices can help prevent its spread. Severe complications are rare but can occur, necessitating medical attention.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) in China exhibits seasonal peaks, particularly from May to July, consistent across years. <br/>

- **Decline in Cases:** A significant decline in cases has been observed since 2020, with the lowest numbers recorded in 2023 and 2024, likely due to public health interventions. <br/>

- **Mortality Rates:** Mortality rates have decreased markedly, with no deaths reported in 2023 and 2024, indicating improved disease management. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of January 2025, the number of cases remains low, reflecting effective control measures and ongoing public health efforts. <br/>

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in the Chinese mainland shows significant fluctuations in cases and deaths from 2015 to 2025. In 2015, cases peaked in May and June, with over 300,000 cases and a higher number of deaths (26 in May, 28 in June). This trend continued into 2016, with June seeing 452,668 cases and 45 deaths. The disease exhibits seasonal patterns, with peaks in warmer months, likely due to increased outdoor activities and social interactions among children, the most affected group.

From 2019 onwards, there was a notable decline in both cases and fatalities, suggesting improvements in public health interventions and treatment protocols. In 2020, cases dropped significantly, with some months reporting fewer than 5,000 cases. By 2023, although July saw 457,212 cases, there were no reported deaths, indicating advancements in healthcare responses and possibly effective vaccination campaigns or improved hygiene practices.

Overall, the data reflects a complex interplay of epidemiological factors influencing HFMD transmission and impact in the region. The decline in mortality rates, despite fluctuations in case numbers, highlights the effectiveness of public health strategies in managing the disease.

In 2015, there was a notable peak in cases during May and June, with 304,863 and 354,308 reported cases respectively, correlating with a rise in deaths, peaking at 28 in June. This trend of high case numbers accompanied by a relatively low death rate suggests that while HFMD can lead to severe outcomes, the majority of cases are manageable with appropriate medical care. The overall mortality rate during this period remained low, indicating effective public health responses and treatment protocols.

As the years progressed, particularly from 2019 onwards, there was a marked decline in both cases and deaths, with several months reporting zero fatalities. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, and better healthcare access. The data from 2023 and 2024 shows a continued trend of low mortality, despite some fluctuations in case numbers. The absence of deaths in many months suggests that the healthcare system has adapted to manage HFMD more effectively, reducing the risk of severe outcomes. Overall, the analysis indicates a positive trajectory in managing HFMD in the Chinese mainland, with ongoing efforts needed to maintain this trend.
