# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting children, characterized by sores in the mouth and a rash on the hands and feet. It is commonly caused by the coxsackievirus and is spread through direct contact with an infected person's saliva, mucus, or feces. Symptoms typically include fever, sore throat, malaise, and blister-like eruptions. HFMD can occasionally affect adults, and its mild symptoms usually resolve within a week. Prevention focuses on good hygiene practices, such as handwashing, to minimize transmission, while treatment involves supportive care to alleviate symptoms.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Hand Foot and Mouth Disease in Chinese mainland has shown significant fluctuations since 2015, with peaks typically occurring during the warmer months, particularly from May to July. <br/>
- There is a marked decrease in mortality rates over time, with zero reported deaths from 2021 onward, highlighting improved disease management and healthcare interventions. <br/>
- The year 2025 shows the lowest monthly incidence rates compared to previous years, indicating an overall downward trend in cases. <br/>
- Overall, the current situation in July 2025 appears more controlled with a significant reduction in disease cases and fatalities, suggesting enhanced public health strategies. <br/>

The data for Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from July 2015 to July 2025 highlights seasonal and annual fluctuations in cases and fatalities. Between 2015 and 2018, peaks occurred from May to July, with the highest cases typically in May and June. For instance, in 2016, June had the most cases (452,668) and fatalities (45 deaths). By 2019, although the seasonal pattern persisted, the case numbers fell, potentially due to enhanced public health measures or shifts in virus circulation.

The period from 2020 to 2023 witnessed dramatic changes, particularly a sharp decline in cases during the COVID-19 pandemic. This reduction likely stemmed from social distancing, increased hygiene, and school closures, profoundly affecting HFMD transmission. However, cases surged again in 2023, peaking at 433,084 in June. Remarkably, fatalities decreased over the years, with zero deaths from mid-2023 onwards, suggesting improvements in clinical management or viral changes.

From 2024 to 2025, a return to lower seasonal peaks was noted, with cases stabilizing and no fatalities reported. This trend might reflect enhanced herd immunity, shifts in public health strategies, or continued preventative practices post-pandemic. Overall, the data underscores significant impacts of broader epidemiological factors on HFMD and potential long-term shifts in disease dynamics.

Analyzing the reported data for Hand, Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals several key trends regarding mortality rates. From July 2015 onward, HFMD cases peaked during the summer months, particularly May through July, a pattern that persisted over the years. Despite these cyclical spikes in case numbers, the death counts associated with HFMD saw a notable decline. The highest number of deaths occurred in May and June 2016, with 43 and 45 deaths respectively. Following this peak, death rates gradually decreased, hitting zero by the end of 2021 and remaining at that level into 2025.

The significant reduction in HFMD-related deaths, even as case numbers remained high, suggests improvements in healthcare management and preventive measures. This could be attributed to more effective medical interventions, public health campaigns, and better hygiene practices that mitigate the severity of HFMD symptoms or prevent complications that could result in fatalities. Notably, while the number of cases in recent years often exceeded those in previous years, the absence of reported deaths suggests substantial progress in controlling the disease's most severe outcomes. This trend underscores the importance of continued monitoring and response strategies to sustain low mortality rates despite fluctuations in disease incidence.
