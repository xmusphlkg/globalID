# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a highly contagious viral illness primarily affecting infants and young children. It is commonly caused by coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. HFMD is characterized by fever, sores in the mouth, and a rash on the hands, feet, and sometimes buttocks. Transmission occurs through direct contact with saliva, nasal discharge, feces, or blister fluid. Outbreaks often happen in childcare settings and preschools. The disease is usually mild and self-limiting, with symptoms resolving within seven to ten days without specific treatment.

## Highlights

- Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) incidence in mainland China displays marked seasonality, with annual peaks between May and July, and secondary rises in autumn.<br/>
- 2025 saw two distinct peaks: a moderate one in July (194,709 cases) and a major surge in October-November (378,738 and 269,337 cases respectively), higher than recent years.<br/>
- The number of reported deaths has drastically declined since 2021; in 2025, only one death was recorded (November), indicating improved disease management and outcomes.<br/>
- Overall, HFMD remains widespread but with very low mortality, reflecting enhanced public health interventions and possibly shifting viral epidemiology.<br/>

Analysis of the hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) data from mainland China reveals a strong seasonal pattern, with case peaks routinely occurring from May to July each year. Between 2015 and 2019, monthly cases often exceeded 300,000 during these summer months, with corresponding increases in reported deaths, particularly in the years 2016–2018. Despite these surges, severe outcomes remained proportionally rare and showed a decreasing trend over time.

The sharp decline in cases throughout 2020 coincided with stringent COVID-19 control measures, demonstrating the dramatic impact of social distancing, school closures, and improved hygiene practices on HFMD transmission. As these measures relaxed, cases rebounded in subsequent years, but mortality rates remained exceptionally low, with 2023 and much of 2024–2025 showing either zero or just one death per month, despite large case numbers. This suggests improvements in clinical management or potential changes in virus virulence or reporting.

This multi-year dataset highlights the sensitivity of HFMD dynamics to public health interventions and broader societal behaviors. The long-term reduction in mortality and the return of pronounced seasonality after pandemic-related disruptions underscore the importance of ongoing disease surveillance and rapid response capacity to detect shifts in epidemiology and protect child health.

A review of hand, foot and mouth disease (HFMD) mortality in the Chinese mainland highlights a clear downward trend in reported deaths from 2015 through mid-2025. Between 2015 and 2017, annual deaths ranged from approximately 60 to 89 per year, occurring predominantly during periods of high case incidence in the spring and summer months. Notably, deaths peaked in the late spring to early summer seasons, especially in May and June, aligning with typical HFMD transmission peaks. However, beginning in 2018, both absolute death counts and death-to-case ratios declined markedly despite continued large outbreaks, and by 2023 onward, months with substantial case counts frequently reported zero deaths.

This pattern likely reflects a combination of improved clinical management, rapid diagnosis, and possibly successful vaccination or public health interventions targeting virulent enterovirus subtypes. The abrupt drop in deaths seen in early 2020 corresponds with a historic decrease in case numbers, possibly due to COVID-19-related public health measures or reporting shifts, and from 2021 forward, deaths remained consistently rare—usually zero or one per month—regardless of case burden. By 2024 and 2025, with tens to hundreds of thousands of cases still reported seasonally, near absence of reported fatalities signals HFMD’s effective shift to a substantially less fatal disease in the Chinese mainland, indicating major progress in pediatric infectious disease control and management.
