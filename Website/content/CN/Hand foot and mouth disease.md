# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral illness primarily affecting children under five, although it can occur in adults. Caused by non-polio enteroviruses, particularly coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71, the disease is characterized by fever, sore throat, and a rash with blisters on the hands, feet, and inside the mouth. Symptoms typically appear three to six days after exposure and last for about seven to ten days. HFMD spreads through direct contact with respiratory droplets, feces, or blister fluid. Preventative measures include good hygiene practices, such as frequent handwashing and disinfecting surfaces.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in the Chinese mainland has fluctuated over the years, with a significant decrease noted during and post-2020, likely linked to pandemic-related interventions. <br/>
- A resurgence in cases was observed in mid-2023, peaking in June and July, indicating a return to the disease’s typical seasonal pattern. <br/>
- Although deaths have been minimal or non-existent since late 2022, the disease continues to primarily affect children under 5, highlighting the importance of continuous monitoring and vaccination efforts. <br/>
- The dominant viral strains have shifted over time, underscoring the necessity for adaptive public health strategies and vaccine updates. <br/>

The Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) data from mainland China between 2014 and 2024 shows seasonal peaks in case numbers, primarily from April to July. The highest incidence years, such as June 2016 and July 2017, highlight the disease's tendency to surge in warmer months, likely due to environmental factors that facilitate virus spread. This pattern underscores the need for targeted public health measures during these peak times to prevent large outbreaks.

Although case numbers fluctuate significantly, ranging from lows in winter months to highs in summer, mortality rates have generally decreased over the years. The peak year of 2016 saw the most significant deaths, aligning with high case numbers. From 2019 onward, fatalities significantly dropped even when case numbers remained high, as seen in the mid-2020s. This trend might suggest improvements in clinical care, public health responses, or a change in the virus's impact.

A notable dip in cases during 2020 and 2021 correlates with the global COVID-19 pandemic, suggesting that widespread public health measures can reduce transmission. The subsequent rebound in cases post-2021, particularly in the mid-2020s, indicates a return to typical seasonal patterns as pandemic restrictions eased. Overall, the data emphasizes the impact of environmental and public health strategies on the disease's transmission dynamics.

An analysis of Hand Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD) fatalities in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals crucial trends in mortality against fluctuating case numbers. Initially, high death counts in 2014 (e.g., 21 deaths in October) correspond with substantial case numbers. In 2016, a mortality peak occurred with 43 deaths in May and 45 in June, despite higher case volumes, suggesting potential gaps in healthcare response effectiveness during this period.

From 2018 onward, a clear reduction in HFMD deaths is observed. Many months reported minimal to zero fatalities despite substantial cases, indicating improvements in healthcare strategies or potentially changing virus virulence. Notably, from 2020, amid the COVID-19 pandemic, deaths continued to minimize—even in months with significant case surges, like June 2023, which saw 433,084 cases but no deaths. This pattern persisted into 2024 with zero deaths recorded.

These observations suggest enhanced public health measures, improved medical responses, or a shift towards less severe disease outcomes over the years. The decreasing mortality trend, amid varying case numbers, underscores an advancing public healthcare infrastructure, reflecting successful mitigation of the disease’s impact. This improvement is vital for future public health strategy, emphasizing both effective response frameworks and disease management practices that potentially reduce HFMD's lethality.
