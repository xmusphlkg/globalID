# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral infection commonly affecting infants and children, though adults can be infected. It is primarily caused by coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. HFMD is characterized by fever, sore throat, and a rash, with painful sores in the mouth and blisters on the hands, feet, and sometimes buttocks and legs. The disease spreads through direct contact with an infected person's secretions or feces. Outbreaks are common in childcare settings. Most cases are mild and resolve without treatment within a week, though hygiene practices are crucial to prevent transmission.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuations in Cases:** HFMD cases in China vary significantly, with peaks in mid-2016 and mid-2023, exceeding 450,000 cases in July 2023. Peaks typically occur between May and July. <br/>

- **Decline in Deaths:** Deaths from HFMD have consistently decreased, with zero deaths reported in most months since 2022, indicating better disease management. <br/>

- **Recent Trends:** In March 2025, 12,203 cases were reported, slightly up from February but much lower than the 2023 peak. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns:** HFMD cases rise during spring and summer, consistent with historical trends of higher infection rates in warmer months. <br/>

The incidence of Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China shows notable fluctuations with a pronounced seasonality, peaking consistently around April to June from 2015 to 2017. For example, cases surged over 300,000 in May and June 2015, and similar peaks persisted in subsequent years. This trend suggests strong seasonal factors influencing transmission, potentially linked to environmental conditions or socio-behavioral patterns. Despite high case numbers during peak seasons, reported deaths remained relatively low, indicating effective clinical management or varying disease severity.

The data from 2020 highlights a significant anomaly with fewer cases, likely due to COVID-19 pandemic measures such as social distancing and improved hygiene, which indirectly reduced HFMD transmission. Increased cases in 2021 and 2022 signal a gradual return to normal patterns, though numbers remain lower compared to pre-2020 levels. In 2023, there is a dramatic surge in cases, particularly in June and July, yet no reported deaths, suggesting improvements in clinical interventions or a shift in disease severity.

Overall, the fluctuations in case numbers underscore HFMD's cyclical nature and the influence of external factors like the COVID-19 pandemic. Consistently low mortality rates highlight the endemic nature of HFMD in China and the importance of public health strategies aimed at prevention during peak transmission periods and ensuring effective clinical responses to reduce fatalities.

The mortality burden of Hand, Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China shows a marked decline from 2015 onward, with notable peaks in both case numbers and fatalities in the earlier years of the dataset. Between 2015 and 2017, the annual peak months (typically May to July) regularly exceeded 300,000 reported cases and recorded monthly fatalities ranging from 26 to 45, suggesting higher virulence or more severe pathogen circulation, likely related to Enterovirus 71 strains. However, from 2018 onward, mortality began to decline significantly despite continued large case volumes in many months, including major outbreaks in 2018 and 2019. By 2020, despite reduced reporting in the wake of pandemic-related public health interventions, the already declining death toll neared zero, indicating an improving clinical response and possibly shifts in circulating enterovirus types to less virulent strains.

From 2021 through early 2025, total deaths remained consistently very low, with most months reporting zero fatalities, even during high-incidence months. The largest single-month case count post-2020—457,212 in July 2023—was not associated with any death, underscoring a dramatic shift in disease severity or public health mitigation success. This decline reflects a combination of improved clinical management, broader public awareness, and possible changes in herd immunity or circulating genotypes. It is also plausible that enhanced surveillance and data standardization have contributed to better case detection and classification, capturing more mild cases while reducing fatality ratios.
