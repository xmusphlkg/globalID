# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) is a contagious illness commonly affecting young children, caused primarily by coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. It is characterized by fever, sore throat, and a rash with blisters on the hands, feet, and inside the mouth. The virus spreads through close personal contact, respiratory droplets, and contaminated surfaces. Although usually mild, it can lead to complications like viral meningitis in rare cases. There is no specific treatment; management focuses on symptom relief. Good hygiene practices, such as frequent handwashing, are crucial in preventing the spread of HFMD.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** HFMD cases have decreased significantly since 2019, with notably lower numbers by 2025. The 2018 peak was followed by reduced transmission. <br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Deaths from HFMD have drastically reduced since 2020, with no fatalities recorded from 2023 to 2025, suggesting improved management or less severe outbreaks. <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks:** The disease continues to follow seasonal patterns, with peaks from April to August, though the overall trend is a decrease. <br/>
- **Current Situation, August 2025:** HFMD cases have decreased to 103,195, continuing the recent trend of reduced incidence in recent years. <br/>

Analyzing the HFMD data from mainland China (2015-2025), we observe a marked seasonal trend, with peaks typically occurring from April to June. Major peaks, such as May 2016 and June 2019, with 442,075 and 337,393 cases respectively, are linked to increased transmission opportunities in warmer months when children are more active outside. These peak periods often coincide with higher death counts, although the overall fatality rate remains low, indicating effective management and control of severe cases.

In 2020, there was a significant decline in cases, likely influenced by COVID-19 mitigation measures, such as school closures and enhanced hygiene, which reduced HFMD transmission. By 2023, case numbers surged to 457,212 in July, suggesting a return to previous transmission patterns as restrictions eased and normal activities resumed. However, from this period onward, no deaths were reported, potentially reflecting improved healthcare responses or changes in virus virulence.

The data suggests that while HFMD continues to follow predictable seasonal trends, public health interventions can substantially impact its transmission. Enhanced awareness, preventative measures, and ongoing surveillance remain crucial in managing HFMD effectively. Understanding these dynamics helps in designing better public health strategies to control outbreaks and minimize their societal impact, particularly in environments where children congregate.

The analysis of reported deaths due to Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in the Chinese mainland reveals notable trends over the years. Initially, during 2015, the number of deaths fluctuated moderately, with slightly higher occurrences in heavy case months such as June, when deaths peaked at 45. By 2016, HFMD cases rose significantly, particularly in the peak month of June, correlating with an increase in mortality. However, by 2017 and 2018, death counts per month generally decreased despite some persistent cases spikes, suggesting possible improvements in healthcare responses, public health measures, or both.

From 2019 onwards, HFMD death rates decreased even as the number of cases remained substantial. This shift became more pronounced during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, where both case numbers and deaths dramatically fell, likely due to stringent public health measures for COVID-19 inadvertently controlling HFMD. These measures, such as increased hygiene awareness and reduced social interactions, seem to have had a lasting positive impact as evidenced by the sustained low mortality despite cases picking up again post-2020. By 2025, even with significant numbers of reported cases, mortality was effectively minimized, pointing to a robust health infrastructure and possibly improved treatment protocols or public health strategies.
