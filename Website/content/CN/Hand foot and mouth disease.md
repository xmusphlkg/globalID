# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting children under five years old. It is caused by enteroviruses, most commonly the coxsackievirus. Symptoms include fever, sore throat, and a rash with blisters on the hands, feet, and inside the mouth. The disease spreads through direct contact with an infected person's secretions, such as saliva or nasal mucus. While HFMD is generally mild and resolves within a week, maintaining good hygiene practices can help prevent its spread. Severe complications are rare but can occur, necessitating medical attention.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** HFMD cases in the Chinese mainland have decreased significantly, from over 33,000 in February 2015 to 5,721 in February 2025. <br/>

- **Reduced Mortality:** Deaths have also dropped, with no deaths reported in February 2025, compared to 3 in February 2015. <br/>

- **Seasonal Trends:** Historically, cases peaked from May to July, but recent data shows a more even distribution throughout the year. <br/>

- **Public Health Impact:** The reduction in cases and deaths suggests effective public health measures and increased awareness over the years. <br/>

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a clear seasonal pattern with peaks typically occurring in the warmer months, particularly from April to July. For instance, in 2015, the number of cases rose sharply from 33,003 in February to a peak of 354,308 in June. This pattern of increased cases during the warmer months is consistent across the years, suggesting a strong seasonal influence on the transmission of HFMD.

A notable observation is the significant drop in cases during 2020, likely due to the COVID-19 pandemic and the associated public health measures such as lockdowns, social distancing, and increased hygiene practices, which inadvertently reduced the transmission of HFMD. For example, cases in April 2020 were only 3,001 compared to 155,696 in April 2019. Post-2020, the data shows a gradual return to pre-pandemic levels, with a resurgence in cases by 2023, reaching 457,212 in July. However, the number of deaths has remained relatively low throughout the years, with a noticeable decline in recent years, indicating improved management and treatment of the disease.

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends in both the number of cases and deaths. Initially, from 2015 to 2016, there is a noticeable increase in both cases and deaths, with peaks in the warmer months, particularly from April to June. For instance, in June 2016, the number of cases reached 452,668 with 45 deaths, marking one of the highest mortality rates in the dataset. This pattern suggests a seasonal trend, likely due to the increased transmission of the virus in warmer weather when children, the primary affected group, are more likely to engage in outdoor activities.

From 2017 onwards, there is a gradual decline in the number of deaths despite fluctuations in case numbers. By 2020, the number of deaths significantly decreased, with many months reporting zero fatalities. This trend could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, and possibly the development of better treatment protocols or vaccines. The data from 2020 onwards also shows a dramatic drop in cases during the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic, likely due to lockdowns and social distancing measures, which inadvertently reduced the transmission of HFMD. By 2023 and 2024, while the number of cases began to rise again, the mortality rate remained at zero, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease.
