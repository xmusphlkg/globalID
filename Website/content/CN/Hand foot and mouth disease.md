# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a common viral illness primarily affecting infants and young children, though it can occur in adults. It is most often caused by coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. HFMD spreads through direct contact with nasal secretions, saliva, fluid from blisters, or stool of an infected person. Symptoms typically begin with fever, sore throat, and reduced appetite, followed by painful sores in the mouth and a skin rash on the hands and feet. The disease is generally mild and self-limiting, resolving within 7 to 10 days without serious complications.

## Highlights

- HFMD cases in China mainland showed a strong seasonal pattern, peaking between May and July each year, with a significant drop during winter months. <br/>
- Following a dramatic decline in 2020 due to COVID-19 restrictions, cases have steadily rebounded, nearing pre-pandemic levels in 2023 and stabilizing in 2024. <br/>
- Mortality has sharply declined since 2019; no deaths have been reported since June 2022 despite high case counts, indicating improved clinical management and early detection. <br/>
- As of April 2025, 26,217 cases were reported with zero deaths, signaling continued containment and low disease severity at the beginning of the epidemic season. <br/>

Reported hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) cases in mainland China exhibit a clear seasonal pattern, with significant surges typically observed from April to August, peaking in May or June. This phenomenon is consistent throughout the pre-pandemic years (2015–2019), with 2016 and 2018 reaching notably high monthly case counts—exceeding 450,000 in June 2016 and over 420,000 in June 2018. Mortality remained relatively low throughout these years, though there were spikes in death counts during peak transmission months, especially in 2016. A sharp and unprecedented drop in incident cases is observed from February 2020 onward, corresponding with the onset of COVID-19 containment measures. This decline likely reflects the impact of heightened hygiene, social distancing, and school closures, rather than a natural reduction in disease prevalence.

Following the pandemic-induced lows, HFMD cases gradually rebounded, especially from 2021 to 2023. By 2023, monthly case volumes returned to pre-pandemic levels, notably surpassing 450,000 in July—the highest since 2016. Interestingly, despite this resurgence of case numbers, reported deaths remained consistently at zero from September 2020 onward, suggesting either improvements in clinical management and early detection or potential changes in case severity, perhaps due to shifts in circulating enterovirus strains or broader vaccination coverage. The sustained zero mortality in recent years, even during high case periods in 2023–2024, marks a significant epidemiological shift from earlier years with periodic fatality clusters.

The mortality burden from hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) in mainland China peaked noticeably from 2015 to 2017, with annual death tolls of 114, 219, and 102 respectively. These deaths were concentrated during the annual epidemic season — typically April to August — when transmission is highest. Notably, May and June demonstrated recurrent spikes in both incidence and fatality, with June 2016 alone recording 45 deaths. Such clustering aligns with enhanced circulation of virulent enterovirus strains such as EV-A71, known to be associated with severe neurological complications and higher fatality risk, particularly in younger children.

A major shift began in 2020, with HFMD-related deaths dropping sharply and remaining near zero through 2024, despite high case numbers surging again post-pandemic. This pronounced decline in case fatality suggests likely contributions from multiple public health interventions, including heightened hygiene practices during COVID-19, clinical management improvements, and possibly expanded immunization with EV-A71 vaccines starting in the late 2010s. The dissociation between case burden and deaths after 2020 highlights successful mitigation of severe outcomes even amidst viral resurgence.
