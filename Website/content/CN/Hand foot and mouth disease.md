# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) is a common viral illness primarily affecting young children, caused by enteroviruses, most commonly coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. It is characterized by fever, sores in the mouth, and a rash on the hands and feet. HFMD spreads through direct contact with infected bodily fluids, such as saliva, mucus, or stool, and through respiratory droplets. The disease typically resolves within a week to 10 days, but in rare cases, complications like viral meningitis may occur. Good hygiene practices can help reduce the spread of infection.

## Highlights

- **Recent Decline in Mortality**: As of November 2024, there have been no reported deaths from Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) since early 2023, marking a significant reduction in fatalities. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends**: HFMD cases generally peak from May to July each year, with a slight decrease in cases during the colder months. <br/>
- **2024 Case Surge**: Cases have increased in 2024, with a marked rise in April (102,204 cases), followed by substantial numbers through June and July. <br/>
- **Overall Decrease in Annual Incidence**: Compared to the peak years (2015-2016), the number of cases in recent years has steadily decreased, with fewer monthly outbreaks. <br/>

The data on Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China shows significant fluctuations in case numbers over the past decade, with notable peaks in 2015 and 2016. The highest surge occurred in June 2015, with over 350,000 cases, followed by another peak in June 2016. Mortality was highest during these years, with deaths reaching 45 in June 2016. This period likely reflects the seasonality of HFMD, which typically peaks in warmer months, and suggests that public health measures may have been less robust at the time.

From 2017 onward, case numbers gradually declined, with a sharp drop starting in 2020. The COVID-19 pandemic likely played a role in reducing transmission due to strict lockdowns and social distancing measures. By 2023, case numbers began to rise again but remained relatively low compared to previous years, with no deaths reported in many months. The trend in 2023 and 2024 shows periodic increases, such as in July 2023 (over 450,000 cases), but the overall mortality remained minimal, indicating improved clinical management and public health responses.

Overall, while the incidence of HFMD remains a concern, especially in summer months, the case fatality rate has significantly decreased in recent years, reflecting advances in treatment and healthcare infrastructure.

The mortality rate for Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) in mainland China has significantly decreased since 2017. In the earlier years (2014–2016), there were considerable fluctuations in both case numbers and deaths, with peaks such as 45 deaths in June 2016. However, after 2017, fatalities sharply dropped, with most months reporting zero deaths, even during periods with high case numbers. For instance, in June 2023, there were over 430,000 cases, but no fatalities.

This decline in mortality suggests improved public health measures, better clinical management, and possibly the reduced virulence of circulating enterovirus strains. From 2020 onward, deaths remained very low, often at zero, even when case numbers were substantial. For example, in 2023 and early 2024, months with tens of thousands of cases reported no fatalities at all. These trends point to effective disease control and medical advancements, which have likely contributed to the reduced fatality rate. The data reflects a notable shift from the early years, where higher death tolls were recorded, to the current situation where HFMD has become far less deadly in China.
