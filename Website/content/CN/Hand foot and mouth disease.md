# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a common, contagious viral illness primarily affecting infants and children under five. It is most commonly caused by coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. Symptoms include fever, sore throat, and a characteristic rash with red spots and sometimes blisters on the hands, feet, and mouth. The disease spreads through direct contact with nasal secretions, saliva, blister fluid, or stool of an infected person. Most cases are mild and self-limiting, resolving in 7 to 10 days. Good hygiene practices help prevent transmission.

## Highlights

- Significant decline in HFMD cases and deaths observed in 2025 May (52,036 cases, 0 deaths), compared to previous peaks such as over 440,000 cases in May–June 2016. <br/>
- Since 2023, the disease shows sustained low mortality, with zero deaths reported for 29 consecutive months up to May 2025. <br/>
- Peak incidence still trends toward warmer months (May–July), though recent years show reduced transmission intensity, likely influenced by public health measures or population immunity. <br/>
- Overall, current 2025 data indicate stable control of HFMD with minimal health impact, signaling effective surveillance and prevention efforts across the Chinese mainland. <br/>

From 2015 to 2025, Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) cases in mainland China show a clear seasonal pattern, peaking during late spring and summer, particularly May to July. Large spikes occurred in June 2016 (452,668 cases) and July 2023 (457,212 cases). Before 2020, seasonal peaks were frequently accompanied by notable death counts, such as 45 deaths in June 2016. Yearly case fluctuations were common, but the general trend preserved this seasonal rhythm.

A sharp decline occurred in 2020, with cases dropping drastically following public health interventions during the COVID-19 outbreak. The case count in May 2020 fell to just 3,919, compared to over 260,000 in May 2019. This suppression trend persisted into 2021. Beyond the drop in incidence, mortality also fell significantly, with deaths nearly eliminated from 2020 onward. Despite case resurgence by 2023—with monthly reports again exceeding 400,000—there were no reported deaths from early 2021 through mid-2025. This suggests a possible shift in disease severity, improved medical response, or increased immunity within the population. Seasonal cycles have resumed, but without the previously associated mortality burden.

From 2015 to 2018, hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) in mainland China showed notable mortality, particularly in peak transmission months between May and July. The highest death tolls were recorded in June 2016 (45 deaths), May 2016 (43), and June 2017 (30), coinciding with seasons of elevated incidence. This pattern suggests seasonal dynamics and potential strain variation influencing lethality. Despite sharp spikes in case numbers, deaths gradually trended downward starting in late 2018. By December 2018, monthly deaths dropped to near zero, a shift that may reflect changes in clinical management, vaccination impact, or dominant circulating viral serotypes transitioning toward less virulent forms.

From 2019 onwards, there was a steep decline in fatalities, even during high-incidence months, with a complete cessation of reported deaths beginning March 2023 and continuing through 2025 despite hundreds of thousands of reported cases — such as over 457,000 in July 2023. The near-elimination of mortality in recent years implies a significant improvement in disease prevention and healthcare response, or a true reduction in disease severity. This trend may also be influenced by improved surveillance, early diagnosis, and possibly health policy changes following pandemic-related interventions beginning in 2020. The post-2020 decline, featuring persistent low or zero death counts regardless of case numbers, marks a fundamental shift in HFMD lethality in China.
