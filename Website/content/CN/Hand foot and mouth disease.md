# Hand foot and mouth disease

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) is a common viral illness primarily affecting infants and children under five years of age, although it can occasionally occur in adults. It is caused by enteroviruses, notably the coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. HFMD is characterized by fever, sore throat, and a rash with flat or raised red spots or blisters on the hands, feet, mouth, and sometimes buttocks. It is highly contagious and spreads through direct contact with saliva, mucus, or feces of an infected person. While generally mild, maintaining good hygiene can prevent transmission.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Mortality Rate**: Since 2020, there has been a significant decrease in mortality associated with hand, foot, and mouth disease in mainland China, with zero reported deaths since March 2023. <br/>
- **Strong Seasonal Patterns**: Historically, cases peak during summer. Although 2023 had significantly high summer cases, 2025 shows a reduction with June reporting 150,557 cases. <br/>
- **Pacifying Epidemic Wave**: A continuous decline in case numbers began in late 2024, indicating a subsiding epidemic wave as of mid-2025. <br/>
- **Impact of Interventions**: The trend suggests that either public health interventions or natural immunity are being effective in managing the disease. <br/>

The hand, foot, and mouth disease (HFMD) data from 2015 to 2025 in Chinese mainland shows significant fluctuations, with pronounced seasonal peaks typically observed in warmer months. In 2015 and 2016, there were substantial outbreaks, with peak cases in June exceeding 350,000. These surges suggest a pattern from May to July. The highest recorded cases occurred in 2016 and 2023, each surpassing 400,000, indicating potential cycles of high transmission periods.

A noticeable decline appears in 2020, likely due to COVID-19 pandemic-related interventions impacting the transmission of other infectious diseases. Post-2020, cases remained relatively high but with minimal deaths, suggesting improvements in medical care or changes in disease severity. From 2021, reported deaths are nearly zero, despite substantial case numbers, indicating perhaps a shift in clinical management or public health strategies.

This dataset illustrates a recurring cyclical epidemic behavior typical of HFMD, with peaks every 2-3 years, as highlighted by the elevated numbers in 2023. The lack of reported deaths despite high cases suggests either a successful public health response or alterations in disease dynamics, potentially influenced by increased awareness and preventive measures. Overall, the trend reflects both natural epidemiological patterns and possible impacts of external health interventions.
