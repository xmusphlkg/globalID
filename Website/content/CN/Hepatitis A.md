# Hepatitis A

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a highly contagious liver infection caused by the Hepatitis A virus (HAV). It is primarily transmitted through the ingestion of contaminated food or water or close contact with an infected person. The virus multiplies in the liver, causing inflammation and affecting liver function. Common symptoms include fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, loss of appetite, fever, and jaundice. While most cases are mild and resolve fully without treatment, maintaining proper hygiene and sanitation, getting vaccinated, and practicing safe food handling are crucial preventative measures. Hepatitis A occurs worldwide but is more prevalent in regions with poor sanitation.

## Highlights

- Early 2025 saw a significant surge in Hepatitis A cases, peaking at 2,293 in January, indicating a potential outbreak or seasonal effect. <br/>
- A declining trend in cases was observed from 2015 to 2022, with a major drop by early 2023, suggesting effective control measures or changes in epidemiological patterns. <br/>
- Deaths associated with Hepatitis A have consistently remained low, typically fluctuating between 0 and 2 per month, reflecting successful case management. <br/>
- The recent increase in cases necessitates enhanced surveillance, a review of vaccination policies, and strengthened public health measures to curb further transmission. <br/>

The reported cases of Hepatitis A in China's mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveal significant fluctuations. Initially, monthly cases in 2015 were above 1800, peaking at 2198 in March 2016. A gradual decline follows, with notable reductions from 2018 onward, where numbers dropped below the 2000 threshold and continued to slide into the mid-thousands through 2019 and 2020. This decline aligns with global vaccination efforts and improved sanitation, reflecting effective interventions to curb Hepatitis A's spread. However, intermittent spikes were recorded, such as in July 2019 and March 2020, indicating potential regional outbreaks or lapses in preventive measures.

From 2021 onward, there is a marked decrease in cases, occasionally dipping below 1000 by 2023 and 2024. Yet, in January 2025, a sudden surge occurs, with cases jumping back above 2000, indicating a possible renewed outbreak. This fluctuation highlights the need for continuous surveillance and public health efforts to maintain low incidence rates. Deaths associated with Hepatitis A remained sporadic and low throughout the period, averaging about one death per month, reflecting the non-fatal nature of the disease with proper management. The data underscores the importance of vaccination, public health education, and sanitation improvements in controlling Hepatitis A while emphasizing the need for vigilance against potential resurgences.
