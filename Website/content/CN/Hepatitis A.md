# Hepatitis A

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a contagious liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV). It primarily spreads through ingestion of contaminated food or water or direct contact with an infected person. Unlike other hepatitis viruses, it does not lead to chronic liver disease but can cause mild to severe illness. Symptoms include fever, jaundice, abdominal pain, dark urine, and fatigue. Most people recover completely, but preventive measures such as improved sanitation, handwashing, and vaccination are crucial in reducing transmission. Hepatitis A is more prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and is preventable through immunization.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend**: Hepatitis A cases have significantly decreased from over 2000 cases monthly in early years to around 1000 cases in late 2023, reflecting improved public health measures. <br/>
- **Recent Increase**: Starting early 2025, a resurgence in cases was observed, peaking at 2293 cases in January, before gradually declining to 1228 by September. <br/>
- **Mortality Rates**: Deaths remain rare throughout the data period, indicating effective treatment and management, with sporadic deaths reported. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variability**: The data suggests some seasonal variation in cases, although the pattern is not consistent across all years, necessitating further investigation. <br/>

Initially, from September 2015 to mid-2018, cases exhibited a general decline, with periodic spikes like in March 2016 (2198 cases). During this period, deaths remained relatively low, ranging from 0 to 4 per month, reflecting a consistent presence of the disease but limited mortality.

From 2019 to 2022, there was a noticeable decrease in cases, reaching a low of 532 in December 2022. This decline might indicate successful interventions such as improved vaccination and sanitation. Throughout these years, the reported deaths remained consistently low, usually 0 to 1 per month, suggesting effective disease management despite the number of cases.

In 2023, cases saw an initial rise in March (1289 cases) but then decreased again. However, entering 2025, cases surged dramatically, peaking at 2293 in January. This rise might reflect cyclical patterns, outbreak occurrence, or epidemiological shifts. Despite fluctuating incidence, mortality rates remained low, emphasizing effective public health responses and healthcare systems' capacity to manage outcomes.
