# Hepatitis A

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a highly contagious viral infection that primarily affects the liver, caused by the Hepatitis A virus (HAV). It is transmitted through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated food or water. Symptoms include fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, loss of appetite, and jaundice. While the infection can be mild and self-limiting, it may lead to severe illness in some individuals. Vaccination is an effective preventive measure, especially for those at higher risk, such as travelers to endemic areas. Good hygiene practices, including handwashing, are crucial in preventing its spread.

## Highlights

- **Surge in 2025**: January 2025 reports 2,293 Hepatitis A cases, the highest since 2015, indicating a potential outbreak. <br/>

- **Decline from 2015 to 2024**: A steady decrease in cases is observed from 2015 to 2024, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: The mortality rate remains low, with few deaths, indicating effective disease management. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Some months consistently show higher cases, hinting at seasonal influences. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis A cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 shows a clear decline in reported cases over the years. In 2015, monthly cases ranged from about 1,500 to 2,500, but by 2023, most months reported fewer than 1,000 cases. This trend suggests improvements in public health measures, vaccination coverage, or changes in public behavior that have effectively reduced transmission.

Despite the overall decline, there are occasional spikes, such as in March 2024, with 1,542 cases, and January 2025, with a significant increase to 2,293 cases. These spikes could be due to localized outbreaks, seasonal variations, or lapses in public health interventions. The death toll remains low, with most months reporting zero deaths and only sporadic instances of one or two deaths, indicating that while the disease persists, its lethality is largely controlled.

A seasonal pattern is evident, with some months consistently showing higher case numbers, particularly early in the year, possibly linked to environmental factors or increased social interactions during holidays. Overall, the data suggests that while Hepatitis A remains a public health concern, the situation has improved, with effective control measures likely contributing to the reduction in cases and deaths.
