# Hepatitis A

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a viral infection of the liver caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV). It is primarily transmitted through ingestion of contaminated food or water or close contact with an infected person. The disease often presents with fever, fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, and jaundice. Hepatitis A usually does not lead to chronic liver disease and most people recover fully in a few weeks. The infection is preventable by vaccination and improved sanitation. Outbreaks may occur in areas with limited access to clean water and poor hygiene practices.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis A incidence in Chinese mainland has shown a substantial decline from over 1900 monthly cases in 2015-2016 to around 1200 cases by December 2025, indicating effective disease control measures.<br/>
- Deaths have remained consistently low, with most months reporting zero fatalities and only sporadic deaths throughout the past decade, reflecting improved clinical management and surveillance.<br/>
- An uptick in cases was observed in early 2025 (peaking at 2293 in January), but numbers quickly decreased, suggesting transient factors rather than sustained outbreaks.<br/>
- Overall, Hepatitis A remains well-controlled nationwide, with low and stable case and mortality levels marking a favorable epidemiological situation as of December 2025.<br/>

Analysis of Hepatitis A cases from 2015–2025 in the Chinese mainland reveals dynamic trends influenced by public health and social factors. Between 2015 and 2019, reported monthly cases typically ranged from 1,300 to over 2,000, showing periodic peaks but overall stable incidence; deaths were infrequent and mostly isolated, indicating low case-fatality. From early 2020, a steady decline in case numbers becomes clear, with monthly totals often falling below 1,500 and reaching as low as 500–900 around 2022–2023. This sharp reduction coincides with the COVID-19 pandemic period, which may have affected both transmission patterns and disease surveillance due to movement restrictions and shifting healthcare priorities.

A notable resurgence in Hepatitis A cases emerges from early 2024, with monthly numbers quickly rising to and exceeding pre-pandemic levels, peaking above 2,200 in early 2025. Such rebound suggests possible factors including waning immunity, changes in exposure or reporting, and the resumption of normal societal activities following pandemic control measures. Deaths remained sporadic and low throughout, reinforcing a consistently low fatality rate. This decade of surveillance underscores how external disruptions can shape disease trends, while the underlying reduction in deaths points to generally effective management and prevention strategies amid fluctuating case counts.

Even during months of higher reported incidence, mortality remains minimal and uncorrelated to dramatic increases in cases. This suggests a low case-fatality rate for Hepatitis A in the observed population, with fluctuations in deaths not driven by proportional surges in reported cases.

Notably, periods with elevated case numbers in spring and summer months do not correspond to marked increases in deaths, further supporting a pattern of low mortality—even when transmission is higher. In the most recent years, deaths frequently register zero, implicating improved clinical management, effective vaccination, or surveillance practices as potentially suppressive factors for fatal outcomes. The few scattered months with isolated deaths are not concentrated in any particular season and do not show clustering, underscoring the sporadic nature of fatality events in this setting.
