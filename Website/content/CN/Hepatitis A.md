# Hepatitis A

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a highly contagious liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV). It primarily spreads through ingestion of contaminated food or water or direct contact with an infected person. Symptoms may include fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, loss of appetite, and jaundice, although some individuals, especially children, may be asymptomatic. Unlike other forms of hepatitis, hepatitis A does not cause chronic liver disease and is usually self-limiting, with most individuals recovering completely without long-term effects. Preventive measures include good sanitation practices and vaccination, which is highly effective in providing immunity against the virus.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis A cases in Chinese mainland showed a marked increase in early 2025, peaking at 2,293 cases in January, indicating potential outbreaks or increased transmission during this period. <br/>
- A steady decline in cases was observed from March to June 2025, reflecting possible containment efforts or natural epidemic cycles, reducing caseload from 1,919 to 1,803. <br/>
- The overall mortality rate remained extremely low throughout the dataset, with sporadic deaths reported without a clear pattern, emphasizing the low lethality of Hepatitis A. <br/>
- Comparison with previous years highlights that the spike in early 2025 is unique in recent trends, warranting continued monitoring and public health interventions. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis A cases in mainland China from June 2015 to June 2025 exhibits noticeable fluctuations and trends. Initially, the data reveals higher numbers ranging from 1,766 to 2,203 monthly cases between June 2015 and July 2019, with several peaks notably in March 2016 and July 2019. Following this period, the cases saw a significant decline starting in late 2019, with numbers dropping below 1,000 by January 2021. This decline could potentially be attributed to improved public health measures, better sanitation, or increased vaccination rates. From early 2021 to late 2023, the case numbers stabilized around a lower range with seasonal fluctuations, but without any dramatic peaks.

The mortality rates associated with Hepatitis A during this period are generally low, indicating successful management and treatment of the disease or the inherent less-severe nature of Hepatitis A in terms of mortality. Deaths were sparse and did not consistently correlate with the peaks in monthly infection rates, suggesting perhaps localized outbreaks or sporadic increases that were quickly controlled. There is a noticeable rise in cases again beginning in early 2025, with dramatic increases in case numbers reaching up to 2,293 in January 2025. This recent upward trend in 2025 might necessitate further investigation and intervention to prevent widespread outbreaks, possibly examining factors contributing to this resurgence, such as changes in population behavior, environmental factors, or lapses in vaccination coverage.
