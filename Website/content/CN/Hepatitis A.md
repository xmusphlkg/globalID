# Hepatitis A

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a highly contagious liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus. It is primarily transmitted through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated food or water, or close contact with an infected person. Symptoms include fever, fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, dark urine, and jaundice, although some individuals may be asymptomatic, particularly children. Unlike hepatitis B and C, hepatitis A does not lead to chronic liver disease, and most individuals recover completely without lasting effects. Vaccination is the most effective means of prevention, offering long-term protection, while maintaining good hygiene practices can reduce the risk of transmission.

## Highlights

**Key Epidemiological Trends and Current Situation of Hepatitis A in Chinese Mainland as of March 2025:**

1. **Increasing Cases:** Hepatitis A cases have significantly increased in early 2025, with January (2293 cases) and February (2222 cases) showing higher numbers than previous months. <br/>

2. **Stable Death Rate:** The death rate remains stable and low, with few fluctuations; no deaths were reported in March 2025. <br/>

3. **Seasonal Variation:** Higher case numbers during specific months suggest possible seasonal influences on disease transmission. <br/>

4. **Overall Decrease:** There was a general decline in cases from 2015 to early 2023, followed by a surge in 2025. <br/>

The Hepatitis A surveillance data from the Chinese mainland between 2015 and early 2025 shows a general declining trend. Monthly reported cases dropped from over 2000 in early 2015 to under 600 by early 2023. A notable low was observed in January 2023 (523 cases). From this point, case numbers gradually recovered, especially in early 2024, followed by a sharp increase to 2293 cases in January 2025. Despite fluctuations, seasonality does not appear strongly pronounced, though March and July often report higher case counts.

Mortality remained low throughout the observed period, with most months reporting zero deaths. Isolated increases in fatalities occurred in December 2015 (4 deaths), March 2024 (2 deaths), and other individual months such as July 2015, September 2015, and July 2019 (each with 1 death). The rise in early 2025, while significant in case count, was not matched by a corresponding increase in deaths, indicating continued low lethality or effective clinical management.

Overall, the long-term decline suggests improved public health interventions or changes in exposure patterns. However, the recent increase in 2025 may signal changes in transmission dynamics, requiring further evaluation. Continuous monitoring remains critical to detect and investigate unexpected surges promptly.
