# Hepatitis A

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a contagious liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV). It is typically transmitted through the ingestion of contaminated food and water or direct contact with an infectious person. Symptoms include fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, loss of appetite, and jaundice, which usually appear a few weeks after exposure. Unlike other forms of hepatitis, it does not cause chronic liver disease and is generally self-limiting. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent hepatitis A, and improved sanitation and hygiene also play crucial roles in reducing transmission. Effective public health strategies focus on vaccination and education.

## Highlights

- From 2014 to 2022, Hepatitis A cases in the Chinese mainland generally trended downward, reflecting an effective control and prevention strategy. Notably, 2022 had the lowest number of annual cases. <br/>
- A spike in cases was observed in early 2024, with March and April recording over 1,500 cases each, indicating a potential outbreak or change in transmission dynamics. <br/>
- Deaths have remained relatively low, showing effective clinical management despite case fluctuations, with occasional spikes such as the two deaths recorded in March 2024. <br/>
- Continuous monitoring and response are critical as case numbers have shown recent variability, suggesting changing epidemiological patterns. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis A cases in mainland China between 2014 and mid-2024 reveal several trends in the incidence and mortality of the disease over this period. Initially, the monthly cases vary significantly, with a peak of 2733 cases in December 2014, gradually decreasing over subsequent years. Throughout 2014 and 2015, the case numbers hover between 1500 and 2700 per month, followed by a general declining trend. By 2016 onwards, there is a noticeable decrease in cases, indicating reduced transmission or improved public health measures. By early 2023, the monthly case numbers drop to less than 1000, highlighting a significant decrease in the incidence of Hepatitis A.

The death toll related to Hepatitis A during this time remains remarkably low, with only a few sporadic deaths reported per month, typically not exceeding two cases. Notably, the deaths do not correlate directly with the number of reported cases, such as in March 2024, which saw 1542 cases but two deaths reported. This suggests effective clinical management and possibly the role of vaccination or improved healthcare access. Moreover, the seasonal variations in case numbers could be attributed to endemic patterns or changes in surveillance intensity, cultural events, or other social determinants. Overall, these trends indicate a successful reduction in both morbidity and mortality from Hepatitis A in mainland China over the observed period.

The peak number of deaths within the given period seems to occur at the end of 2015, with a total of four deaths in December. This spike in deaths was an anomaly, as the surrounding months had lower or no reported deaths despite similar case numbers. Generally, the case-fatality rate remains low throughout the timeline, highlighting perhaps effective management or less severe strains affecting the population.

Throughout this period, there were several months, especially in the later years, when no fatalities were recorded despite a perceptible baseline of cases. It is also notable that after the initial two years, the number of deaths significantly lessens, and cases trend slightly lower from the initial years. This decline could suggest either improvement in public health interventions, access to better healthcare, or increased immunity within the population due to prior exposure. The decrease in both case numbers and fatalities toward the latter part might also relate to better vaccine coverage or improved sanitation practices, contributing to controlling the spread and severity of hepatitis A infections.
