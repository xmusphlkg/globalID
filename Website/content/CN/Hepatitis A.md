# Hepatitis A

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis A is a highly contagious liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV). It is primarily spread through the ingestion of contaminated food and water or close contact with an infected person. The disease is acute and usually self-limiting, with symptoms including fever, fatigue, jaundice, nausea, and abdominal pain. While most individuals recover fully within weeks to months, Hepatitis A can occasionally cause severe illness. Vaccination is effective in preventing infection, and improved sanitation and hygiene practices are critical in reducing transmission, particularly in areas with poor sanitation infrastructure.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases Over Time:** Hepatitis A cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant decline from over 2600 in 2014 to just over 1000 by September 2024.<br/>
- **Current Stability:** Recent months in 2024 display stable case numbers, maintaining around the 1000 case mark, indicating a controlled situation.<br/>
- **Decreased Mortality:** Deaths associated with Hepatitis A have remained low over the years, with only 2 deaths reported in March 2024, reflecting improved disease management and outcomes.<br/>
- **Seasonal Variations:** Case fluctuations indicate seasonal trends, with occasional peaks, such as in March 2024, often followed by declining numbers.<br/>

The data on Hepatitis A cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a significant decline in the number of reported cases over time. In the earlier years (2014–2016), monthly cases ranged between 1,600 and 2,700, with occasional fluctuations. Notably, the number of cases steadily decreased from 2017 onwards, reaching a low point in 2020-2022, with monthly cases often dropping below 1,000. This trend is particularly evident from 2020, where the cases consistently stayed under 1,300, even during peak months. The overall death toll remained very low, with 0 to 4 deaths per month throughout the years, indicating relatively low mortality associated with the disease.

In terms of seasonality, Hepatitis A appears to exhibit some variability, with higher cases often reported in the spring months (March and April). For instance, in March 2016 and March 2019, the cases spiked to over 2,000, while in other months they were lower. This pattern of spring spikes and summer declines may suggest environmental or behavioral factors influencing transmission. The sharp decline in cases during 2022 and 2023, especially compared to previous years, may be linked to public health interventions or changes in population immunity and behavior, possibly influenced by broader health policies during the COVID-19 pandemic.

The sporadic nature of the deaths, combined with the relatively low death-to-case ratio, suggests that while Hepatitis A remains a public health concern, its lethality appears to be well-managed over time.
