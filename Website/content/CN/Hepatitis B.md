# Hepatitis B

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV). It is a significant global health issue, transmitted through exposure to infectious body fluids, such as blood, semen, and vaginal secretions. Common modes of transmission include sexual contact, sharing needles, and mother-to-child during childbirth. Symptoms range from mild fever and jaundice to more severe liver complications. Chronic infection can lead to cirrhosis or liver cancer. Vaccination is the most effective prevention measure, typically administered in a series of doses. Early detection and antiviral treatment can help manage the disease, reducing long-term health risks.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Hepatitis B cases in the Chinese mainland has shown fluctuations, with peaks observed in March 2024 (152,967 cases), reflecting an ongoing challenge in controlling the spread.  
<br/>
- Deaths remain relatively low compared to the number of cases, though there was a notable peak in January 2020 (80 deaths), suggesting potential seasonal factors or healthcare system impacts.  
<br/>
- A concerning upward trend in cases emerges from 2023 onwards, surpassing previous years, indicating potential lapses in vaccination or prevention efforts.  
<br/>
- Despite fluctuations, mortality rates have generally stabilized, suggesting improvements in healthcare responses to Hepatitis B complications and management.

The monthly death toll remained fairly stable, ranging from 23 to 44, suggesting effective healthcare interventions in controlling mortality rates.

From 2018 onwards, there was a gradual increase in cases, with notable peaks, like March 2018 reaching 120,659 cases, and subsequent peaks in March 2019 and April 2023, each surpassing 110,000 cases. This increase might indicate factors such as population growth and healthcare access disparities impacting transmission. An anomaly occurred in February 2020, where cases fell sharply to 51,506, possibly due to global impacts of COVID-19 disrupting healthcare systems. However, by 2023 and beyond, cases rose, peaking at 152,967 in March 2024. Despite rising cases, deaths largely remained stable, occasionally decreasing to as low as 17 in May 2023, suggesting improvements in medical management.

Overall, the data reflects complex dynamics influenced by healthcare policies, seasonal changes, and demographic shifts. The consistent death rates highlight advances in disease management, while the fluctuating case numbers call for ongoing evaluation of vaccination and public health strategies.
