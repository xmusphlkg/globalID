# Hepatitis B

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection that attacks the liver, causing both acute and chronic diseases. It is caused by the Hepatitis B virus (HBV), which is transmitted through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood, semen, and vaginal secretions. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and loss of appetite. Chronic infection can lead to serious complications, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent Hepatitis B, and antiviral medications can help manage chronic cases. Global efforts continue to reduce the incidence and impact of this disease.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers**: From 2015 to January 2025, Hepatitis B cases have increased, with a notable surge from 2023 onwards, indicating a growing public health issue. <br/>

- **Variable Death Rates**: Deaths have fluctuated without a clear trend, but January 2025 shows a relatively high count, raising concerns about disease management. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Case numbers show seasonal peaks, often in the first quarter, suggesting the need for targeted interventions during these times. <br/>

- **Recent Surge**: A sharp increase in cases during 2023 and 2024, reaching the highest levels by January 2025, calls for urgent epidemiological action. <br/>

The subsequent years showed a gradual decline in both cases and deaths, with 2022 and 2023 reflecting a more stabilized situation. The data suggests that while Hepatitis B remains a significant public health concern, ongoing efforts in healthcare may be leading to better management and outcomes for patients, as evidenced by the decreasing death rates despite the persistent number of cases.
