# Hepatitis B

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection that affects the liver, caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV). It is transmitted through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood, semen, and vaginal secretions. The infection can be acute or chronic, with chronic cases potentially leading to liver cirrhosis or liver cancer. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and dark urine, though some individuals remain asymptomatic. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent hepatitis B, and antiviral medications can help manage chronic infections. Early detection and treatment are crucial for reducing complications.

## Highlights



- **Rising Case Numbers**: From 2015 to 2025, Hepatitis B cases have generally increased, peaking in March 2024 with 152,967 cases, indicating a growing public health issue. <br/>

- **Variable Death Rates**: Deaths have fluctuated, with a notable peak in January 2020 (80 deaths) and a recent rise in January 2025 (46 deaths), suggesting changes in disease severity or healthcare response. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Higher cases are often reported in the first half of the year, possibly due to environmental or social factors. <br/>

- **Recent Case Decline**: As of February 2025, cases decreased to 120,756, indicating potential improvements in control measures. <br/>

The peak in cases is observed in March 2024, with 152,967 cases, indicating a significant rise compared to previous years. This could suggest an outbreak or improved detection and reporting mechanisms.

In terms of mortality, the number of deaths remains relatively low compared to the number of cases, indicating a low case-fatality rate. However, there are occasional spikes in deaths, such as in January 2020, which saw 80 deaths, possibly due to complications or co-infections. The data also shows a slight increase in deaths towards the end of the dataset, which may warrant further investigation to understand the underlying causes. Overall, while the number of cases has increased over the years, the mortality rate has not shown a corresponding rise, suggesting improvements in medical care and management of Hepatitis B in China.
