# Hepatitis B

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) that primarily affects the liver, leading to both acute and chronic disease. It is transmitted through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood, semen, or vaginal secretions, often through sexual contact, sharing of needles, or from mother to child during childbirth. Symptoms of acute infection can include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea, though some individuals may remain asymptomatic. Chronic HBV can lead to severe liver complications, including cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. Vaccination provides effective prevention against hepatitis B.

## Highlights

- Over the past decade, Hepatitis B case numbers have demonstrated an increasing trend, peaking in March 2024 with 152,967 cases before showing a slow decline towards June 2025. <br/>
- From January 2015 to June 2025, monthly Hepatitis B cases consistently remained above 50,000, indicating a substantial endemic presence. <br/>
- While the cases have increased over time, fatalities have not shown a proportional rise, suggesting possible improvements in treatment and healthcare response. <br/>
- With the highest death toll of 80 in January 2020, subsequent years have seen fluctuating but lower mortality, suggesting effective disease management, despite rising case numbers. <br/>

The hepatitis B data from mainland China reveals a pattern of fluctuations with seasonal trends. Between mid-2015 and 2017, monthly cases ranged from approximately 85,000 to 110,000, gradually increasing until 2018. During this time, the average monthly deaths were between the mid-30s and low-40s, reflecting the nature of chronic hepatitis B which often doesn't result in immediate fatalities.

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 saw a notable drop in cases during February, potentially due to reduced hospital visits and disruptions in data collection. However, post-2020, cases increased significantly, peaking in March 2023 at 152,967 cases. Despite this rise, the mortality rate remained relatively constant, with early 2023 reporting monthly deaths in the mid-20s. This could point to improved disease management and increased preventive measures like vaccinations that did not keep pace with rising cases.

The consistent high numbers of cases through to the end of 2024 highlight a need for ongoing public health interventions and awareness campaigns. The stable or slightly reduced mortality rate suggests advancements in treatment and management, but continued efforts are necessary to address this persistent health challenge effectively. Overall, these trends emphasize the importance of sustained surveillance and proactive measures against hepatitis B in the region.
