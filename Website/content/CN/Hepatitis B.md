# Hepatitis B

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection caused by the Hepatitis B virus (HBV) that affects the liver. It is transmitted through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood, vaginal secretions, or semen. Common routes of transmission include unprotected sex, sharing needles, and from mother to child during childbirth. Acute infection can lead to symptoms like fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal pain, while chronic infection may result in liver cirrhosis or cancer. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, and antiviral treatments are available for managing chronic cases. Early detection and regular monitoring are crucial for effective management.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Case Numbers:** Hepatitis B cases have shown a significant upward trend since 2022, peaking in March 2024 with 152,967 cases.<br/>
- **Fluctuating Death Rates:** Deaths have varied, with a peak in February 2020 at 80 deaths, but remain relatively low compared to the number of cases, showing a decreasing trend in 2023-2024 except for occasional spikes.<br/>
- **Seasonal Variations:** Case numbers typically increase from February to July each year, possibly indicating seasonal factors influencing transmission.<br/>
- **Stable Mortality Rate:** Despite increasing cases, the mortality rate remains low, suggesting effective management and treatment strategies.<br/>

The fluctuation of cases across the years shows that Hepatitis B continues to be a persistent public health concern, with a notable spike occurring in recent years. This increase could be attributed to various factors such as changes in reporting practices, testing capabilities, or transmission dynamics.

On the other hand, the death rates have remained relatively low in comparison to the number of cases, ranging between 17 and 55 deaths per month across the 10-year span. Although some months saw higher death tolls, particularly in January 2020 (80 deaths) and August 2020 (53 deaths), the majority of months reported deaths below 50, reflecting potentially improved treatment, management, or early detection of the disease. However, the consistent number of cases, coupled with the fluctuating mortality rates, indicates ongoing challenges in controlling Hepatitis B transmission and its associated health burden in mainland China.

The analysis of Hepatitis B mortality in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals a low death-to-case ratio throughout the period, though the number of cases and deaths shows some variation. Monthly cases generally ranged between 80,000 to 150,000, with spikes observed, such as in March 2024 (152,967 cases). Despite the high case numbers, monthly deaths remained below 100, with most months reporting deaths in the 20 to 50 range. For example, the year 2020 saw the highest single-month death toll in January with 80 deaths, possibly linked to healthcare disruption due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

The death rate does not appear to be strongly correlated with case surges, as significant increases in cases (e.g., August 2023 and March 2024) did not lead to proportional spikes in deaths. This pattern suggests improved management or treatment of Hepatitis B infections over the years, keeping fatality relatively low despite high caseloads.
