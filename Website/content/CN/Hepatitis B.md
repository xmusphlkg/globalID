# Hepatitis B

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection that primarily affects the liver, caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV). It is a major global health concern, leading to both acute and chronic diseases. The virus spreads through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood, vaginal secretions, and semen. While many individuals experience mild symptoms or remain asymptomatic, the virus can lead to severe liver conditions, including cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method. Early diagnosis and antiviral treatments can manage the infection and reduce the risk of long-term liver damage.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases:** There is a significant upward trend in Hepatitis B cases in the Chinese mainland, particularly noticeable from 2023 onwards, with a peak in March 2024 reaching 152,967 cases. <br/>
- **Stabilized Death Rates:** Despite the increasing number of cases, the monthly death toll has remained relatively stable, fluctuating between 20 to 35 deaths per month from 2023 to mid-2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variation:** Monthly case counts suggest potential seasonal patterns, with lower counts typically seen in winter months and higher counts in the summer. <br/>
- **Recent Peak:** July 2024 recorded 145,530 cases, indicating a persistent and substantial public health challenge. <br/>

Mortality trends are less consistent than case numbers but show spikes aligned with high case counts, such as in early 2020, particularly January, which saw significantly elevated deaths. The noticeable spikes in both cases and deaths at various points highlight the importance of robust public health responses, including vaccination efforts and sustained monitoring, to control this chronic viral infection's spread and impact. An analysis of the reported data on Hepatitis B deaths in the Chinese mainland from July 2014 to July 2024 reveals some notable patterns and trends. Over the decade, the monthly reported deaths associated with Hepatitis B exhibit a moderate level of variability, with no extreme outliers except for a noticeable spike in January 2020, where the death toll reached 80, significantly higher than in other months. This spike could be attributed to overlapping health crises or seasonal factors that compounded the vulnerabilities of individuals with Hepatitis B.

Despite the fluctuating number of cases reported each month, the death rate remains relatively low, consistently below 55 deaths per month, with most months recording deaths in the 20s to 40s range. This suggests a potential improvement in healthcare interventions, early detection, and treatment efficacy over the years. However, the consistent occurrence of deaths, despite high case numbers, indicates that while the disease management has improved, it still poses a significant public health challenge. The data highlights the need for continued focus on prevention, timely treatment, and comprehensive healthcare strategies to further reduce the mortality associated with Hepatitis B.
