# Hepatitis B

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection that attacks the liver, caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV). It is transmitted through contact with infectious bodily fluids, such as blood, semen, and vaginal secretions. HBV can cause both acute and chronic liver disease, leading potentially to cirrhosis or liver cancer. Many people with chronic HBV are asymptomatic, making it vital for individuals at risk to undergo screening. Vaccination offers effective prevention, while antiviral medications can help manage chronic cases. Worldwide, efforts continue to increase HBV awareness, improve access to healthcare, and enhance vaccination coverage.

## Highlights

- **Overall Increase in Cases:** Hepatitis B cases have shown an overall increasing trend from 2015 to 2025, peaking recently in early 2024 with monthly cases exceeding 150,000. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Deaths:** The number of deaths attributed to Hepatitis B varies, but a noticeable spike was experienced in January 2020 with 80 deaths, while recent months show fewer than 50 deaths monthly. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** There are seasonal changes in both cases and deaths, with higher numbers typically seen during the early months each year. <br/>

- **Recent Decline:** As of mid-2025, both cases and deaths appear to be declining slightly from previous peaks, indicating potential improvements or interventions. <br/>

The Hepatitis B case data in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 presents a complex picture of fluctuation and eventual increase over the decade. Monthly reports show considerable variability, with a general pattern of notably high cases around the turn of each year, especially between January and March. A dramatic peak in cases appeared in March 2024, with 152,967 cases, indicating possible seasonal patterns or changes in reporting that may have increased counts. These spikes are followed by decreases, only to be followed by subsequent increases, suggesting consistent transmission dynamics possibly influenced by seasonal variations.

Associated deaths, however, show a much smaller annual pattern and do not always align with case peaks. For instance, March 2023 saw high reported cases but relatively fewer deaths, implying potential improvements in healthcare response, better management of Hepatitis B, or reporting discrepancies. Generally, while deaths vary, they don't increase sharply alongside case surges, pointing to effective intervention strategies or inconsistency between infection severity and fatality rates. This data underscores the need to explore underlying factors such as healthcare effectiveness or environmental conditions that might impact these statistics. These insights are crucial for refining public health strategies to prevent and manage future outbreaks effectively, particularly by addressing potential seasonal or healthcare system-related influences.
