# Hepatitis B

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) that affects the liver and can lead to both acute and chronic disease. It spreads through contact with infected blood, semen, or other body fluids, and can be transmitted via sexual contact, sharing needles, or from mother to child at birth. Symptoms include fatigue, jaundice, abdominal pain, and nausea, though some people may remain asymptomatic. Chronic infection increases the risk of liver cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. Hepatitis B is preventable through vaccination and manageable with antiviral medications in chronic cases.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis B cases in mainland China have shown a rising trend from early 2023 to early 2024, peaking at over 152,000 in March 2024, then decreasing steadily through April 2025. <br/>
- Annual seasonal surges continue to occur in March and summer months, consistent with historical seasonal trends in infection rates. <br/>
- Although absolute case numbers have increased since 2023, the fatality rate remains low and relatively stable, suggesting improved disease management. <br/>
- As of April 2025, the monthly number of reported cases declined to 113,974, marking a 25% drop from the peak in 2024, indicating potential effectiveness of recent public health interventions. <br/>

From 2015 to 2025, the data reflect a persistently high burden of Hepatitis B in the Chinese mainland, with monthly reported cases fluctuating between approximately 82,000 and 153,000. A progressive increase in reported cases becomes evident over the examined period, particularly from 2023 onwards, when monthly figures consistently exceed 120,000. The seasonal trend present in earlier years—with peaks typically in Q1 and Q3 and troughs in February—remains discernible but narrows over time. This surge may partly reflect improvements in case detection and reporting systems or increased population testing. Notably, 2020 shows a marked dip in cases during February and March, likely linked to disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic, followed by a gradual recovery.

Mortality data, while less variable, remain relatively stable throughout the period, generally ranging from the low 20s to around 50 monthly deaths. However, there is no strong proportional trend corresponding with the rise in reported cases, which may suggest enhanced early detection and better clinical management mitigating severe outcomes. Noteworthy is the spike in deaths around early 2020 and late 2024, which could correlate with delayed treatments or episodic health system strain. Overall, while infection detection appears to increase, mortality has remained comparatively flat, raising questions about the underlying true incidence versus surveillance artifacts.

While monthly deaths fluctuate, certain years exhibit notable deviations. For instance, January 2020 recorded 80 deaths despite a relatively typical case load (~91,000), likely influenced by the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, straining healthcare access and indirectly impacting Hepatitis B outcomes. Other spikes, such as 55 deaths in January 2019 and 51 in August 2021, may reflect periodic surveillance variations or reporting consolidations.

Over time, particularly post-2022, a divergence emerges: case counts steadily climb, reaching a peak of over 150,000 in March 2024, yet the death toll does not escalate accordingly. Instead, fatalities appear more stable or even decreasing in some intervals amid rising detection. This decoupling suggests potential improvements in clinical detection, early treatment access, or a demographic shift toward lower-risk populations being diagnosed. Thus, while disease burden in terms of incidence remains substantial, mortality control indicates progress in public health response and management efficiency.
