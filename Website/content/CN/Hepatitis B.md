# Hepatitis B

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) that affects the liver and can lead to both acute and chronic disease. It spreads through contact with infected blood, semen, or other body fluids, often via unprotected sex, needle sharing, or from mother to child at birth. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea, though many cases are asymptomatic. Chronic infection can result in liver cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma. A safe and effective vaccine is available and offers long-term protection, making prevention and early detection critical.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis B cases in mainland China rose steadily from 2022 to a peak of over 150,000 in March 2024, but declined to 108,245 cases in May 2025, suggesting improved surveillance or intervention. <br/>
- Monthly deaths attributed to Hepatitis B have remained relatively low and stable, with May 2025 recording 32 deaths—down from some monthly peaks of over 50 during earlier years. <br/>
- A seasonal trend persists, with higher case counts often appearing in spring and summer months; this may indicate environmental or behavioral influences. <br/>
- Overall, while incidence remains high, the relatively low fatality rate implies effective disease management and access to treatment. <br/>

From mid-2015 to mid-2025, reported monthly Hepatitis B cases in mainland China exhibit a clear increasing trend, with average monthly totals rising from roughly 90,000 in 2015 to a peak exceeding 140,000 by 2024. Seasonal fluctuations are evident — lower case numbers often appear in February, likely related to healthcare access limitations during the Spring Festival period, while March consistently shows a sharp rebound in diagnoses. The data suggest improvements in case detection and reporting over time rather than a true epidemic expansion, as the increase aligns with growing medical surveillance capacity and public health mobilization.

Despite this rise in case volumes, mortality linked to Hepatitis B remains relatively stable and low, averaging 30–45 monthly deaths across most years. Notably, even as reported cases almost doubled from 2015 to 2024, deaths did not proportionally increase, implying improved management, treatment access, and earlier diagnosis. Exceptions such as the death spike in early 2020 could be attributed to reporting disruptions or secondary effects from the COVID-19 pandemic. The decoupling between case detection and fatality rates may indicate a shifting disease burden from acute to chronic asymptomatic infections detectable via expanded screening.

An analysis of the monthly death data for Hepatitis B in mainland China from May 2015 through May 2025 reveals several key patterns in mortality trends. Despite relatively stable case numbers over the years—with minor seasonal and reporting variations—the number of monthly deaths experienced significant variability. There were notable mortality peaks, such as in January 2020 (80 deaths), likely influenced by broader healthcare disruptions concurrent with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Similarly, elevated death counts appeared sporadically (e.g., 55 in January 2019, 53 in September 2020). However, 2023 and beyond reflect a generally downward mortality shift, with multiple months below 25 deaths despite increased reported case counts, suggesting improved disease management, earlier diagnosis, or broader antiviral therapy access.

The overall mortality rate appears to decrease when considering the rising case numbers post-2022 alongside stabilized or declining death counts. For instance, while monthly cases rose steadily to over 140,000 in mid-2024, the corresponding deaths often remained within the 20–40 range, lowering the deaths-to-cases ratio compared to earlier years. These shifts may point toward enhancements in public health interventions, extended vaccination impact, or changes in disease surveillance practices, aligning with national goals for viral hepatitis control.
