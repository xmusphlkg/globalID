# Hepatitis B

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV), which primarily affects the liver. It is transmitted through contact with infected blood or bodily fluids, commonly via unprotected sexual contact, sharing needles, or from mother to child at birth. Acute infection may be asymptomatic or present with jaundice, fatigue, and abdominal pain, while chronic infection can progress to cirrhosis, liver failure, and hepatocellular carcinoma. Vaccination is available and highly effective in preventing transmission and disease. Early detection and appropriate management are essential in controlling its spread.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis B case numbers in the Chinese mainland showed a rising trend from 2015 to mid-2024, reaching monthly peaks near 152,967 in March 2024 before declining through late 2025.<br/>
- Despite case increases in recent years, monthly deaths remained relatively stable, ranging from 18 to 55 over the past decade, with no sharp upward or downward shifts.<br/>
- In 2025, cases steadily declined throughout the year, dropping from 145,071 in January to 109,580 in December, indicating improved disease control.<br/>
- The overall situation suggests ongoing endemicity with periodic surges, but stable mortality reflects effective management and possibly improved healthcare interventions.<br/>

Analysis of reported Hepatitis B cases in the Chinese mainland from late 2015 to 2025 shows marked monthly and annual variability. Early years (2015–2019) featured relatively stable numbers with cases generally ranging between 85,000 to 113,000 and deaths fluctuating between 19 and 55 most months. In 2020, reported cases dropped sharply in February (51,506), coinciding with the onset of COVID-19 and likely reflecting disruptions in health services and surveillance, but numbers soon returned to prior levels. From 2021 onward, cases began trending upward, frequently exceeding 120,000 per month and peaking at over 150,000 in March 2024—all while deaths largely remained steady and low, typically below 50 per month.

Seasonal variations are apparent, with higher notifications during spring and summer months and lower counts in winter, possibly linked to health-seeking behaviors, reporting patterns, or social factors. The divergence between rising case numbers and stable low mortality in recent years suggests expanded screening or improved chronic disease management; it may also point to changes in surveillance or reporting practices. The spike in cases seen from 2023 onward calls for careful interpretation, considering both epidemiological and administrative drivers. Overall, these findings highlight the persistent burden of Hepatitis B as a public health issue in China, and reinforce the need for continued vigilance in vaccination programs, case detection, and care strategies.

Analysis of Hepatitis B deaths in Chinese mainland from late 2015 to the end of 2025 demonstrates stable monthly mortality, mostly ranging from 17 to 55 despite consistently high case numbers. This stability persists over time, even as reported cases increase notably from 2023 onward, with monthly counts frequently exceeding 120,000. Death spikes—such as the peak of 80 in January 2020—are rare and likely linked to exceptional circumstances, including potential healthcare disruptions during the COVID-19 pandemic, but do not reflect a sustained rise.

Crucially, the absence of a corresponding surge in deaths during periods of increased case reporting suggests improvements in detection, reporting policy, or treatment access, rather than an escalation in severe hepatitis B outcomes. Periodic mild increases in deaths align imperfectly with case surges, further supporting the view that case fatality remains low. These trends point to effective public health interventions, such as widespread antiviral therapy and chronic disease management, contributing to low and stable mortality. Overall, the chronic burden of hepatitis B persists, yet the stable and relatively modest death toll underscores successes in limiting progression to fatal outcomes at the population level. This pattern highlights enduring resilience and effectiveness in China’s hepatitis B control and clinical management efforts over the decade.
