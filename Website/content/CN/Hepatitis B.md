# Hepatitis B

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV), posing a significant global health risk. Transmitted through contact with infected body fluids like blood, semen, or vaginal fluids, HBV spreads via childbirth, unprotected sex, or shared needles. Symptoms vary from mild, such as fatigue and jaundice, to severe, potentially leading to chronic liver disease or cancer. Effective prevention is available through vaccination, and treatments can manage chronic conditions. Control efforts include regular screening and adherence to preventive practices, underscoring the importance of vaccination in curtailing the infection's impact worldwide.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis B cases in China show fluctuating trends, with a peak in March 2024 at 152,967 cases, then declining to 105,033 in June 2025, indicating episodic surges.<br/>
- Death rates are relatively low compared to cases, with variability seen; high deaths occurred in January 2020 at 80, though recent months show a decline.<br/>
- Seasonal variation is evident, with summer months (July 2023 and July 2024) experiencing higher cases.<br/>
- The trends emphasize the endemic nature of Hepatitis B, necessitating ongoing vigilance and preventive healthcare measures to manage sustained transmission.<br/>

An analysis of Hepatitis B cases in the Chinese mainland from mid-2015 to mid-2025 shows an increase with typical fluctuations. Initially, around mid-2015, the cases hover near 90,000, occasionally exceeding 100,000, especially in months like March in 2016, 2017, and 2018. This consistency shows the entrenched nature of the disease before a notable dip occurs in February 2020 with 51,506 cases, likely due to the COVID-19 pandemic affecting healthcare operations.

Despite the upward trend, there are distinct declines in December 2022 (59,498 cases) and early 2023 (74,790 cases in January), deviating from previous patterns. These dips may reflect successful public health interventions or changes in reporting practices. The mortality data also shows some variability but remains relatively stable, usually between 23 and 53 monthly deaths. Interestingly, there’s a spike in January 2020 with 80 deaths, suggesting a complex interaction of factors affecting mortality beyond case count alone.

Overall, the data give insight into the persistent and adaptive nature of Hepatitis B in China, hinting at the effectiveness of disease management and the impact of wider public health challenges like the COVID-19 pandemic. This trend analysis underlines the importance of sustained public health efforts and continuous monitoring for disease control.

The total number of deaths per month generally remains consistent, with occasional spikes. On average, monthly deaths often oscillate between the mid-30s to high 40s, with sporadic decreases or increases beyond these ranges. This is indicative of varying factors potentially influencing fatality rates, including access to healthcare, public health interventions, variations in reporting, or perhaps seasonal fluctuations in viral transmission.

A particular pattern that emerges over time is the seemingly stable mortality despite an increase in cases over the years. For instance, the period between 2023 and 2025 reflects a steady increase in reported cases. However, the number of deaths, rather than increasing proportionally, exhibits a more subdued rise, suggesting improvements in medical management or earlier detection and treatment, leading to better outcomes. The notable exception is the year 2020, where January shows an unusually high number of deaths; this could reflect either a true spike or an anomaly due to reporting or transcription discrepancies.
