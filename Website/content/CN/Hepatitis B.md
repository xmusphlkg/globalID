# Hepatitis B

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis B is a viral infection that affects the liver and can lead to both acute and chronic disease. It is caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) and is transmitted through contact with infectious body fluids, such as blood or semen. Chronic hepatitis B can result in liver cirrhosis or liver cancer. Prevention is primarily through vaccination, which is highly effective. Hepatitis B is a major global health issue, with significant prevalence in Asia and Africa. Early diagnosis and antiviral treatments can manage the infection and prevent severe liver damage.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases**: Hepatitis B cases have shown a general upward trend from 2014 to 2024, peaking at 152,967 cases in March 2024. <br/>
- **Fluctuations in Deaths**: The number of deaths has varied, with a notable spike in January 2020 at 80 deaths, but generally remained below 50 per month. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variations**: Cases often increase in the first quarter of each year, followed by a decline towards the end of the year. <br/>
- **Recent Decline**: As of June 2024, there has been a slight decline in cases compared to earlier months of the same year. <br/>

The reported cases of Hepatitis B in mainland China from 2014 to mid-2024 show an increasing trend. Starting in 2014, monthly cases ranged from approximately 85,000 to 96,000, with minor fluctuations in the number of deaths. In early 2015, a peak of over 104,000 cases was recorded in March, followed by relatively stable numbers until 2017. From 2017 onwards, monthly cases frequently surpassed 100,000, reaching new highs. The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic saw a temporary drop in February 2020, but cases quickly rebounded, with over 120,000 reported in early 2023.

The mortality rate associated with Hepatitis B remained relatively stable, generally below 50 deaths per month, despite occasional spikes such as 80 deaths in January 2020. This suggests that while the number of cases is rising, the healthcare system's ability to manage and treat the disease has kept the death rate relatively low. The trend of increasing cases continues into 2024, with the highest number recorded in March 2024 at nearly 153,000 cases, indicating a persistent public health issue.

An analysis of the Hepatitis B deaths reported in the Chinese mainland from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals significant variability in mortality figures over the decade, despite generally consistent monthly case numbers. From 2014 to 2019, the number of deaths per month typically ranged from the high teens to mid-40s, with only minor fluctuations around these values. For instance, January 2015 saw a notable peak with 43 deaths, while September 2014 recorded a low of 19 deaths, illustrating the relatively stable yet fluctuating pattern of mortality rates.

However, the period from 2020 onwards shows more pronounced variations, with some notable peaks and troughs. January 2020 saw an exceptionally high spike in deaths at 80, which stands out markedly from the surrounding months. This anomaly might be associated with external factors such as concurrent health crises or reporting changes. From 2021 to 2024, while the deaths generally hover around the mid-30s to low 40s, there are sporadic instances of higher numbers, such as 51 deaths in August 2021 and 53 in September 2020, suggesting potential periods of increased virulence or outbreaks. Overall, while the data indicates a broadly stable trend in deaths relative to case numbers, periodic spikes and drops reflect the complex interplay of epidemiological, healthcare, and possibly external socioeconomic factors impacting Hepatitis B mortality in China.
