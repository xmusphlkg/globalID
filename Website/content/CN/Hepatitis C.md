# Hepatitis C

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis C virus (HCV). It is primarily transmitted through contact with infected blood, often via sharing needles, inadequate sterilization of medical equipment, or transfusions with unscreened blood. Many people are asymptomatic in the early stages, but chronic infection can lead to serious complications such as liver cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma. Diagnosis is typically made through blood tests, including HCV antibody and RNA tests. While no vaccine exists, antiviral medications can cure most cases of hepatitis C with high success rates when properly administered.

## Highlights

- **Significant increase in deaths**: From fewer than 20 deaths per month in early years to a peak of 589 in December 2024, indicating a severe escalation in mortality associated with Hepatitis C. <br/>

- **Fluctuations in reported cases**: While monthly cases consistently remained above 15,000, they peaked periodically, demonstrating cyclical trends possibly influenced by seasonal or reporting variability. <br/>

- **Recent decline in both cases and deaths**: By March 2025, a reduction in cases to 17,661 and deaths to 122 suggests possible improvements in treatment or reporting accuracy. <br/>

- **Potential factors**: Emergence of drug resistance, treatment failures, or reporting changes could account for the increasing mortality despite fluctuating case numbers. <br/>

From the dataset, Hepatitis C cases in mainland China between March 2015 and March 2025 show a relatively stable monthly report rate fluctuating between 17,000 and 24,000 cases, with seasonal troughs typically around February, likely due to reduced reporting or testing during the Lunar New Year. There are observable peaks in spring and mid-summer months, suggesting periodic intensification. However, starting in early 2023, case fatality dynamics shift markedly: despite stable incidence levels, deaths increase dramatically, rising from mid-double digits in early 2023 to nearly 600 by the end of 2024, even while case numbers follow their seasonal plateau trend. This divergence indicates a disproportionate increase in severity or complications among reported cases.

This shift in mortality post-2022 may relate to delayed detection, changes in reporting standards, a higher burden of advanced liver disease, or health system disruptions such as therapy access limitation. The mortality pattern becomes particularly acute from mid-2023 onward, where several months report triple-digit deaths without commensurate spikes in case volume. Factors such as co-infection, population aging, or policy changes in disease confirmation and coding are potential drivers. The sudden return to lower deaths in January 2025, followed again by a rising trend, suggests the need to investigate systemic or structural factors influencing both diagnosis and outcome classification during this period.

From 2015 to mid-2022, reported Hepatitis C deaths in mainland China remained relatively stable, hovering mostly in the single to low double digits monthly, despite moderate fluctuations in case numbers. This suggests consistent disease management, with mortality largely decoupled from incidence rates. Seasonal variations are present but minor, and no major mortality spikes occurred during this period, indicating effective control and treatment efforts.

However, starting in late 2022, there was a marked and sustained rise in deaths. Monthly fatalities rapidly escalated from under 20 to well over 100 by mid-2023 and peaked at nearly 600 deaths in December 2024, despite case numbers not increasing at the same rate. This substantial spike in mortality suggests a possible degradation in health system response, delays in diagnosis or treatment, or an emergence of more severe disease presentations. By late 2023 through 2024, triple-digit death counts became consistent, indicating a new and serious phase in Hepatitis C outcomes requiring urgent attention. Even as deaths declined modestly in early 2025, they remained elevated over earlier years. This shift highlights a public health threat demanding immediate investigation into the causes driving mortality—be it treatment access, disease progression, or co-morbidities—and warrants renewed prevention and care interventions to reverse the elevated fatality trend.
