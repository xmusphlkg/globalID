# Hepatitis C

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV). It's primarily transmitted through blood-to-blood contact, often via contaminated needles or unscreened blood transfusions. HCV can lead to both acute and chronic infections, with chronic Hepatitis C potentially causing severe liver damage, cirrhosis, and liver cancer. Many infected individuals may not exhibit symptoms for years, making early detection challenging. Treatment options have improved significantly, with antiviral medications now achieving cure rates exceeding 90%. Preventive measures include screening blood products and safe injection practices.

## Highlights

- **Significant Increase in Deaths (2022-2024)**: There has been a sharp rise in deaths from Hepatitis C, with a peak of 393 deaths in December 2023 and consistently high numbers into 2024.<br/>
- **Fluctuating Case Numbers**: Hepatitis C cases have shown considerable monthly variation, peaking at 24666 in March 2018 and generally ranging between 18000 and 24000 cases per month.<br/>
- **Recent Decline in Cases**: In 2024, there has been a gradual decrease in reported cases from 22215 in March to 18600 in June.<br/>
- **Monthly Trends**: Hepatitis C cases typically show an increase in early months each year, with notable peaks in March and a decline by mid-year.<br/>

Between 2014 and 2024, Hepatitis C cases in mainland China exhibit fluctuating yet substantial numbers with several discernible trends. Notably, the incidence generally rises from 2014, peaking sporadically but remaining above 17,000 cases monthly. The highest case numbers are observed from March 2015 (22,288 cases) and March 2018 (24,666 cases) to peaks in 2023, specifically in March (23,625 cases) and August (23,214 cases). These peaks highlight periods of heightened transmission or reporting efficacy. However, the trends for deaths show a dramatic increase starting in late 2022. For instance, the monthly death toll climbs from an average of below 20 before July 2022 to staggering numbers in 2023, peaking at 393 in December 2023, suggesting possible factors like emerging viral strains, healthcare resource strain, or reporting changes.

Moreover, the data presents a conspicuous escalation in reported deaths from mid-2022 onwards, diverging sharply from earlier years' relatively stable and low death counts. For example, deaths rise sharply from 18 in July 2022 to unprecedented numbers such as 151 in May 2023 and 393 in December 2023. This stark increase may indicate a significant shift in the disease's impact, potentially due to factors such as decreased treatment efficacy, increased disease severity, or reporting accuracy improvements. The later years, particularly from 2023 onwards, underscore an urgent need for public health interventions focusing on Hepatitis C management and mortality reduction.

For instance, monthly deaths typically ranged between 5 and 15, reflecting a controlled but persistent public health challenge. However, starting in mid-2022, a noticeable upward trend in mortality rates began to emerge, with deaths increasing sharply from 45 in August 2022 to an unprecedented 393 in December 2023. This dramatic rise points to a possible exacerbation of the disease's impact, which could be due to several factors such as changes in virus virulence, healthcare system strains, or demographic shifts.

This period of escalating deaths also coincided with relatively high but fluctuating numbers of reported cases, suggesting a potential correlation between the increased incidence and mortality rates. The most alarming increase in deaths occurred despite some months experiencing lower case counts, indicating that factors other than the sheer number of cases might be contributing to the increased fatalities. The data for 2024 continues to show elevated death counts, with June reporting 239 deaths, which although lower than the peak in late 2023, remains significantly higher than earlier years. This persistent high mortality rate warrants urgent investigation and intervention to identify and mitigate the underlying causes driving this deadly trend.
