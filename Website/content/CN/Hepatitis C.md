# Hepatitis C

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV). It is primarily transmitted through blood-to-blood contact, often via intravenous drug use, unsafe medical practices, or unscreened blood transfusions. Approximately 70-85% of infected individuals develop chronic Hepatitis C, which can lead to serious complications such as cirrhosis, liver cancer, and liver failure. Many people with Hepatitis C remain asymptomatic for years, making early diagnosis challenging. Antiviral medications can effectively treat and often cure the infection, highlighting the importance of screening and early intervention.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers:** Hepatitis C cases have fluctuated but generally shown an increasing trend from about 19,182 in May 2014 to 20,462 in May 2024, indicating a persistent public health issue. <br/>
- **Notable Increase in Deaths:** There has been a significant rise in the number of deaths, from 8 in May 2014 to a alarming 174 in May 2024, suggesting increased severity of infections or reporting improvements. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variations:** Monthly fluctuations in reported cases suggest possible seasonal or reporting influences on hepatitis C incidence. <br/>
- **Recent Sharp Spikes:** Particularly from late 2023 to early 2024, the fatalities spiked, necessitating further investigation into potential outbreaks or treatment challenges. <br/>

This trend shows some peaks in specific months, particularly in the early months of each year (e.g., March 2015, March 2016, and March 2017), suggesting possible seasonal variabilities or related public health interventions during these periods. 

From 2020 onwards, the fluctuation pattern continues with a few extraordinary months, like February 2020, showing a dramatic decrease to 9,068 cases, potentially related to disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic and its effects on healthcare reporting and services. Entering the year 2022, peculiar spikes in fatality rates are observed contrasting earlier years. Deaths in August 2022 rise dramatically to 45 and continue escalating, reaching an unprecedented peak of 393 in December 2023. This sudden rise indicates a potential exacerbation in Hepatitis C outcomes possibly due to changes in the disease virulence, healthcare access issues or testing and reporting. The pattern in 2024 still shows substantial deaths with sporadic reductions, indicating a need for robust epidemiological scrutiny to understand these mortality surges and tailor intervention strategies accordingly.

A detailed examination of the mortality data associated with Hepatitis C in the Chinese mainland reveals a generally low and stable trend in deaths from 2014 to early 2022. During this period, monthly deaths oscillated mostly between single digits and low double digits, with occasional minor increases that did not demonstrate any significant upward trend. For example, in 2017 March, the highest number for that year reached 21 deaths. This suggests that while Hepatitis C cases were substantial, the fatality rate remained relatively controlled.

However, a notable deviation appears from mid-2022 onwards, with a significant surge in deaths starting from August 2022, where mortalities drastically increased to 45 and then continued to rise substantially in the subsequent months. By October 2023, the situation became more concerning as deaths peaked at an unprecedented 224, with the highest recorded in December 2023 at 393. This sharp increase indicates a potential surge in the mortality rate of Hepatitis C, which could be attributed to several factors such as a change in the viral strain, resistance to treatment, lapsing healthcare infrastructure, or a reporting discrepancy. Moreover, although case numbers did not change dramatically, the proportional increase in deaths suggests a critical need for healthcare evaluation and intervention strategies to manage this dramatic escalation effectively.
