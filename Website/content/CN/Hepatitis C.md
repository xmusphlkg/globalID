# Hepatitis C

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV). It is primarily transmitted through blood-to-blood contact, often during activities such as sharing needles or through unscreened blood transfusions. The virus can lead to both acute and chronic liver conditions, with chronic cases potentially resulting in severe complications like liver cirrhosis or liver cancer. Many individuals with Hepatitis C experience few or no symptoms initially, making early detection challenging. Effective antiviral treatments are available that can cure the infection, emphasizing the importance of testing and early intervention to prevent long-term liver damage.

## Highlights

- **Surging Mortality**: From 2022 to mid-2025, Hepatitis C-related deaths in mainland China rose sharply, with deaths peaking at 589 in December 2024 before slightly decreasing in 2025.  
<br/>
- **Declining Cases**: Monthly cases, fluctuating between 15,000 and 25,000 from 2015, began decreasing in early 2023, with 2025 showing lower averages.  
<br/>
- **Recent Stabilization**: By June 2025, both cases and deaths show signs of stabilization compared to previous peaks. Continued monitoring remains vital.  
<br/>
- **Public Health Alert**: The rising mortality highlights the need for enhanced diagnostic, treatment, and preventive measures to mitigate Hepatitis C's impact in the region.  
<br/>

Initially, from mid-2015 to late 2019, case numbers fluctuated, with spikes typically observed in March each year, notably in March 2017, 2018, and 2019. Despite high case counts, deaths remained consistently low, usually in single or low double digits, indicating effective management and treatment strategies during this period.

A significant trend shift emerged post-2023 when death numbers began to increase dramatically even as cases remained high. This suggests possible lapses in healthcare interventions or new strains contributing to increased mortality rates. From late 2022, death tolls reached concerning levels, with a peak of 589 deaths in December 2024â€”a sharp rise from previous years. This rise indicates potential worsening health complications or deficiencies in healthcare delivery despite consistent case counts.

While case numbers began to decline modestly in early 2025, high death counts persisted, underscoring the need for improved healthcare strategies. This trend points to critical health system challenges requiring urgent attention to enhance diagnostic, treatment, and prevention measures. Focused resource allocation and policy interventions are necessary to mitigate mortality rates linked to Hepatitis C, ensuring better patient outcomes and healthcare sustainability in the region.

A historical analysis of hepatitis C mortality data in the Chinese mainland from June 2015 to June 2025 reveals significant fluctuations. In the earlier stage of the timeline, deaths were relatively low, averaging around 10 per month, despite varying case numbers. For instance, between June 2015 and June 2020, mortality rarely exceeded 13 deaths monthly even as cases fluctuated between 15,000 and nearly 25,000. This period reflects a stable mortality rate, indicating that treatment was likely effective in preventing fatalities or that the recorded cases were not progressing to the advanced stages of liver disease typically associated with hepatitis C, such as cirrhosis or liver cancer.

Starting from mid-2022, however, there is a dramatic and concerning surge in the number of fatalities that continues into 2025. This shift marks a critical period where monthly deaths escalated from tens to several hundreds. For example, deaths reached 151 in May 2023, sharply increasing to 589 by December 2024. This alarming trend suggests either an increase in the severity of reported hepatitis C cases, potentially indicating a higher progression to severe liver complications, or possibly an emergent issue with healthcare access or treatment efficacy. This escalation calls for an urgent review of public health strategies, availability and accessibility of antiviral treatments, and perhaps investigation into other factors such as coinfection rates, which might have compounded the mortality rate during this period.
