# Hepatitis C

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis C virus (HCV), which is transmitted primarily through blood-to-blood contact. It often occurs through practices such as sharing needles, inadequate sterilization of medical equipment, and transfusions of unscreened blood. While many individuals with Hepatitis C experience no symptoms, chronic infections can lead to severe liver damage, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Diagnosis is usually made through blood tests. Treatment involves antiviral medications that can clear the virus from the body, and new therapies offer high cure rates, providing hope for reducing global disease burden.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers:** Hepatitis C cases in mainland China have fluctuated over the years, showing a general decline since late 2023 into 2025. <br/>
- **Increased Mortality:** There has been a notable rise in Hepatitis C-related deaths, peaking in late 2023 and staying high through 2024, with some decrease noted in early 2025. <br/>
- **Effects of Control Measures:** The recent decrease in cases by August 2025 suggests improvements in screening, public health interventions, and treatment strategies. <br/>
- **Ongoing Public Health Issue:** Despite fewer cases, the high mortality rates in 2025 highlight that Hepatitis C remains a critical public health concern requiring ongoing attention. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis C in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 highlights several trends and shifts. From 2015 to 2019, monthly cases ranged from approximately 16,000 to 25,000, with deaths averaging around 9 per month. A consistent pattern of case spikes was noted in March over these years, although mortality rates remained stable, indicating a constant health burden from Hepatitis C.

In 2020, the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic corresponds with a drastic drop in cases, particularly in February, which may reflect decreased reporting or healthcare access. Post-2020, case numbers returned to prior levels, but a stark contrast is observed in the rising death tolls starting from 2022. Deaths significantly increased, reaching a peak of 589 in December 2024, despite stable case numbers, suggesting changes in healthcare delivery, potential shifts in viral characteristics, or socio-economic impacts.

By 2025, a reduction in both cases and deaths is evident, though mortality rates remain higher compared to pre-2022 levels. These patterns underscore the need for focused epidemiological investigations to assess healthcare challenges, potential viral mutations, and changes in socio-economic conditions affecting patient outcomes. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for improving management and treatment strategies for Hepatitis C in China.

In the early years, from 2015 to 2019, the number of deaths remained relatively stable and low, with monthly deaths often fluctuating between 5 and 17. During these years, the highest recorded death toll in a month was 21, which was documented in March 2017. The consistency in these figures suggests a level of control in managing the disease or a steady epidemiological environment with regard to healthcare interventions targeted at Hepatitis C.

However, beginning in 2020, a more variable and increasingly severe pattern emerges. From April 2020 onwards, there is a noticeable uptick in the death counts which becomes significantly pronounced by mid-2022. These escalations reach alarming peaks from 2023 to 2025 with deaths climbing from below a hundred to several hundreds per month, notably reaching as high as 589 in December 2024. This sharp rise indicates a worsening situation, possibly associated with systemic challenges in healthcare, emerging strains, reduced treatment efficacy, or gaps in preventive measures, necessitating urgent public health interventions and further investigations into underlying causes.
