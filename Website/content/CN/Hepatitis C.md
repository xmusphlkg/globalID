# Hepatitis C

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a viral infection primarily affecting the liver, caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV). It is mainly transmitted through blood-to-blood contact, often through sharing needles or improperly sterilized medical equipment. The infection can be acute, resolving on its own, or chronic, leading to long-term liver problems such as cirrhosis or liver cancer. Many individuals may be asymptomatic for years, complicating timely diagnosis. Antiviral medications can effectively cure most cases of Hepatitis C, and recent advancements have improved treatment success rates significantly. Early detection and treatment are crucial for preventing serious liver complications.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases**: From 2014 to August 2024, the number of Hepatitis C cases has fluctuated but remained high, with a peak in recent years, particularly in 2023 and 2024, where monthly cases exceeded 22,000.  
<br/>
- **Surge in Deaths**: A significant increase in deaths occurred from 2022 onwards, reaching a dramatic peak of 395 deaths in August 2024.  
<br/>
- **2023-2024 Crisis**: The most notable spike in both cases and deaths occurred between 2023 and 2024, suggesting a worsening epidemic with an alarming rise in mortality.  
<br/>
- **Seasonal Variability**: Cases show some seasonal variability, with typically lower case numbers in the early months of each year.

The data for Hepatitis C in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows fluctuations in both cases and deaths. From 2014 to 2020, monthly cases ranged from around 14,000 to 24,000, with deaths generally under 20 per month. The case counts fluctuated, peaking at around 24,000 in March 2017 and March 2019, but deaths remained relatively stable. This suggests that while cases varied, the mortality rate was consistently low during this period.

From 2022 onward, a noticeable increase in deaths occurred, particularly from August 2022, when fatalities began to rise significantly. By late 2023, deaths spiked dramatically, reaching 292 in November and 393 in December, even though case numbers did not show a corresponding sharp rise. This suggests the increase in deaths might be due to factors like healthcare capacity issues or a more virulent strain. The trend from 2023 highlights a critical shift, with the rise in mortality becoming a key concern, indicating a need for more focused public health interventions to address the increasing death rate despite relatively stable case numbers.
