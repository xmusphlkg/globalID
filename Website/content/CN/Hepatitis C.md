# Hepatitis C

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV), primarily transmitted through contact with infected blood. It often leads to chronic liver disease, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Most newly infected individuals are asymptomatic, making early detection difficult. The infection can become chronic in about 75–85% of cases. HCV is commonly spread via shared needles, blood transfusions before 1992, and unsterile medical equipment. There is no vaccine, but antiviral medications can cure over 95% of cases. Early diagnosis and treatment are essential to prevent serious complications.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis C case numbers in mainland China have fluctuated moderately since 2015, with monthly counts generally ranging from 16,000 to 24,000 and a slow decline observed in early 2025. <br/>
- A sharp and sustained rise in reported deaths began in early 2023, peaking at 589 deaths in December 2024—a dramatic increase compared to previous years where monthly deaths rarely exceeded 20. <br/>
- Since January 2025, while case numbers remain relatively stable, mortality remains significantly elevated, suggesting worsening outcomes or reporting changes. <br/>
- The sudden spike in death counts from 2023 onward warrants urgent investigation into diagnostic accuracy, disease management effectiveness, and potential comorbidities or healthcare access issues. <br/>

From 2015 to early 2023, the monthly reported Hepatitis C case numbers on the Chinese mainland demonstrated seasonal oscillations with mild long-term growth. Case counts typically fluctuated between 17,000 to 24,000 per month, peaking in March and dipping in February, likely reflecting healthcare access cycles tied to the Spring Festival. Death counts remained relatively stable during this period, often in single digits to low teens monthly. A shift began in mid-2022: while overall case numbers remained within prior ranges, mortality began to rise unexpectedly—culminating in a sharp divergence from historical patterns starting in early 2023.

From May 2023 onward, a significant public health inflection point becomes apparent. Deaths associated with Hepatitis C rose dramatically despite no corresponding surge in case numbers. This trend intensified through late 2023 into 2024, with monthly fatalities surpassing 300 and reaching nearly 600 in December 2024, even as cases trended down or stagnated. This steep rise suggests a possible deterioration in treatment efficacy, late diagnosis, underreporting during earlier periods, or changes in disease progression dynamics. The mortality surge retained high levels into 2025 despite gradual case declines, indicating a persistent critical lapse in disease management or reporting methodology requiring urgent epidemiological review.

Between 2015 and early 2023, the reported monthly deaths from Hepatitis C in mainland China remained relatively stable, generally ranging between 5 and 20 deaths per month despite moderate fluctuations in case counts. This suggests a consistent clinical management practice and stable mortality-to-case ratios. However, starting from August 2022, a notable shift occurred with death counts rising sharply—first modestly from typical levels (e.g., 45 deaths in August 2022) and then accelerating dramatically through 2023. By December 2023, the number of deaths reached 393 in a single month, a nearly 20-fold increase over the previous baseline, with a corresponding continued surge through 2024. This divergence occurred without a proportionate change in reported case numbers, indicating a decoupling between incidence and mortality.

The sudden and sustained elevation in deaths may reflect systemic disruptions or new co-factors, such as delays in diagnosis or treatment, reductions in healthcare access, emergence of more pathogenic HCV strains, or misclassification of other liver-related mortalities. Another plausible factor is revised surveillance protocols or increased transparency in death reporting. From March 2023 to December 2024, deaths consistently surpassed 100 per month, illustrating either a public health crisis or a reporting artifact that requires urgent epidemiological scrutiny. The marked increase in lethality during this period—without a commensurate surge in case volume—raises concern for measurable changes in disease severity, healthcare delivery capabilities, or data classification practices.
