# Hepatitis C

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV). It's primarily transmitted through blood, often via shared needles, blood transfusions, or through childbirth. The infection can be acute or chronic, with many individuals unaware of their status as symptoms may not appear for years. Chronic Hepatitis C can lead to severe liver damage, including cirrhosis or liver cancer. Diagnosis is typically through blood tests, while treatment options include antiviral medications with a high success rate. Preventive measures focus on reducing blood exposure and increasing awareness of transmission routes.

## Highlights

- **Rising death toll in recent years**: Hepatitis C mortality has sharply increased, particularly since mid-2023, with over 300 deaths monthly in 2024 compared to under 20 deaths per month in 2020. <br/>
- **Fluctuating case counts**: Case numbers have remained relatively stable, ranging between 15,000 and 24,000 monthly since 2020, with no significant surges observed. <br/>
- **High fatalities in 2023â€“2024**: 2023 saw a dramatic rise in deaths, peaking in late 2023 and continuing into 2024, with some months exceeding 300 deaths. <br/>
- **Moderate decline in recent months**: By September 2024, cases dropped to 16,896, the lowest since early 2023, while mortality remained high. <br/>

The data for Hepatitis C cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a fluctuating trend in both reported cases and deaths. From 2014 through mid-2019, cases were relatively stable, averaging between 17,000 to 24,000 per month, with deaths ranging from 4 to 21. Notably, from mid-2020 to early 2021, the number of cases initially dipped, possibly due to the COVID-19 pandemic's impact on healthcare access and reporting. However, after this period, the data shows a return to pre-pandemic levels of around 20,000 monthly cases.

Starting in 2023, a sharp increase in mortality is observed, despite the number of reported cases remaining relatively consistent with prior years. This trend is particularly pronounced from May 2023 onward, where deaths rise significantly, peaking in 2024 with monthly death counts exceeding 300. This suggests either an outbreak of more severe Hepatitis C cases or potentially new complications in diagnosis, treatment, or healthcare system strain. The rapid escalation in deaths amidst steady case numbers highlights the need for further investigation into the underlying causes of this mortality spike.

The analysis of Hepatitis C-related deaths in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals several significant trends. Initially, the number of deaths associated with Hepatitis C remained relatively low and stable, generally fluctuating between single digits and low double digits. This period signified a level of disease management where mortality did not drastically outpace incidence despite a consistent number of cases reported each month. However, a marked shift occurs from late 2022 onwards, with a dramatic increase in the mortality figures. This escalation coincides with consistently high case numbers, suggesting potential changes in virus virulence, healthcare access, or demographic susceptibility.

Beginning around August 2022, deaths increased significantly, reaching alarming levels by the end of 2023 and throughout 2024. This uptrend persists despite some monthly fluctuations in reported cases, indicating a potential decoupling of incidence and mortality rates, which is worrying. Such a pattern may imply either a change in the strains prevalent within populations, inadequate responses to medical management, or a possible healthcare capacity crisis. The spike in mortality particularly warrants epidemiological investigation to identify underlying causes, such as resistance to treatment protocols or socio-economic factors affecting healthcare access. Additionally, these trends might reflect data reporting variations or efforts within the healthcare infrastructure. Understanding and addressing these dynamics is crucial for mitigating the public health impact of Hepatitis C in the region.
