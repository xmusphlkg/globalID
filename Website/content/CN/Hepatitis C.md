# Hepatitis C

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C Virus (HCV), often silent until severe liver damage occurs. The virus spreads primarily through contact with infected blood, typically via shared needles among drug users, but also through unscreened blood transfusions or organ transplants. Less frequently, it can be transmitted sexually or from mother to child during childbirth. Chronic infection can lead to liver cirrhosis and cancer. With advancements in treatment, antiviral medications can cure most cases. Regular screening is crucial for early detection and effective management, significantly improving outcomes for those affected.

- **Stabilization of Cases**: Hepatitis C cases in the Chinese mainland showed stabilization from early 2024 to September 2025, with monthly cases ranging from 14,000 to 22,000. <br/>

Analyzing Hepatitis C data from the Chinese mainland reveals fluctuations in cases and deaths from 2015 to 2025. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, monthly cases ranged between 15,000 to 24,000, and deaths remained mostly in single to low double digits. This period shows a slight seasonal trend with increased cases often seen around March, potentially indicating reporting dynamics. Although deaths were low, certain months like March 2017 saw peaks in mortality, suggesting variations in healthcare access or disease severity during those times.

A significant shift occurs post-2020, with a noticeable rise in reported deaths peaking from 2023 onwards. Death counts increased dramatically, climbing into the hundreds monthly by late 2023 and sustaining high levels through 2025. This suggests either enhanced disease severity, healthcare access challenges, or improved accuracy in reporting fatalities that were possibly underreported earlier. Concurrently, case numbers persist in a high, steady trajectory, suggesting ongoing transmission despite intervention efforts. The dramatic rise in mortality highlights urgent issues in healthcare delivery or management of hepatitis C complications, urging immediate public health actions and investigation into underlying factors influencing these trends. Addressing these trends is crucial for improving outcomes and reducing the disease burden in the region.

The analysis of Hepatitis C deaths in the Chinese mainland from September 2015 to September 2025 reveals some noteworthy patterns and trends. Initially, the number of deaths reported per month remained relatively low and stable, typically fluctuating between 5 and 17 deaths from 2015 to 2021, with insignificant month-to-month variations. During this period, the death rate per reported case was quite marginal, indicating effective control or management of the disease, possibly through medical interventions or public health measures.

However, starting in 2022, a notable upward shift in the number of reported deaths can be observed, with a significant spike in August 2022, when deaths jumped to 45, escalating further in subsequent months. This increase correlated with relatively stable numbers of reported cases, suggesting a potential deterioration in the clinical management of Hepatitis C, emergence of a more virulent strain, or reduced access to healthcare services. The escalation continued through 2023, peaking dramatically towards the end of that year and into 2024, before a bit of oscillation yet remaining high into 2025. Such a trend points to an urgent need for public health intervention to address the underlying causes of this increase, whether they be inadequate public health infrastructure, changes in strain virulence, reduced efficacy of existing treatments, or other socio-economic factors influencing disease outcomes.
