# Hepatitis C

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis C virus (HCV). It is primarily spread through blood-to-blood contact, often via shared needles or transfusions of unscreened blood. The infection can be acute or chronic, with the latter potentially leading to severe liver problems such as cirrhosis, liver failure, or liver cancer if left untreated. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, dark urine, and abdominal pain, although many individuals remain asymptomatic. Antiviral medications can effectively treat and often cure Hepatitis C, making early diagnosis critical.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases and Deaths**: Hepatitis C cases and deaths have significantly increased since 2022, peaking in July 2024 with 20,236 cases and 305 deaths.<br/>
- **Surge in Mortality**: There has been a sharp rise in deaths, particularly since late 2022, indicating potential challenges in disease management.<br/>
- **Seasonal Trends**: Cases often decrease in February, suggesting possible underreporting or seasonal factors.<br/>
- **Need for Action**: The escalating trend in cases and deaths highlights the urgent need for stronger public health interventions and improved access to treatment.

The reported data for Hepatitis C in mainland China between 2014 and 2024 reveals a complex trend in both the number of cases and associated deaths. From 2014 to 2020, the number of reported cases fluctuated around 18,000 to 24,000 per month, with relatively stable but low death rates, typically ranging from 4 to 16 deaths per month. This stability in mortality despite the fluctuations in case numbers suggests consistent healthcare management of Hepatitis C during this period.

However, a noticeable shift occurs beginning in 2023, where there is a significant increase in reported deaths, peaking dramatically in late 2023 and continuing into 2024. While the number of cases remains within a similar range as previous years, the mortality rate escalates sharply, with deaths reaching up to 393 in December 2023 and 305 in July 2024. This sharp increase in mortality, despite a stable or modest rise in case numbers, indicates a potential worsening in the severity of the disease, possible changes in the virus strain, or a decline in the effectiveness of medical interventions. This alarming trend highlights an urgent need for public health interventions to address the rising death toll associated with Hepatitis C in the region.
