# Hepatitis C

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis C is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis C virus (HCV), primarily spread through contact with infected blood. It often becomes a chronic condition, leading to serious complications like liver cirrhosis or liver cancer if untreated. Many people with hepatitis C are asymptomatic for years, making early diagnosis challenging. The disease is commonly transmitted via shared needles, unscreened blood transfusions, or unsafe medical practices. Effective antiviral treatments can cure over 95% of cases. There is no vaccine, so prevention focuses on harm reduction and infection control practices in healthcare settings and among at-risk populations.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis C case numbers in mainland China remained relatively stable from 2015–2022, averaging around 20,000 monthly, with low fatality rates (typically under 15 deaths/month). <br/>
- A marked shift occurred beginning in early 2023, where deaths surged sharply despite only modest case increases, indicating a possible shift in disease severity, reporting, or healthcare access. <br/>
- From April 2023 to April 2025, monthly deaths rose dramatically, peaking at 589 in December 2024, before declining slightly but staying elevated in early 2025. <br/>
- As of April 2025, fatalities remain high (176 deaths), suggesting an ongoing public health concern needing urgent epidemiological investigation and targeted interventions. <br/>

Between 2015 and 2019, reported monthly Hepatitis C cases in mainland China were relatively stable, ranging from 17,000 to 24,000. Seasonal drops, especially in February, were consistent with holiday-related reporting delays. Deaths during this period remained low, typically under 15 per month, reflecting a relatively stable public health burden despite endemic transmission. In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted reporting, with February cases dropping sharply to 9,068. However, mortality remained consistent, showing that health outcomes for Hepatitis C patients were not immediately affected.

A marked shift is visible from mid-2022 onward. While case numbers remained within the historical range, deaths began rising rapidly—from 45 in August 2022 to over 500 monthly by late 2024. The discrepancy between stable case counts and sharply rising deaths suggests dynamic changes in healthcare access, disease management, or population vulnerability. By late 2023 and throughout 2024, death counts reached unprecedented highs, peaking at 589 in December 2024. The sharp and persistent surge in mortality despite consistent infection levels points to potential changes in viral genotype virulence, treatment failure, or systemic issues affecting care for chronic liver disease. Though 2025 began with a decline in case numbers, fatality remained elevated, signaling an urgent need for clinical, epidemiological, and surveillance investigations.

From 2015 to mid-2022, deaths from Hepatitis C in the Chinese mainland remained relatively stable, typically ranging between 5 and 15 per month despite some variation in case numbers. This period reflects a consistent case-fatality pattern, suggesting effective disease management or a less severe clinical course during those years. Occasional spikes—such as 21 deaths in March 2017 or 18 in July 2022—remained limited and did not persist.

However, starting in August 2022, a sustained rise in deaths becomes apparent. The numbers climbed from 45 in August 2022 to 393 by December 2023. In 2024, the surge intensified, peaking at 589 deaths in December, with similarly elevated monthly figures throughout the second half of the year. Case numbers remained relatively steady, indicating that the fatality rate increased rather than the incidence alone. This sharp, sustained elevation in mortality may indicate a shift in disease severity, delays in diagnosis or treatment, increased co-morbidities among the infected population, or systemic strains on healthcare services.

By early 2025, monthly deaths appear to decline slightly—for example, 25 in January—potentially suggesting a break in the upward trend or early effects of intervention. Nonetheless, the late 2022 to 2024 period marks a significant departure from previous patterns and warrants focused epidemiological review to determine the underlying drivers of rising Hepatitis C mortality.
