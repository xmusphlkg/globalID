# Hepatitis D

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV). HDV is a defective RNA virus that requires the presence of hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate and infect liver cells. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood or body fluids, often via sharing needles or unprotected sex. Co-infection with HBV and HDV can lead to severe liver disease, including rapid progression to cirrhosis and increased risk of liver cancer. Effective prevention relies on hepatitis B vaccination.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis D remains a rare disease in mainland China, with monthly case counts consistently below 60 and no reported deaths throughout 2016–2025. <br/>
- Annual cases show a gradual decline over the decade, dropping from monthly averages near 40 in 2016–2018 to about 20 per month in 2023–2025, signaling improved control or lower transmission. <br/>
- No mortality has been observed, indicating adequate clinical management and potentially mild disease presentations among reported cases. <br/>
- Overall, the current situation as of December 2025 is stable, with very low incidence and no evidence of epidemics or heightened public health threat. <br/>

Analysis of Hepatitis D data for Chinese mainland from January 2016 to December 2025 shows persistently low case numbers, with a noticeable decline over time. Initial monthly counts in 2016 and 2017 ranged mostly from 30 to 60 cases, while subsequent years saw totals generally stabilizing between 10 and 30. The most pronounced reduction began in 2020, aligning with the broad health system changes brought about by the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have affected disease transmission, reporting, or healthcare access.

Throughout the ten-year period, not a single death was reported, indicating low severity, successful management, or possible predominance of mild cases in reported data. The total absence of mortality, despite continued case occurrence, implies Hepatitis D has minimal public health impact in the region. Monthly fluctuations, occasionally showing slight increases or decreases, do not display consistent seasonal peaks, which suggests sporadic rather than regular transmission. The lowest numbers were documented from 2022 onward, with cases rarely exceeding the teens in any month. 

This pattern underscores ongoing control of Hepatitis D, likely tied to hepatitis B management due to HDV’s co-infection requirement. While the data suggest transmission remains limited and outcomes are mild, ongoing surveillance is important to detect any significant epidemiological changes, considering the dependency of HDV on HBV prevalence and programmatic hepatitis control efforts.

The number of cases per month has ranged from single digits to approximately 60, with some modest fluctuations but an overall trend toward lower case numbers in more recent years. Throughout this period, however, the absolute absence of reported fatalities is striking, especially given Hepatitis D's known potential for severe outcomes in individuals co-infected with Hepatitis B.

The prolonged zero-deaths record strongly suggests either an extremely low case fatality rate in the context of China’s clinical management or a patient population that is experiencing milder forms of the disease. This pattern might reflect early detection and effective intervention within the healthcare system, or perhaps a reporting focus on less severe, chronic cases. Alternatively, if surveillance or diagnostic criteria have tended to select for lower-risk patients, mortality due to acute/fulminant hepatitis D may not be captured. In summary, although Hepatitis D remains present at low incidence, the absence of fatalities over a decade marks a significant epidemiologic feature, emphasizing either effective healthcare responses or a distinctive epidemiologic profile within mainland China.
