# Hepatitis D

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV), a small RNA virus. It is unique because it requires the presence of hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate, making it a co-infection or superinfection in individuals already infected with HBV. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood or body fluids, often via shared needles or unprotected sex. Hepatitis D can lead to severe liver disease, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Prevention primarily involves vaccination against HBV, as there is no specific vaccine for HDV.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** From 2016 to 2024, there is a noticeable decline in Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland, with a peak in 2016 and a significant reduction by 2024. <br/>
- **Stability in Mortality:** Throughout the period, there have been no reported deaths, indicating effective management or low virulence of the disease. <br/>
- **Recent Fluctuations:** While the overall trend is downward, 2024 shows some fluctuations with a low in September and a rise by December, suggesting potential seasonal or reporting variations. <br/>
- **Public Health Success:** The consistent decrease in cases over the years reflects successful public health interventions and awareness campaigns. <br/>

The data for Hepatitis D cases in mainland China from 2016 to 2024 shows a general decline in the number of reported cases over the years. In 2016, the monthly cases ranged from 33 to 60, with a noticeable peak in January and November. The trend continues with fluctuations, but a gradual decrease is evident, particularly from 2020 onwards. By 2020, the monthly cases dropped significantly, with the lowest numbers recorded in May (11 cases) and February and March (12 cases each). This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, or changes in reporting practices.

From 2021 to 2024, the number of cases remained relatively low compared to earlier years, with occasional minor increases. For instance, December 2021 saw a spike to 37 cases, while the numbers in 2022 and 2023 generally hovered around the low twenties, with some months like September 2022 and October 2022 reporting as few as 10 cases. The data for 2024 shows a slight increase in December with 27 cases, but overall, the trend suggests a stabilization at lower levels. Throughout the entire period, there were no reported deaths, indicating that while the disease persists, it may be managed effectively, or the cases are not severe enough to result in fatalities.
