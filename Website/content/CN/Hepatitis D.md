# Hepatitis D

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV), a small circular RNA virus that is considered a satellite virus because it requires the presence of hepatitis B virus (HBV) for replication. It is transmitted through contact with infectious blood, via injections, or through close personal contact with an infected individual. HDV infection can occur as a co-infection with HBV or as a super-infection in individuals previously infected with HBV, leading to severe complications like cirrhosis and liver failure. Preventing HBV through vaccination also helps avert HDV infection.

## Highlights

- **Overall decline in cases**: Hepatitis D cases have shown a gradual decrease from an average of 40-50 cases per month in 2016-2019 to around 15-25 cases monthly by 2023-2024. <br/>
- **No deaths recorded**: There have been no reported deaths due to Hepatitis D throughout the period from 2016 to August 2024, indicating that while the disease persists, mortality remains controlled. <br/>
- **Recent stabilization**: From mid-2023 to mid-2024, the number of cases has largely stabilized between 14-24 cases per month, reflecting a steady but manageable disease burden. <br/>

The reported data on Hepatitis D cases in mainland China from 2016 to 2024 shows a consistent pattern of relatively low incidence, with the number of monthly cases fluctuating between a minimum of 10 (October and November 2022) and a maximum of 60 (January 2016). The data shows a notable decrease in cases during the COVID-19 pandemic years (2020-2021), with the lowest monthly counts recorded in February 2020 (12 cases) and a gradual decline observed throughout 2020. This decrease might be related to the reduced overall medical consultations and changes in healthcare access during the pandemic period.

Post-2021, the cases seem to stabilize with slight fluctuations, although the numbers remain lower than in pre-pandemic years, especially compared to the 2016-2019 period. The data shows no recorded deaths from Hepatitis D during this time, indicating either successful management of the disease or that those affected are not succumbing to complications directly related to the virus. As of August 2024, the most recent data suggests a slight increase in cases compared to the earlier months of 2024, though still lower than the peaks observed in the early years of the dataset.
