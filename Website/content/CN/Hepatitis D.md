# Hepatitis D

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as Hepatitis Delta, is a viral infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV), a small, circular RNA virus. It is unique in that it requires the presence of Hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. Co-infection with both HDV and HBV can lead to more severe liver disease than infection with HBV alone, often resulting in more rapid progression to cirrhosis or liver cancer. The virus is primarily transmitted through blood or other bodily fluids, similar to HBV. Prevention of Hepatitis D focuses on hepatitis B vaccination, as there is no specific vaccine for HDV.

## Highlights

- The number of Hepatitis D cases in Chinese mainland has significantly declined from a peak in early 2016, indicating successful disease control measures. <br/>  
- Recent data shows monthly cases typically hover below 30, reflecting a consistent and stable control compared to earlier years. <br/>  
- No deaths have been reported throughout the years, emphasizing improved healthcare and management strategies. <br/>  
- The disease trend reveals slight fluctuations, but overall, there is evident progress towards reducing the incidence rate since 2021. Sustained efforts likely contribute to maintaining low case numbers. <br/>

From January 2016 to June 2025, the data on Hepatitis D cases in mainland China depicts several key trends. The initial period in 2016 shows fluctuating monthly cases, peaking at 60 in January and hitting a low of 33 in December, with consistent fluctuation through 2017 and 2018. This variability might indicate local outbreaks or improved case detection rather than constant transmission. A notable decrease occurs in 2019, with cases dropping significantly by 2020, possibly influenced by public health measures during the COVID-19 pandemic. This trend of reduced cases continued into 2021, marking a decline from previous years.

By 2022, the data reflects further decreases, reaching the low of 10 cases in October and November. This suggests improved control efforts or a natural decline, potentially due to herd immunity or changes in behavior. In 2023, cases slightly fluctuate, indicating minor resurgences but not returning to the initial higher numbers of the mid-2010s. Moving through 2024 to early 2025, cases stabilize at low levels with mild variability. Importantly, no deaths were reported throughout the period, suggesting effective clinical interventions or demographic factors influencing disease outcomes. Overall, the trends imply successful containment and highlight an effective public health response while indicating ongoing surveillance needs.
