# Hepatitis D

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as hepatitis delta virus (HDV) infection, is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis D virus, a defective RNA virus that requires the presence of hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. It can lead to severe liver complications, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. HDV is primarily transmitted through contact with infected blood or bodily fluids, similar to HBV transmission routes. Vaccination against HBV can prevent HDV infection. Diagnosis involves detecting HDV RNA or antibodies in the blood. Treatment options are limited, with pegylated interferon being the main therapy, although new antiviral treatments are under investigation.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend:** Hepatitis D cases in China showed a gradual decrease from 60 cases in January 2016 to around 20 per month by 2023. <br/>
- **No Deaths Reported:** No deaths were recorded from 2016 to 2024, indicating effective disease management or low mortality. <br/>
- **Slight Increase in 2024:** A minor rise in cases was noted in 2024, with July reporting 23 cases, suggesting the need for continued monitoring. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variation:** Some months, such as January and September, displayed slight fluctuations in case numbers, hinting at possible seasonal effects. <br/>

The reported cases of Hepatitis D in mainland China from January 2016 to July 2024 demonstrate a generally low and fluctuating incidence. The number of cases each month typically ranged between 10 to 60, with an overall downward trend observed after 2016. The initial higher levels of cases in 2016, peaking at 60 cases in January, steadily decreased to the lowest levels in subsequent years, especially noticeable from 2020 onwards. The reduction in cases is evident from mid-2020, where monthly reported cases frequently fell below 30, coinciding with global public health efforts and possibly influenced by heightened hygiene and healthcare measures during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Throughout the period, there were no reported deaths attributed to Hepatitis D, indicating a relatively low mortality impact of the disease in this population. The lack of fatalities could suggest that the cases were either mild or effectively managed within the healthcare system. Additionally, the fluctuations in monthly case numbers could be reflective of seasonal trends, public health interventions, or changes in diagnostic and reporting practices. The data overall suggest that while Hepatitis D remains present in the population, its public health impact in terms of mortality is minimal, with cases being sporadically reported in relatively low numbers.
