# Hepatitis D

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV). It occurs only in individuals already infected with hepatitis B, as HDV requires the hepatitis B virus (HBV) for its replication. Transmission primarily occurs through blood, sexual contact, or from mother to child during birth. Hepatitis D can lead to severe liver diseases, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. It is preventable through hepatitis B vaccination, as preventing HBV infection also prevents HDV. Diagnosis involves detecting HDV RNA and antibodies in blood tests.

## Highlights

- **Overall Decrease in Cases**: Hepatitis D cases in mainland China have significantly decreased over the past several years, with monthly cases dropping from an average of 40-50 in 2016 to 10-20 by 2024. <br/>
  
- **Stable Mortality**: There have been no reported deaths associated with Hepatitis D throughout the entire period from 2016 to November 2024. <br/>
  
- **Seasonal Variability**: Monthly case numbers show some fluctuation, but no consistent seasonal peaks or trends are evident. <br/>
  
- **Recent Decline in New Infections**: The number of cases continues to decline in 2024, with a notable reduction to just 7 cases in September 2024, reflecting ongoing improvements in disease control. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis D cases in mainland China from 2016 to 2024 shows fluctuating monthly case numbers without any reported deaths. Over the eight-year period, cases peaked in certain months, such as May and September 2017 with 59 cases, but generally varied seasonally. For instance, in 2020, case counts were notably lower, with figures as low as 11 in May and 12 in February. This variability may reflect differences in regional outbreaks, reporting practices, or seasonal transmission.

The most significant trend is the gradual decline in reported cases after 2020, with monthly numbers stabilizing around 10-30 cases by 2022. The lowest counts occurred in October 2022 (10 cases) and September 2024 (7 cases), suggesting a decrease in Hepatitis D incidence. This reduction could be attributed to successful public health measures, such as improved vaccination and surveillance, although the persistence of cases indicates that Hepatitis D remains a concern, particularly in regions with higher risks of co-infection with Hepatitis B. The absence of deaths across the years suggests that most cases were less severe, but continued monitoring and intervention remain crucial to prevent potential outbreaks or complications.
