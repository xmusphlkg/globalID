# Hepatitis D

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV). It is considered a satellite virus, dependent on the presence of the Hepatitis B virus (HBV) for replication. HDV is transmitted through exposure to infected blood, making shared needles and unscreened blood transfusions key risk factors. Co-infection or super-infection with HBV leads to more severe liver disease, including rapid progression to cirrhosis and increased risk of hepatocellular carcinoma. Diagnosis involves detecting HDV antibodies or RNA in the blood. Prevention focuses on HBV vaccination, as there is currently no dedicated vaccine for HDV.

## Highlights

- Decrease in cases: From 2016 to 2025, a notable reduction in Hepatitis D cases is observed with early years seeing monthly averages above 40, dropping to less than 25 by 2025. <br/>
- Stability in mortality: Throughout the documented period, no deaths have been reported, indicating effective management and treatment of Hepatitis D in the Chinese mainland. <br/>
- Recent fluctuations: Despite overall decline, 2025 exhibits fluctuating monthly cases with September showing an uptick to 27 after several months of lower counts. <br/>
- Consistent reporting: Regular data collection reflects ongoing surveillance efforts, maintaining awareness and control over Hepatitis D infection trends. <br/>

Analyzing the Hepatitis D data in the Chinese mainland from 2016 to September 2025 highlights key trends in cases and responses. In 2016, the number of cases varied significantly, with fluctuations from 33 cases in December to 60 in January, reflecting potential seasonal impacts. Despite the presence of 525 cases throughout that year, the absence of deaths suggests effective management and treatment, which might have continued to evolve over the years.

From 2017 onward, we see a general decline in cases, indicating improved control measures or adjustments in healthcare strategies. While there were sporadic peaks each year—such as May 2017 and August 2018—these surges hint at challenges like environmental changes or lapses in public health interventions. Notably, from 2020 onwards, the case numbers decreased further, though cyclic patterns remained, particularly during winter months, pointing to potential factors such as coexisting infections or seasonal behaviors affecting disease transmission.

The consistently low mortality rate across the years suggests that clinical management has been quite effective. Despite fluctuations in monthly case counts, the overall trend points towards declining prevalence. Continued attention to transmission dynamics, hepatitis vaccination coverage, and social behaviors will be critical in sustaining these gains. This analysis underscores the importance of targeted public health strategies and vigilant monitoring to further reduce Hepatitis D's impact.
