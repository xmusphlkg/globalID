# Hepatitis D

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, or Hepatitis Delta, is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV), reliant on the Hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. It occurs as a co-infection or superinfection with HBV. Transmitted through contact with infected blood or bodily fluids, it is common among intravenous drug users and unsafe medical practices. Hepatitis D can lead to severe liver damage, increasing cirrhosis and liver cancer risk. Prevention involves vaccination against HBV, as there is no specific HDV vaccine. Treatment options are limited, focusing on managing HBV co-infection to reduce disease progression.

## Highlights

- **Trend Observation:** From 2016 to 2024, the number of Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland decreased significantly, peaking in early years and declining steadily after 2019. <br/>

- **2020 Onward Decline:** A sharp decline in cases was evident starting in 2020, indicating effective control measures or changes in reporting. <br/>

- **Zero Mortality:** Throughout this period, no deaths were reported, which could imply effective treatment or a less severe disease course. <br/>

- **Recent Fluctuations:** Slight fluctuations are observed in 2023 and 2024, but numbers remained low, with a notable decrease to 7 cases in September 2024. <br/>

The reported Hepatitis D cases in mainland China from 2016 to 2024 show a notable decline over time. In 2016, the number of cases fluctuated between 33 and 60 per month. However, there was a clear downward trend in subsequent years, with cases generally staying below 50 per month from 2017 to 2024, except for isolated spikes, such as in May 2017 and August 2024. The lowest monthly count occurred in September 2024, with only 7 reported cases. This gradual decrease in reported cases suggests either improved management and control measures or a possible reduction in transmission rates of the virus over time.

Throughout the eight-year period, no deaths were reported, which could indicate effective treatment strategies or perhaps a lower fatality rate associated with Hepatitis D in the region. It is also important to note that during the early months of 2020, the cases dropped significantly, likely influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic and its associated public health interventions, which might have reduced overall viral transmission, including that of Hepatitis D. Despite the occasional fluctuations, the overall trend points towards a consistent decline in new cases by 2024.
