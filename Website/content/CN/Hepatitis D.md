# Hepatitis D

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV), a defective RNA virus that requires the presence of hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood or body fluids, often via injection drug use or unprotected sex. Coinfection or superinfection with HBV can lead to more severe liver diseases, including cirrhosis or liver cancer. Hepatitis D is preventable through HBV vaccination, as HDV cannot infect without HBV. The disease is most prevalent in parts of Africa, the Middle East, eastern Europe, and Latin America.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis D incidence in the Chinese mainland has steadily declined from a monthly peak of over 60 cases in early 2016 to approximately 15–25 cases per month in 2025.<br/>
- No deaths were reported throughout the entire period (2016–May 2025), indicating continued effective clinical management or low severity.<br/>
- A noticeable drop in reported cases occurred in 2020, likely associated with COVID-19-related disruptions to healthcare access or surveillance.<br/>
- From 2022 to 2025, the number of monthly cases stabilized at lower levels, suggesting consistent control and limited local transmission.<br/>

Between 2016 and 2025, reported monthly Hepatitis D cases in mainland China show a gradual, irregular decline from a high baseline in 2016–2017, where monthly cases regularly hovered between 30 and 60, to lower averages from 2020 onward, mainly ranging between 10 and 30. The absence of reported deaths throughout this entire period is notable and suggests either extremely low mortality, underreporting of fatalities, or classification under broader hepatitis-related mortality categories. The initial decline may partly correspond to improved management of HBV co-infection, which is a precondition for HDV persistence, stronger surveillance systems, or public health interventions aimed at viral hepatitis control.

While some seasonal variability appears, particularly with occasional increases in late summer or at year-end, no consistent seasonal pattern is sustained across all years. The sharp declines following 2019 coincide temporally with COVID-19 emergence and its impact on healthcare access, diagnostic prioritization, and potentially case detection itself. From 2022 onward, monthly case counts stabilize at relatively low levels, with most months reporting under 25 cases. This long-term suppression of case numbers, paired with the lack of reported deaths, suggests effective containment or reduced transmission dynamics, although data completeness and diagnostic consistency over time warrant careful consideration in interpretation.

Over the entire observation period from January 2016 through May 2025, the data for Hepatitis D in mainland China show a consistent trend of zero reported deaths despite fluctuating monthly case counts. This uninterrupted absence of mortality strongly suggests an extremely low case fatality rate, or alternatively, an effective clinical management regime for diagnosed cases, potentially due to early detection, advances in antiviral treatments, or cases being predominantly subclinical. The reporting pattern implies sustained surveillance and case identification, with no evidence of underreporting of deaths if they had occurred. Importantly, this data may reflect either an actual absence of fatal progression or successful integration of Hepatitis D interventions within broader hepatitis management programs.

The high-resolution monthly dataset further highlights stability over time in terms of disease severity, showing no episodic spikes in fatal outcomes even when monthly case numbers exceeded 50 between 2016 and 2017. Although a general gradual decline in case numbers is noticeable from 2018 onward, with minor seasonal or administrative reporting variability, the persistent zero-death statistic indicates either a low virulence in circulating HDV strains or possible confounding from co-infection patterns with Hepatitis B, upon which HDV depends for replication. The absence of deaths across nearly a decade of national reporting underscores the need to focus further investigation on transmission, prevention, and diagnostic methods rather than acute fatal outcomes.
