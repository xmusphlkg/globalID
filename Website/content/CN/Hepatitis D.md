# Hepatitis D

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as hepatitis delta, is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV). It is unique because it requires the presence of hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. HDV can exacerbate the severity of a hepatitis B infection, leading to more rapid progression of liver disease, cirrhosis, and increased risk of liver cancer. It is transmitted through contact with infected blood and other bodily fluids, making intravenous drug users and those with HBV at higher risk. Preventative measures include HBV vaccination, which indirectly reduces the risk of HDV infection.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis D cases in mainland China have shown a marked decline since 2016, dropping from over 600 cases annually to fewer than 250 per year by 2024. <br/>
- No deaths have been reported from Hepatitis D over the entire reporting period (2016–April 2025), indicating very low mortality or effective case management. <br/>
- Monthly case numbers have remained relatively stable since 2021, typically ranging between 15 and 25 cases per month, showing consistent disease control. <br/>
- The sustained decline and plateau suggest that public health measures, surveillance, and possibly HBV control (as HDV requires HBV) have contributed to lowering the disease burden. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis D cases reported in mainland China from January 2016 to April 2025 show a consistently low incidence pattern with modest month-to-month variation and no reported deaths throughout the entire period. The highest levels of monthly case counts occurred in early years—2016 and 2017 regularly saw monthly figures above 40 cases, peaking at 60 in January 2016. However, a noticeable decline in case numbers begins from 2018, stabilizing further during and beyond the COVID-19 pandemic years, where most monthly figures fell below 30, and in many instances below 20, especially from 2020 onward.

This trend suggests a potential reduction in new transmissions or improved HBV control (given HDV's reliance on HBV co-infection), as well as possibly reflecting public health measures or surveillance changes during the COVID-19 period affecting reporting. Despite several decade-spanning surveillance cycles, no deaths were recorded, underscoring either effective management of identified cases or generally low pathogenicity in the reported context. The data also lack distinct seasonal patterns, showing more stochastic fluctuation instead of consistent peaks or troughs across years.
