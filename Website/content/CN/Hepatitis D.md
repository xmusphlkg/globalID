# Hepatitis D

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as Hepatitis Delta, is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV), which is a unique virus that requires the presence of Hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. Co-infection with HDV and HBV leads to more severe liver disease than HBV alone, resulting in faster progression to cirrhosis and increased risk of liver cancer. HDV is transmitted similarly to HBV, primarily through blood and bodily fluids, and is prevalent in areas where HBV is common. Preventive measures include HBV vaccination, as no specific HDV vaccine exists yet.

## Highlights

- Between 2016 and 2025, Hepatitis D cases in mainland China showed a general decreasing trend, with cases peaking at 60 in January 2016 and reaching a low of 13 in August 2025. <br/>
- The absence of reported deaths throughout the entire period indicates a non-fatal clinical outcome, suggesting effective management of the disease. <br/>
- Monthly fluctuations are evident, with periodic increases in cases such as 27 cases in December 2024 before dropping to 13 cases by August 2025. <br/>
- The reported data highlights sustained surveillance and efficient public health interventions contributing to overall case reduction over the years. <br/>

The reported cases of Hepatitis D in mainland China from January 2016 to August 2025 show notable fluctuations and an overall decline over time. In 2016, monthly cases varied between 33 and 60, with occasional spikes, particularly in November. The trend continued into 2017, with counts mostly remaining between 30 and 59 each month, indicating a stable but impactful presence of the disease. From 2018 onwards, a significant decline in yearly averages is observed, with case numbers generally dropping below previous highs. By 2019, cases mostly stayed below 44, marking a notable reduction compared to earlier years.

In 2020, this declining trend accelerated, with monthly cases plummeting as low as 11, possibly due to enhanced prevention and treatment strategies or effective public health campaigns. The decline persisted through subsequent years, although post-2022 presented sporadic fluctuations, suggesting changes in surveillance, external factors, or reporting methods. Despite these variations, no deaths were reported throughout, reflecting advancements in disease management.

From 2023 to mid-2025, case numbers saw a slight resurgence, yet remained significantly lower overall compared to the early years. This trend suggests successful long-term control, though the peak in 2024 warrants attention. The data underscores the importance of adaptive health strategies and continued monitoring to address the dynamic nature of infectious disease patterns.
