# Hepatitis D

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV), which depends on the presence of Hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. It is considered one of the most severe forms of viral hepatitis due to the rapid progression to liver cirrhosis and increased risk of liver cancer. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood or body fluids, similar to HBV. Symptoms can range from mild to severe, including jaundice, fatigue, and abdominal pain. Diagnosis involves detecting HDV RNA or antibodies. Preventing HBV infection is vital for HDV prevention.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Case Numbers**: Hepatitis D cases in Chinese mainland have significantly decreased from a peak of 60 cases in early 2016 to an average of about 19 cases monthly in 2025. <br/>
- **Zero Mortality Rate**: Throughout the observed period, there have been zero reported deaths from Hepatitis D, indicating successful clinical management. <br/>
- **Stability in Recent Trends**: Since 2020, case numbers have stabilized, averaging around 20 cases per month with slight fluctuations, suggesting consistent disease control efforts. <br/>
- **Potential Seasonality**: Slight seasonal fluctuations are evident, with cases typically declining in the fall and winter months and increasing in the spring and summer. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland from 2016 to 2025 reveal a fluctuating pattern with significant changes over time. Initially, from 2016 to 2017, the number of cases varied between 30 to 60 cases monthly. This relatively high incidence may reflect variations in public health measures, testing, or awareness during these years. Importantly, despite these fluctuations, no deaths were reported, suggesting effective management and treatment of the infections.

Starting in 2020, there is a noticeable decline in the reported cases, with numbers often dropping below 30 per month through 2025. This decline could indicate improved preventive measures, better public health awareness, and successful vaccination programs targeting Hepatitis B, given Hepatitis D requires co-infection with Hepatitis B. The continuation of this trend into 2025, with no associated fatalities, reflects the successful control of the disease in the population.

The consistent report of zero deaths throughout these years highlights the effectiveness of healthcare interventions and suggests that the disease is being managed successfully, possibly due to prompt medical attention or effective treatment protocols. By 2025, the stable low numbers emphasize the sustained efforts by medical and public health authorities in managing and reducing the impact of Hepatitis D in the region.
