# Hepatitis D

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as Hepatitis Delta, is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV). It is a defective virus that requires the presence of Hepatitis B virus (HBV) to replicate. HDV infection can lead to more severe liver disease compared to HBV infection alone, increasing the risk of cirrhosis and liver cancer. It is transmitted through blood and bodily fluids, similar to HBV, and is prevalent in areas with high HBV infection rates. Vaccination against HBV can prevent HDV infection, highlighting the importance of immunization programs.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland have been on a declining trend since 2016, with peak incidence in 2016 followed by a gradual decrease over the years. <br/>
- From 2020 onwards, the number of reported cases noticeably dropped, indicating better disease management or changes in transmission dynamics. <br/>
- Between January and March 2025, the monthly cases stayed relatively low, ranging from 18 to 25, without any reported fatalities, suggesting controlled disease spread. <br/>
- The consistent absence of deaths across all data points indicates effective healthcare interventions for managing Hepatitis D complications in the region. <br/>

An analysis of Hepatitis D cases reported in the Chinese mainland from January 2016 to March 2025 reveals key trends. Initially, case numbers fluctuated, with relatively high peaks in 2016 and 2017, such as 53 cases in November 2016 and 59 cases in both May and September 2017. This pattern suggests potential environmental or behavioral factors driving these fluctuations. However, from 2018 onwards, a general decline is evident, culminating in notably low numbers during 2020, particularly from February to May, where monthly cases dropped significantly, possibly reflecting containment efforts or seasonal dampening effects.

Post-2020, a gradual increase in reported cases is observed with small spikes throughout 2021 and 2022, indicating a potential resurgence influenced by changing public health strategies or viral dynamics. The winter months of 2024 witnessed another rise, peaking in December at 27 cases, which might suggest a seasonal resurgence or other factors such as interruptions in public health measures or Covid-19 impacts on healthcare systems.

Throughout this period, it is notable that despite the fluctuation in case numbers, the death rate consistently remained at zero, suggesting effective clinical management and less virulent strains. This highlights the importance of sustained surveillance and proactive health interventions to stabilize incidence rates and reduce potential re-emergence periods. Overall, these patterns underscore the dynamic nature of Hepatitis D epidemiology and the necessity for adaptive public health responses.
