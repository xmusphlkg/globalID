# Hepatitis D

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV), a small RNA virus. It is unique because it can only infect individuals who are already infected with hepatitis B virus (HBV), as it requires the presence of HBV to replicate. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood or body fluids, often via shared needles or unprotected sex. Hepatitis D can lead to severe liver disease, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Prevention is primarily through vaccination against HBV, as there is no specific vaccine for HDV.

## Highlights



- **Decline in Cases:** From 2016 to 2025, Hepatitis D cases in mainland China decreased significantly, from 60 in January 2016 to 19 in February 2025. <br/>
- **Zero Mortality:** No deaths were reported throughout the period, indicating effective disease management. <br/>
- **Monthly Fluctuations:** Despite the overall decline, monthly cases fluctuated, with spikes like 59 cases in May 2017 and 27 in December 2024. <br/>
- **Recent Trends:** January and February 2025 show a slight increase in cases, highlighting the need for ongoing surveillance. <br/>

The data for Hepatitis D cases in mainland China from 2016 to early 2025 shows a general trend of decreasing incidence over the years. In 2016, the monthly cases ranged from 33 to 60, with a noticeable decline in the number of cases by 2020, where the monthly cases ranged from 11 to 25. This downward trend continues into 2024, with some months reporting as few as 7 cases. The data for 2025, although incomplete, suggests a slight increase in cases compared to the previous year, with January and February reporting 25 and 19 cases, respectively.

Throughout the entire period, there were no reported deaths, indicating that while the disease was present, it was not fatal in the reported cases. The consistent absence of deaths could suggest effective management and treatment strategies or a less severe strain of the virus. The data also shows some seasonal fluctuations, with certain months experiencing higher cases, which could be attributed to various factors such as environmental conditions, public health interventions, or changes in population behavior. Overall, the data reflects a positive trend in controlling Hepatitis D, with a significant reduction in cases over the years.
