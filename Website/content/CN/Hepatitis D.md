# Hepatitis D

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as delta hepatitis, is a liver infection caused by the hepatitis D virus (HDV), a small RNA virus. It is unique because it can only infect individuals who are already infected with hepatitis B virus (HBV), as it requires the HBV surface antigen to replicate. Transmission occurs through contact with infected blood or body fluids, often via shared needles or unprotected sex. Hepatitis D can lead to severe liver disease, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Prevention is primarily through HBV vaccination, which indirectly protects against HDV infection.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** From 2016 to 2025, there is a noticeable decline in Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland, with a peak in 2016 and a significant reduction by 2025. <br/>
- **Stability in Mortality:** Throughout the period, there have been no reported deaths, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease. <br/>
- **Fluctuations in Monthly Cases:** While the overall trend is downward, there are fluctuations in monthly cases, with occasional spikes, such as in December 2024. <br/>
- **Recent Trends:** As of January 2025, the number of cases remains low, suggesting continued control efforts are effective in maintaining low transmission rates. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland from 2016 to 2025 reveals a fluctuating trend in reported cases, with a notable decline observed in the years following 2019. Initially, the number of cases was relatively high, peaking in 2016 with 60 cases reported in January. However, a gradual decrease in cases is evident, particularly from 2020 onwards, where the numbers dropped significantly, reaching as low as 12 cases in February 2020. This decline may suggest improvements in public health measures, vaccination efforts, or increased awareness and screening for Hepatitis D.

Despite the fluctuations, it is important to note that there have been no reported deaths associated with Hepatitis D during this period, indicating that while the disease remains a public health concern, it has not resulted in fatalities. The data for 2024 shows a slight resurgence in cases, with 27 reported in December, which could warrant further investigation into the factors contributing to this increase. Overall, the trends suggest a need for ongoing monitoring and targeted interventions to manage and control Hepatitis D effectively in the region.
