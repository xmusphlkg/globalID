# Hepatitis D

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis D, also known as Hepatitis Delta, is a liver infection caused by the Hepatitis D virus (HDV). It occurs only in individuals who are already infected with Hepatitis B, as the HDV virus requires the Hepatitis B surface antigen to replicate. Transmission routes include percutaneous exposure, such as through blood, and mucosal contact with infectious body fluids. Co-infection or superinfection with HDV typically leads to more severe liver disease compared to HBV infection alone, increasing the risk of cirrhosis and liver cancer. Effective prevention hinges on vaccination against Hepatitis B.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis D cases in the Chinese mainland have generally declined from 2016 to May 2024, dropping from highs of 60 cases to lower monthly figures in recent years. <br/>
- No reported deaths occurred throughout the period, indicating a low case fatality rate or effective treatment. <br/>
- A significant drop in cases was observed around 2020, possibly due to COVID-19 and related public health measures. <br/>
- As of 2024, cases have stabilized in the low teens to low twenties per month, indicating a plateau at a lower incidence rate. <br/>

The data on Hepatitis D cases in mainland China from January 2016 to May 2024 reveals several interesting trends. There is a visible decline in the number of reported cases from the start of 2016, where case numbers were relatively high, peaking at 60 in January 2016, to subsequent years. For instance, the data shows a significant drop in cases beginning around early 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic's onset, and inquiries about potential underreporting or changes in healthcare-seeking behavior during this time can be raised. Monthly trends show seasonal fluctuations, with higher numbers occasionally reported at the start and the end of years.

While January 2016 had the highest number of cases (60), subsequent years generally saw fewer cases with occasional spikes, such as in May 2017 and September 2017 (59 cases each). These variations might be linked to diagnostic, reporting efficiencies, public health initiatives, or other social factors like seasonal migrations. The reporting of zero deaths throughout this period suggests either effective clinical management or potential underreporting of mortality, indicating a need for more granular data on patient outcomes. Overall, the dataset reflects a predominantly declining trend in Hepatitis D cases reported, especially noticeable post-2020, with the lowest monthly cases occurring in late 2022 and early 2023.
