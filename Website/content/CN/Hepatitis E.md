# Hepatitis E

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a viral infection caused by the Hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily transmitted through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated water. It is common in developing regions with poor sanitation. Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea, typically lasting for a few weeks. While most individuals recover without complications, it can be severe in pregnant women, leading to liver failure or death. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but supportive care can help manage symptoms. Vaccines for HEV are available in some countries, offering prevention.

## Highlights

1. **Sustained case burden**: Hepatitis E continues to affect a large number of people in China, with monthly case numbers typically ranging from 1,800 to 3,700, showing stable levels over the years. <br/>
2. **Decline in mortality**: Deaths have generally decreased over the years, with most months now reporting zero or very few deaths, except for occasional spikes. <br/>
3. **Seasonality**: Hepatitis E cases appear to have seasonal fluctuations, with higher incidence in the spring and autumn months, particularly from March to May. <br/>
4. **Recent trends**: In 2024, monthly cases remain relatively high, but deaths are mostly low, with only occasional reports of fatalities (e.g., November 2024 recorded 1 death). <br/>

The data on Hepatitis E cases in the Chinese mainland over the past decade reveals some notable trends in both the incidence and mortality associated with the disease. From 2014 to 2024, the number of reported cases fluctuated, with certain months experiencing sharp peaks, especially in the spring and autumn months. For instance, March consistently saw high case numbers, with 3,000 or more cases reported during several years, reflecting potential seasonal variations or outbreaks. There was also a noticeable decline in reported cases in the latter part of 2022 and the beginning of 2023, potentially indicating a reduction in the intensity of outbreaks or successful public health interventions.

Mortality rates, although relatively low, showed some fluctuation over the years. Deaths from Hepatitis E typically ranged from zero to a few cases per month, with sporadic increases, such as in December 2017, when six deaths were recorded. These increases were often associated with significant spikes in case numbers, suggesting that the severity of outbreaks could correlate with higher fatality rates. Despite the overall increase in cases during certain periods, the mortality rate remained low, with most months reporting zero or just one death. This stability in mortality, despite rising case numbers, may reflect improvements in medical care or a shift in the demographics of those affected, such as younger, healthier populations experiencing milder forms of the disease.
