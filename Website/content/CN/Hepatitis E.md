# Hepatitis E

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a viral liver disease caused by the Hepatitis E virus (HEV). Transmitted primarily through the fecal-oral route, it commonly occurs in regions with poor sanitation and contaminated water supplies. HEV is classified into different genotypes, with genotypes 1 and 2 affecting humans and 3 and 4 largely affecting animals but occasionally humans. While often asymptomatic and self-limiting, it can cause acute liver inflammation, manifesting as jaundice, fever, fatigue, and nausea. Pregnant women and individuals with compromised immune systems are at risk for severe complications. Globally, hepatitis E is a significant public health concern, requiring targeted preventive measures.

## Highlights

- There has been a noticeable increase in reported Hepatitis E cases since 2023, peaking in March 2025, which suggests an ongoing epidemic.<br/>
- Despite the higher case numbers, the mortality rate remains relatively low, with most months recording fewer than five deaths.<br/>
- Seasonal variations are evident, with case numbers typically higher from late winter to spring, indicating possible environmental or human factors influencing transmission.<br/>
- Recent data indicate a slight decline in cases from the peak in March 2025, offering a tentative sign of control measures potentially taking effect.<br/>

The Hepatitis E data from 2015 to 2025 in mainland China reveals annual fluctuations, with cases peaking typically between March and April and decreasing towards the year's end. This pattern may suggest environmental or behavioral factors influencing transmission, given the virus’s primary spread through contaminated food and water. The fluctuations in case numbers could also be impacted by public health policies, sanitation efforts, or variations in disease surveillance.

The mortality data indicates that, although deaths occurred, the mortality rate remained low relative to the cases reported monthly. This suggests effective treatments or a generally strong population resilience against severe outcomes of the infection. An isolated surge in deaths, such as the six reported in December 2017, suggests variability possibly influenced by health interventions or differences in reporting methods over time.

The noticeable decrease in cases from 2020 to early 2021 might reflect the influence of COVID-19-related health measures reducing Hepatitis E transmission. Conversely, the post-2022 increase indicates a potential relaxation of such measures or a resurgence due to various unstudied factors. Overall, the data underscores the need for continual public health vigilance and adaptive strategies to manage Hepatitis E risks in mainland China, ensuring prepared health systems and communities to address fluctuations in disease prevalence successfully.
