# Hepatitis E

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is an infectious disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily affecting the liver. It is transmitted mainly through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated water. Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea, but infections can be asymptomatic. While most cases resolve spontaneously, hepatitis E can cause life-threatening acute hepatitis, especially in pregnant women. It occurs worldwide, with high prevalence in areas with poor sanitation. Diagnosis is confirmed by detecting HEV RNA or specific antibodies. Preventive measures focus on improved hygiene and access to clean water.

## Highlights

- After a low in 2020, monthly Hepatitis E case numbers have gradually risen, peaking around March and April in recent years; December 2025 reports 2,543 cases.<br/>
- Mortality remains low, with deaths rarely exceeding three per month; the overall case fatality rate is stable and consistently below 0.1%.<br/>
- Seasonal spikes in spring persist, but annual totals indicate no dramatic outbreaks; endemic transmission continues nationwide.<br/>
- Despite increased case counts in 2024–2025, disease severity appears unchanged, suggesting ongoing improvements in clinical management and public health response.<br/>

From 2015 to early 2025, Hepatitis E cases in the Chinese mainland display marked seasonality, with clear annual peaks in March or April (often exceeding 3,000 cases), followed by declines over the rest of the year. This cyclical pattern likely aligns with environmental exposures or food habits associated with spring, such as agricultural practices or dietary changes. The lowest case numbers consistently appear in late autumn and winter, before a gradual increase toward the next spring peak.

Monthly deaths are low throughout, generally zero to three, with rare months observing higher numbers, demonstrating a low case fatality rate relative to the incidence. Notably, 2020 case counts were much reduced compared to prior years; this anomaly coincides with the initial COVID-19 pandemic and related public health interventions, which likely limited both transmission and health care utilization, thereby affecting reporting. From 2021 onward, case numbers rebounded, returning to the historical seasonal pattern, and by 2023–2025, spring peaks approached pre-pandemic levels. Over time, no sustained increase in deaths was observed, suggesting stable clinical outcomes.

Overall, Hepatitis E continues as a seasonally endemic disease in this region. While incidence varies each year, the mortality rate remains minimal, possibly due to improved awareness, clinical care, and public health interventions targeted to food safety and hygiene.

Reported deaths from Hepatitis E in mainland China from late 2015 to the end of 2025 consistently remain low in relation to case numbers, with most months seeing 0–2 fatalities despite thousands of cases. Occasional minor surges are observed—such as 6 deaths in December 2017, and peaks of 4 deaths in June 2018 and January 2024—yet these events are exceptional rather than indicative of a trend. Overall, deaths do not display a clear correlation with seasonal case peaks or high-incidence months, suggesting mortality is not primarily driven by incidence fluctuations, but influenced by other factors such as individual susceptibility or access to care.

During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022), both cases and deaths dipped, likely due to altered healthcare utilization or reporting. However, as case counts accelerated post-pandemic, fatality rates remained flat, reinforcing the pattern of low overall mortality. The persistently low case fatality rate across nearly a decade points to improvements in clinical management, better diagnostic capacity, and possibly effects from preventive public health measures. Hepatitis E thus appears characterized by endemic transmission but limited virulence in most cases, with deaths typically confined to scattered, high-risk scenarios rather than wider population trends. Ongoing monitoring remains important to quickly identify and respond to any change in disease severity or affected population.
