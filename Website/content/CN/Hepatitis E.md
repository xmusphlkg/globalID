# Hepatitis E

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a viral liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily transmitted via the fecal-oral route, often through contaminated water. It is prevalent in regions with poor sanitation infrastructure, particularly in parts of Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. The virus has four genotypes, with genotypes 1 and 2 associated with human infections and genotypes 3 and 4 found in animals. Symptoms range from mild fever, fatigue, and jaundice to severe liver disease, especially in pregnant women. Though generally self-limiting, HEV can lead to acute liver failure in immunocompromised individuals.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend**: Overall, from 2023 to 2025, the reported cases of Hepatitis E show a generally increasing trend, with noticeable peaks in March of each year, indicating possible seasonal variation. <br/>
- **Stable Fatality Rates**: Despite the rising number of cases, deaths remain low, reflecting stable fatality rates and potential improvements in medical management or early diagnosis. <br/>
- **Recent Dip**: The latest data from October 2025 shows a decrease in cases compared to earlier in 2025, suggesting potential improvements in public health interventions. <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks**: Higher case numbers are observed in March and the first quarter of each year, suggesting potential seasonal or behavioral factors influencing transmission. <br/>

The analysis of Hepatitis E cases and deaths in mainland China from October 2015 to October 2025 reveals several patterns. Starting from October 2015, we observe an initial rise in cases, which peaks in March 2016 with 3401 cases. This increase is followed by a subsequent decline until October 2016, after which there's a nominal fluctuating pattern with peaks aligning generally in the first quarter of each year. Notably, deaths remain relatively low across the years, although there are occasional increments such as December 2017, which recorded six deaths, representing the highest mortality month within this dataset.

Considering year-on-year trends, there's a notable decrease in case numbers post the first quarter of 2020, likely influenced by external factors like interventions or other broader public health initiatives, given the coinciding COVID-19 pandemic. This decline in cases continues until early 2023 when numbers rise again with March recording 3117 cases. The consistent annual increase beginning each January or February, and peaking by March, may suggest seasonality influences possibly linked to demographic movements or shifts in environmental conditions conducive to Hepatitis E transmission. The spike in cases coupled with varying mortality across the dataset underscores potential healthcare responses that have mitigated the severity despite fluctuations in transmission.
