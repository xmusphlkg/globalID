# Hepatitis E

Version: November 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily affecting the liver. It is transmitted through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated water sources, and is prevalent in areas with poor sanitation. The disease is generally self-limiting but can pose severe risks to pregnant women and individuals with weakened immune systems. Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, nausea, and fever, typically appearing 2-10 weeks after exposure. While it is usually acute, chronic cases may occur, particularly in immunocompromised individuals. Treatment focuses on supportive care, and prevention hinges on improving sanitation and hygiene practices.

## Highlights

- **Recent Decline in Cases**: After peaking at 3748 cases in March 2025, Hepatitis E cases have steadily decreased, reaching 2287 in November 2025.<br/>
- **Mortality Rates Remain Low**: Despite fluctuations in case numbers, fatalities have remained low, with no reported deaths since October 2025.<br/>
- **Higher Seasonal Incidence**: Consistent with previous years, March typically sees a spike in cases, indicating a seasonal trend or reporting pattern.<br/>
- **Overall Longitudinal Stability**: The epidemiological data show variable but generally stable case numbers since a significant low in 2020, with typical monthly fluctuation patterns resuming post-2020.<br/>

The data for Hepatitis E in mainland China from November 2015 to November 2025 highlights distinct trends over this period. Notably, there are recurring peaks in case numbers, often emerging in the early months of each year, particularly around March. This points to a potentially seasonal pattern to Hepatitis E virus (HEV) infections. The notable spikes in March 2016, 2017, 2018, 2022, and 2023 align with this trend, suggesting increased HEV transmission following winter.

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020 brought a marked reduction in reported cases, reaching a low in February 2020 with 1045 cases. This decline likely reflects the impact of pandemic-related public health measures and behavioral changes. As these measures eased, case numbers returned to pre-pandemic levels, showing a normalization of transmission patterns. Despite fluctuations in case numbers, mortality rates remain low across the period, with occasional increases in deaths not consistently mirroring case surges. This could imply effective clinical management or variability in the population's vulnerability.

In summary, while HEV presents ongoing public health challenges in China, the data suggests a focus on controlling transmission given the periodic increases in cases. The consistently low mortality indicates that while Hepatitis E is prevalent, it remains less lethal, highlighting the importance of preventative measures to manage outbreaks effectively.
