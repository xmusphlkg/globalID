# Hepatitis E

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily transmitted through contaminated water in areas with poor sanitation. The virus has four main genotypes: HEV1 and HEV2 occur in humans, while HEV3 and HEV4 can be found in several animals, including pigs, and occasionally infect humans. Common symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, and nausea, although many cases are asymptomatic. Most infections are self-limiting, but severe outcomes can occur, particularly in pregnant women or immunocompromised individuals. Improved public health infrastructure and hygiene practices are essential for preventing its spread.

## Highlights

- The data reveals seasonal peaks in Hepatitis E cases, typically rising in March and stabilizing by September, suggesting potential environmental or behavioral factors influencing these trends. <br/>
- An overall decline in cases is observed from 2019 to 2020, likely influenced by broader public health measures or changes in reporting, followed by a notable increase again from late 2023 onwards. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations in case numbers, the death rate remains consistently low, indicating effective clinical interventions or improvements in disease management strategies. <br/>
- The upward trend in cases since 2023, peaking in 2025, suggests a need for ongoing public health monitoring and potential review of prevention strategies. <br/>

Deaths remained generally low, often not exceeding two per month, indicating manageable disease progression or improving healthcare interventions.

Throughout the period, a cyclical pattern emerged, with spikes in cases typically occurring in the first quarter of each year, such as March 2017, March 2018, and March 2023. This likely points to seasonal influences or reporting practices. The data portrays an upward trend from 2021 onwards, after a decline in 2020 potentially due to the COVID-19 pandemic affecting healthcare and surveillance systems. Sporadic increases in deaths, like six in December 2017, hint at occasional lapses in effective disease management.

The surge toward the dataset's end in 2024 and early 2025, peaking at 3748 cases in March 2025, necessitates further inquiry into contributing factors, whether environmental or social.
