# Hepatitis E

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily transmitted through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated water. It is most prevalent in regions with poor sanitation, including parts of Asia, Africa, Central America, and the Middle East. Hepatitis E typically leads to an acute and self-limiting condition, though it can result in fulminant hepatitis in pregnant women and those with weakened immune systems. Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea. While vaccines exist in some countries, the prevention mainly focuses on ensuring safe drinking water and good hygiene practices.

## Highlights

- **Steady Incidence:** Hepatitis E cases have remained stable, with monthly cases averaging around 2,500–3,000 from 2014 to August 2024, showing no dramatic increase or decrease.<br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Despite consistent case numbers, the mortality rate has been low, with most months reporting 0–2 deaths and occasional spikes (up to 6 deaths).<br/>
- **Seasonal Variation:** Peaks in case numbers are generally observed between March and May, suggesting possible seasonal factors influencing transmission.<br/>
- **Recent Trends:** In 2024, there has been an increase in cases from March to May, reaching 3,676 cases in March, but numbers have slightly decreased in the following months.<br/>

The reported cases of Hepatitis E in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 show considerable fluctuation, with peak periods typically occurring in March and April of each year. For instance, March 2015 saw 3,121 cases, and March 2024 recorded a high of 3,676 cases. There is a general trend of increased cases during the first quarter of the year, followed by a decline towards the mid and latter parts of the year. Notably, February 2020 marked a significant drop in cases, potentially influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic, with the lowest number reported during this time (1,045 cases). The data shows that despite fluctuations, Hepatitis E cases remain consistently present, with seasonal patterns evident over the years.

Death rates from Hepatitis E remain relatively low in comparison to the number of cases. While some months, like March 2016, recorded a spike in fatalities with 5 deaths, most months saw fewer than three deaths, often recording zero fatalities. The years 2020 and 2021 experienced lower deaths, which might suggest improved clinical management or external factors limiting the spread, but the fatality rate increased slightly again in late 2023 and early 2024. This indicates that while Hepatitis E continues to cause a consistent public health burden in China, it remains a low-mortality disease overall.

This suggests possible seasonal or outbreak-related fluctuations in Hepatitis E fatalities. Moreover, while the number of cases shows moderate variation across months, deaths do not appear to directly correlate with case surges. For instance, in March 2016, a high number of cases (3,401) coincided with a peak in deaths (5), but similar case volumes in 2024 did not yield comparable fatality increases.

Further, over the years, the death rate per cases remains low but relatively steady. The number of deaths per month rarely exceeds three, and many months, even with significant case counts, report zero deaths. This pattern underscores the fact that while Hepatitis E is widespread in certain regions, its mortality impact is relatively limited, which may reflect effective management and treatment protocols in place. However, occasional increases in the death toll could hint at localized healthcare challenges or specific vulnerable populations affected during those periods.
