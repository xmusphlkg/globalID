# Hepatitis E

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), which primarily spreads through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated water. It is a significant public health concern in developing countries with poor sanitation. While it usually causes a mild, self-limiting illness, it can lead to severe acute hepatitis, particularly in pregnant women, resulting in high mortality rates. Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, and nausea, typically manifesting two to nine weeks after exposure. Hepatitis E is diagnosed through serological tests detecting HEV antibodies and is generally preventable by improving hygiene and sanitation, and through vaccination in some regions.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis E cases on the Chinese mainland increased significantly from early 2023, peaking in March 2025 with 3,748 cases. This marks a rising trend in recent years. <br/>
- The number of deaths remained relatively low, though February 2025 noted the highest with three fatalities, suggesting stable mortality rates. <br/>
- Seasonal fluctuations in cases were observed, with higher numbers in spring months and a decline starting in June 2025. This might indicate season-linked transmission factors. <br/>
- The recent data underline the need for reinforced public health interventions, especially in high-incidence periods, to address the evolving Hepatitis E epidemiological landscape. <br/>

The reported data for Hepatitis E in the Chinese mainland from June 2015 to June 2025 shows fluctuations in case numbers, with some notable patterns. Initially, monthly cases from mid-2015 were steady, averaging between 1,900 and 2,500. In early 2016, there was a significant spike, especially in March with 3,401 cases, marking the year's peak. Afterward, cases fluctuated seasonally, usually ranging from 2,000 to 3,000. A noticeable decline occurred in early 2020, likely due to disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, affecting disease surveillance and healthcare services.

Throughout the years, there is consistent seasonal elevation in March, as seen in 2017, 2018, 2019, 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024. This suggests environmental or societal factors may impact virus transmission during these months. Mortality rates remained low across the period, typically in single digits, indicating that while the disease persists, it is not excessively lethal. This low mortality, paired with high seasonal incidence, suggests effective healthcare management combined with the self-limiting nature of Hepatitis E. Overall, the consistent presence and seasonal variation of Hepatitis E highlight an ongoing public health concern in China, necessitating continuous monitoring and public health strategies to control its spread.
