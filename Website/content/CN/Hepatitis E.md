# Hepatitis E

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily transmitted through the fecal-oral route, often via contaminated water. It is a major public health concern in developing countries with poor sanitation. The infection usually results in acute hepatitis, with symptoms like jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea. While most cases are self-limiting, it can lead to severe complications in pregnant women and immunocompromised individuals. Genotypes 1 and 2 infect humans only, while genotypes 3 and 4 are zoonotic. Improved sanitation and safe drinking water are key preventive measures.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis E cases in China showed seasonal peaks around March-April annually, with 2025 continuing this trend (3,748 cases in March, highest monthly figure recorded).<br/>
- Overall case numbers have increased gradually since 2022, reversing a prior decline seen from 2019–2021 likely influenced by COVID-19-related healthcare disruptions.<br/>
- Deaths remain low relative to case numbers, with 2025 data indicating improved outcomes—only 5 deaths from January to May compared to 10 in the same period of 2024.<br/>
- While no major outbreaks are evident, sustained high incidence and recurring seasonal surges underscore the need for strengthened preventive measures and vaccination consideration.<br/>

Between 2015 and 2025, Hepatitis E cases in mainland China show a consistent seasonal trend, with sharp increases peaking in March and April each year. Notable spikes occurred in March 2016 (3,401 cases), March 2017 (3,352), and March 2025 (3,748), indicating spring as a high-transmission period. Case numbers typically decline through the summer and into autumn. This pattern suggests environmental or behavioral factors may drive seasonal transmission. An exception occurred in 2020, where reports dropped sharply around February and remained lower throughout the year, likely due to disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic rather than a true decline in incidence. Afterward, cases rose again, particularly from 2023 to 2025.

Deaths remained relatively low throughout the period and did not follow the same seasonal variation observed in case numbers. Occasional spikes—such as in December 2017 (6 deaths) and June 2018 (4)—appear irregular and may reflect vulnerabilities among specific patient groups rather than broader trends. Despite rising case numbers in recent years, fatality rates have stayed low, suggesting improved detection or healthcare management. Overall, the persistently high spring peaks and post-pandemic case rebound warrant targeted prevention and surveillance efforts before and during spring each year.

Between 2015 and mid-2025, Hepatitis E in mainland China has shown a consistently low case fatality rate. While monthly case counts generally ranged from 1,800 to over 3,700, deaths rarely exceeded 3 per month, with occasional peaks (e.g., 6 deaths in December 2017 and 5 in March 2016). These scattered death events suggest that while the disease maintains a notable presence in the population, its lethality remains modest in the general context of infections. No pattern of exponential mortality growth is evident, even during months with high incidence, indicating a relatively stable mortality trend over time.

Monthly fluctuations show some seasonal mortality clustering—most notably, gradual upticks appear to begin late in the year, with December and January occasionally recording more deaths. However, acute spikes are inconsistent and not strongly correlated with the number of detected cases. For instance, months with over 3,000 cases such as March 2023 and March 2025 recorded zero deaths, while much lower-incidence months like October 2017 still saw spikes in death counts. These findings suggest that environmental or access/response factors, rather than incidence alone, influence mortality risk from Hepatitis E.
