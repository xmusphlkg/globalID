# Hepatitis E

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), transmitted primarily through the fecal-oral route, mainly via contaminated drinking water. It is a significant public health concern in many developing countries with poor sanitation. The infection is usually self-limiting but can cause acute liver failure, especially in pregnant women. HEV has several genotypes, with genotypes 1 and 2 affecting humans and genotypes 3 and 4 also found in animals. Outbreaks are common in areas with inadequate water treatment, and sporadic cases can also arise from zoonotic transmission.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis E in Mainland China has shown a significant increase in case numbers from January 2024 onwards, peaking with 3748 cases in March 2025.  
<br/>
- Monthly incidence fluctuates but consistently rises above 3000 cases during peak months from March to May.  
<br/>
- While deaths are relatively low, there have been occasional increases, with March 2025 showing zero fatalities despite high case numbers.  
<br/>
- Seasonal patterns are evident, with increased cases in late winter and early spring, suggesting potential environmental or behavioral factors.  
<br/>

Hepatitis E cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 display a clear seasonal trend, with peaks appearing consistently in the spring, especially in March. These seasonal surges are most notable in years like 2016, 2017, 2022, and 2025, with March 2025 reaching the highest reported number of cases at 3,748. This pattern suggests a potential link to environmental or behavioral changes around early spring, such as food practices or sanitation conditions, which may be more conducive to Hepatitis E virus transmission.

Despite case fluctuations, the reported deaths remain comparatively low and loosely associated with monthly case numbers. Some high-incidence months show few or no deaths, indicating variability in disease severity, healthcare access, or demographic vulnerability. For example, March 2024 recorded 3,676 cases and zero deaths, whereas lower case months like October 2017 reported three deaths. The relatively stable fatality figures, even during large outbreaks, suggest that medical management or early detection may be effective at preventing severe outcomes. Overall, while seasonal surges persist, year-to-year totals don't indicate a long-term upward or downward trend, implying periodic endemic behavior rather than an expanding epidemic. Continued surveillance and preventive measures, especially ahead of spring, are essential to mitigating recurrent spikes.
