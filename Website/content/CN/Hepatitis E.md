# Hepatitis E

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis E is a liver disease caused by the hepatitis E virus (HEV), primarily transmitted through the fecal-oral route due to contaminated water. It is prevalent in developing regions with limited access to clean water and sanitation. The disease often results in acute, self-limiting infection, but can be severe in pregnant women, potentially leading to liver failure. Most cases are asymptomatic or mild, with symptoms like jaundice, fatigue, and nausea. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but preventive measures include ensuring safe drinking water and proper sanitation. Vaccines are available in some countries.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing case count in 2024**: Hepatitis E cases in September 2024 (2,446) showed a steady decrease from March 2024 (3,676), suggesting a potential decline in new infections. <br/>
- **Low mortality rate**: Despite fluctuating case numbers, the death count remains low, with zero deaths reported in September 2024, continuing the trend of low mortality over recent months. <br/>
- **Seasonal variation**: Higher case numbers are consistently observed in spring months (March-May), indicating possible seasonal patterns of transmission. <br/>
- **Long-term stabilization**: The overall number of cases since 2020 has been relatively stable compared to earlier years, reflecting possible improvements in public health measures. <br/>

The analysis of Hepatitis E cases in mainland China from September 2014 to September 2024 reveals a fluctuating trend, with cases peaking during early months, especially in March. Notably, March consistently sees the highest case counts, such as 3401 in 2016, 3352 in 2017, and 3676 in 2024, suggesting that the winter-spring transition may contribute to increased transmission. Environmental or behavioral factors, such as waterborne outbreaks, may be responsible for this pattern.

Mortality remains relatively low, though there are occasional spikes, such as five deaths in March 2016. This suggests that healthcare access and system strain could play a role in outcomes. A decline in cases around 2020 may reflect public health interventions or COVID-19-related disruptions, though cases rebounded in 2021 and beyond, signaling the ongoing importance of surveillance and preventive efforts to manage Hepatitis E.
