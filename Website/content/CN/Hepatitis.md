# Hepatitis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis refers to inflammation of the liver, typically caused by viral infections, though other factors like alcohol abuse, toxins, medications, and autoimmune diseases can also contribute. There are several types, including Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each varying in transmission modes, severity, and long-term impact. Symptoms may encompass jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and flu-like signs. While Hepatitis B and C are linked to chronic conditions with potential liver damage, cirrhosis, and cancer, vaccines and treatments are available to manage and prevent the infections.

## Highlights

- The number of hepatitis cases in China has fluctuated over recent years, with noticeable peaks in March 2018, March 2021, and March 2023, suggesting potential seasonal trends or outbreaks.<br/>
- An alarming increase in deaths is observed from late 2023 onwards, peaking in December 2024 with 632 deaths, indicating a rise in severe outcomes or complications.<br/>
- Despite the reduction in deaths at the beginning of 2025 compared to the previous months, March 2025 still shows significant mortality figures, suggesting ongoing challenges in controlling severe cases.<br/>
- Overall, consistent monitoring and interventions are crucial to address the increasing case count and prevent mortality as the disease progresses into 2025.<br/>

Over ten years, from 2015 to 2025, hepatitis cases on the Chinese mainland showed notable fluctuations and periodic peaks. The data highlights an overall increase in case numbers, with significant spikes such as 166,606 cases in August 2023 compared to a low of 72,630 cases in December 2022. Notably, March through May often reported higher cases, suggesting seasonal or behavioral influences on transmission.

Mortality associated with hepatitis reflects a concerning trend, with deaths peaking at 632 in December 2024, compared to typically under 60 deaths per month from 2015 to 2019. This rise might indicate increasing severity of cases, complications, or reduced effectiveness of treatments. Possible factors include emerging virulent strains, healthcare resource strain, and socio-economic impacts. While case counts fluctuate, deaths exhibit a clear upward trend, underscoring the urgent need for tailored public health strategies and resource allocation to manage both incidence and mortality effectively.
