# Hepatitis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis is liver inflammation caused by viruses, alcohol abuse, medications, or autoimmune disorders. Common viral types include Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each differing in transmission and health impact. Hepatitis A and E are often spread via contaminated food or water, while B, C, and D are transmitted through blood and bodily fluids. Symptoms range from mild to severe, such as fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal pain. Chronic infections, especially B and C, can cause significant liver damage, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Prevention includes vaccination, safe practices, and proper sanitation.

## Highlights

- Hepatitis cases in Chinese mainland increased significantly from 2023 onward, reaching a peak of 181,006 in March 2024, followed by a decrease to 136,194 by July 2025. <br/>
- Deaths showed a sharp increase post-2023, peaking at 632 in December 2024, then dropped to 283 by July 2025, indicating improved disease management or healthcare access. <br/>
- Seasonal variation is evident, with higher instances in March and July across years, suggesting possible seasonal transmission influences. <br/>
- Continuous monitoring and intervention remain essential to control fluctuations in cases and deaths, supporting enhanced public health outcomes and outbreak management efforts. <br/>

The reported data for hepatitis cases in mainland China from 2015 to mid-2025 indicates a dynamic pattern with waves of both cases and fatalities. Initially, the monthly cases hover around the 115,000 to 130,000 range, illustrating a relatively stable but significant burden of the disease. In the early years, peaks are visible around March and April, potentially suggesting seasonality or reporting phenomena. Death counts during this period are generally stable, albeit with minor fluctuations, typically staying below 60 per month, which corresponds to a relatively low case-fatality rate.

However, a noticeable shift occurs towards the mid-2023 period. A surge in deaths, starting from May 2023 with an unprecedented spike reaching 428 and continuing into 2024, suggests a considerable increase in case severity or a change in how cases and deaths are reported. The year 2024 continues to show a higher number of cases and significantly elevated deaths compared to previous years, peaking at 632 deaths in December. While the recent data shows a decrease in cases in early 2025, deaths remain comparatively high, highlighting possible systemic health challenges or an emerging hepatitis strain with heightened virulence. These trends underscore a need for targeted public health responses, heightened surveillance, and possibly, enhanced healthcare resources to attend to the increased burden.
