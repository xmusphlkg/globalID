# Hepatitis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis refers to the inflammation of the liver, commonly caused by viral infections. The condition can be acute or chronic and is classified into five main types: Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each caused by different viruses. Hepatitis A and E are usually transmitted through contaminated food or water, while B, C, and D are primarily spread through contact with infected bodily fluids. Symptoms include fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal pain, although many cases can be asymptomatic. Chronic hepatitis can lead to serious liver damage, including cirrhosis and liver cancer. Vaccines are available for Hepatitis A and B.

## Highlights

- Rising Cases: From 2015 to June 2025, there is a notable upward trend in hepatitis cases, peaking in March 2024 with 181,006 cases. This highlights growing public health concerns demanding focused interventions. <br/>

- Increasing Deaths: Deaths have sharply risen since 2023; June 2025 reported 336 deaths, indicating significant mortality and necessitating urgent public health responses. <br/>

- Seasonal Patterns: Cases and deaths show seasonal variability, often peaking in warmer months, calling for adaptive health strategies. <br/>

- Recent Trends: Although cases remain high, there is a slight decline by mid-2025 from peaks in 2023 and 2024, suggesting potential stabilization through public health measures. <br/>

Through 2015 to 2019, hepatitis cases and associated deaths remained relatively consistent, averaging around 50 deaths per month, suggesting a period of effective disease management, possibly due to stable healthcare access and treatments. January 2020 experienced an unusual spike in deaths to 100, potentially indicating specific incidents or healthcare disruptions requiring further inquiry.

A dramatic shift occurs from late 2022 onwards, with deaths escalating significantly. By late 2023 and throughout 2024, mortality rates rose abruptly, reaching over 600 deaths monthly by December 2024. This surge suggests an increasing severity of hepatitis or a strain that is more virulent, alongside potential stress on the healthcare infrastructure. These increasing death trends, contrasted against fluctuating but not dramatically increasing case numbers, highlight possible serious challenges such as treatment delays, access issues, or decreased efficacy in existing health interventions. The numbers in 2025 indicate some measures may be beginning to impact the situation with lower deaths in some months, yet remain critically high, stressing the ongoing public health situation. This situation should prompt urgent investigation into potential mutations, healthcare delivery assessments, and adjustments to public health strategies to curb what appears to be a severe ongoing health crisis.
