# Hepatitis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis refers to the inflammation of the liver, often caused by viral infections, including Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E. Each type is associated with different modes of transmission, such as contaminated food or water (Hepatitis A and E), or blood and bodily fluids (Hepatitis B, C, and D). Symptoms can range from mild fever and jaundice to severe chronic illness, potentially leading to liver cirrhosis or cancer. Hepatitis can also result from non-infectious causes, including alcohol use, toxins, medications, and autoimmune diseases. Vaccinations exist for Hepatitis A and B, serving as effective preventative measures.

## Highlights

- **Rising cases**: Hepatitis cases have steadily increased, with over 154,000 reported in November 2024. <br/>
- **Increased mortality**: Deaths have also risen, reaching 495 in November 2024, reflecting a growing mortality trend. <br/>
- **Peak in 2023-2024**: Both cases and deaths peaked in 2023 and 2024, with notable surges in May 2023 (170 deaths) and August 2024 (438 deaths). <br/>
- **Persistent challenge**: The ongoing high incidence, especially in 2024, highlights hepatitis as a continuing public health concern in China. <br/>

Hepatitis cases in mainland China have shown notable fluctuations over the years, with significant spikes observed in 2023 and 2024, especially between March and November 2024, when case numbers exceeded 150,000 in multiple months. This surge could be due to factors like better detection, reporting changes, or an increase in transmission, indicating potential outbreaks or public health challenges.

While the case fatality ratio has generally remained low, there is a concerning rise in deaths in recent months. From late 2023 into 2024, deaths have increased sharply, peaking at 495 in November 2024, compared to lower death tolls in earlier years. The growing number of cases alongside this rise in fatalities suggests emerging issues such as a more virulent hepatitis strain or healthcare system pressures. This warrants closer scrutiny to understand the reasons behind both the rising morbidity and mortality, and to assess the effectiveness of ongoing control measures. Continued monitoring and a tailored response are essential to manage the evolving hepatitis burden and prevent further escalation.
