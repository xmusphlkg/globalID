# Hepatitis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis refers to an inflammation of the liver, commonly caused by viral infections but also due to other factors such as alcohol consumption, toxins, and autoimmune diseases. The most prevalent types are Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each caused by different viruses and varying in transmission methods and outcomes. Hepatitis B and C can lead to chronic liver disease, cirrhosis, or liver cancer if untreated. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea. Vaccinations are available for Hepatitis A and B, while antiviral medications are used to manage Hepatitis B and C infections.

## Highlights

- A notable trend is the overall fluctuation in both hepatitis cases and deaths over the years, with a particularly sharp spike in deaths noted from 2023 onwards, peaking in 2024. <br/>

- The number of cases reached their highest peaks in March 2024 and fluctuated throughout 2025, with a gradual decline approaching October 2025. <br/>

- Deaths exhibit a concerning increase in 2024, reaching a peak in December, followed by fluctuations in 2025, with the highest numbers in mid-year before settling slightly by October. <br/>

- The data suggests a need for intensified public health interventions and monitoring to address the rise in fatalities despite relatively stable case numbers. <br/>

The hepatitis data from 2015 to 2025 in the Chinese mainland highlight dynamic epidemiological trends. Between late 2015 and early 2020, reported cases of hepatitis fluctuated between approximately 100,000 and 150,000 per month, while deaths stayed relatively stable, generally between the mid-30s to mid-60s monthly. An anomaly occurs in February 2020, with cases dropping sharply to 63,330, likely influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic and associated public health measures, which may have altered data collection or reduced transmission through changes in social behavior.

Starting in 2023, there is a marked increase in both the number of hepatitis cases and deaths. By late 2023 into 2024, deaths rise significantly, peaking at 632 in December 2024, alongside high case numbers, peaking at 181,006 in March 2024. This surge in mortality suggests several potential factors, including a more virulent strain, reduced healthcare system resilience due to the prolonged pandemic, or limited treatment accessibility. 

Despite a slight reduction in cases and deaths in 2025, numbers remain higher than the pre-2023 period, indicating ongoing challenges. This sustained elevation underscores the need for targeted public health interventions, policy reforms, and resource allocation to effectively manage and mitigate the impact of hepatitis in China's mainland region.
