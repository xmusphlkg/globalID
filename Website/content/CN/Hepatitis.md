# Hepatitis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver commonly caused by viral infections, specifically hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E viruses, but it can also arise from toxins, medications, and autoimmune disorders. Symptoms include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and dark urine. It varies in severity, with acute cases potentially leading to chronic conditions like cirrhosis or liver cancer. The transmission routes differ: hepatitis A and E spread through contaminated food and water, whereas B, C, and D are largely transmitted through blood and bodily fluids. Vaccines exist for hepatitis A and B, helping prevent infection.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Cases:** Hepatitis cases in China have risen from around 110,000 in 2014 to peaks over 180,000 in 2024, indicating a growing public health concern. <br/>
- **Sharp Mortality Rise:** Deaths have increased significantly, with 438 recorded in August 2024, compared to just 42 in October 2014, highlighting a serious issue. <br/>
- **Recent Volatility:** The last two years show significant fluctuations in cases and deaths, suggesting possible outbreaks or changes in data reporting. <br/>
- **Public Health Challenge:** The upward trend in hepatitis morbidity and mortality underscores a need for strengthened intervention and control strategies. <br/>

From October 2014 to October 2024, Hepatitis cases in mainland China exhibited distinct patterns. Initially, from 2014 to 2019, monthly cases hovered around 120,000 with moderate seasonal fluctuations, while deaths remained consistently low, averaging about 48 per month. In February 2020, a marked decline in cases was likely linked to the COVID-19 pandemic's impact, but figures soon returned to pre-pandemic levels.

Starting in 2020, both Hepatitis cases and deaths began to rise significantly, with pronounced spikes in 2023 and 2024. The number of reported cases reached unprecedented levels in 2023, exceeding 150,000 in multiple months. This surge in cases coincided with a dramatic increase in mortality, especially from May 2023, when deaths rose sharply, peaking at 428 in December 2023. This upward trend continued into 2024, with death rates reaching their highest in August at 438.

This alarming rise in both cases and fatalities suggests potential outbreaks of more virulent virus strains, health system challenges, or changes in reporting practices, which warrant further investigation and targeted public health interventions. Understanding these trends is crucial for developing responsive measures and ensuring improved health outcomes in the future.

An analysis of the Hepatitis death data from Chinese mainland spanning from October 2014 to October 2024 reveals distinct trends and variations over the years. Initially, the number of deaths fluctuated moderately around a relatively low range from about 25 to 61 deaths per month between 2014 and late 2019. This period reveals no significant or consistent upward or downward trend, suggesting effective control measures or stable reporting conditions. However, a notable rise in deaths starts to emerge in January 2020, potentially linked with other concurrent events affecting public health, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, although the precise reasons for this increase would require further contextual epidemiological investigations.

From mid-2023 onwards, a drastic surge in the number of deaths is observed, climbing sharply from 170 deaths in May 2023 to an alarming peak of 438 deaths in August 2024. This surge could be indicative of either an outbreak of a more virulent strain or failures in the healthcare system's response capacities—possibly affected by external factors like reduced healthcare access or reporting changes. Observing the accompanying case numbers, which also increase but not as dramatically as the death toll, suggests other systemic issues might exacerbate the fatality rates. The stark rise in deaths calls for urgent public health interventions to identify the underlying causes and implement measures to curb the fatalities effectively.
