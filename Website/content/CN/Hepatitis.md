# Hepatitis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver commonly caused by viral infections, toxins, or autoimmune reactions. The main viral types are hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each differing in transmission routes and severity. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and abnormal liver enzyme levels. Acute hepatitis often resolves spontaneously, while chronic hepatitis can lead to long-term liver damage, such as cirrhosis or liver cancer. Screening, vaccination, and safe practices are crucial for prevention and control. Hepatitis remains a significant public health concern worldwide.

## Highlights

- **Significant rise in deaths**: Since mid-2023, Hepatitis deaths in mainland China have increased sharply, with 585 reported in December 2025, compared to under 70 per month in previous years. <br/>
- **Consistently high case numbers**: Monthly cases remained above 120,000 since early 2020, peaking near 180,000 in early 2024, and slightly declining to 128,222 by December 2025. <br/>
- **Seasonal and periodic fluctuations**: Both cases and deaths showed expected monthly variation, but the recent surge in deaths signals an emerging public health challenge. <br/>
- **Current situation**: As of December 2025, Hepatitis remains a significant epidemic in China, with persistently high cases and escalating mortality rates. <br/>

Analysis of hepatitis data from mainland China shows that, from 2015 through 2022, reported cases remained largely consistent month-to-month, typically ranging between 110,000 and 150,000, with usually lower counts in winter and some peaks in spring and summer. Mortality during this period was stable, with deaths rarely exceeding 60 per month. There was a notable dip in case reporting during February 2020, likely due to disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, but the numbers soon returned to usual levels, suggesting the decrease was due to reporting or healthcare access issues rather than a true decline in incidence.

Starting in early 2023, both hepatitis case numbers and deaths soared. Monthly deaths began exceeding 100, escalating to over 600 by the end of 2024, while cases frequently surpassed 150,000 and peaked above 180,000. These abrupt increases could indicate significant shifts, such as the emergence of more severe hepatitis strains, outbreaks in high-risk groups, declining healthcare capacity, or changes in reporting methods and definitions. The persistence of elevated mortality with ongoing high case numbers into 2025 signals a sustained public health challenge. Further investigation is needed to clarify the underlying drivers, whether epidemiologic, clinical, or related to surveillance, and to guide effective interventions and resource allocation to control hepatitis and prevent future spikes.

Reported hepatitis deaths in the Chinese mainland show stability from 2015 through 2022, with monthly deaths typically ranging between 35 and 74. This suggests a consistent mortality burden relative to reported cases, which also remained fairly stable. Notably, early 2020, coinciding with the onset of COVID-19, did not substantially disrupt this pattern except for isolated months with modest spikes, such as January 2020 (100 deaths). Mild seasonal variation is evident, but overall, the trend remains flat during these years.

A dramatic shift emerges in 2023, with hepatitis deaths rising sharply from 96 in April to over 300 per month by November, peaking at 632 in December 2024. This increase is not mirrored by a proportional shift in case numbers, pointing to potential changes in disease severity, management, or reporting practices. The heightened mortality persists into 2025, with only brief dips before returning to elevated levels. The stark recent rise signals a concerning transformation in the epidemiological profile of hepatitis, possibly reflecting an outbreak of a more lethal strain, changes in healthcare access, or other systemic issues impacting vulnerable populations. This warrants focused epidemiological investigation to clarify whether these trends are artifacts or represent a genuine public health emergency, with major implications for prevention and control strategies going forward.
