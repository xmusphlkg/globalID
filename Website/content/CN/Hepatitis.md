# Hepatitis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis is the inflammation of the liver, primarily caused by viral infections. The main types are hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each linked to specific viruses. Transmission varies: A and E spread through contaminated food and water, while B, C, and D are often transmitted via blood and bodily fluids. Symptoms range from fatigue and jaundice to severe liver damage. Vaccines are available for hepatitis A and B, reducing incidence rates. Effective management relies on early detection and treatment, crucial to preventing chronic conditions and liver complications associated with the disease.

## Highlights

- The number of hepatitis cases in the Chinese mainland fluctuated over the years, with some peaks observed such as in 2024 March, indicating potential outbreaks. <br/>
- Case fatalities have shown significant variation, particularly with increased deaths noted from mid-2023 onwards, suggesting a possible shift in disease severity or healthcare challenges. <br/>
- Monthly data reveals periodic spikes in cases and deaths, with the highest mortality reported in late 2023 and 2024, possibly due to evolving epidemic dynamics. <br/>
- As of September 2025, while case numbers have somewhat stabilized compared to previous peaks, the persistently high death toll necessitates strengthened public health measures and further investigation into contributing factors. <br/>

From 2015 to 2019, hepatitis cases and deaths in mainland China showed relative stability. The cases slightly increased in 2018 and 2019, but deaths remained fairly steady with minor month-to-month variations. A notable disruption occurred in 2020, corresponding with the COVID-19 pandemic. In February 2020, both cases and deaths dropped significantly, likely reflecting healthcare system disruptions or changes in disease reporting due to the pandemic's focus.

Significant changes appear from 2023 onward. There is a marked increase in reported deaths, with an alarming peak in December 2023 at 428 deaths. From mid-2023, the death toll consistently rose, suggesting either an increase in the severity of hepatitis cases or systemic healthcare challenges. By 2024, cases and deaths are significantly higher, possibly indicating a shift in the virus's virulence or a crisis in healthcare capacities. The consistent rise in deaths, reaching 632 by December 2024, highlights a severe public health concern.

In early 2025, although cases slightly decline, the death numbers remain high, pointing to ongoing healthcare issues. This trend necessitates urgent public health interventions, enhanced treatment protocols, and strategic healthcare improvements to tackle the rising fatalities and control the disease's impact across the region effectively.

An analysis of hepatitis deaths in mainland China from September 2015 to September 2025 reveals a concerning trend, particularly in recent years. Initially, the data from 2015 shows a relatively stable pattern with monthly deaths ranging between 36 and 56. This stability continues into subsequent years, suggesting a manageable scenario despite fluctuating case numbers.

However, in 2023, the situation changes dramatically with a rapid increase in deaths, rising to triple digits. The monthly fatalities exceed 300 by 2025, reaching 632 in December 2024. This escalation coincides with an increase in cases, hitting as high as 181,006 in March 2024, indicating potential systemic or environmental shifts affecting disease management.

This trend suggests strain on the healthcare system in managing hepatitis effectively, with possible factors including changes in viral strains, population health declines, or treatment strategy lapses. The sharp rise in deaths calls for urgent investigation into underlying causes and intervention strategies. Identifying contributing factors is crucial to curb this mortality surge, highlighting the need for improved hepatitis management practices, increased research on effective treatments, and public health initiatives to reduce disease impact. The alarming rise accentuates the need for coordinated action to improve patient outcomes and prevent further increases in hepatitis-related deaths.
