# Hepatitis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis refers to inflammation of the liver, typically caused by viral infections, but it can also result from toxic substances, autoimmune diseases, or medications. The most common viral types are hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each varying in transmission, severity, and prevention methods. Hepatitis A and E are often transmitted through contaminated food or water, whereas hepatitis B, C, and D primarily spread through blood-to-blood contact. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and dark urine. Effective vaccines exist for hepatitis A and B, and antiviral treatments can manage chronic infections like hepatitis B and C.

## Highlights

- Fluctuating Monthly Cases: From 2015 to 2025, monthly hepatitis cases in China showed variability, peaking in March 2018, March 2024, and July 2023. Recent data indicates a declining trend from early 2025.<br/>
- Rising Mortality: A notable increase in hepatitis-related deaths occurred from late 2023 into 2024, peaking in December 2024. Despite reduced cases in 2025, mortality remains elevated compared to pre-2023 figures.<br/>
- Seasonal Variability: Higher case counts generally coincide with late winter and spring months, suggesting potential seasonal influences.<br/>
- Recent Decrease: By August 2025, both cases and death counts show a downward trend from previous highs, indicating potential improvements in disease management and intervention efforts.<br/>

Analyzing hepatitis data from the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 highlights significant trends in both cases and deaths. Initially, cases hovered around 120,000 to 130,000 monthly, from 2015 to 2019, with peaks often in March. However, 2020 shows a decrease in cases, possibly due to the impact of COVID-19 on healthcare access or reporting. Despite this decline, from 2023 onwards, there is a noticeable increase in cases, consistently exceeding 150,000 by 2024, indicating heightened transmission or better detection strategies.

The mortality data reveals concerning trends. From 2015 to around 2019, deaths remained fairly stable, averaging between 30-60 per month. Starting in 2023, there is a sharp escalation in deaths, peaking dramatically in 2024 with 632 deaths in December. This rise could signal increased hepatitis virulence, new strain emergence, complications in disease management, or healthcare intervention shortcomings. The spike calls for urgent epidemiological investigation to identify and address contributing factors, enhance containment measures, and improve healthcare responses. Understanding the underlying causes and implementing strategic interventions is crucial to mitigate this growing public health threat efficiently. This situation highlights the importance of tracking and acting upon epidemiological data to manage disease impacts proactively.

Occasional peaks, such as the 100 deaths in January 2020, possibly correlate with global factors impacting healthcare access and mortality reporting.

Starting in 2023, a marked increase in deaths emerges sharply. In May 2023, deaths surged to 170, continuing upward to 206 in June, peaking at 257 in October 2023, and then escalating to 632 by December 2024. This alarming rise could suggest increased viral virulence, healthcare service strain, or reporting changes, potentially due to enhanced diagnostics or outbreak conditions. Though case numbers rose, the disproportionate increase in mortality underscores the need for in-depth analysis into contributing factors, such as health infrastructure challenges, treatment efficacy, and potential new Hepatitis strains affecting outcomes.

Post-peak, data from 2025 indicates a relative normalization, yet deaths remain elevated compared to pre-2023 levels, suggesting an altered epidemiological landscape. Identifying the drivers behind these mortality spikes and addressing structural healthcare weaknesses could be crucial in controlling future Hepatitis-related fatalities and improving overall public health outcomes.
