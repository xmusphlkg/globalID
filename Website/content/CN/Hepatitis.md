# Hepatitis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver, commonly caused by viral infections, excessive alcohol consumption, or autoimmune diseases. There are several types of viral hepatitis, including Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each with distinct transmission routes and health implications. Hepatitis A and E are typically transmitted through contaminated food and water, while Hepatitis B, C, and D are spread through blood and bodily fluids. Symptoms may range from mild to severe and can include fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal pain. Chronic hepatitis can lead to serious complications, such as liver cirrhosis and liver cancer.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases:** Hepatitis cases in mainland China have increased significantly from 2015 to January 2025, with a peak of 181,006 cases in March 2024. <br/>

- **Increasing Mortality:** Deaths have surged, especially from 2023, peaking at 632 in December 2024, indicating worsening severity or reporting. <br/>

- **Seasonal Trends:** There are seasonal fluctuations, with higher cases and deaths typically in the first half of each year. <br/>

- **Recent Decline in Deaths:** January 2025 shows a decrease in deaths to 72, despite high case numbers, suggesting improvements in treatment or reporting. <br/>

The data on hepatitis cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several key trends. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, the number of cases fluctuated moderately, with monthly cases ranging from approximately 92,978 to 144,519. During this period, deaths remained relatively stable, with monthly figures generally between 25 and 68. This suggests a consistent but controlled public health situation regarding hepatitis, with no significant spikes in mortality despite variations in case numbers.

However, starting in 2020, there is a noticeable increase in both cases and deaths, with a particularly sharp rise in deaths from 2023 onwards. For instance, deaths surged from 100 in January 2020 to 632 by December 2024, despite fluctuations in case numbers. This dramatic increase in mortality could indicate a shift in the disease's severity, potential changes in the virus's virulence, or challenges in healthcare response and management. The data from 2023 to 2024, in particular, highlights a critical period where deaths consistently exceeded 100 per month, peaking at 632 in December 2024, which calls for an urgent investigation into the underlying causes and a reassessment of public health strategies to mitigate this trend.
