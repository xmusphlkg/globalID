# Hepatitis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver, often caused by viral infections, but it can also result from toxins, alcohol, medications, or autoimmune diseases. The most common viral types are Hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each differing in transmission methods and severity. Hepatitis A and E are typically spread through contaminated food or water, while B, C, and D are primarily transmitted via blood and bodily fluids. Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and nausea. Chronic hepatitis, particularly types B and C, can lead to severe liver damage, cirrhosis, or liver cancer.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers:** Hepatitis cases in the Chinese mainland have generally increased from 2015 to 2025, peaking in March 2024 with 181,006 cases. <br/>

- **Increasing Mortality Rates:** Deaths have risen significantly, especially from 2023, with the highest in December 2024 at 632 deaths. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** There are seasonal fluctuations, with higher cases typically in the first half of each year. <br/>

- **Recent Decline:** As of February 2025, there is a slight decrease in cases and deaths compared to previous months, suggesting potential stabilization or effective interventions. <br/>

The data on hepatitis cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several trends and fluctuations. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, the number of reported cases and deaths remained relatively stable, with minor fluctuations. The monthly cases ranged from approximately 104,000 to 151,000, and deaths varied between 25 and 68. This period suggests a relatively controlled situation, possibly due to effective public health measures and consistent healthcare responses.

However, starting in 2020, there is a noticeable increase in both cases and deaths, with a significant spike in deaths observed from 2023 onwards. For instance, in 2023, the number of deaths rose dramatically, peaking at 428 in December, despite the number of cases not showing a proportionate increase. This trend continues into 2024, with deaths reaching 632 in December, indicating a potential shift in the disease's severity or changes in reporting or healthcare challenges. The data suggests a need for further investigation into the causes of increased mortality, which could include factors such as changes in the virus's virulence, healthcare system strain, or other socio-economic factors impacting health outcomes.

The data on hepatitis cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several key trends. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, the number of hepatitis cases fluctuated moderately, with monthly cases ranging from approximately 104,000 to 144,000. During this period, the number of deaths remained relatively stable, generally between 25 and 68 per month. This suggests a consistent management of the disease, with a relatively low mortality rate compared to the number of cases.

However, starting in 2020, there is a noticeable increase in the number of deaths, particularly from 2023 onwards. For instance, deaths rose sharply from 100 in January 2020 to 632 by December 2024, despite the number of cases not increasing proportionally. This significant rise in mortality could indicate several potential issues, such as the emergence of more virulent strains, reduced effectiveness of treatment protocols, or challenges in healthcare delivery. The data from 2023 and 2024, in particular, show a dramatic increase in deaths, peaking at 632 in December 2024, which warrants further investigation to understand the underlying causes and to develop strategies to mitigate this trend.
