# Hepatitis

## Introduction

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver, often caused by viral infections, toxins, or autoimmune conditions. The most common types are hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E, each differing in mode of transmission and severity. Hepatitis A and E are typically transmitted through contaminated food and water, while B, C, and D are spread via blood and bodily fluids. Symptoms may include fatigue, jaundice, abdominal pain, and nausea, but some cases remain asymptomatic. Chronic hepatitis, particularly types B and C, can lead to serious liver damage, cirrhosis, or liver cancer, necessitating timely diagnosis and management.

## Highlights

- **Rising Hepatitis Cases:** From 2014 to July 2024, Hepatitis cases in the Chinese mainland have steadily increased, reaching 170,397 in July 2024, up from 133,888 in June 2023.  
- **Escalating Deaths:** There is a notable increase in deaths, peaking at 428 in December 2023, with 330 reported in July 2024.  
- **Seasonal Trends:** Higher case numbers are observed in early to mid-year, with mortality spikes toward the yearâ€™s end.  
- **Public Health Concern:** The consistent rise in cases and deaths indicates an escalating public health issue that requires immediate attention and intervention.

The reported hepatitis cases in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to mid-2024 show significant fluctuations. Cases display a seasonal pattern, with peaks occurring at the start of each year. The number of cases gradually increased from 2017 to 2019, reaching a peak in March 2019 with 144,519 cases. A drop in cases occurred in early 2020, likely influenced by public health measures during the COVID-19 pandemic, but cases rose again sharply in 2023 and 2024, with a peak of 181,006 in March 2024.

Mortality rates followed a concerning upward trend from 2023 onward. Initially, deaths fluctuated with no clear correlation to case numbers, but starting in early 2023, the number of deaths significantly increased, even as cases rose. For example, July 2024 saw 330 deaths, despite fewer cases than in earlier months. This rise in deaths, particularly from 2023, suggests either a more severe form of hepatitis or healthcare challenges exacerbating the fatality rate. 

This data highlights the need for further investigation into the factors driving increased deaths, such as healthcare system strain or changes in hepatitis severity, to prevent further morbidity and mortality.
