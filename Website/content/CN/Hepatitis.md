# Hepatitis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver, commonly caused by viral infections, notably hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E viruses. It can also result from toxins, alcohol use, medications, and autoimmune diseases. Viral hepatitis is a major global health concern, leading to both acute and chronic liver disease. Transmission varies by type: through contaminated food or water (A, E), blood and bodily fluids (B, C, D). Symptoms may include jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and loss of appetite. Chronic hepatitis, especially B and C, increases the risk of liver cirrhosis and cancer. Prevention includes vaccination and safe practices.

## Highlights

1. **Case Surge with Recent Stabilization**: Hepatitis cases rose sharply from 2023, peaking in March 2024 (181,006), but showed a decline into May 2025 (130,327).<br/>

2. **Sharp Rise in Deaths**: Deaths increased dramatically from mid-2023, reaching a record 632 in December 2024. Elevated mortality continued into 2025.<br/>

3. **Seasonal Trends**: Peaks in cases occur in spring (Marchâ€“April), consistent across years, suggesting seasonal drivers or reporting patterns.<br/>

4. **Signs of Control**: The recent drop in both cases and deaths by May 2025 suggests the impact of improved surveillance or interventions, though mortality remains above pre-2023 levels.<br/>

From mid-2015 to late 2022, monthly hepatitis cases in mainland China remained relatively stable, typically ranging from 110,000 to 140,000. Deaths during this period were consistent, mostly between 30 and 60 per month. Starting in early 2023, a sharp increase occurred both in reported casesâ€”often exceeding 150,000â€”and in mortality, which rose dramatically from 96 deaths in April 2023 to 632 by December 2024. This surge in deaths was disproportionate to the rise in cases, suggesting possible factors such as a more severe hepatitis strain, healthcare system strain, or changes in case classification and death reporting.

Seasonal patterns appear throughout the dataset, with peaks common in March and late summer months, aligning with known environmental and behavioral factors influencing transmission. The post-2023 escalation in deaths, without a corresponding initial spike in cases, may hint at delays in diagnosis or underestimation of case severity earlier. From January 2025 onward, mortality began to decline despite sustained high case numbers, possibly indicating the impact of intervention efforts or a reversion to less virulent strains. The transition suggests shifting dynamics in disease presentation or management during and after the 2023â€“2024 period. Overall, the late-period mortality spike stands out as a key anomaly requiring further investigation into clinical, policy, or surveillance changes.
