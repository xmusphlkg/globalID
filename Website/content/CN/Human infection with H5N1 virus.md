# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, is a severe respiratory disease caused by the influenza A virus subtype H5N1. This virus primarily affects birds but can occasionally infect humans who have close contact with infected birds or contaminated environments. H5N1 infections in humans are rare but often result in severe respiratory illness with high mortality rates. Symptoms typically include fever, cough, sore throat, and muscle aches, progressing to severe respiratory complications. Human-to-human transmission is limited and rare, but the potential for the virus to mutate raises global health concerns.

## Highlights

- Human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland have remained sporadic, with limited cases and deaths observed primarily in 2015 and 2016. <br/>
- There was a notable absence of reported cases and deaths from 2017 until early 2022, suggesting a period of inactive viral transmission. <br/>
- Following a case in September 2022 and one early in 2023, no further cases or deaths have been recorded as of June 2025, indicating continued low-level risk or interrupted transmission. <br/>
- Overall, the disease situation for H5N1 in the Chinese mainland shows a downward trend, likely due to effective control measures and reduced zoonotic spillover. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to mid-2025 depict a sparse and sporadic pattern of cases. Initially, from mid-2015 to 2016, there were three reported cases, with two resulting in death, indicating a high case fatality rate of 66.7%. This early period was characterized by isolated instances without suggesting a widespread outbreak, hinting at localized exposure or transmission.

After 2016, the data show a prolonged interval with no reported cases until late 2022, suggesting a possible interruption in the virus's transmission cycle or effective containment measures. In September 2022, a single case was reported, followed by another in February 2023, both non-fatal. This minor resurgence interrupts a general lull in activity, but the absence of subsequent cases into 2025 indicates limited or isolated transmission events.

The sporadic nature of the cases, with years of inactivity, highlights the effectiveness of surveillance and control measures, though the reasons for the isolated cases could include contact with infected animals or enhanced detection capabilities. Despite low incidence, maintaining vigilant surveillance and public health measures remains crucial to prevent potential future outbreaks and ensure quick response to any new cases.
