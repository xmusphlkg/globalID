# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, primarily occurs through direct or close contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. First identified in 1997 in Hong Kong, this virus is of particular concern due to its high pathogenicity in birds and potential to cause severe respiratory illness in humans. Although human-to-human transmission remains rare, H5N1 infections have a high mortality rate, prompting scrutiny and monitoring for signs of genetic mutations that could facilitate easier transmission. Preventive measures focus on controlling outbreaks in poultry, ensuring biosafety, and developing vaccines.

## Highlights

- Human infection with H5N1 in the Chinese mainland has been extremely rare, with only a few isolated cases since 2016. <br/>
- The last recorded H5N1 case occurred in February 2023, showing sporadic transmission with no cases in 2024 or 2025. <br/>
- There was a peak in cases in early 2016, with three cases and two deaths, indicating a higher mortality rate during that period. <br/>
- The absence of new cases from late 2023 to August 2025 suggests effective control measures and surveillance, maintaining the virus at non-endemic levels. <br/>

The data from the Chinese mainland concerning human infections with the H5N1 virus over a span from August 2015 to August 2025 indicates a very low incidence of cases, with minimal deaths. Starting from 2015, there is an absence of cases and deaths recorded until January 2016, which marked the first reported human infection, though with no recorded deaths. A subsequent death occurred in February 2016 without an associated new case, suggesting either an earlier undocumented infection or a complication in reporting. In May 2016, another case resulted in one death, marking the highest mortality month in the dataset with a 100% case-fatality rate.

Following the sporadic cases in the first part of 2016, there was a long stretch without any reported cases or deaths extending until late 2022. September 2022 broke this relative silence with another case report, mirrored again in February 2023, showing a similar pattern of isolated, non-clustered cases. The prolonged periods without cases through 2024 and up to mid-2025 suggest effective control measures or changes in virus transmissibility dynamics. The data reveals intermittent transmission with no indication of sustained human-to-human spread, reflecting successful containment efforts and perhaps sporadic zoonotic transmission events without persistent community transmission. This pattern necessitates continuous surveillance and prompt public health responses to maintain low case numbers and prevent potential outbreaks.
