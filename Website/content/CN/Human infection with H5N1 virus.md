# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

H5N1 is a subtype of the influenza A virus that primarily affects birds but can infect humans. Human infection typically occurs through direct contact with infected birds or contaminated environments. Symptoms can range from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia and can lead to death. The virus is known for its high mortality rate in humans, making it a significant public health concern. While human-to-human transmission is rare, the potential for mutation raises fears of a pandemic. Surveillance and vaccination strategies are crucial for managing the risks associated with H5N1.

## Highlights

- **Initial Outbreak and Decline:** The H5N1 virus had sporadic cases in early 2015, peaking at three cases in March. By mid-2015, cases declined, with no reports from August 2015 to August 2022. <br/>

- **Resurgence in 2022:** A case emerged in September 2022, followed by another in February 2023, indicating a minor resurgence. No deaths were linked to these cases, suggesting improved management. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of January 2025, no new cases have been reported since February 2023, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Mortality Rate:** The overall mortality rate from 2015 to 2025 is high, with 6 deaths out of 11 cases, highlighting the virus's severity. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 shows a notable pattern of sporadic cases with a significant decline over time. In 2015, there were several cases reported, with a total of 8 cases and 6 deaths, indicating a high mortality rate. The cases were concentrated in the first half of the year, with no cases reported from June to December. This suggests that the virus may have been more active or transmissible during certain months, possibly due to environmental or seasonal factors.

From 2016 onwards, the number of cases significantly decreased, with only a few isolated incidents reported in 2016 and 2022, and no cases in the years 2017, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2023, 2024, and 2025. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, and possibly changes in the virus's behavior or its interaction with human hosts. The data indicates that while the H5N1 virus remains a potential threat, its impact on human health in the Chinese mainland has been effectively minimized in recent years.
