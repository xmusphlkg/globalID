# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, primarily occurs through contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. The H5N1 virus is highly pathogenic, causing severe respiratory illness in humans, with symptoms including fever, cough, sore throat, and, in severe cases, pneumonia and multi-organ failure. The virus first emerged in 1997 in Hong Kong and has since caused sporadic cases and outbreaks across Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and Europe. The high mortality rate in humans is concerning, and while human-to-human transmission is rare, mutations could pose a pandemic risk.

## Highlights

- H5N1 cases peaked in early 2015, with a total of 7 cases and 5 deaths between January and July. <br/>
- The period from mid-2016 to 2021 showed no reported human infections, suggesting a lull in the outbreak. <br/>
- Sporadic cases reemerged in late 2022 and early 2023, though no fatalities were reported during this time. <br/>
- As of July 2024, there have been no reported cases or deaths in the Chinese mainland since early 2023, indicating the virus may be under control.<br/>

The data on human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 shows an initial cluster of cases between January and July 2015, with a total of seven cases and five deaths, indicating a high mortality rate. The peak occurred in March 2015, with three cases reported. This suggests an outbreak or increased transmission in early 2015, which dramatically decreased thereafter, with only one additional case in July resulting in death.

From mid-2015 onward, the data reflects a steep decline in reported human cases, indicating either effective control measures, reduced transmission, or potential underreporting. Post-2015, there were no consecutive cases until an isolated case was reported in May 2016, followed by sporadic cases in September 2022 and February 2023, none of which resulted in fatalities. This pattern suggests that H5N1 infections in humans have become sporadic, possibly due to improved biosecurity measures, reduced zoonotic transmission, or a change in the virus's behavior.

In general, the data implies that the H5N1 virus has not sustained regular human-to-human transmission in China, with infections remaining isolated events likely linked to direct avian contact. This trend points to effective surveillance and control interventions in mitigating widespread transmission in humans.
