# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, is a severe respiratory disease caused by a subtype of the influenza A virus. It primarily affects birds but can occasionally infect humans, often through direct or indirect contact with infected poultry. Human cases are rare but can be fatal, with a high mortality rate. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, and severe respiratory distress. The virus does not easily transmit between humans, but there is concern about its potential to mutate and cause a pandemic. Monitoring and control measures are crucial to prevent outbreaks.

The data on human infections with the H5N1 virus in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows a sporadic pattern of cases, with a notable concentration in 2015 and a few isolated cases in subsequent years. In 2015, there were six reported cases, with four resulting in death, indicating a high case fatality rate. This suggests that while the virus was not widespread, it was highly lethal to those who contracted it. The cases were spread across several months, with no cases reported from June to December, which might indicate a seasonal pattern or effective containment measures during that period.

From 2016 onwards, the data shows a significant decline in reported cases, with only a few isolated incidents in 2016, 2022, and 2023. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, or changes in the virus's transmission dynamics. The absence of cases from 2017 to 2021 and from 2024 onwards suggests that the virus was either effectively controlled or that it did not have a significant presence in the human population during these periods. The isolated cases in 2022 and 2023, with no reported deaths, might indicate improved clinical management or a less virulent strain. Overall, the data reflects a successful containment of the virus, with sporadic cases that did not lead to widespread outbreaks.
