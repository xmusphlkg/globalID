# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with H5N1 virus, a subtype of avian influenza (bird flu), is a rare but serious zoonotic disease. It primarily spreads from infected poultry to humans through direct contact, with limited human-to-human transmission observed. First identified in humans in 1997 in Hong Kong, H5N1 has caused sporadic outbreaks with high case fatality rates. The virus can cause severe respiratory illness, including pneumonia and acute respiratory distress. Due to its potential to cause a pandemic, H5N1 is closely monitored by global health authorities for mutations that may increase human transmissibility.

## Highlights

- Human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland have remained sporadic, with only a few cases reported since 2015. The last recorded case was in February 2023. <br/>
- The death toll has been limited; however, the case fatality rate is high, particularly in the initial years from 2015 to 2016. <br/>
- There has been a notable period of no reported cases or deaths from mid-2023 to April 2025, indicating a possible containment or reduction in transmission during this interval. <br/>
- Overall, the data suggests that the H5N1 virus is currently under control in the Chinese mainland, with no new cases reported in recent months. <br/>

Analysis of the H5N1 human infection data for mainland China shows a brief but notable cluster of cases during 2015 and 2016. In 2015, three confirmed human infections occurred across April, July, and one additional death was recorded in May with no associated case, potentially indicating a delayed report or retrospective diagnosis. A lull in reported activity followed, with one case and zero deaths in January 2016, and another isolated fatal case in May 2016. This 24-month span had a total of four infections and five deaths, suggesting a case-fatality ratio exceeding 100%, which may point to documentation anomalies or classification issues. Notably, from mid-2016 onward, data shows an abrupt and sustained absence of cases for over six years.

The reappearance of isolated cases in September 2022 and February 2023 marked the only other documented H5N1 infections in recent years, both non-fatal and not linked to any resurgence. This sporadic recurrence may imply effective surveillance and control practices or a shift in zoonotic transmission dynamics. The extended absence of cases from 2017–2021, and again post-February 2023 through April 2025, suggests that human H5N1 infection has been largely contained or has become exceedingly rare in the mainland during this time.

Between 2015 and 2016, human deaths due to H5N1 infection in mainland China were sporadic but notable, with a concentration in the spring and early summer months. In this period, five deaths occurred, distributed irregularly across seven reported cases, indicating a high case fatality rate of approximately 71%. Deaths were reported even in months without identified cases, such as May 2015 and February 2016, suggesting either retrospective classification of fatalities or reporting lags that might complicate real-time surveillance and control strategies. The mortality pattern exhibits a sudden cessation after May 2016, with no subsequent deaths reported despite sporadic cases emerging later.

From mid-2016 onwards through early 2025, although only three additional H5N1 cases were reported—one in September 2022 and one in February 2023—none resulted in death. This divergence from earlier mortality trends could suggest better clinical outcomes associated with improved management, possible changes in viral virulence, or different patient risk profiles. The complete absence of deaths for over eight years reflects a significant shift in fatality dynamics and may underline the effectiveness of early detection, isolation practices, and clinical response, even while rare human infections continue to occur.
