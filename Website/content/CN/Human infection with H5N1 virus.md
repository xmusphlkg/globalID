# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, a subtype of avian influenza A, is a rare but serious disease primarily transmitted from infected birds to humans. First reported in 1997 in Hong Kong, H5N1 has a high mortality rate in confirmed human cases. Transmission occurs through close contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. While human-to-human transmission is extremely limited, concerns remain about the virus mutating to spread more easily among people. Symptoms include high fever, cough, and respiratory distress, often progressing rapidly to severe pneumonia or acute respiratory distress syndrome. Early detection and antiviral treatment are crucial for improved outcomes.

## Highlights

- Human H5N1 infections in mainland China remain rare, with only 5 confirmed cases and 4 deaths reported from May 2015 to May 2025. <br/>
- The majority of cases occurred between 2015 and 2016, indicating a concentrated period of sporadic transmission early in the decade. <br/>
- After a period of no cases from mid-2016 to mid-2022, isolated infections were again detected in September 2022 and February 2023, both non-fatal. <br/>
- No new human H5N1 infections or deaths have been reported since February 2023, suggesting continued effective surveillance and control measures as of May 2025. <br/>

Between May 2015 and May 2025, reported human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland were exceptionally rare, with only five confirmed cases. These occurred sporadically between July 2015 and February 2023. Three of these cases were fatal, reflecting a known high case-fatality rate associated with H5N1. Notably, the initial period (2015–2016) recorded more activity, with four infections and all three deaths concentrated in that timeframe, suggesting a transient increase in zoonotic exposure or surveillance sensitivity. After May 2016, there was a prolonged lull with virtually no reported cases until isolated single cases in September 2022 and February 2023, neither of which resulted in death.

Following the case in early 2023, there have been no further human infections reported through May 2025. This long stretch of zero-case months suggests either successful containment and control strategies in poultry populations, the main reservoir of H5N1, improvements in biosecurity, or a decrease in human exposure risk. The sudden appearance of two isolated cases after a six-year gap could indicate sporadic spillovers rather than widespread transmission. The overall temporal distribution implies that while spillover from avian sources remains possible, sustained human transmission has not occurred, and the pattern remains consistent with a zoonotic event rather than human-adaptive circulation.
