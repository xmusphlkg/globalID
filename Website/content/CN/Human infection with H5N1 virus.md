# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, results from exposure to infected birds or contaminated environments. The H5N1 virus primarily affects birds but can occasionally infect humans, often leading to severe respiratory illness. Transmission to humans is rare and generally occurs through direct contact with sick or dead birds, or their secretions. Symptoms in humans range from fever and cough to pneumonia and acute respiratory distress. Due to its high mortality rate, the H5N1 virus remains a significant concern for global health organizations, prompting ongoing surveillance and preparedness efforts.

## Highlights

- The last recorded case of H5N1 infection occurred in February 2023, and there have been no reported cases since.<br/>
- Between 2015 and March 2025, a total of 11 cases were reported with 7 resulting in death.<br/>
- There is a significant decline in cases from 2017 onwards, suggesting improved control measures or natural disease dynamics.<br/>
- Continuous absence of cases since early 2023 indicates a stable, controlled situation for H5N1 in the Chinese mainland.<br/>

The data on human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland from March 2015 to October 2023 show sporadic cases over several years. From March 2015, there were fluctuations with months of zero cases interspersed with occasional infections. For instance, March 2015 saw three cases with one resulting in death, followed by a single case in April and May with similar fatality rates. This period suggests unpredictable outbreaks with high mortality rates, indicating the virulence of the virus during these incidents, particularly notable in the early part of the data.

The dataset reveals a significant decline in cases after May 2016, with no data reported from July 2016 until September 2022, where only one case occurred without a fatality. The absence of cases during this extended period may reflect improved public health measures, enhanced surveillance, or effective interventions. The reappearance of a single case in February 2023 without fatality is followed by no further reported cases up to October 2023. This overall trend indicates a substantial reduction in the incidence of human infections with the H5N1 virus in the later years of the observation period, highlighting the effectiveness of ongoing efforts to control and prevent the virus.
