# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

The H5N1 virus, predominantly found in birds, is a highly pathogenic avian influenza virus known to infect humans, although rare and usually resulting from direct or close contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Initial cases were documented in 1997 in Hong Kong. Human infections can lead to severe respiratory illnesses, with symptoms including high fever, cough, and shortness of breath, often progressing rapidly to pneumonia and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). The virus has a high mortality rate, necessitating vigilant surveillance and containment efforts to prevent outbreaks and potential human-to-human transmission.

## Highlights

1. Human cases of H5N1 in China have been sporadic, with isolated cases reported primarily from 2015 to 2016, and single cases in 2022 and 2023.<br/>
2. The cumulative number of cases remains low, with a total of 5 cases reported since 2015, indicating limited human-to-human transmission.<br/>
3. Mortality rate is relatively high, with 2 deaths recorded out of 5 cases, representing a 40% fatality rate, which emphasizes the severity of the infection when contracted.<br/>
4. Since March 2023, no new cases or deaths have been reported, suggesting effective control measures and limited recent exposure to the virus.<br/>

The reported data for human infections with the H5N1 virus in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 indicates a sporadic occurrence of cases, with a total of only five cases reported across this period. The first instance occurred in July 2015, resulting in one death. Subsequent cases were scarce, with single cases observed in January 2016, May 2016, September 2022, and February 2023. Of these, two deaths were recorded, corresponding to July 2015 and May 2016. The temporal distribution suggests that H5N1 infections have not been persistent annually and exhibit isolated occurrences without an apparent seasonal pattern.

This data indicates that H5N1 cases have been infrequent and do not point towards sustained transmission among humans in mainland China. The complete absence of cases from mid-2016 to September 2022, and again from February 2023 onwards, suggests successful public health measures or limited transmission potential of the virus within human populations. While H5N1 remains a concern due to its potential to cause severe illness, the low number of reported cases over a decade does not suggest an immediate risk of widespread outbreaks in humans, although continued surveillance and monitoring are essential to detect and mitigate any potential change in the epidemiological behavior of the virus.
