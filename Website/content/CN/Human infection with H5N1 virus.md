# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 avian influenza virus primarily occurs through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. The virus can cause severe respiratory illness, including pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and multi-organ failure. Symptoms typically appear within 2-8 days of exposure and may include fever, cough, sore throat, and muscle aches. Although human-to-human transmission is rare, some cases have occurred. The case fatality rate is high, and antiviral treatment, along with supportive care, may improve survival. Monitoring and control measures are crucial to preventing further outbreaks.

## Highlights

1. **Limited Human Infections**  
   Since 2015, human cases of H5N1 infection in mainland China have been sporadic, with only a few occurrences reported, particularly in early 2015, 2016, and 2022.  
   <br/>

2. **No Reported Deaths Since 2016**  
   Although some cases resulted in fatalities, there have been no deaths due to H5N1 infections in China since 2016.  
   <br/>

3. **Recent Period of No Activity**  
   No new cases or deaths were reported from 2023 onwards, indicating a period of low or no active transmission.  
   <br/>

4. **Potential for Re-emergence**  
   While current data show no recent cases, H5N1 remains a potential public health threat due to the virus's persistence in poultry populations.  
   <br/>

The data on H5N1 human infections in mainland China shows a period of relative calm with occasional, isolated cases. Between 2014 and 2019, no infections were reported, suggesting effective containment or reduced transmission. However, in 2015, several cases occurred, including deaths, with a total of 5 fatalities. These sporadic infections highlight the virus's potential for severe outcomes. The years from 2017 to 2021 show no cases, which may indicate successful containment or underreporting.

In 2022 and 2023, there were two reported human cases, one in September 2022 and one in February 2023, but no deaths. This suggests a small resurgence or occasional spillover from animal reservoirs to humans. The absence of further cases or fatalities in the subsequent months may indicate that the risk remained low, potentially due to improved monitoring or public health measures. However, these sporadic cases demonstrate that H5N1 continues to pose a threat, particularly in areas with frequent human-animal interactions. While the risk appears minimal, continued vigilance is necessary to prevent future outbreaks.
