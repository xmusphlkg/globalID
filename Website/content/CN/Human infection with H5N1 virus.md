# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, primarily occurs through direct or close contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Since its emergence in 1997, H5N1 has caused significant concern due to its high mortality rate in humans, often exceeding 50%. Symptoms typically include high fever, cough, sore throat, and severe respiratory distress, which can progress rapidly to pneumonia and multi-organ failure. H5N1 is not easily transmissible between humans, but its potential to mutate and gain efficient human-to-human transmission capabilities remains a serious global health threat.

## Highlights

- Human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland showed a peak in early 2015, with a total of 7 cases reported from January to March of that year. <br/>
- The period from mid-2016 through mid-2022 reported no cases, indicating a significant lull in the incidence of the virus. <br/>
- There were two isolated cases reported in 2022 and early 2023, but no cases have been reported since February 2023, showing recent containment success. <br/>
- Overall, the current situation as of May 2024 indicates that the H5N1 virus is well-contained with no new cases reported in over a year. <br/>

The data on human H5N1 infections in the Chinese mainland from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals several noteworthy trends. Initially, there is a conspicuous cluster of cases between January 2015 and May 2015, with 7 reported cases and 5 deaths, resulting in a high case-fatality ratio (71.4%). This period stands out because it represents a compact and concentrated rise in cases and deaths, emphasizing a period of increased H5N1 activity. Following this upsurge, the data show sporadic cases in 2016, totaling 2 cases with 2 deaths, maintaining the high case-fatality ratio.

After May 2016, there are no reported cases until September 2022, indicating a significant period of inactivity or effective control, potentially reflecting successful public health interventions, environmental factors, or changes in virus transmission dynamics. The re-emergence of a single case in September 2022 and February 2023, both without fatalities, suggests a possible isolated event or breakthrough in transmission but does not indicate a recurring pattern. The absence of any reported cases from March 2023 to February 2024 continues to support a low-to-no transmission scenario during this latter period. The analysis implies intermittent outbreaks rather than continuous or widespread human-to-human transmission, with a significant decline in cases post-2016 and minimal recurring activity through to 2024.
