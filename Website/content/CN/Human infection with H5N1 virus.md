# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, a subtype of the influenza A virus, primarily occurs through contact with infected birds or contaminated environments. First identified in humans in 1997 in Hong Kong, it is known for its potential to cause severe respiratory illness and high mortality rates. Human-to-human transmission is rare and inefficient, but the risk of the virus mutating to enable efficient transmission remains a significant concern. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, and muscle aches, but the disease can rapidly progress to severe pneumonia. Control measures focus on surveillance, biosecurity, and vaccination strategies in poultry.

## Highlights

- Human cases of H5N1 in mainland China have been sporadic, with most cases occurring between 2015 and 2016, peaking in 2015 with 8 cases and 5 deaths.<br/>
- Since 2017, there has been a significant decrease in human infections, with only two cases reported in 2022 and 2023 combined.<br/>
- The mortality rate remains high, with a significant portion of cases resulting in fatalities, especially during 2015-2016.<br/>
- As of August 2024, no new human cases of H5N1 have been reported in the past year, suggesting a stable or controlled situation.<br/>

The reported data for human infections with the H5N1 virus in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a relatively low incidence of cases, with only sporadic infections and fatalities. Notably, the years 2014 to 2021 display a general absence of cases and deaths, except for a small cluster of cases in 2015. In this year, there were seven cases, with five fatalities, indicating a significant case-fatality ratio. Most cases in 2015 occurred in the early months, with no cases reported from August onwards. After a single case in May 2016, followed by a gap of six years without any reported infections, a few isolated cases reappeared in 2022 and 2023. However, these recent cases did not result in fatalities.

The data highlights an overall sporadic pattern of infections, suggesting limited human-to-human transmission or exposure events. The year 2015 stands out as the only period with a concentration of cases and fatalities, while the subsequent years up to 2024 report no cases or fatalities, apart from the isolated infections in 2022 and 2023. This pattern might reflect effective public health measures, changes in virus behavior, or alterations in human exposure to infected birds, which are typically the primary source of H5N1 infections in humans.
