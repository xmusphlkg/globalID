# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, also known as avian influenza A (H5N1), occurs primarily through direct or close contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. H5N1 is a highly pathogenic avian influenza virus that can cause severe respiratory illness in humans. Symptoms often include high fever, cough, and difficulty breathing, and the virus can lead to severe pneumonia, acute respiratory distress, and even death. Human-to-human transmission is rare but possible, raising concerns about potential pandemic risks if the virus mutates to spread more easily among humans.

## Highlights

- Sporadic cases were reported between 2015 and 2016, with a peak in early 2015. <br/>
- There were no new cases from July 2016 to August 2022, indicating a period of inactivity. <br/>
- A resurgence occurred in September 2022 and February 2023, each with one case and no deaths. <br/>
- No cases have been reported since February 2023, indicating control over the disease. <br/>

The analysis of reported data for Human infection with H5N1 virus in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals a period of heightened activity primarily in early 2015, followed by sporadic cases in the subsequent years. Notably, the year 2015 saw the highest incidence with eight reported cases and six deaths, concentrated in the first half of the year. This period was marked by a peak in March 2015, with three cases and one death. Following this spike, the number of cases drastically decreased, indicating a possible effective public health response or a natural decline in virus transmission.

From mid-2016 to early 2022, the reported cases were infrequent, with occasional single cases such as those in January 2016, May 2016, and isolated instances in 2022 and 2023. The data suggests a successful containment and management of the H5N1 virus, as indicated by the absence of cases from July 2017 to August 2022 and no deaths from February 2016 onward. The period from September 2022 onwards shows a resurgence with isolated cases in September 2022 and February 2023, but without fatalities, reflecting continued vigilance and possibly improved healthcare responses. Overall, the pattern suggests effective control measures post-2015, with a need for ongoing surveillance to address occasional resurgence.
