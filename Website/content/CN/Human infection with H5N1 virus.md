# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, commonly known as avian influenza or bird flu, is a severe respiratory disease caused by a subtype of the influenza A virus. It primarily affects birds but can occasionally infect humans, often through direct or indirect contact with infected poultry. Human cases are rare but can be fatal, with a high mortality rate. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, and severe respiratory distress. The virus does not easily transmit between humans, but there is concern about its potential to mutate and cause a pandemic. Monitoring and control measures are crucial to prevent outbreaks.

The data on human infections with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a sporadic pattern of cases, with a notable concentration in the early months of 2015. During this period, there were a total of 8 reported cases and 5 deaths, indicating a high case fatality rate. The highest number of cases was reported in March 2015, with three cases and one death. After May 2015, the incidence of new cases significantly decreased, with only isolated cases reported in subsequent years, specifically in January 2016, May 2016, September 2022, and February 2023. 

The data suggests a decline in the number of cases over the years, with no reported cases from March 2016 to August 2022, and again from March 2023 to December 2024. This could indicate effective control measures, changes in virus transmissibility, or reporting practices. The absence of cases for extended periods, particularly from 2017 to 2021, and again from 2023 onwards, suggests that the H5N1 virus may not have been circulating widely in the human population during these times. However, the sporadic nature of the cases that do appear highlights the need for continued surveillance and preparedness to prevent potential outbreaks.
