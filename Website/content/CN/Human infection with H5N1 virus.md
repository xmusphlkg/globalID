# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, also known as avian influenza or bird flu, originates from direct contact with infected birds such as poultry. First identified in Hong Kong in 1997, this highly pathogenic virus causes severe respiratory illness in humans, with symptoms ranging from fever and cough to pneumonia and multi-organ failure. The mortality rate is notably high. Although human-to-human transmission remains rare, there is concern about potential mutations that could facilitate widespread transmission. Preventative measures focus on controlling outbreaks in avian populations and monitoring human cases to prevent an epidemic or pandemic.

## Highlights

- Human cases of H5N1 in mainland China peaked in 2015, with seven cases and five deaths reported, indicating a high case fatality rate. <br/>
- Following 2015, H5N1 cases remained sporadic, with zero cases reported between 2017 and 2021, suggesting a period of low transmission. <br/>
- In recent years, isolated cases re-emerged, with one case in both September 2022 and February 2023, though no deaths were reported, reflecting lower recent mortality. <br/>
- As of September 2024, there have been no new cases or deaths reported in mainland China, indicating a stable situation over the past year. <br/>

The data on human infection with the H5N1 virus in mainland China from September 2014 to September 2024 shows a limited but concerning pattern of sporadic cases and fatalities. The peak of reported cases occurred in early 2015, with five cases and three deaths over the first four months of that year. Following this, the number of cases sharply declined, and from mid-2016 to mid-2022, there were no reported human infections. However, isolated cases re-emerged in late 2022 and early 2023, although none resulted in deaths.

This epidemiological trend highlights two key patterns: a brief but significant surge in cases in early 2015, followed by a long period of inactivity, and then the reappearance of cases in 2022-2023. The case fatality rate (CFR) remains high, as in most H5N1 infections, where approximately 50% of recorded cases in the dataset resulted in death. The resurgence of cases in recent years, albeit at low numbers, suggests the virus has not been completely eliminated and continues to pose a potential public health risk, especially considering the high mortality rate associated with human H5N1 infections.
