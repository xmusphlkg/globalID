# Human infection with H5N1 virus

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H5N1 virus, also known as avian influenza or bird flu, refers to a highly pathogenic viral infection primarily affecting birds but occasionally transmitted to humans. First identified in 1997 in Hong Kong, the virus causes severe respiratory illness in humans, often leading to pneumonia and multi-organ failure. Human cases are rare and usually linked to direct or indirect contact with infected birds or contaminated environments. Symptoms typically include high fever, cough, and sore throat, progressing rapidly to severe respiratory distress. The virus has a high mortality rate, raising concerns about its potential for a future pandemic.

## Highlights

- Sporadic cases of H5N1 infection were recorded in the Chinese mainland primarily between 2015 to 2016, with a peak in early 2015 when a few cases emerged and resulted in some fatalities. <br/>
- After 2016, there was a significant decline in reported cases, with nearly a complete absence of H5N1 infections from 2017 onwards, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- The most recent cases appeared in late 2022 and early 2023, but they were isolated events with no fatalities, demonstrating continued vigilance. <br/>
- As of October 2024, there have been no reported human cases of H5N1 in the current year, suggesting the situation is stable and under control. <br/>

The data on human infection with the H5N1 virus in the Chinese mainland presents a concentrated period of cases primarily between late 2014 and mid-2016, with sporadic cases observed in the subsequent years up to 2024. In 2015, there was a notable occurrence of cases with a peak between January and May, where each month recorded at least one case, cumulating in eight cases and five deaths overall for the year. This peak phase exhibited a relatively high case fatality rate, indicating a serious outbreak period. Notably, May 2015 is marked anomalously since no new cases were reported, but one death occurred, suggesting potential reporting lag or a previous undiagnosed case.

Following the active outbreak in 2015, 2016 witnessed a substantial decline in cases, with only two cases reported in the entire year, while deaths downgraded to two, aligning with a reduction in the epidemic's intensity. Remarkably, from 2017 onwards to the end of the dataset in 2024, the data show a significant reduction, almost cessation, in reported human infections, with only isolated instances in September 2022 and February 2023 showing singular cases, both without mortality. The long interval of zero cases suggests successful containment and control measures, possibly including improved surveillance, public health interventions, or changes in virus transmissibility among humans. Overall, the dataset reflects an initial outbreak phase, a subsequent decline, and prolonged control stability subsequently.
