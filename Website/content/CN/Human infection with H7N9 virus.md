# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus, an avian influenza strain, was first reported in China in 2013. It primarily spreads through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, septic shock, and multi-organ failure. The virus has a high mortality rate among those hospitalized. There is no sustained human-to-human transmission, but limited cases have been reported. Monitoring and control measures are crucial to prevent outbreaks and manage potential pandemics.

## Highlights

- The peak of H7N9 infections occurred in early 2017, with January and February recording 192 and 160 cases, respectively. <br/>
- A significant decline in cases began in mid-2017, with sporadic cases reported until early 2019. <br/>
- From mid-2019 onwards, no new cases or deaths have been reported, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- As of June 2024, there have been no reported cases or deaths for over five years, suggesting the H7N9 virus is no longer a public health threat. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H7N9 virus in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 shows significant fluctuations. Initially, from June 2014 to May 2015, the number of cases was relatively low, peaking in January 2015 with 83 cases and 28 deaths. Following a decline in mid-2015, another peak occurs in January 2016 with 28 cases and 5 deaths. The most substantial surge happens from December 2016 to June 2017, particularly in January 2017 with 192 cases and 79 deaths, indicating a severe outbreak.

From mid-2017, there is a sharp decline in cases and deaths, with sporadic reports through mid-2018. By mid-2018, the numbers drop to zero and remain so until mid-2024. This decline suggests effective public health interventions, changes in the virus's epidemiology, or improved biosecurity measures and vaccination efforts in poultry, likely breaking the transmission cycle of H7N9 to humans. The data indicates that efforts to control and prevent H7N9 infections have been successful, leading to no new reported human cases in recent years.
