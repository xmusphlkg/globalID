# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

H7N9 is a subtype of the influenza A virus that primarily affects birds but can infect humans. First identified in China in 2013, human cases often arise following exposure to infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild respiratory illness to severe pneumonia, and complications can be fatal, especially in individuals with pre-existing conditions. Human-to-human transmission is rare, but possible. Seasonal spikes in cases are often linked to increased poultry exposure. There is a concern about the virus mutating to allow easier human transmission, thus posing a pandemic threat, necessitating ongoing surveillance and research.

## Highlights

- The H7N9 virus showed a notable epidemic pattern with peaks in cases during the winter months, particularly in January 2015 and January 2017, with significant increases in infections and fatalities. <br/>
- After a sharp rise in cases and deaths in early 2017, a marked decline is observed from late 2017 onwards, with sporadic cases by early 2018. <br/>
- Since January 2019, there have been no reported cases or deaths, indicating effective control measures or a natural decline in transmission. <br/>
- As of October 2024, the Chinese mainland has reported no new H7N9 cases or deaths for over five years, suggesting the virus is potentially under control in the region. <br/>

The reported data on human infection with the H7N9 virus in Mainland China indicates significant fluctuations between October 2014 and October 2024. The initial phase, starting in late 2014, shows cases increasing modestly, peaking in early 2015, with January and February witnessing 83 and 59 cases, respectively. This period also recorded a high number of deaths, with February 2015 seeing 27 fatalities. This surge suggests a critical public health period possibly driven by increased transmission rates or exposure to infected poultry.

Following the initial spike, there’s a notable decline throughout the remainder of 2015, indicating successful containment or a natural decline in transmission. However, late 2016 signals a sharp resurgence, with cases peaking at 192 in January 2017 and deaths reaching 79, marking the deadliest phase. This peak suggests either a mutation leading to increased virulence or failure in preventive measures.

From mid-2017 onwards, cases sharply decrease, with few reported incidences and no cases for extended periods post-2018, continuing through to October 2024. This sustained period without new cases highlights effective public health interventions and possibly successful vaccination strategies. This epidemiological trend underscores the importance of maintaining vigilant monitoring and response strategies to manage and prevent recurrence of such infections.

The initial surge in cases and deaths started in late 2014, with the highest numbers observed in early 2015. January 2015 saw the most significant proportion of deaths to cases, peaking at 28 deaths out of 83 cases. This trend of high mortality continued into the first quarter of 2015, followed by a gradual decline as the year progressed. The recurrence of cases with accompanying deaths spiked again in December 2016, carrying into the first half of 2017. Interestingly, there was a substantial drop in reported cases and mortality starting in 2018. This period marks a significant decline in viral activity, with only a couple of isolated cases in 2018 and 2019, and no new documented cases or deaths thereafter up until October 2024. This dramatic reduction suggests effective control measures, public health interventions, and possibly changes in virus-host dynamics or immunity in the population. The absence of cases and deaths from 2018 onwards reflects the containment of the virus and stresses the importance of continued monitoring and preparedness to prevent potential outbreaks.
