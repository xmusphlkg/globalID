# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus, a subtype of avian influenza A, was first reported in China in 2013. This virus primarily circulates in poultry, but can infect humans, causing severe respiratory illness and high mortality rates. Most cases have been linked to exposure to live poultry or contaminated environments. H7N9 does not easily transmit from person to person, but its potential for mutation raises concerns for public health. Surveillance, biosecurity measures, and public awareness are crucial in controlling and preventing outbreaks.

## Highlights

- Significant peaks were observed in January and February of 2015 and 2017, with high numbers of cases and deaths during these months.<br/>
- A sharp decline in cases and deaths occurred beginning in mid-2017, leading to no reported cases from January 2018 onwards.<br/>
- Sustained zero reporting of new H7N9 cases from 2018 to May 2024 indicates effective control measures or eradication of the virus in the mainland.<br/>
- The last reported case and death were in April 2019, showcasing over five years free of new infections as of May 2024.<br/>

The data on Human infection with H7N9 virus in the Chinese mainland from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals distinct trends. Initially, there are fluctuations in cases and deaths, with noticeable peaks in early 2015 and early 2017. For instance, January 2015 saw 83 cases with 28 deaths, followed by February with 59 cases and 27 deaths. Another surge occurred in January 2017 with 192 cases and 79 deaths, continuing through February and March.

Post-2017, there is a gradual decline, culminating in negligible numbers by late 2018. From 2019 to May 2024, there were only sporadic isolated cases, such as a single case in April 2019. The complete absence of cases and deaths from May 2020 to May 2024 indicates effective public health interventions or changes in virus transmissibility.

It's notable that months with high case counts often have high death counts, suggesting a link between increased infections and severity. The dramatic fall after 2017 and the sustained absence of cases and deaths suggest successful containment or a shift in the virus's epidemiological dynamics in the region.

Specifically, the data show sporadic death counts throughout 2014, with an increase in the number of deaths towards the end of 2014 and the beginning of 2015. January and February of 2015 exhibit the highest monthly death counts at 28 and 27 deaths, followed by a notable reduction in subsequent months, although fatalities spiked again in March with 18 deaths. This period marks one of the more critical phases, demonstrating a concentrated impact in early 2015.

The year 2017 represents another significant period with the highest accumulative deaths observed, where January and February report 79 and 61 deaths respectively. This spike is significantly larger compared to the previous years, and continues into the subsequent months with elevated death counts. A gradual decline is observed mid-2017, with fewer reported cases and deaths until the end of the year. Post-2017, the data show a sharp decrease, with almost no deaths post-2018, suggesting effective control measures and possibly widespread immunity or other factors leading to the containment of the virus. This sustained absence of fatalities indicates a significant epidemiological shift in the management of the H7N9 virus on the Chinese mainland.
