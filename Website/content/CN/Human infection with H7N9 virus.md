# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus, an avian influenza strain, was first reported in China in 2013. Primarily affecting poultry, H7N9 can transmit to humans through direct contact with infected birds or contaminated environments. Many human cases have been linked to exposure in live bird markets, with the virus displaying limited human-to-human transmission. Symptoms range from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia and can lead to death. With no widespread human immunity, the virus poses a pandemic risk, necessitating active surveillance, timely reporting, and rigorous preventive measures to control potential outbreaks.

## Highlights

- The peak of H7N9 human infections occurred from December 2016 to May 2017, with over 600 cases and significant fatalities, indicating a major outbreak period. <br/>
- Since mid-2017, a sharp and sustained decline has been observed, with only sporadic cases reported through early 2019. <br/>
- From May 2019 to May 2025, there have been no reported human cases or deaths, suggesting effective public health interventions and possible virus containment. <br/>
- The current epidemiological situation as of May 2025 shows no active transmission of H7N9 among humans in mainland China, indicating the virus is not a present public health threat. <br/>

The data on H7N9 virus infections in mainland China from May 2015 to May 2025 reveals a distinct epidemic peak during the 2016–2017 winter season. A pronounced rise begins in December 2016 with 106 cases, surging to a monthly peak of 192 cases in January 2017, followed by a gradual decline through mid-2017. Correspondingly, deaths also spike during this period, including 79 in January 2017 alone. This seasonal pattern strongly suggests the virus followed a winter-dominant transmission trend, likely related to environmental or behavioral exposure factors, such as increased poultry trade activity or seasonal human interaction with live poultry markets.

Post-2017, cases rapidly declined. By March 2018, the virus appeared nearly eradicated, with only sporadic data points — a single case in April 2019 — followed by complete silence through mid-2025. This stark disappearance, contrasting earlier epidemic behavior, may correspond to systemic interventions such as large-scale market closures, enhanced surveillance, poultry vaccination, or virus evolution reducing zoonotic transmission. Overall, the epidemic peak and its abrupt cessation underscore both the epidemic potential of zoonotic influenza and the impact of timely public health interventions.
