# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus, a subtype of avian influenza, was first reported in China in 2013. This virus primarily affects birds but can occasionally infect humans, leading to severe respiratory illness. Transmission to humans typically occurs through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe pneumonia and acute respiratory distress syndrome. The virus has a high mortality rate, especially among older adults and those with underlying health conditions. Public health measures focus on monitoring outbreaks, controlling poultry infections, and developing vaccines.

## Highlights

- **Initial Outbreak and Peak:** The H7N9 virus outbreak peaked in January 2017 with 192 cases and 79 deaths, marking the highest transmission and mortality rates. <br/>

- **Decline in Cases:** After the peak, there was a significant decline, with sporadic cases until mid-2018. By the end of 2018, virus transmission had effectively ceased. <br/>

- **Sustained Control:** From 2019 onwards, no new cases or deaths have been reported, indicating successful control and containment. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of February 2025, the Chinese mainland has maintained zero reported cases and deaths for over six years, reflecting effective public health interventions and surveillance. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H7N9 virus in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a significant outbreak pattern followed by a sharp decline in cases. Initially, in 2015, the number of cases fluctuated, with a peak in February (59 cases) and a notable decrease by mid-year. However, the virus saw a resurgence in late 2016, with December reporting 106 cases. This trend continued into early 2017, with January and February experiencing the highest number of cases (192 and 160, respectively). The mortality rate during these peak months was also high, indicating the severity of the outbreak.

By mid-2017, the number of cases began to decline significantly, and by 2018, the incidence of new cases had almost ceased, with only sporadic cases reported. From 2019 onwards, the data shows no new cases or deaths, suggesting effective control measures were implemented, leading to the containment of the virus. This pattern highlights the importance of public health interventions and surveillance in managing infectious disease outbreaks. The absence of cases from 2019 to 2025 suggests that the H7N9 virus was effectively controlled, possibly due to improved biosecurity measures, vaccination efforts, or changes in poultry farming practices.
