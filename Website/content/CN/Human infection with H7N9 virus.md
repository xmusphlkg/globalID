# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus is an avian influenza strain first identified in China in 2013. It primarily affects poultry but can infect humans through direct contact with infected birds or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild respiratory illness to severe pneumonia, potentially leading to acute respiratory distress syndrome and death. Most cases occur in adults with exposure to live poultry markets. While human-to-human transmission is rare, cases have prompted public health concerns and monitoring due to the virus's potential to mutate and cause pandemics. Vaccination and preventive measures are in development to control its spread.

## Highlights

- Peak H7N9 virus activity occurred between 2014 and 2017, with the highest case and death counts in early 2017. January 2017 saw 192 cases and 79 deaths.  
<br/>
- Significant outbreaks occurred during the winter and early spring months, with a sharp decline in cases by late 2017.  
<br/>
- After sporadic cases in 2018 and 2019, no human infections with the H7N9 virus have been reported since April 2019, marking over five years without any cases as of September 2024.  
<br/>
- The current disease situation indicates that H7N9 human infections have been effectively controlled, with no recorded cases for over five consecutive years.  
<br/>

The data on human infection with the H7N9 virus in mainland China illustrates several trends over the observed years. The initial cases reported from September 2014 showed a sporadic onset, escalating noticeably towards the end of the year and peaking in the first half of 2015. The highest number of cases was reported in January 2015, with a total of 83 cases, while the associated deaths for this month were 28, indicating a significant mortality rate. This period in early 2015 reflects an intense outbreak phase, characterized by sustained transmission and relatively high mortality.

After a lull in mid-2015, the number of cases and deaths fluctuated through 2016. A sharp increase was observed again from late 2016 onwards, with December 2016 and January 2017 reporting substantial surges at 106 and 192 cases, respectively. However, similar to the previous peak, these high case numbers were followed by a gradual decline over the remainder of 2017. Interestingly, 2018 onwards saw a dramatic reduction to near elimination, with only sporadic cases and no further outbreaks through to 2024. This long period of no cases suggests successful containment or mitigation efforts that effectively halted transmission of the virus.
