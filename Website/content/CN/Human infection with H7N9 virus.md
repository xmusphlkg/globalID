# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus, an avian influenza strain, was first identified in China in 2013. This virus primarily affects birds but can infect humans through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms in humans range from mild respiratory illness to severe pneumonia and acute respiratory distress syndrome. The virus has a high mortality rate, particularly among older adults and those with underlying health conditions. While human-to-human transmission is rare, the potential for the virus to mutate and spread more easily among people remains a significant public health concern.

The data on human infections with the H7N9 virus in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a significant fluctuation in the number of cases and deaths over the years. Initially, there was a notable increase in cases from December 2014 through early 2017, with a peak in January 2017 when 192 cases and 79 deaths were reported. This period marks the most intense phase of the outbreak, characterized by high transmission rates and a substantial number of fatalities, indicating a severe impact on public health during these months.

Following the peak in early 2017, there was a marked decline in both cases and deaths. By mid-2017, the numbers began to decrease significantly, and by 2018, the incidence of new cases had dropped to nearly zero, with only sporadic cases reported thereafter. This trend suggests effective control measures and possibly changes in the virus's transmission dynamics. From 2018 onwards, the data shows no new cases or deaths, indicating that the outbreak was effectively contained. This could be attributed to improved surveillance, public health interventions, and possibly changes in poultry management practices, which are often linked to the transmission of avian influenza viruses.
