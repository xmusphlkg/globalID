# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus is associated with avian influenza A, primarily emerging from poultry in China since 2013. It occasionally infects humans who have direct or indirect exposure to infected birds or contaminated environments. Often manifesting with symptoms similar to seasonal flu, including fever and cough, it can progress to severe respiratory illness like pneumonia, and sometimes result in death. The virus has shown limited human-to-human transmission, raising concern for potential pandemic threats. Early detection, monitoring, and preventive strategies are crucial to managing its public health impact. Continued surveillance and research are vital for preparedness.

## Highlights

- The peak of H7N9 cases occurred between 2015 and 2017, with significant surges in early 2015 (83 cases in January) and early 2017 (192 cases in January).<br/>
- Mortality was also highest during these periods, with 79 deaths reported in January 2017 alone.<br/>
- After a steady decline, no new cases have been reported since April 2019, indicating a significant reduction in transmission.<br/>
- As of August 2024, there have been no reported cases or deaths for over five years, suggesting successful control measures in the Chinese mainland.<br/>

The reported data on human infection with the H7N9 virus in mainland China shows a distinct pattern of infection peaks followed by a sharp decline. Initially, sporadic cases were observed in 2014, with a rise starting in late 2014 and reaching a significant surge during the winter months of 2015. The number of cases peaked in January and February 2015, with 83 and 59 cases respectively, accompanied by high death counts of 28 and 27. This trend reflects the seasonality of the virus, with higher transmission rates occurring during colder months. The death-to-case ratio during this period was particularly high, signaling a substantial mortality risk.

After the peak in 2015, the data shows fluctuating case numbers through 2016, with a notable surge again in the winter of 2016-2017, when cases sharply rose to 192 in January 2017, resulting in 79 deaths. This surge was followed by a steady decline through the remainder of 2017. By 2018, cases dramatically reduced, with only a few sporadic reports, and by 2019 onward, infections virtually ceased, with no new cases reported from 2020 to 2024. The eradication of the virus could be attributed to increased public health interventions, improved monitoring, and possibly changes in poultry management practices, as the virus is primarily transmitted through birds.

The human infection with the H7N9 virus in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows significant fluctuations in both case numbers and mortality. Notably, there were two major waves, one peaking in early 2015 and another in early 2017. January 2015 saw 83 cases and 28 deaths, while the highest surge occurred in January 2017 with 192 cases and 79 deaths. These waves correspond to seasonal variations, with most cases concentrated in the winter and spring months. Mortality in these waves was notably high, with case fatality rates sometimes exceeding 30%, particularly in February and March of 2015 and 2017.

From mid-2018 onward, cases and deaths sharply declined, with only isolated incidents, such as a case with one death in April 2019. The virus seems to have been effectively contained from 2020 onwards, as no new cases or deaths were reported after March 2019. The absence of new cases and fatalities from 2020 to 2024 suggests that public health measures or viral attenuation may have played a role in mitigating the impact of H7N9 during this period.
