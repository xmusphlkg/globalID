# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

The H7N9 virus is a subtype of the influenza virus that primarily affects birds but can infect humans, leading to severe respiratory illness. It was first identified in humans in China in 2013. Human infections are often linked to exposure to infected poultry or contaminated environments, such as live bird markets. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe pneumonia and acute respiratory distress syndrome. H7N9 has a high mortality rate and has raised concerns about its pandemic potential due to its ability to mutate and potentially gain human-to-human transmission capability, although sustained transmission has not been observed.

## Highlights

- The H7N9 virus showed a significant spike in cases between late 2014 and early 2017, peaking at 192 cases in January 2017. <br/>
- There was a high case fatality rate observed during the peak months, particularly in January and February 2017, where deaths reached 79 and 61, respectively. <br/>
- Since 2018, there has been a marked decline in cases, with no reported infections or deaths from August 2018 to July 2024, indicating effective control measures and potential eradication. <br/>
- The sustained absence of cases for over five years suggests the H7N9 virus is currently not a public health threat in the Chinese mainland. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H7N9 virus in the Chinese mainland reveals significant fluctuations in case numbers and associated deaths over the period from July 2014 to July 2024. The outbreak appears to have peaked in early 2017, with the highest number of cases reported in January (192 cases) and February (160 cases) of that year, accompanied by substantial mortality (79 and 61 deaths, respectively). This surge in cases suggests a major epidemic wave during this period. However, by July 2017, the cases had dropped significantly, indicating the possible effectiveness of public health interventions or changes in the virus's transmission dynamics.

From late 2017 onwards, the number of reported cases sharply declined, eventually reaching zero cases by November 2017 and remaining at that level through July 2024. This prolonged period with no new reported cases suggests successful containment and possibly the eradication of the virus in the human population, reflecting improved surveillance, vaccination efforts, or changes in the virus-host dynamics. The initial rise and subsequent fall in cases underscore the importance of continuous monitoring and quick response strategies to control outbreaks of zoonotic diseases like H7N9.
