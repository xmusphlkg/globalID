# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

H7N9 is an avian influenza virus that primarily affects birds but can infect humans, leading to severe respiratory illness. First identified in China in 2013, human infections typically occur through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild to severe, including fever, cough, and difficulty breathing. Severe cases may progress to pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and death. Human-to-human transmission is rare, but ongoing surveillance and preventive measures are critical to controlling outbreaks. The virus has raised concerns due to its potential for mutation and increased transmissibility.

## Highlights

- **Sharp decline in H7N9 cases**: After a peak in 2017 with over 200 cases, there has been a dramatic drop in reported human infections, with no cases recorded from 2019 onwards. <br/>
- **Mortality rates fluctuated**: In the earlier years, particularly 2017, mortality rates were high, with dozens of deaths occurring in each outbreak wave. <br/>
- **No reported cases in 2023 and 2024**: The virus appears to be absent in the population in recent years, with no new infections or fatalities reported as of November 2024. <br/>
- **Ongoing vigilance recommended**: Despite the lack of current cases, continued monitoring is essential to detect potential future outbreaks of the H7N9 virus. <br/>

The data for H7N9 infections in mainland China shows a sharp rise in cases from late 2013 to early 2015, with significant peaks during winter months. The highest incidence occurred in January 2015, with 83 cases and 28 deaths. These early years saw fluctuating case numbers, with notable outbreaks each winter, and a substantial death toll, often exceeding 30% in certain months. This prompted heightened public health responses, including monitoring of poultry markets and health advisories. 

From 2017 onwards, the number of infections began to decline sharply, with very few reported cases in the latter part of the year. By 2018, no new human cases were recorded, and this trend continued through 2019. The absence of reported infections from 2019 to 2024 suggests that H7N9 may have become less active or its transmission was successfully controlled. The virus's disappearance may be attributed to improved monitoring, poultry control measures, and possibly changes in the virus itself. Despite this, the virus remains a subject of ongoing surveillance due to its potential for re-emergence.
