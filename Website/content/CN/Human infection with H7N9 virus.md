# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

The H7N9 virus is an avian influenza strain that primarily affects birds but can infect humans through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. First reported in China in 2013, the virus can cause severe respiratory illness in humans, including pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and sometimes death. The virus has a high mutation rate, raising concerns about potential pandemic outbreaks. Most human cases have resulted from exposure to live poultry markets, and human-to-human transmission is rare but possible in close-contact scenarios. Surveillance, precautionary measures, and preventive healthcare practices are critical in managing H7N9 outbreaks.

## Highlights

1. **Initial Outbreak with Peak in 2017**: H7N9 cases surged significantly from late 2016 to early 2017, peaking in January 2017 with 192 cases and 79 deaths, marking the height of the epidemic. <br/>

2. **Decline and Control**: Following the peak, cases and deaths declined rapidly, with zero cases consistently reported from November 2017 onwards, indicating effective control. <br/>

3. **Zero New Cases Since 2019**: No new H7N9 cases or deaths have been reported from April 2019 through July 2025, showing successful containment. <br/>

4. **Current Status**: As of July 2025, mainland China remains free of new H7N9 infections, highlighting sustained public health measures. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H7N9 virus in the Chinese mainland highlights key trends and provides insights into outbreak dynamics. Initially, between July and September 2015, no cases were reported. However, by December 2015, there was a notable rise to 10 cases, with deaths starting to occur. Early 2016 marked a significant increase, with January and February reporting a combined 57 cases and 20 deaths, suggesting heightened transmission possibly linked to seasonal factors or virus virulence.

2017 was a pivotal year, with a substantial peak in January and February, showing 192 and 160 cases respectively, alongside high fatalities. This surge points to factors like virus mutations or environmental and human behavioral influences facilitating spread. After this peak, a decline followed, with cases dropping through 2017 and into 2018, essentially fading out by the end of that year. Sporadic reports in early 2019 indicate localized control, and from mid-2019 onward, no new cases emerged, suggesting successful intervention measures.

The absence of cases from 2019 to mid-2025 reflects effective surveillance and control measures. This sustained inactivity indicates advancements in public health strategies, including possible shifts in human-poultry interactions or improved containment procedures. Overall, the pattern of fluctuations followed by a long period of stability underscores the importance of public health vigilance in preventing future outbreaks.
