# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus, a subtype of avian influenza, was first reported in China in 2013. This virus primarily affects birds but can occasionally infect humans, leading to severe respiratory illness. Transmission to humans typically occurs through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe pneumonia and acute respiratory distress syndrome. The virus has a high mortality rate among those infected. Public health measures focus on monitoring outbreaks, controlling poultry exposure, and developing vaccines to prevent human cases.

## Highlights

- The H7N9 virus saw a significant outbreak in early 2017, with a peak of 192 cases and 79 deaths in January. This was the highest monthly count in the dataset. <br/>
- After 2017, the number of cases and deaths sharply declined, with sporadic cases reported until April 2019. <br/>
- From May 2019 onwards, there have been no reported cases or deaths, indicating effective control measures or a natural decline in the virus's spread. <br/>
- As of January 2025, the H7N9 virus appears to be under control in the Chinese mainland, with no new cases reported for several years. <br/>

The data on human infections with the H7N9 virus in mainland China reveals a significant decline in reported cases and deaths over the years. In 2015, the virus peaked with 83 cases in January, followed by a gradual decrease throughout the year, culminating in only 10 cases in December. The mortality rate during this period was notably high, with a total of 28 deaths in January alone. The subsequent years, particularly 2016 and 2017, saw fluctuations in case numbers, with a notable spike in December 2016 (106 cases) and January 2017 (192 cases), indicating potential seasonal patterns or outbreaks. However, the overall trend suggests a gradual decline in both cases and fatalities, with the virus seemingly becoming less prevalent after 2018.

From 2018 onwards, the data shows a stark reduction in reported cases, with no infections recorded from 2019 to 2025, suggesting either a successful containment of the virus or a potential change in reporting practices. The absence of cases during this extended period raises questions about the virus's transmission dynamics and the effectiveness of public health interventions. The initial high mortality rates associated with H7N9 infections highlight the virus's severity, but the subsequent years of inactivity may indicate a shift in the epidemiological landscape, warranting further investigation into the factors contributing to this decline.

After 2017, there was a sharp decline in both cases and deaths, with sporadic cases reported in 2018 and 2019, and no cases from 2020 onwards.
