# Human infection with H7N9 virus

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Human infection with the H7N9 virus is caused by an avian influenza A strain, first identified in China in 2013. Transmission primarily occurs through direct contact with infected poultry or contaminated environments. Symptoms range from mild respiratory issues to severe pneumonia, and can be fatal. Strains of H7N9 have demonstrated genetic diversity, with some capable of human-to-human transmission, although such cases are rare. Public health measures include monitoring poultry populations, controlling animal outbreaks, and conducting international surveillance to prevent widespread transmission. Vaccines and antiviral treatments are being developed to mitigate infection risk.

## Highlights

- Human infections with the H7N9 virus in China have shown a dramatic decline, with no reported cases from January 2019 through March 2025. <br/>
- The significant decrease in cases since 2018 indicates effective control measures and suggests a low risk of transmission. <br/>
- From 2015 to early 2018, there were periods of heightened activity, peaking in early 2017 with high mortality during that time. <br/>
- Continued absence of cases since 2019 suggests sustained public health efforts have successfully mitigated the H7N9 virus threat, rendering the disease situation stable and controlled. <br/>

An analysis of H7N9 virus infection data in mainland China highlights trends spanning several years. In 2015, the year begins with moderate cases peaking at 20 in March, then declines to zero in July and August. A resurgence occurs in December with 10 cases, continuing into early 2016. Notably, January and February 2016 see spikes to 28 and 29 cases, respectively, with February’s 15 fatalities marking a concerning month. The cases fluctuate but trend lower throughout the year, culminating in another sharp rise in December with 106 cases and 20 deaths, indicating a significant outbreak.

The peak of infection occurs in early 2017, showing a dramatic rise with 192 cases and 79 deaths in January alone, followed by 160 cases and 61 deaths in February. From March onward, the numbers steadily decrease, reaching single digits by July and maintaining low to negligible levels for the remainder of the year, indicating strong containment efforts. From 2018 to October 2023, there are zero reported cases, suggesting that interventions halted virus transmission effectively. This pattern persists up to 2025, highlighting the successful mitigation and control strategies that curbed the spread. Understanding these trends is essential for evaluating public health responses and guiding future efforts in managing zoonotic infections like H7N9.
