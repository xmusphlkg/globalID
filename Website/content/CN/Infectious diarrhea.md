# Infectious diarrhea

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition characterized by the inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract, primarily caused by microbial pathogens such as viruses, bacteria, and parasites. Common culprits include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, and Salmonella. The infection typically leads to symptoms like frequent, loose, and watery stools, abdominal cramps, nausea, and sometimes fever. Transmission often occurs through the consumption of contaminated food or water, poor sanitation, and direct contact with infected individuals. While often self-limiting, it can result in severe dehydration, particularly in children and vulnerable populations. Prompt diagnosis and management are crucial for effective treatment and prevention of complications.

## Highlights

- Infectious diarrhea cases in the Chinese mainland show a generally increasing trend since 2014, with notable peaks in 2018, 2021, and 2024, indicating ongoing control challenges.  
- March 2024 recorded a significant spike with 196,347 cases, marking the highest monthly total in the dataset, suggesting possible seasonal or outbreak-related factors during this time.  
- Despite fluctuating case numbers, deaths remain consistently low across the years, highlighting improved healthcare responses and effective management of severe cases.  
- High case numbers persisted into October 2024, though lower than the peak in March, indicating ongoing transmission but potentially effective mitigations reducing severity.

Infectious diarrhea in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows varying incidence patterns. Initially, monthly cases fluctuated significantly, with winter months like December and January experiencing peaks, possibly due to seasonal factors or reporting delays. Mortality was low, ranging from 0 to 6 deaths per month, indicating the condition’s high infection but low mortality rate.

From 2020, there is a noticeable decline and stabilization in case numbers, particularly from 2021 to 2023, likely reflecting successful public health interventions, increased hygiene practices, or pandemic-imposed behavioral changes. Despite fluctuating case counts, deaths became sparse during this period, suggesting enhanced healthcare responses or less severe strains.

However, by 2024, there is an observable increase, especially in March, possibly due to factors like a new strain, reduced vigilance in preventive measures, or changes in environmental conditions promoting transmission. Mortality remained low consistently, highlighting improved clinical management and effective health care systems. This data underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and adaptable public health strategies to manage infectious diarrhea.

Despite these fluctuations, the data shows a remarkably low number of deaths linked to infectious diarrhea relative to the number of cases. The death toll varies minimally, with the most deaths reported in some months not exceeding six. Notably, even during months with extremely high incidence rates, the number of fatalities remains low, underscoring a stable case-fatality rate.

In terms of trends, there appears to be a gradual reduction in reported deaths from 2019 onwards. Despite several peaks in case numbers, particularly noticeable during the usual flu seasons, death rates have not correspondingly increased, suggesting improvements in medical treatment, public health interventions, or perhaps changes in the reporting or classification of diarrhea-related fatalities. The year 2021 stands out with a consecutive monthly report of zero deaths from February to October, indicative of a possible effective containment or treatment strategy during this period. Overall, while infectious diarrhea remains prevalent, the mortality rate remains minimal, reflecting potentially effective healthcare responses or intrinsic characteristics of the disease other than the severity found elsewhere.
