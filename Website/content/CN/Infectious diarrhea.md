# Infectious diarrhea

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition characterized by frequent, loose, or watery stools resulting from intestinal infection by bacteria, viruses, or parasites. It is primarily transmitted through contaminated food, water, or person-to-person contact. Key pathogens include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, and Campylobacter. Symptoms often include abdominal cramps, nausea, fever, and dehydration. The condition is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide, especially in children and vulnerable populations in low-resource settings. Prevention focuses on improving sanitation, ensuring safe drinking water, promoting hand hygiene, and vaccination. Early diagnosis and rehydration are crucial for effective management and reducing complications.

The data on infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China from July 2014 to July 2024 indicates notable patterns and variations over the years. Initially, the cases fluctuated moderately with seasonal peaks, as seen in 2014 and 2015, where winter months such as December 2014 and January 2015 showed significant case counts, possibly correlating with seasonal factors like colder temperatures and indoor crowding. A sharp drop in deaths in recent years is observed, which could be attributed to advancements in medical intervention or increased public health awareness. The substantial increase in reported cases during the winter months, with spikes occurring in early 2018 and early 2021, highlights potential factors including holiday travel, clustering indoors, and viral viability in cooler conditions that might contribute to these spikes.

The analysis of recent data from 2020 onward suggests a dramatic decline in cases in the early pandemic months (February and March 2020) but a significant surge by early 2021. This pattern might suggest the impact of COVID-19-related lockdowns and hygiene measures initially reducing the spread, followed by normalization of activities. Interestingly, by 2022, infections indicate a consistent decrease in mortality despite fluctuating caseloads. This could point towards improved treatments or prevention strategies that reduced mortality risks. Post-pandemic years, particularly leading up to 2024, reflect a more stable but high caseload with seasonality, suggesting persistent transmission but improved management or immunity levels reducing deaths.

Analysis of the deaths associated with infectious diarrhea in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals a generally low mortality rate despite substantial fluctuations in case numbers. Across the 11-year period, the number of deaths per month remained consistently low, rarely exceeding 5 deaths in any given month. The months with relatively higher mortality, such as December 2017 (5 deaths), January 2018 (5 deaths), and December 2014 (3 deaths), still indicate that fatality rates are extremely minimal when considering the tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of reported cases during the same periods. This suggests that while the disease remains widespread, it is largely non-lethal, likely due to effective public health measures, treatment availability, or the mild nature of the disease in most cases.

However, there are noticeable trends over time. Mortality remained relatively consistent until around 2021, after which there was a marked decline in deaths, with multiple months showing zero fatalities, particularly from 2021 onward. This trend aligns with possible improvements in public health interventions, such as better sanitation, access to medical care, or public awareness regarding hygiene practices. The consistency in low death rates, despite occasional spikes in cases, reflects an overall effective containment and management strategy against severe outcomes of infectious diarrhea in the Chinese mainland.
