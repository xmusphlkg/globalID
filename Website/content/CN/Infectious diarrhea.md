# Infectious diarrhea

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a gastrointestinal illness caused by pathogenic microorganisms such as viruses, bacteria, or parasites. It is typically transmitted through contaminated food, water, or person-to-person contact. Common pathogens include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, Salmonella, and Giardia lamblia. Symptoms usually include watery stools, abdominal cramps, nausea, and sometimes fever. While most cases are mild and self-limiting, severe infections can lead to dehydration, especially in young children and the elderly. Prevention strategies include improved sanitation, safe food handling, access to clean water, and vaccination where applicable, particularly in low-resource settings with high disease burden.

## Highlights

- A sharp increase in cases has been observed in early 2025, with May 2025 reporting 179,418 cases—over 50% higher than May 2024's figure, suggesting an intensifying trend. <br/>
- The annual spring surge persists, with peak cases in February and March across multiple years; February 2025 saw the highest ever monthly count at 242,791 cases. <br/>
- Despite the surge in case numbers, fatality rates remain low, with 1 death reported in May 2025—consistent with the generally low mortality associated with infectious diarrhea. <br/>
- Long-term data indicate a steady upward trend in reported cases since 2015, possibly reflecting both increased transmission and improved surveillance or reporting. <br/>

Infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 exhibit a pronounced seasonal trend, with peaks typically occurring during the summer and winter months. Early years (2015–2016) showed relatively moderate fluctuations with monthly cases ranging between 57,000 and just over 100,000. However, a notable upward trend begins in 2017, where monthly peaks notably exceed earlier years, with occurrences climbing above 170,000 by January 2018. This growth plateaued slightly in 2019 before the COVID-19 pandemic appeared to drastically disrupt reporting trends in 2020, where case numbers dropped sharply between February and April. The disruption was short-lived, as counts quickly returned to pre-COVID levels by mid-2020 and then dramatically escalated post-2021.

The years 2023 to 2025 saw a substantial rise in incidence. By March 2024, monthly cases surpassed 190,000, and by early 2025, they exceeded 240,000, marking this period as historically high. Despite this rise in reported cases, death counts remained remarkably low and declined further in recent years, especially notable with consistently zero fatalities reported from 2023 onward outside of a few exceptions. This separation between case volume and mortality suggests improvements in clinical management, early detection, or changes in the demographic profile of affected individuals. The consistent case increases could reflect better detection/reporting infrastructure or genuine shifts in transmission dynamics, possibly influenced by urbanization, climate variability, or socio-behavioral factors.

From May 2015 to May 2025, reported deaths from infectious diarrhea in mainland China remained consistently low relative to case numbers. Monthly deaths rarely exceeded three, with isolated peaks—such as six deaths in January 2016 and five in December 2015 and 2017—primarily occurring in winter. Despite a rising trend in case counts after 2018, particularly from 2020 onward, mortality showed no proportional increase. In fact, from 2021 through 2024, many months reported zero deaths, even as monthly cases surpassed 100,000.

This decoupling of deaths from incidence suggests significant improvements in disease management, early treatment, and perhaps increased public awareness. The trend continued into 2025, where cases peaked at over 240,000 in February but resulted in only one death, yielding a case fatality rate near 0.0004%. The ongoing decline in deaths despite surging case volumes highlights effective public health responses, possible changes in pathogen virulence, or demographic resilience.
