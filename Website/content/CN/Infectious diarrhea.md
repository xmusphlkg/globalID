# Infectious diarrhea

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition caused by various pathogens, including viruses, bacteria, and parasites, leading to inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract. It is characterized by the frequent passage of loose, watery stools and is often accompanied by abdominal pain, cramping, nausea, and dehydration. Common causative agents include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, Salmonella, and Giardia lambia. Transmission usually occurs via the fecal-oral route, through contaminated food, water, or direct contact with infected individuals. Infectious diarrhea poses a significant health burden globally, particularly in developing countries, where inadequate sanitation and limited access to clean water exacerbate its spread.

## Highlights

- **Rising cases in recent years**: There has been a significant increase in reported cases from 2022 to 2024, with August 2024 reaching 144,594 cases, up from 100,107 cases in August 2022. <br/>
- **Low mortality rate**: Despite the large number of cases, the number of deaths remains very low. In August 2024, only 1 death was reported. <br/>
- **Seasonal variation observed**: Peaks in case numbers tend to occur during summer months (June to August), consistent across multiple years, highlighting seasonal trends. <br/>
- **Gradual recovery post-pandemic**: After a dip in cases in 2020, cases have steadily risen back to pre-pandemic levels, indicating a normalization of reporting. <br/>

An analysis of infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals significant fluctuations in the number of reported cases, with a general upward trend over the years. Notably, cases peaked in the winter and summer months, reflecting potential seasonal factors influencing disease transmission. For instance, the months of December, January, and August consistently saw higher case counts compared to other months, with 2018 January reporting the highest number of cases (186,071) during that time frame. Conversely, in early months of 2020, particularly February and March, there was a noticeable drop in cases, potentially due to the influence of public health measures implemented in response to the COVID-19 pandemic.

In terms of mortality, the death toll remained low overall, with most months reporting zero to a few deaths, and no significant increase in mortality rates over the years despite rising case numbers. For example, there were no deaths recorded in multiple months across 2021, 2022, and into 2023, suggesting that while the disease burden remained high in terms of case numbers, advances in healthcare or public health interventions might have reduced the fatality risk. The data shows a relatively stable pattern, with intermittent spikes, such as 2024 March (196,347 cases and 1 death), highlighting the importance of ongoing surveillance and control measures to mitigate the spread of infectious diarrhea.

The analysis of infectious diarrhea deaths in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows consistently low mortality rates despite fluctuating case numbers. Most months report deaths between 0 and 3, with occasional spikes such as in January 2016 (6 deaths). Interestingly, some months, particularly in 2021, saw high case numbers but zero reported deaths, highlighting effective healthcare interventions and treatment availability.

A key observation is the lack of a direct correlation between case surges and death rates. For example, in 2018 and 2019, while cases rose steadily, the death toll remained low. This suggests effective management of severe cases, even during periods of increased transmission. However, occasional spikes in deaths during specific months, such as September 2015 and January 2016, indicate that certain factors may have impacted healthcare response during those times.

From 2021 onward, there were extended periods with no deaths despite high case numbers, pointing to improvements in disease management and public health efforts. By 2024, the pattern remains consistent, with very few months reporting deaths, even as cases continued to rise. This trend suggests sustained progress in controlling mortality related to infectious diarrhea across China.
