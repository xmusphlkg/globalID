# Infectious diarrhea

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition characterized by frequent, watery bowel movements resulting from infections by bacteria, viruses, or parasites. Common causative agents include pathogens like Escherichia coli, Salmonella, Vibrio cholerae, rotavirus, and Giardia lamblia. Transmission usually occurs via the fecal-oral route, often through contaminated food, water, or close person-to-person contact. Symptoms typically include abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, and fever. While generally self-limiting, severe cases can lead to dehydration, especially in vulnerable populations like children and the elderly. Prevention strategies include proper sanitation, safe drinking water, and vaccination where available.

## Highlights

- Infectious diarrhea cases show a significant surge in 2025, reaching a peak of 242,791 cases in February, suggesting seasonality or external factors affecting transmission. <br/>
- There is a notable reduction in deaths from the disease, with zero deaths recorded in July 2025, which may reflect improved healthcare responses or changes in the pathogen's severity. <br/>
- Data from 2023 indicated a lower number of cases compared to recent months, with 67,461 cases in December, suggesting fluctuating epidemic dynamics or interventions impacting disease rates. <br/>
- Steadily increasing case numbers from 2023 to mid-2025 imply ongoing transmission issues, warranting strengthened epidemiological monitoring and public health strategies. <br/>

The analysis of infectious diarrhea deaths in Mainland China from 2015 to mid-2025 highlights a consistently low mortality rate despite variable case numbers. Over the decade, monthly fatalities have rarely surpassed single digits, indicating effective management and prevention practices. For instance, peaks in cases such as January 2018, February 2019, and February 2025—with 186,071; 120,881; and 242,791 cases respectively—show minimal impact on the death toll, proving the effectiveness of local health systems in controlling fatal outcomes.

Several months, particularly between July 2021 and February 2022, reported zero deaths despite high case numbers, suggesting efficient healthcare interventions. Over the years, a pattern has emerged with certain months regularly observing increased case numbers, such as during summer, yet this has not translated to higher fatalities. From mid-2023 through mid-2025, consecutive months reported no deaths despite high incidence rates, indicating potential advancements in public health measures or increased population resilience. The consistently low fatality rate compared to case numbers reflects the non-lethal nature of many diarrhea cases when treated adequately, along with improved access to healthcare resources, sanitation education, and timely outbreak management. This enduring low mortality amidst fluctuating case numbers underscores the importance of strengthening healthcare frameworks and public education to sustain favorable outcomes.
