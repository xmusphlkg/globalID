# Infectious diarrhea

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition characterized by frequent loose or watery stools caused by infection from various pathogens, including bacteria, viruses, and parasites. Common culprits include *Escherichia coli*, *Salmonella*, *Norovirus*, and *Giardia*. Transmission typically occurs through contaminated food, water, or direct contact with infected individuals. Symptoms may include abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, and dehydration. Diagnosis is based on clinical evaluation and stool testing. Treatment primarily focuses on rehydration, with antibiotics or antiparasitic medications prescribed for specific infections. Preventive measures include proper sanitation and hygiene practices.

## Highlights

- **Increasing cases in 2024**: Infectious diarrhea cases have significantly risen in 2024, with notable peaks in March (196,347 cases) and August (144,594 cases). <br/>
- **Low mortality**: Despite the high number of cases, deaths remain relatively low, with only 1-2 deaths per month in most of 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal fluctuations**: The disease shows a seasonal pattern, with higher incidence during warmer months (March to August) and a decline in winter months (November to February). <br/>
- **Decline in 2022 and early 2023**: There was a notable decrease in reported cases during late 2022 and early 2023, potentially reflecting improved control measures. <br/>

The data on infectious diarrhea in mainland China reveals notable seasonal variations, with peaks typically occurring in the colder months, especially January and February. These months consistently report the highest case numbers, followed by a decline in the warmer months, suggesting that environmental factors like temperature and humidity play a role in transmission. The data also indicates that human activities, such as holiday gatherings during the Chinese New Year, may contribute to higher case counts in early months. Despite fluctuations in cases, the number of deaths has remained relatively low, with most months reporting no or very few fatalities, especially after 2020. This decline in mortality suggests improvements in healthcare access, public health interventions, and disease management strategies.

While the overall case count has risen in recent years, particularly in 2024, the death toll has remained minimal, highlighting the success of preventative measures and effective treatment. For example, even with high case numbers in 2024, deaths stayed low, with only a few reported in March and August. This pattern of low mortality relative to case numbers points to better public health systems and improvements in managing infectious diseases. Overall, the data suggests that while infectious diarrhea remains a persistent public health issue, effective responses have helped minimize its fatal impact.

In examining the mortality rates associated with infectious diarrhea in the Chinese mainland, there is a clear trend of low and fluctuating death counts, particularly in recent years. From 2014 to 2019, the number of deaths remained relatively low, with the highest observed in December 2017 (5 deaths) and several other months showing zero fatalities. The overall case numbers, however, exhibited a consistent rise, especially during the winter months, which may reflect seasonal factors such as colder weather, which could contribute to higher infection rates or reduced access to healthcare during the peak seasons.

From 2020 onward, the number of reported deaths dropped even further, with many months recording zero fatalities, particularly in 2021 and 2022. This decrease in death rates, despite a large number of reported cases, suggests an improvement in healthcare response, such as better prevention, treatment, and management strategies. The continued low mortality, even as cases reach higher levels in 2024 (with March 2024 seeing over 196,000 cases), reflects the potential advancements in medical care, public health interventions, and overall infection control in the region. The near-zero death toll during most months in 2023 and 2024 also points toward enhanced monitoring and rapid response systems in place to mitigate fatal outcomes.
