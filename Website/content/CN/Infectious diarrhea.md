# Infectious diarrhea

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a gastrointestinal condition caused by pathogens such as bacteria, viruses, or parasites. Common agents include *Escherichia coli*, *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, *Norovirus*, and *Giardia*. Transmission typically occurs through contaminated food or water, or via person-to-person contact. Symptoms include frequent loose stools, abdominal cramps, nausea, and sometimes fever or vomiting. It is a major cause of morbidity and mortality, especially among young children in low-resource settings. Prevention strategies focus on sanitation, safe drinking water, hygiene practices, and vaccination. Prompt rehydration is essential in treatment to prevent dehydration-related complications.

## Highlights

- The number of reported infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China has significantly increased, reaching a peak of 242,791 cases in February 2025. <br/>
- There is a continuous increasing trend from December 2023 (67,461 cases) through April 2025 (227,604 cases), indicating a resurgence in cases. <br/>
- The mortality rate remains low despite the spike in cases, with minimal deaths reported in recent months (1-2 deaths per month), suggesting improvements in treatment or disease management. <br/>
- Notably, infection rates often peak during cooler months (January to April), a consistent seasonal trend observed throughout the dataset. <br/>

Over the span from 2015 to early 2025, reported cases of infectious diarrhea on the Chinese mainland displayed a generally rising long-term trend with marked seasonal fluctuations. Initial monthly case counts in 2015 hovered in the 50,000–100,000 range, but by 2024 and 2025, monthly counts regularly exceeded 120,000, peaking at over 240,000 in February 2025. Summer months (June to August) typically saw higher case loads, consistent with known environmental and behavioral transmission factors, while winter (December to February) began showing progressively higher incidence in later years, suggesting a shift in either surveillance sensitivity, reporting efficiency, or seasonality dynamics. A significant anomaly occurred in early 2020, where case numbers dropped sharply in February and March, likely linked to COVID-related public health interventions that suppressed transmission of many infectious diseases.

Despite the increase in detected cases, death counts remained very low, with annual totals rarely exceeding single digits per month. In recent years, particularly from 2021 onward, mortality dropped to nearly zero, even as case numbers surged. This divergence suggests substantial improvement in clinical outcomes, possibly due to enhanced access to care, earlier diagnosis and treatment, or better community health management. Additionally, more robust public health surveillance may have captured a higher proportion of mild or non-severe cases, lowering the observed fatality rate. Overall, the data reflects both an increasing burden of reported disease and stronger health system responses mitigating its lethality.

From 2015 to early 2025, the mortality burden of infectious diarrhea in the Chinese mainland showed a significant downward trend despite large case volumes. Early years such as 2015 through 2018 featured frequent but low-magnitude death tolls, often between 1–5 deaths per month, even during high-incidence periods such as summer. The death-to-case ratio peaked modestly in colder months like January and December, consistent with known seasonal mortality vulnerability in young or elderly populations. For instance, December 2017 and January 2018 each experienced 5 deaths, despite similar or slightly lower case counts than months with fewer fatalities. However, the absolute death rate never reached high levels, indicating improved access to effective supportive care and early treatment.

From 2021 onward, the data reveal a sustained period of negligible or zero deaths per month, concurrent with a high and growing number of reported cases. This decoupling suggests systemic improvements such as early identification, public health interventions, or expanded outpatient treatment coverage. Even as caseloads surpassed 200,000 per month in early 2025, deaths remained sporadic and isolated. This stark contrast to earlier years implies a transition in the clinical impact of infectious diarrhea, favoring reduced severity or enhanced medical response. The effectiveness of national disease control strategies, public health infrastructure, or changing pathogen profiles may underlie the near-complete suppression of mortality despite enduring transmission.
