# Infectious diarrhea

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition characterized by frequent, watery bowel movements caused by pathogens such as bacteria, viruses, or parasites. Common culprits include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, and Salmonella. Transmission often occurs through contaminated food, water, or direct contact with an infected person. Symptoms typically include abdominal cramps, nausea, and dehydration, which can be severe, especially in young children and the elderly. Prevention strategies focus on proper sanitation, hygiene, and safe food handling practices. Treatment usually involves rehydration and, in some cases, antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, depending on the causative agent.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers**: From 2015 to 2025, infectious diarrhea cases have increased, reaching 162,257 in January 2025, indicating a growing health issue. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Cases typically rise in summer (June-August) and fall in winter (December-February), showing clear seasonal trends. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite more cases, deaths remain low, often zero, suggesting effective healthcare and management. <br/>

- **Recent Increase**: A notable rise in cases from late 2023 to early 2025, especially in March 2024 and January 2025, underscores the need for better surveillance and prevention. <br/>

The data on infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months (June to August) and lower numbers in the winter months (December to February). This pattern is consistent with the behavior of many infectious diseases, which often see increased transmission during warmer months due to factors such as increased human interaction and favorable conditions for pathogen survival and spread.

From 2015 to 2019, the number of cases generally increased year over year, with a significant spike in December 2017 and January 2018. This could be attributed to various factors, including changes in reporting practices, population growth, or increased transmission rates. The data for 2020 shows a sharp decline in cases during February and March, likely due to the impact of COVID-19-related public health measures, which may have inadvertently reduced the spread of other infectious diseases. However, from 2021 onwards, the number of cases began to rise again, reaching a new peak in March 2024. Despite these fluctuations in case numbers, the death toll remained relatively low throughout the period, suggesting effective medical interventions and public health responses in managing the disease's severity.
