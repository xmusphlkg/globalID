# Infectious diarrhea

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a condition characterized by frequent, watery bowel movements caused by pathogens such as bacteria, viruses, or parasites. Common culprits include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, and Salmonella. Transmission often occurs through contaminated food, water, or contact with infected individuals. Symptoms typically include abdominal cramps, nausea, and dehydration, which can be severe, especially in young children and the elderly. Prevention strategies focus on proper sanitation, hygiene, and safe food handling practices. Treatment primarily involves rehydration and, in some cases, antibiotics or antiparasitic medications, depending on the causative agent.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers**: Infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China have surged, with February 2025 recording 242,791 cases, the highest in the dataset, indicating a potential outbreak. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite high case numbers, the mortality rate remains low, with only one death in February 2025, suggesting effective management and treatment. <br/>

- **Seasonal Trends**: Historically, cases peak in summer, but recent data shows increased cases in winter months, particularly January and February. <br/>

- **Recent Surge**: The sharp rise from 162,257 cases in January to 242,791 in February 2025 highlights an urgent need for public health interventions. <br/>

The data on infectious diarrhea in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows several key trends. Initially, from 2015 to 2017, there is a gradual increase in cases, peaking in December 2017 at 174,969 cases. During this period, deaths remain low, typically between 0 to 5 per month. This rise in cases could be due to seasonal variations, changes in reporting, or specific pathogen outbreaks. Notably, higher cases are often reported in summer months, aligning with the behavior of many diarrheal diseases that thrive in warmer conditions.

From 2018, the data shows fluctuations, with a significant drop in February 2020, likely due to COVID-19 measures like lockdowns and improved hygiene, which reduced the transmission of other infectious diseases. However, from 2021 to 2025, there is a general upward trend, peaking at 242,791 cases in February 2025. Despite the increase in cases, deaths remain low, indicating improvements in medical treatment and healthcare access.

Overall, the data underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and public health interventions to manage and prevent outbreaks of infectious diarrhea, especially in densely populated areas. The seasonal patterns and the impact of external factors like COVID-19 highlight the need for adaptive public health strategies to mitigate the spread of infectious diseases.

The data on infectious diarrhea in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows notable trends in case numbers and mortality. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, cases fluctuate with seasonal peaks, particularly in summer, reflecting the typical pattern of infectious diseases. During this period, deaths per month remain low, ranging from 0 to 6, indicating a low mortality rate relative to the number of cases. This suggests that while the disease is widespread, it is not highly lethal, possibly due to effective medical interventions or the non-severe nature of the infections.

From 2020 onwards, there is a marked increase in reported cases, especially in 2021 and 2022, with monthly cases often exceeding 100,000. Despite this rise, deaths remain low, with many months reporting zero fatalities. This trend continues into 2023 and 2024, with few exceptions where deaths are reported, but still at a low frequency. The data for 2025 shows a dramatic spike in cases in February, reaching over 240,000, yet only one death is reported, further emphasizing the low mortality rate. This pattern could indicate improvements in public health measures, better access to healthcare, or changes in the reporting or classification of cases. Overall, while infectious diarrhea remains a significant public health issue in terms of case numbers, its impact in terms of mortality is minimal.
