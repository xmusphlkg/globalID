# Infectious diarrhea

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a gastrointestinal condition caused by microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses, and parasites. It is characterized by frequent, loose, or watery stools, abdominal pain, and dehydration. Common pathogens responsible include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, Salmonella, and Giardia lamblia. Transmission occurs through contaminated food, water, or surfaces, and is more prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and hygiene. Infectious diarrhea can have a significant impact on children and vulnerable populations, leading to malnutrition and morbidity. Prevention focuses on hygiene, safe water, and food practices, while treatment often involves rehydration and, in some cases, antibiotics or antiparasitics.

## Highlights

- Significant increase in cases observed in early 2025, with March recording 239,224 cases. <br/>
- Steady decline in fatalities over recent years, remaining low with only 1 death in March 2025. <br/>
- Peaks traditionally occur in summer months, but high cases continue into colder months. <br/>
- The trend in recent years shows fluctuating but overall increasing number of cases, indicating possible heightened transmission. <br/>

From March 2015 to March 2025, infectious diarrhea cases across the Chinese mainland demonstrated strong seasonality and an upward trajectory in total case counts. Peaks generally occurred in summer months (June to August), corresponding with high temperatures and increased bacterial activity, while troughs were seen in cooler months like February and November. A noticeable increase in cases began around late 2017, with significant escalations in January 2018 (186,071 cases) and subsequently higher winter/spring peaks, culminating in a particularly high wave beginning in 2023 and continuing into 2025, reaching a new record of 242,791 cases in February 2025. This suggests a systematic climb in annual baseline incidence, potentially due to population growth, improved surveillance, or environmental factors conducive to pathogen transmission.

Despite the rise in cases, annual deaths remained low and showed no corresponding sharp increase, with most months recording between 0–3 deaths. Post-2022 data indicate a notable mortality decline: from 2023 onward, most months reported zero deaths, which may reflect advancements in case management, earlier diagnosis, or shifts in pathogen virulence. Interestingly, even in months with record-breaking case levels post-2023, including March 2024 and February 2025, mortality did not rise proportionately. This decoupling of cases and fatalities might point to improved healthcare access, expanded immunization coverage, or public health interventions mitigating severity.

From 2015 to early 2025, the Chinese mainland reported a consistent burden of infectious diarrhea with pronounced seasonality in case numbers, typically peaking during summer and early fall. However, death counts remained low across all years, rarely exceeding 5 per month. Initial years (2015–2019) witnessed periodic monthly death peaks—up to 5—occurring in high-incidence months like December 2015 and 2017. Following this, 2020 onward marks a distinct downward trend in fatalities despite continually high and even increasing case counts. Notably, the years 2021 to 2023 show long, uninterrupted sequences of months with zero reported deaths, suggesting either enhanced clinical management, improvements in healthcare access, or better early intervention.

This decline may reflect structural changes in surveillance quality, public health infrastructure, or population immunity. The sharp decoupling of mortality from incidence, especially evident from 2021 forward, implies that while the incidence rose (e.g., a record 242,791 cases in February 2025), fatal outcomes have been contained—only 1 death reported that month. This extraordinary drop in case fatality ratio, particularly in high-density urban outbreaks likely driven by improved diagnostics and reporting, hints at a maturing public health response more adept at managing common diarrheal pathogens before complications arise.
