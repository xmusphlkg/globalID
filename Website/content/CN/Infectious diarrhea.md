# Infectious diarrhea

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a common condition caused by viruses, bacteria, or parasites that contaminate food, water, or surfaces. It leads to frequent, loose, watery stools and abdominal discomfort, potentially causing dehydration and electrolyte imbalances. Transmission typically occurs via the fecal-oral route, through ingestion of contaminated substances or contact with infected individuals. Common pathogens include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, and Salmonella. Symptoms can vary from mild to severe, depending on the causative agent and the individual's immune status. Preventative measures include proper sanitation, hand hygiene, and safe food handling practices to reduce the spread of infections.

## Highlights

- A significant rise is noted in cases from 2020 onwards, with unprecedented peaks in 2024 and 2025, highlighting an upward trend in disease transmission. <br/>
- Despite higher cases in 2024 and 2025, the death count remains low, indicating potential improvements in disease management or healthcare access. <br/>
- Seasonal patterns are visible, with higher incidences typically observed during the summer and fall months. <br/>
- The data for August 2025 indicates stabilization compared to prior months but still represents a higher case count compared to the historical average, reflecting ongoing public health challenges. <br/>

The data on infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China from 2015 to mid-2025 reveals seasonal and long-term epidemiological patterns. Initially, from 2015 through 2019, cases often peak in late summer (July to September), which could be linked to environmental factors such as higher temperatures and humidity that aid in the spread of enteric pathogens. Interestingly, December frequently records the highest number of cases annually, hinting at possible seasonal influences or increased year-end reporting. Despite fluctuations, the mortality rate remains consistently low, suggesting high recovery rates.

In early 2020, there's a distinct decline in cases, coinciding with the COVID-19-related lockdowns and heightened hygiene practices, which might have inadvertently reduced other infectious diseases, including diarrhea. However, as restrictions eased, the number of cases gradually returned to prior levels, evidenced by the steady increase in cases from late 2024 through 2025. This increase, peaking at over 200,000 cases in early 2025, could reflect changing environmental conditions, shifts in behavior, or improved detection and reporting. Despite these rising cases, the number of deaths remains low throughout, underscoring effective public health interventions and clinical management advancements in managing infectious diarrhea.
