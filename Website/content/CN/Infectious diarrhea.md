# Infectious diarrhea

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Infectious diarrhea is a gastrointestinal disorder caused by pathogens including bacteria, viruses, and parasites. Common culprits include rotavirus, norovirus, Escherichia coli, and Salmonella. It presents with symptoms such as frequent loose or watery stools, abdominal pain, nausea, and dehydration. Transmission often occurs through consumption of contaminated food or water, or via contact with infected individuals. While infectious diarrhea can affect anyone, children in developing countries are particularly vulnerable, often leading to severe dehydration and malnutrition. Proper sanitation, hygiene, and access to clean water are crucial preventive measures. Treatment typically focuses on fluid replenishment and addressing underlying infections.

## Highlights

- **Recent Surge:** From January to April 2025, infectious diarrhea cases in mainland China significantly increased, peaking at 242,791 cases in February. Numbers have decreased steadily since May. <br/>
- **Death Rates:** Despite case fluctuations, mortality rates remain low, with consistent reports of zero or one death in most months. This suggests effective management and treatment. <br/>
- **Year-on-Year Fluctuations:** There is a cyclical pattern of infection spikes, particularly in winters from previous years, but the early 2025 peak was exceptionally high compared to previous winters. <br/>
- **Current Status:** As of September 2025, the case count declined to 124,055. Efforts to reduce cases are successful, indicating improved control measures. <br/>

The data on infectious diarrhea in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals fluctuating cases with seasonal variation and consistently low death rates. Initially, peaks in cases appear during the winter months, such as December 2015 and January 2016, where cases exceeded 100,000. This trend suggests a correlation with factors like climate or social behavior during holidays. Similar patterns continue through 2017 to 2019, with high cases during colder months and declines in warmer periods. However, in 2020, a sharp drop in cases in February and March stands out, coinciding with COVID-19 interventions that likely reduced transmission, showcasing the impact of public health measures on infectious disease trends.

By 2021, there is a recovery and gradual increase in cases noted, with smaller peaks in subsequent years. The resurgence into 2023 and further escalation in 2024 and 2025 mark a return to pre-pandemic patterns, with early 2025 showing notably high counts. Despite rising cases, the death rate has remained low and stable overall, indicating improved clinical outcomes or management strategies. The data collectively highlights the interaction between pandemic measures and general infectious disease trends, emphasizing the role of environmental and behavioral factors in seasonal case variance.
