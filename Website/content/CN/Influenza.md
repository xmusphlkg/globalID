# Influenza

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, or the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses, mainly affecting the nose, throat, and lungs. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, headaches, and fatigue. There are four types of influenza viruses: A, B, C, and D, with A and B responsible for seasonal flu epidemics. The virus spreads through respiratory droplets from coughs, sneezes, or talking. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, especially important for high-risk groups such as young children, the elderly, and individuals with chronic health conditions, reducing the risk of severe complications.

## Highlights

- Influenza cases in Chinese mainland have generally shown seasonal spikes, with substantial increases during colder months such as December and January. <br/>
- From 2023 onwards, there has been a notable rise in cases, with peaks exceeding millions during the winter months, although deaths have remained relatively low. <br/>
- The current situation in March 2025 indicates a decrease in cases compared to previous months, with 354,842 cases reported, while fatalities have not increased significantly. <br/>
- Continuous monitoring and preparedness are essential due to the unpredictability of future infection rates, considering past trends of periodic surges and fluctuations in case numbers. <br/>

Influenza data for mainland China shows notable seasonal variation and significant fluctuations across the years. In 2015, cases were relatively stable, with peaks during colder months, continuing into 2016 with a sharp increase in March, reaching over 76,000 cases. This could indicate a particularly intense flu season or heightened surveillance. In subsequent years, case numbers generally increased, with January 2018 marking a dramatic rise to nearly 274,000 cases, possibly signaling an outbreak or improved reporting.

By 2019, cases surged further, with January alone exceeding 600,000, highlighting a particularly severe flu season. However, from mid-2020 through 2021, reported influenza cases dropped significantly, likely due to COVID-19-related public health measures such as social distancing and mask-wearing, which also curbed influenza transmission. Despite this dip, 2022 saw a sharp resurgence, with mid-year cases exceeding 700,000, pointing to a renewed outbreak or relaxed containment measures.

The period from 2023 onward witnessed extreme case peaks, especially in March and December, surpassing several million cases. These spikes may indicate heightened public health surveillance, changes in viral dynamics, or emergence of a more transmissible strain. Mortality rates remained relatively stable through these years, suggesting advancements in treatment or less virulent strains. Overall, the data illustrates complex interactions between influenza patterns, public health responses, and external factors.

The reported influenza-related deaths in mainland China over the years show considerable variation, with distinct seasonal and annual trends. Notably, while the total influenza cases often surged during certain months, especially early in the year (January-February), the number of deaths did not consistently mirror these peaks proportionally. For example, in January 2019, there were over 600,000 cases with 143 deaths, whereas December 2023 recorded over 4 million cases but only 6 deaths, indicating either improved clinical outcomes, reporting practices, or other systemic changes over time. Sharp increases in cases, such as March 2023's record 3.7 million cases, also show relatively subdued mortality, with just 38 deaths noted that month.

An overall review highlights several years with mortality rates momentarily spiking around substantial case surges (e.g., 2018, 2019), but recent trends post-2020 suggest a noticeable reduction in deaths relative to case counts. This could reflect advancements in healthcare, public health interventions, vaccine coverage, or virus characteristics. The data also emphasizes the low-case fatality rates of influenza, even during massive outbreaks. Nonetheless, monitoring sporadic death spikes alongside case surges is critical to identifying shifts in the epidemiology of influenza in the region and optimizing public health responses.
