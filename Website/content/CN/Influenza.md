# Influenza

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, or the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses, primarily impacting the nose, throat, and lungs. It spreads via droplets from coughs, sneezes, or talking. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, muscle aches, headaches, and fatigue, ranging from mild to severe. Most recover in a few days to less than two weeks, but serious complications can occur, especially in vulnerable groups like the elderly, young children, and those with chronic health conditions. Annual vaccination is crucial to prevention, reducing the risk of infection and mitigating the impact of seasonal flu outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Periodic High Incidence:**
  Noticeable seasonal peaks occur predominantly in winter months, particularly from December to February, with massive surges seen in 2019 and 2023.  

- **Recent Stabilization:**
  **From mid-2023 to mid-2025, the number of cases decreased significantly compared to prior peaks, suggesting improved control or shifting dynamics.**  

- **Mortality Rate Trends:**
  Death rates remain marginal relative to case numbers, with fluctuations in mortality indicating potential changes in virus virulence or healthcare responses over time.  

- **Current Situation:**
  **As of September 2025, influenza cases show moderate levels, aligning with post-peak declines, and mortality remains low, suggesting a stable epidemiological situation.**

Influenza cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit noticeable fluctuations from 2015 to 2025, characterized by seasonal peaks and a few extraordinary surges. Initially, between 2015 and early 2016, cases gradually increased, peaking in March 2016 with 76,498 cases, indicative of typical influenza seasonality. This trend persisted, with December 2017 registering 121,800 cases, foreshadowing a larger spike in January 2018 with 273,949 cases, potentially linked to viral mutations or improved diagnostics.

Significant surges were noted in 2019 and 2023, with January 2019 experiencing 608,511 cases, reflecting potential shifts in influenza strain virulence, vaccination effectiveness, or reporting practices. The 2020 decline, corresponding with COVID-19 pandemic measures, likely reflects the impact of public health interventions on influenza transmission. March 2023 records a tremendous spike of over 3.7 million cases, underscoring potential factors like health system strain, environmental variations, or localized outbreaks.

Throughout these years, a notable aspect is the relatively low case fatality rate, suggesting improvements in healthcare delivery and treatment protocols. This data demonstrates ongoing fluctuations in influenza incidence while highlighting healthcare resilience amid rising infection numbers. The overarching trends underline the challenge in predicting influenza dynamics, emphasizing the importance of adaptive health strategies and continuous surveillance to manage future outbreaks effectively.
