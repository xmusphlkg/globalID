# Influenza

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets released when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. Influenza is characterized by sudden onset symptoms such as high fever, cough, sore throat, muscle aches, fatigue, and headaches. While it can affect people of all ages, certain groups, like the elderly, young children, and individuals with weakened immune systems, are at higher risk for severe illness. Annual vaccination is recommended to mitigate the impact of the virus and prevent widespread outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Surge in Early 2023:** Influenza cases spiked dramatically in 2023, peaking in March with over 3.7 million cases, indicating significant virus transmission. <br/>

- **Decline in 2024:** After the peak, the cases steadily declined in 2024, with October reporting around 103,568 cases, suggesting effective containment or seasonal effects. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite high case numbers, the mortality rate remained low throughout, indicating successful clinical treatments or less severe virus strains. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of October 2024, influenza appears stabilized with controlled case numbers and minimal fatalities, reflecting successful public health efforts or natural virus decline. <br/>

The analysis of influenza data from the Chinese mainland between 2014 and 2024 showcases significant fluctuations in reported cases and relatively stable death counts. Initially, cases were moderate, but a marked rise is observed in early 2016, peaking at 76,498 cases in March. This trend of seasonal increases continued, with substantial spikes notably in early 2019 and a massive surge to nearly 1.2 million cases in December 2019, reaching another peak in January 2020. These patterns suggest influenza peaks during winter months. Despite a high number of cases, deaths remained low throughout the years, indicating a relatively low mortality rate.

In 2023, a dramatic increase in cases was recorded, especially in March with over 3.7 million cases, influencing numbers throughout the year, even during typically low-incidence months. These substantial increases could result from higher transmission rates, changes in public health surveillance, viral changes, or enhanced diagnostic capabilities. Notably, the number of deaths remained low compared to the soaring case numbers, suggesting factors such as improved healthcare, availability of effective treatments, milder cases, or higher levels of immunity among the population. This large increase in cases with relatively low fatalities may indicate a shift in the disease's impact, with healthcare systems potentially better equipped to deal with outbreaks or the virus presenting in less severe form among the population.

Analyzing the data on influenza-related deaths in mainland China reveals several trends over the years. Starting from 2014, the number of deaths remained relatively low until a noticeable increase around late 2015 and early 2016. The data indicate that while influenza cases surged at different times, deaths did not always correspondingly increase at the same rate, remaining somewhat manageable during certain peak periods. In particular, January and February of 2018 and 2019 saw significant spikes in both cases and deaths, aligning with the typical seasonal flu pattern, where influenza-related complications can lead to increased mortality rates at the start of the year.

Furthermore, the data show notable fluctuations over the years, with periods of high reported cases not always resulting in proportional rises in deaths. For example, December 2019 and the early months of 2020 reported exceedingly high case numbers, but the deaths remained lower in proportion. This discrepancy could suggest improvements in healthcare responses or variations in virus strains. The data for recent years, such as 2023 and 2024, show a substantial increase in cases but relatively low death tolls, particularly in the latter part of 2023 and onwards. This trend could reflect successful public health interventions, vaccination campaigns, or changes in the population's susceptibility or immunity to the virus.
