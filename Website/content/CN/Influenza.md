# Influenza

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a highly contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses, primarily types A and B. These viruses infect the nose, throat, and sometimes the lungs, leading to symptoms such as fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, and fatigue. The flu is typically spread through droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. It can cause mild to severe illness and, in some cases, lead to hospitalization or death. Annual vaccination is recommended as the most effective way to prevent infection and control seasonal outbreaks, primarily occurring in the colder months.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Trends:** Influenza cases in mainland China have seen significant fluctuations, with strong spikes in early 2019 and late 2023. These reflect seasonal outbreaks and potential epidemics. <br/>
- **Recent Decline in 2025:** A noticeable decline in cases from February to June 2025 suggests stabilization and improved disease control as the epidemic season wanes. <br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Despite variable case numbers, influenza-related deaths have remained low, indicating effective healthcare response and management. <br/>
- **Unpredictable Peaks:** Past years, especially 2022 and 2023, showed unexpected peaks, underscoring the need for preparedness against sudden outbreaks. Continued vigilance remains essential. <br/>

These peaks typically align with global influenza dynamics, highlighting heightened vulnerability during colder months. Surges, such as those in December 2019 and March 2023, suggest possible strain variations or public health changes, potentially exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic or shifts in public behaviors affecting transmission rates. The dramatic increase in January 2019 reflects an extraordinary seasonal surge, likely influenced by specific epidemiological factors or enhanced reporting.

Despite surges in case numbers, mortality rates remain comparatively low, indicating effective management of severe outcomes. The months with pronounced case spikes, like January 2019 and March 2023, showed death counts that, while elevated, did not increase proportionally to cases. This may be attributed to robust healthcare systems and vaccination efforts successfully controlling severe outcomes. The data reveals a consistent pattern where larger outbreaks do not necessarily lead to high deaths, likely due to the strain-specific pathology and public health interventions in place.

These trends necessitate a thorough understanding of multiple dimensions—seasonality, sociocultural behaviors, strain mutations, and health intervention efficacy. The abnormal transmission spikes require further investigation to pinpoint underlying drivers, such as virological factors or external influences.

Analyzing influenza-related deaths in mainland China from June 2015 to June 2025 shows patterns closely tied to fluctuations in case numbers. Significant peaks in cases often correlate with increased deaths. In January 2019, for instance, cases surged to over 608,500, with deaths reaching 143. Similarly, 2020 saw nearly one million cases in January, resulting in 108 deaths. March 2023 experienced a record spike of over 3.7 million cases, with 38 deaths—highlighting the close relationship between case numbers and mortality.

Despite these peaks, periods of lower case numbers generally show fewer deaths, demonstrating the influence of influenza’s seasonality. This is seen in post-peak months where both cases and deaths typically decline. However, there are instances where increased cases do not lead to a sharp rise in mortality, suggesting the influence of improved healthcare responses, including vaccination and treatment efforts. These efforts may help control death rates even amid high transmission periods.

Additionally, over the years, the ratio of deaths to cases seems to decrease, possibly indicating better healthcare infrastructure and public health strategies. This trend could reflect advancements in managing influenza, such as timely interventions, increased awareness, and effective public health measures, which collectively help reduce the impact of epidemics in China, despite the high case numbers during seasonal peaks.
