# Influenza

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It primarily affects the nose, throat, and sometimes the lungs, leading to symptoms such as fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, and fatigue. The flu is highly contagious and spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Seasonal flu epidemics occur annually, with varying severity. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent infection and its complications. Antiviral medications can also help reduce the severity and duration of symptoms if administered early.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers**: February 2025 reported 1,348,394 influenza cases in the Chinese mainland, marking a significant increase from previous years. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite the high number of cases, February 2025 saw 14 deaths, indicating a relatively low mortality rate. <br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns**: Influenza cases typically peak in winter months, with notable surges in December 2024 and January 2025. <br/>
- **Ongoing Trends**: The trend from late 2024 into early 2025 shows a continued high incidence of influenza, highlighting the need for sustained public health measures. <br/>

The influenza data from the Chinese mainland between 2015 and 2025 shows significant fluctuations in cases and deaths, with notable peaks. From 2015 to 2017, cases were relatively stable, with a gradual increase in 2016 and a more pronounced rise in 2017. Deaths were moderate, with slight increases during winter months, typical for influenza due to seasonal factors.

A dramatic surge in cases began in late 2018, peaking in January 2019 with over 600,000 cases, and again in December 2019 with nearly 1.2 million cases. This trend continued into early 2020, suggesting a severe influenza season, possibly due to environmental or social factors. The decline in cases during the latter half of 2020 and into 2021 could be attributed to public health interventions or natural disease cycles. Another significant increase occurred in 2022, peaking in June and July, followed by a massive spike in March 2023, reaching over 3.7 million cases, indicating potential new strains or changes in population immunity.

The data highlights a relatively low mortality rate compared to the number of cases, suggesting effective healthcare responses or less virulent strains. However, spikes in deaths during peak months, such as January 2019 and March 2023, underscore the ongoing public health challenge of managing influenza outbreaks. The data from 2024 and 2025 shows continued high case numbers, indicating influenza remains a persistent public health issue.

The data on influenza cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several notable trends. Initially, from 2015 to 2017, the number of cases and deaths remained relatively low, with a gradual increase in both metrics. However, starting in 2018, there was a significant surge in reported cases, peaking in January 2019 with over 600,000 cases and 143 deaths. This spike suggests a possible outbreak or increased reporting and testing during this period. The subsequent years, particularly 2019 and 2020, continued to show high case numbers, with a notable peak in December 2019, which coincides with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, potentially affecting influenza reporting and healthcare system responses.

From 2021 onwards, the data indicates a fluctuating pattern in influenza cases, with significant peaks in March 2023 and December 2023, reaching over 3.7 million and 4.1 million cases, respectively. Despite these high case numbers, the death toll remained relatively low, suggesting improvements in healthcare responses or changes in the virus's virulence. The data for 2024 and early 2025 shows a decline in cases, with a notable peak in January 2025. Overall, the analysis highlights the dynamic nature of influenza epidemiology in China, influenced by various factors such as public health interventions, viral mutations, and possibly the impact of concurrent global health events like the COVID-19 pandemic.
