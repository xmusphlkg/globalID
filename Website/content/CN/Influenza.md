# Influenza

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It primarily infects the nose, throat, and sometimes the lungs, leading to symptoms such as fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, and fatigue. Influenza spreads easily through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It can cause mild to severe illness and sometimes lead to hospitalization or even death, especially in high-risk groups like the elderly, young children, and individuals with weakened immune systems. Annual vaccination is the most effective way to prevent influenza and its complications.

## Highlights

- **Recent Decline in Cases:** The number of influenza cases in the Chinese mainland has decreased significantly since a peak in early 2023, with cases dropping to 117,403 in September 2024. <br/>
  
- **Seasonal Variations:** Influenza cases show pronounced seasonal peaks, particularly in the winter months, with the most recent major peak occurring in December 2023. <br/>
  
- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite high case numbers in some months, the mortality rate remains relatively low, with only a few deaths each month in recent data. <br/>

- **Current Status:** The overall trend suggests a stabilization of cases post-winter peak, and current figures indicate a manageable influenza situation as of September 2024. <br/>

The influenza data from the Chinese mainland reveals a fluctuating pattern of cases over the years, with noticeable peaks and troughs. Between 2014 and 2019, influenza cases demonstrated a general upward trend, with significant spikes occurring in various parts of 2016 and 2019. A pronounced peak is observed in December 2019, with over a million reported cases. Deaths, while present, remained relatively low compared to the number of cases during these years. The seasonal nature of influenza, with higher case numbers typically witnessed in colder months, aligns with global observations of influenza epidemiology.

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020 appears to coincide with a marked reduction in influenza cases. This decline may be attributed to public health interventions such as lockdowns, social distancing, and increased hygiene measures, which also mitigated influenza transmission. However, an unusual resurgence occurs in 2022, notably in June and July, with cases again reaching into the hundreds of thousands after a lull in 2021. This rise could indicate changes in public health policies or the populationâ€™s immune landscape post-COVID-19 restrictions. The data for 2023 and 2024 points to yet another surge, reaching several million cases in some months, though the death rate remains low. This period of increased cases could be a result of changes in viral strains, waning immunity, or variations in surveillance and reporting mechanisms.

Initially, the number of influenza-related deaths was low, with sporadic single deaths reported from 2014 through 2015, despite moderate case numbers. Starting in 2016, there was a notable increase in both cases and deaths, peaking sharply during 2018-2019. This period marks an intense rise in cases, particularly in January 2019, when over 600,000 cases were reported with 143 deaths, indicating a higher mortality trend. This sharp rise persisted into early 2020 before cases dropped, likely influenced by the emergence of COVID-19, which may have led to public health interventions that impacted influenza transmission.

Interestingly, after the initial reduction in 2020, there were consistent fluctuations in case numbers, yet mortality remained relatively low. A significant surge re-emerged in 2023, with an astounding 3.7 million cases in March and 4.1 million in December, but deaths remained low relative to case numbers. This pattern, where cases surge dramatically but deaths remain controlled, may reflect improved medical interventions, influenza vaccination coverage, or other public health measures that reduced fatal outcomes despite high transmission levels.
