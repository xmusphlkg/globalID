# Influenza

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses, primarily types A and B. It spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, and fatigue, which can range from mild to severe and can occasionally lead to complications like pneumonia. Seasonal flu epidemics occur annually, and the virus can rapidly mutate, necessitating annual updates to flu vaccines for effective prevention. Ensuring vaccination, maintaining good hand hygiene, and practicing respiratory etiquette are key preventive measures against influenza infection.

## Highlights

- After a dramatic peak in March 2023 (over 3.7 million cases), influenza activity has declined steadily through April 2025, with 143,986 cases and zero deaths reported last month. <br/>
- Seasonal trends continue, with large winter surges observed in late 2023 and early 2024, consistent with typical influenza seasonality in China. <br/>
- Mortality remains relatively low despite large case volumes, suggesting improved healthcare response or lower virulence in recent strains. <br/>
- Compared to previous years, 2025 shows a later-than-usual decline in cases post-winter, indicating a more prolonged influenza season that warrants continued surveillance. <br/>

From early 2015 to 2017, Influenza cases in mainland China showed regular seasonal fluctuations, marked by winter peaks and lower summer incidence. A dramatic surge occurred starting in December 2017, climaxing with over 600,000 cases in January 2019 and a peak of nearly 1.2 million cases in December 2019. This abrupt rise—nearly a tenfold increase in winter caseloads compared to pre-2018 figures—suggests increased testing, improved surveillance, or the emergence of more transmissible strains. Following this, a sharp drop in early 2020 coincided with the emergence of COVID-19 and associated public health interventions, such as mask mandates and lockdowns, disrupting typical flu transmission patterns through most of 2020 and 2021.

Influenza activity rebounded in mid-2022 with notable peaks in June and July (747,038 and 648,465 cases respectively), which was atypical seasonally. The most substantial wave occurred in 2023, with unprecedented case counts in March (over 3.7 million), December (over 4.1 million), and continuing into January 2024 (nearly 3 million). This extreme rise could reflect both post-COVID population immunity gaps and viral shifts enhancing transmissibility. Despite high volumes, mortality remained low relative to the case spike—indicating either lower virulence, expanded access to treatment, or underreporting of deaths. Subsequently, while case numbers remained elevated throughout 2024 and early 2025 compared to pre-2020 levels, a downward trend was visible by April 2025.

The mortality pattern for Influenza in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 shows notable temporal and amplitude variations, often marked by pronounced seasonal spikes. Significant mortality peaks occurred during winter months, with extreme events in January and February 2019 (143 and 86 deaths), and again in early 2018 and 2020. These coincide with massive case surges, particularly in December 2019 through February 2020, suggesting a strong seasonal component compounded by potential strain virulence or vaccine mismatch. Mortality rates began to stabilize post-2020, despite case numbers remaining occasionally high, especially during the 2023 spring peak (over 3.7 million cases in March with only 38 deaths), reflecting possible improvements in healthcare response, treatment access, or population immunity.

Notably, mortality remained disproportionately low during major case surges from 2022 onward—particularly in March-April 2023 and early 2024—indicating a substantial shift in case fatality. The data post-2021 suggest that while detection or reporting of influenza cases dramatically increased, fatal outcomes did not trend upward equivalently. This divergence implies either changes in clinical severity of circulating strains or a broader public health impact from post-COVID surveillance, prevention measures, or vaccine strategies. Mortality thus transitioned from being a prominent epidemic signal to a more muted indicator despite large case numbers, emphasizing the evolving nature of epidemic impact assessment.
