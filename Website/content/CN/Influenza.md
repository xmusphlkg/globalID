# Influenza

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a highly contagious viral infection that affects the respiratory system. Caused by the influenza virus types A, B, C, and D, it primarily spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. Symptoms include fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, and fatigue, often resembling those of a common cold. Influenza can lead to severe complications, particularly in the elderly, young children, and individuals with weakened immune systems. Annual vaccination is the most effective way to prevent infection, as the virus evolves rapidly, requiring updates to the vaccine formulation.

## Highlights

- Case numbers peaked dramatically in March 2023, with 3,721,370 reported cases, indicating an outbreak during this period.  
<br/>
- There is a notable case reduction in late 2023 to early 2025, with numbers declining to around 143,986 cases by April 2025.  
<br/>
- Despite high case numbers in early 2025 (e.g., 2,870,849 cases in January), mortality rates remain low, with fewer than 15 deaths per month reported.  
<br/>
- Recent data shows an uptick in cases in October 2025 to 427,083, highlighting potential seasonal increases and the need for continued monitoring and preventive measures.  
<br/>

The data indicates significant fluctuations in influenza cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025, highlighting recurring seasonal peaks typically in winter months. This pattern aligns with the influenza virus's known behavior, influenced by factors like colder temperatures, indoor crowding, and possibly altered immune responses. A dramatic surge in cases is noted at the end of 2019 and early 2020, with numbers exceeding 1 million, coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. This parallel suggests that pandemic-related factors potentially heightened influenza transmission during this period.

In the latter part of 2020, influenza cases dropped significantly, possibly due to COVID-19 public health measures such as masking, improved hygiene, and reduced travel, which likely tempered influenza spread. A significant spike occurred again in March 2023, with cases reaching over 3.7 million, the highest recorded incidence, signifying a potential change in disease dynamics or response effectiveness.

Despite these case fluctuations, mortality associated with influenza remained relatively low throughout, suggesting effective clinical management, public health interventions, or shifts in virus virulence. As 2025 approaches, while case numbers oscillate, peaking during expected seasonal intervals, death counts stay minimal. This trend hints at enhancements in disease management, reflecting improved healthcare strategies or changes in influenza impact, warranting continued vigilance and adaptation of health policies.

The analysis of influenza-related deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals significant seasonal variations, with deaths typically peaking during the winter months, especially in January and February. For instance, notable surges occur in January 2019 and January 2025, recording 143 and 9 deaths, respectively. Such data underscores influenza's seasonality, guiding public health strategies like vaccination campaigns to alleviate winter impacts.

The death-to-case ratio remains relatively low over these years, even during periods of increased influenza activity. In March 2023, despite nearly 3.7 million cases, there were only 38 deaths, indicating a very low mortality rate. This aligns with the typical global pattern for seasonal influenza, characterized by high transmission but low mortality. Notably, significant case surges, as seen in 2024, do not proportionally increase death rates, suggesting effective interventions and medical care.

Although case numbers show dramatic fluctuations, death totals remain stable, emphasizing the success of ongoing influenza control in China. This consistency in mortality could reflect improvements in public health responses, medical care, or both, effectively limiting deaths during high transmission periods. In summary, while seasonal influenza continues to circulate widely, the data indicates robust management and control measures in place to prevent significant increases in mortality rates.
