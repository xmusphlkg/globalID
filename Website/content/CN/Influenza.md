# Influenza

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It primarily infects the nose, throat, and sometimes the lungs, leading to symptoms like fever, cough, sore throat, muscle aches, and fatigue. Influenza viruses are classified into types A, B, C, and D, with types A and B responsible for seasonal epidemics in humans. Transmission mainly occurs through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Influenza can cause mild to severe illness and may lead to complications, particularly in young children, elderly individuals, and those with underlying health conditions.

## Highlights

- Influenza cases in the Chinese mainland reached unprecedented levels in late 2025, with December recording over 6.7 million cases and 14 deaths, the highest monthly count in the past decade.<br/>
- A sharp increase is seen throughout 2025, particularly in Q4, suggesting an intense epidemic wave; this surge greatly surpasses previous seasonal peaks such as those observed in early 2019 and 2023.<br/>
- Despite record case numbers, the case fatality rate remains very low, indicating widespread but relatively mild disease presentation.<br/>
- Overall, surveillance indicates unusually strong influenza transmission in 2025, warranting heightened public health attention and reinforced control measures.<br/>

The surveillance data for influenza in the Chinese mainland shows pronounced seasonal and interannual fluctuations, with notable peaks typically occurring in winter and early spring months. From 2015 through early 2017, case counts were moderate, with gradual winter upticks and low fatality counts. Starting in December 2017, activity sharply escalated, with a dramatic surge during January and February 2018 and even more pronounced increases in 2019—peaking at nearly 1.2 million cases in December 2019 and again with close to one million cases in January 2020. This period marks a significant amplification likely influenced by heightened viral activity and possible improvements in detection/reporting.

From early 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic, influenza cases dropped markedly for several months, likely reflecting both behavioral interventions and healthcare system shifts. By 2022, and especially in 2023, case counts rebounded, reaching unprecedented levels—3721370 cases reported in March 2023 and over 4 million in both December 2023 and December 2025, with similar surges observed in the surrounding months. Despite enormous case burdens, influenza deaths remained proportionally low, never rising above 143 deaths in one month, and the case fatality rate appears to decline relative to earlier years. This pattern suggests expanded testing, improved clinical management, or modifications in reporting criteria, alongside a shifting epidemic dynamic potentially related to post-pandemic population immunity and changes in social contact patterns.

Influenza deaths in mainland China display marked seasonal and annual variation, with the majority of fatalities occurring during winter and early spring case surges. High-mortality periods, such as January 2019 (143 deaths) and January 2020 (108 deaths), coincided with unusually large case counts, contrasting with most other months where deaths stayed low despite substantial infections. In recent years like 2023 and 2025, unprecedented case numbers were recorded during peak months, yet deaths did not rise proportionally; for example, March 2023 saw over 3.7 million cases with only 38 deaths. This shift might reflect improved disease management or changing influenza strains.

Deaths are nearly absent in summer and early autumn months regardless of variations in case numbers, suggesting strong seasonality and possibly the influence of healthcare access or reporting practices. During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021), both reported cases and deaths declined sharply, likely due to widespread public health measures. Over the entire reporting period, influenza lethality fluctuated, with episodic spikes but overall low fatality rates relative to case counts; this pattern could result from enhanced clinical response, vaccination uptake, or differences in annual strain virulence. The data signals effective control outside peak seasons and points to evolving dynamics in influenza severity and surveillance in the Chinese mainland.
