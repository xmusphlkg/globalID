# Japanese encephalitis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral disease caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), a flavivirus transmitted predominantly by Culex mosquitoes. It is prevalent in rural and agricultural regions of Asia and the Western Pacific. Most JEV infections are asymptomatic, but a small percentage develop severe symptoms, including high fever, headache, neck stiffness, and seizures, potentially leading to swelling around the brain. There is no specific antiviral treatment, so prevention focuses on vaccination, mosquito control, and public health strategies. Vaccination is particularly recommended for travelers to affected regions and residents in endemic areas.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks**: Japanese encephalitis cases in China peak during summer months, especially July and August, due to its vector-borne nature. <br/>

- **Decline in Cases**: There has been a notable decline in cases since 2019 compared to the mid-2010s, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends**: Deaths generally follow case trends, with occasional spikes. As of October 2024, fatalities persist, highlighting ongoing risks. <br/>

- **Current Situation (October 2024)**: October 2024 reported 21 cases and 2 deaths. While significantly lower than previous peaks, this underscores the need for sustained preventive efforts.

The data on Japanese encephalitis cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals clear seasonal spikes, primarily in the summer months of July, August, and September. These peak periods align with heightened mosquito activity, the primary vector for this virus. Notably, August has consistently recorded the highest number of cases, such as in 2018 with 904 reported cases, reflecting the environmental suitability for vector propagation during this time.

Over the decade, there is an observable trend of declining cases and fatalities, particularly from 2020 onwards. This decline may suggest the impact of enhanced public health measures, including vaccination efforts, improved mosquito control, and health education campaigns. Despite this downtrend, some years show significant surges, indicating periodic challenges in controlling transmission. For instance, a resurgence in September 2024, with 71 cases, underscores the need for vigilance.

Although the mortality rate per case appears to fluctuate, a general decrease in death counts correlated with high case numbers, especially in later years, suggests improvements in clinical management and treatment strategies. These observations highlight the importance of continued monitoring, preventive strategies during high-risk periods, and sustained healthcare improvements to further reduce the impact of Japanese encephalitis in the region.

August 2018 was a particularly severe month with 55 deaths, aligning with high case numbers. This pattern suggests environmental factors, like optimal conditions for mosquito breeding, are influential, given mosquitoes are the virus vectors. 

Annual fluctuations depict peaks and declines, pointing to varying effectiveness in public health responses. While there has been a general decline in mortality since the 2018 peak, sporadic spikes like in April 2019 and other isolated months highlight ongoing challenges. These could result from localized outbreaks or lapses in effective control measures. Indeed, while overall deaths have decreased, the occurrence of high fatalities in certain months and years illustrates that efforts in mosquito control and healthcare access remain crucial. 

The data implies that continuous regional monitoring and intervention are necessary to manage the disease effectively and prevent resurgence. Focused public health strategies should target high-incidence times and areas to ensure preparedness and swift response. Enhancing control measures can help mitigate the seasonal impact and reduce the number of fatalities from the disease, underscoring the importance of timely intervention and sustained vector management efforts.
