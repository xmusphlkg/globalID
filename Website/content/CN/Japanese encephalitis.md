# Japanese encephalitis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), which primarily affects the central nervous system. The disease is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected Culex mosquitoes, commonly found in rural and agricultural areas of Asia and the Western Pacific. Symptoms range from mild fever and headache to severe neurological impairments, including encephalitis, seizures, and paralysis. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure, and control efforts focus on reducing mosquito breeding sites and using insect repellent. Early diagnosis and supportive care are crucial for severe cases.

## Highlights

- Significant seasonal variation exists, with peaks in cases and deaths occurring mainly from June to September each year.<br/>
- The highest monthly cases were reported in August 2018 (904 cases), indicating occasional severe outbreaks.<br/>
- There has been a general decline in cases and deaths from 2020 onwards, with sporadic small outbreaks still observed.<br/>
- The current situation in May 2024 reveals low activity, with only 3 cases and no deaths, suggesting effective control measures.<br/>

The data on Japanese encephalitis in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals distinct seasonal and yearly fluctuations. Cases peak between July and September each year, likely due to climatic factors conducive to mosquito breeding, the vector for the disease. For instance, August 2018 saw 904 cases, correlating with 55 deaths, while August 2017 had 580 cases and 36 deaths. In contrast, the winter and early spring months, such as February 2018 and January/February 2021, recorded minimal to no cases, aligning with reduced mosquito activity.

Year-to-year trends show significant fluctuations. For example, 2018 had a substantial increase in cases and deaths compared to previous years, while 2020-2021 saw a marked decline, potentially due to improved public health interventions or natural variability. Notably, discrepancies between case numbers and fatalities are present, such as March 2019 with 8 deaths but only 1 reported case, suggesting possible data reporting issues. From 2022 onward, there is a general decline in both cases and deaths, which might indicate effective control measures.

Overall, the cyclical nature of Japanese encephalitis, combined with annual variances, highlights the importance of continuous surveillance and adaptive public health strategies to manage and mitigate the disease’s impact.

Analyzing the reported data for Japanese encephalitis in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 illustrates seasonal trends and yearly fluctuations in both case counts and mortality rates. Peaks in cases and deaths occur predominantly during the warmer months of July through September, consistent with the vector-borne nature of the disease. For instance, cases spiked in July 2014 (251 cases, 11 deaths) and August 2018 (904 cases, 55 deaths). This pattern underscores the need for enhanced vector control and public health interventions during these periods.

The data reveals significant yearly variation in fatalities, with notable high death counts in months with lower case numbers, such as February 2019 (6 deaths) and March 2019 (8 deaths). This variation may reflect differences in healthcare accessibility, changes in virulence, or reporting artefacts. From 2020 onwards, there is a visible decrease in both cases and deaths, particularly in 2021 and 2022, suggesting potential improvements in public health measures or environmental conditions reducing mosquito populations. However, a slight uptick is noted again in mid to late 2023, indicating the need for sustained vigilance and adaptive strategies.

Overall, the data emphasizes the importance of targeted seasonal interventions, continuous monitoring, and flexible public health responses to mitigate Japanese encephalitis in mainland China.
