# Japanese encephalitis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral brain infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted through mosquito bites, especially by the Culex species. It is prevalent in rural and agricultural regions of Asia and the Western Pacific. Pigs and wading birds act as amplifying hosts. Most infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can lead to high fever, headache, seizures, and inflammation of the brain, resulting in neurological damage or death. Vaccination serves as the primary prevention strategy, alongside mosquito control measures. Travelers to endemic areas are advised to consider vaccination.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal trend**: Japanese encephalitis cases peak during summer, with August and September showing the highest incidence across all years, indicating a strong seasonal pattern. <br/>
- **Decline in case severity**: While the number of cases fluctuated annually, recent years show a decline in fatality rates compared to earlier periods, suggesting improved disease management. <br/>
- **Recent stabilization**: In 2024, case numbers remain low through most months, with 71 cases reported in September, showing a moderate increase but no deaths, highlighting stabilization in the disease situation. <br/>
- **Significant outbreaks in previous years**: August 2018 saw the highest recorded case count (904), with a notable outbreak contributing to 55 deaths that year. <br/>

The data for Japanese encephalitis (JE) in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 displays noticeable seasonal and yearly fluctuations in both cases and deaths, typically peaking in the summer and early autumn months. This trend aligns with the epidemiology of mosquito-borne diseases, where transmission is most prevalent during warmer months due to increased mosquito activity. For instance, between 2014 and 2018, peaks were observed notably each August, with the highest case count reaching 904 in August 2018, accompanied by 55 deaths. In contrast, the cooler months generally show significantly reduced transmission, often recording minimal to zero cases.

Examining the more recent data from 2019 to 2024, there is a gradual decrease in annual peaks and overall cases. For example, the highest number in August 2022 was 57 cases, and in August 2023, it was 80 cases, both significantly lower than in previous years. Similarly, deaths appear to decrease, with many months reporting zero fatalities. These declining trends may suggest the impact of successful public health interventions such as improved vaccination coverage, vector control efforts, and heightened public awareness. However, despite decreased cases and deaths, occasional spikes, like those observed in July and September 2023, underscore an ongoing risk and the need for sustained vigilance and adaptive strategies in managing JE.

The analysis of the deaths associated with Japanese encephalitis in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals key trends. The data reveal seasonal peaks predominantly in the summer months, with corresponding increases in deaths. Notably, August consistently shows a higher incidence of both cases and deaths over several years. The summer peaks are characteristic of Japanese encephalitis, a mosquito-borne disease influenced by climatic conditions that favor mosquito proliferation.

Closer examination reveals significant variability in monthly case-fatality ratios. For instance, while August sees the highest case numbers, deaths also spike, reflecting both the increased exposure and potential disease severity during this period. Interestingly, the data indicates occasional spikes in deaths disproportionate to case numbers, such as in March 2019, which suggests possible reporting anomalies or delayed outcomes manifesting in certain months. The overall long-term trend indicates a general decline in both cases and deaths from 2017 onwards, potentially reflecting improved public health interventions, increased vaccination coverage, or better healthcare access.
