# Japanese encephalitis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis (JE) is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted through the bite of infected mosquitoes, particularly Culex species. It is endemic in many parts of Asia and the Western Pacific. Symptoms can range from mild flu-like signs to severe neurological complications, including seizures and coma. The disease can lead to long-term disabilities or death, especially in children. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure, particularly for those living in or traveling to high-risk areas. Public health efforts focus on mosquito control and awareness to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Japanese encephalitis in mainland China peaks in summer, especially July and August, consistently across years. <br/>

- **Decline in Cases:** A significant decline in cases is observed from 2018 onwards, with notable reductions in 2021 and 2022, indicating effective control measures or vaccination. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** Despite fewer cases, mortality remains low, with sporadic deaths. The highest mortality was in 2018. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of January 2025, the incidence is low, with only one case and no deaths, reflecting successful ongoing control efforts. <br/>

The data on Japanese encephalitis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals distinct seasonal and annual trends. Notably, the incidence of cases peaks during the summer months, particularly from July to September, which aligns with the mosquito breeding season, as the disease is primarily transmitted through mosquito bites. For instance, in 2018, the highest number of cases was recorded in August with 904 cases, followed by September with 528 cases. This pattern is consistent across the years, indicating a strong seasonal influence on the transmission of the disease.

Over the years, there is a noticeable fluctuation in the number of cases and deaths. The year 2018 stands out with a significant spike in both cases and deaths, with August and September showing the highest numbers. In contrast, the subsequent years show a decline in both cases and fatalities, suggesting possible improvements in public health interventions, vaccination coverage, or changes in environmental factors affecting mosquito populations. The data from 2021 onwards shows a marked decrease in cases, with 2021 and 2022 having notably lower numbers compared to previous years, which could be attributed to enhanced control measures or natural fluctuations in disease dynamics. Overall, the data underscores the importance of targeted interventions during peak transmission months to effectively manage and reduce the impact of Japanese encephalitis in the region.

The analysis of Japanese encephalitis (JE) mortality data in mainland China reveals significant fluctuations in both cases and deaths over the years. In 2015, the disease exhibited a concerning trend, particularly in the summer months, with a peak of 259 cases in August and a total of 6 deaths that month. This pattern of seasonal outbreaks continued into 2016, where the highest mortality was recorded in September with 15 deaths, coinciding with a peak of 459 cases. The data suggests that the incidence of JE is closely tied to environmental factors, such as the rainy season, which likely influences mosquito breeding and, consequently, transmission rates.

As the years progressed, a notable decline in both cases and deaths was observed, particularly from 2021 onwards. The year 2022 showed a marked decrease in reported cases, with only 1 death in November despite some months reporting cases. This decline could indicate improved public health interventions, such as vaccination campaigns and vector control measures, which may have effectively reduced the incidence of JE. However, the sporadic nature of deaths, even in years with low case numbers, underscores the need for continued vigilance and monitoring to prevent potential outbreaks and ensure timely medical responses.
