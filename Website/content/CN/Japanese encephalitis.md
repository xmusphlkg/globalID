# Japanese encephalitis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. It is the leading cause of viral encephalitis in Asia, affecting both humans and animals, with pigs and wading birds acting as amplifying hosts. Most human infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can lead to high fevers, headaches, seizures, and neurological complications. The disease predominantly occurs in rural agricultural areas, especially in regions prone to mosquito breeding. Vaccination is the most effective prevention strategy, alongside mosquito control measures, to mitigate the risk of this potentially life-threatening illness.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal spikes:** Japanese encephalitis cases peak between July and September, with August 2018 seeing the highest at 904 cases and 55 deaths.  
<br/>
- **Decline in cases and fatalities:** A steady decline is evident, with August 2024 reporting only 16 cases and no deaths, indicating better control measures.  
<br/>
- **Fluctuating mortality:** Mortality rates have varied, with August 2017 seeing 36 deaths, but recent years have shown fewer fatalities, reflecting improved healthcare.  
<br/>
- **Minimal activity in winter/spring:** Case counts remain low from January to April, consistent with the seasonal nature of the disease.

The analysis of Japanese encephalitis (JE) cases in mainland China from 2014 to August 2024 reveals several key trends. Peak transmission occurs consistently between July and September, with August typically seeing the highest number of cases each year. For instance, in 2017 and 2018, the number of cases surged to 580 and 904, respectively, with corresponding spikes in mortality during the same months. This seasonal pattern aligns with the known transmission cycle of JE, which peaks during the summer and early fall due to the increased activity of Culex mosquitoes, the primary vector of the virus.

Despite fluctuations in case numbers, the data shows a general decline in both cases and deaths after 2018, particularly from 2020 onwards. For example, in 2020, the number of cases dropped significantly compared to previous years, with August recording only 69 cases and two deaths. A notable reduction in cases is also observed in 2021 and 2022, where the number of monthly cases rarely exceeded 100, suggesting potential improvements in disease control or vaccination coverage. However, sporadic increases, such as in August 2023, indicate that the disease remains a significant public health concern, requiring continued surveillance and preventive measures.

This seasonal surge is most pronounced in years such as 2017 and 2018, with August 2018 recording the highest case count of 904, accompanied by 55 deaths. This trend suggests a strong correlation between higher temperatures and increased transmission risk. The data also shows a sharp rise in deaths during high transmission periods, with significant spikes in fatality rates observed in 2017 and 2018, especially during the months of August and September.

Although cases are generally higher in the summer, the fatality rate varies by year and season. For instance, the death count remained relatively low in some peak transmission months, such as in July 2016, which had 140 cases and no reported deaths. However, 2019 saw an unusual spike in deaths during non-peak months like March and April, where the case count was low but the fatality rate was disproportionately high, signaling potential concerns about disease management or health system capacity during off-peak periods. Overall, the trends point to the importance of targeted prevention efforts during the summer months and a need for further investigation into outlier months where deaths spiked unexpectedly despite fewer cases.
