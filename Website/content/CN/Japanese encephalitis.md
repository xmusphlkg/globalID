# Japanese encephalitis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Japanese Encephalitis (JE) is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. It is endemic in parts of Asia and the Western Pacific, with rural and agricultural regions being high-risk zones. The virus primarily infects pigs and wading birds, serving as reservoirs, while humans are incidental hosts. Most human infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can lead to encephalitis, characterized by rapid onset of high fever, headache, neck stiffness, disorientation, coma, seizures, and potential long-term neurological complications. Vaccination and mosquito control are crucial for preventing JE.

## Highlights

- Japanese encephalitis cases show significant seasonal variation, with peaks typically observed in the months of August and September, indicating potential links to climate or mosquito activity. <br/>

- From 2015 to 2018, the reported cases were generally high, with a peak in August 2018 at 904 cases. Subsequent years show a declining trend in reported cases, suggesting improvements in public health interventions. <br/>

- Deaths related to Japanese encephalitis are considerably lower and have also experienced a downward trend, indicative of better treatment strategies or increased awareness. <br/>

- As of September 2025, cases have increased compared to previous months, signaling a potential resurgence that may require heightened surveillance and preventive measures. <br/>

The epidemiological analysis of Japanese encephalitis (JE) in mainland China highlights recurring seasonal patterns and annual fluctuations in cases and mortality. Peaks in JE cases consistently occur during late summer months, particularly from July to September, suggesting a strong association with climatic conditions and mosquito activity. For example, 2016 saw a notable spike with 459 cases in September, and similar peaks in subsequent years, most notably in August 2018 with 904 cases, indicating environmental influences on transmission.

Annual fluctuations are evident, with volatility seen in monthly case numbers; despite low overall mortality, occasional spikes exist, like April 2019, which recorded 26 deaths with just 6 cases. Such anomalies may arise from unique environmental conditions, reporting discrepancies, or variations in vector populations such as Culex mosquitoes. Despite decreased cases and fatalities post-2021, persistent occurrences merit continuous public health vigilance. Factors contributing to reduced incidence could involve improved vector control, vaccination, or environmental changes.

Recent data signals improvements in JE control, yet ongoing risks during peak transmission months indicate a need for sustained surveillance, timely interventions, and robust vaccination strategies. This analysis underscores the impact of environmental and socio-economic dynamics on JE transmission and highlights the importance of strategic disease management efforts, including monitoring mosquito populations and climatic shifts.
