# Japanese encephalitis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral infection spread by mosquitoes, primarily affecting rural and agricultural areas in Asia. Caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), it is most prevalent during the rainy season when mosquito populations are highest. The disease can cause inflammation of the brain, leading to symptoms such as fever, headache, vomiting, and, in severe cases, seizures, coma, and neurological damage. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, as there is no specific antiviral treatment. Japanese encephalitis poses a significant public health threat, especially in endemic regions.

## Highlights

- Japanese encephalitis cases in the Chinese mainland show a strong seasonal pattern, with peaks typically occurring between July and September. <br/>
- The highest number of cases was recorded in August 2018, with 904 cases and 55 deaths, indicating a severe outbreak that year. <br/>
- There has been a general decline in the number of cases and deaths since 2018, with only sporadic cases reported in recent years. <br/>
- As of June 2024, the number of cases remains low, with only 5 reported cases and no deaths, reflecting effective control and prevention measures. <br/>

Analysis of the reported data for Japanese encephalitis in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a clear seasonality in case numbers, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months of July and August. For instance, August 2018 reported the highest number of cases (904) and deaths (55) within the period, reflecting a significant seasonal outbreak. This seasonal trend is consistent across the years, indicating higher transmission rates during warmer months, which are conducive to the breeding of the primary vector, Culex mosquitoes.

The data also shows fluctuations in the mortality rates, with some years like 2018 and 2017 exhibiting higher death counts (55 in August 2018 and 36 in August 2017), while others like 2019 and 2020 had relatively fewer deaths despite notable case counts. This variation could be attributed to differences in healthcare responses, public health interventions, and possibly variations in virus virulence or strain differences year to year. Notably, in recent years, there seems to be a decrease in both cases and deaths, suggesting potential improvements in control measures, vaccination coverage, and public health awareness.

This trend underscores the impact of seasonal epidemics on mortality rates. Furthermore, the data indicates occasional anomalies, like March 2019, with only one case but eight deaths, suggesting potential discrepancies in reporting or extraordinary clinical circumstances.

From 2020 onwards, there is a noticeable decline in both cases and deaths, reflecting potential improvements in public health interventions, vaccination coverage, or environmental factors influencing the vector population.
