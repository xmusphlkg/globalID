# Japanese encephalitis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis (JE) is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted by Culex species mosquitoes. It is prevalent in rural and agricultural regions of Asia and the Western Pacific. The virus cycles between mosquitoes, pigs, and wading birds, occasionally infecting humans. Most human infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can cause inflammation of the brain, leading to high fever, seizures, and neurological complications. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but JE is preventable through vaccination, mosquito control, and protective measures. The disease has a case fatality rate of 20–30% among severe cases.

## Highlights

- Japanese encephalitis cases have significantly declined since the peak years of 2017–2018, with high August case counts (up to 904), indicating strong seasonal transmission. <br/>
- From 2022 to April 2025, annual cases remained low with no more than 80 monthly cases, and deaths remained under control, suggesting heightened surveillance and control efforts. <br/>
- A consistent seasonal pattern persists, with cases increasing in summer and early autumn (July–September), though with lower magnitude in recent years. <br/>
- As of April 2025, there are zero reported cases or deaths, indicating a sustained low-incidence period and possible benefit from long-term vaccination and mosquito control strategies. <br/>

Between 2015 and 2018, Japanese encephalitis (JE) in mainland China displayed strong seasonal surges, consistently peaking in August and September. The highest recorded monthly case count was in August 2018, with 904 cases and 55 associated deaths, which corresponded with a broader trend of elevated incidence and mortality in that year. This period's high summer-to-autumn transmission likely reflects the mosquito-borne nature of JE, influenced by temperature, precipitation, and vector density. From 2015 to 2018, the case fatality rate (CFR) remained volatile, with notably high mortality in some low-incidence months (e.g., April 2019, where only 6 cases led to 26 deaths, suggesting possible reporting artifacts or case misclassification).

From 2019 onward, both incidence and mortality declined markedly, with a particularly sharp drop starting in 2020 and sustained through 2024. Post-2019 patterns display not only substantially fewer cases but also lower CFRs, even during former peak months. This dramatic shift may reflect expanded vaccination coverage, improved vector control, or reporting changes possibly influenced by reallocation of public health resources during the COVID-19 pandemic. Notably, since 2022, seasonal peaks persist but with very low magnitude and minimal deaths, suggesting a decoupling of seasonal vector presence and clinical case presentation, possibly due to highly localized outbreaks and infection in residual vulnerable populations.

An analysis of Japanese encephalitis–related deaths in mainland China from April 2015 through April 2025 reveals a clear seasonal concentration and a notable peak in mortality between 2016 and 2019. The highest death tolls occurred during the summer and early autumn months, particularly from August to October, aligning with the traditional transmission season. The years 2017 and 2018 marked significant spikes, with August 2017 reporting 36 deaths and August 2018 peaking even higher at 55 deaths. September in both years followed with high fatalities, showing clusters consistent with heightened case counts, although not always proportionate to them—suggesting potential shifts in disease severity, diagnostic patterns, or healthcare response.

Beginning in 2020, a marked and sustained decline in annual deaths is observed, with exceptional drops in peak-season fatalities. This shift is especially pronounced from 2021 onward, where annual deaths did not exceed two in any single month and hovered close to zero in several consecutive months. While incidence also declined in these years, the mortality rate decreased more steeply, implying an improvement in case outcomes. Factors potentially contributing to this trend may include wider vaccination coverage, improved early detection and management, or public health interventions that curtailed mosquito exposure. Yet, outliers remain—such as April 2019 with an anomaly of 26 deaths from just 6 reported cases, which suggests possible reporting discrepancies or retrospective updates.
