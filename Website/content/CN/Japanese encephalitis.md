# Japanese encephalitis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis (JE) is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), a flavivirus related to dengue, yellow fever, and West Nile viruses. It primarily affects rural and agricultural areas in Asia and parts of the Western Pacific. The virus spreads through the bite of infected Culex mosquitoes, particularly Culex tritaeniorhynchus. Most infected individuals experience mild or no symptoms, but severe cases can lead to encephalitis, resulting in headache, high fever, disorientation, coma, seizures, and death. Children are especially susceptible. Prevention includes mosquito control measures and vaccination, with no specific antiviral treatment available.

## Highlights

1. **Seasonal Trends**: Japanese encephalitis cases peak during the summer months, with August historically seeing the highest incidence, while winter months observe significantly lower cases. <br/>
2. **Decreasing Incidence**: Since 2018, there is a notable decline in the number of cases yearly, indicating better control measures and public health interventions. Recent months in 2025 reflect minimal cases. <br/>
3. **Mortality Trends**: Deaths attributed to Japanese encephalitis have gradually decreased, suggesting improved medical care and possibly enhanced vaccination efforts. Most recent data show no deaths. <br/>
4. **Current Situation**: As of July 2025, Japanese encephalitis incidence is almost negligible, indicating effective disease management and prevention strategies. <br/>

The data for Japanese encephalitis (JE) in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 provides a comprehensive overview of the temporal distribution and severity of cases. It reveals that JE is predominantly a summer disease, with marked peaks occurring consistently in the months of August and September across the analyzed years. For instance, in 2018, there was a notable peak of 904 cases in August, the highest in the dataset, followed by a gradual decline as the colder months approached. However, there is a discernible downward trend in total cases over the years, evidenced by fewer cases during the peak months in the most recent years like 2024 and 2025 compared to earlier years.

The analysis also sheds light on the fatalities associated with these cases. The data indicates that while the number of cases varied significantly, mortality showed less variability. In many peak months, such as September 2018 and August 2017, the case-fatality ratios were relatively high, demonstrating the potential severity of the disease. However, in the following years, particularly from 2021 onwards, the number of deaths significantly decreased even during peak case months, suggesting improvements in either treatment protocols, vaccination coverage, or public health interventions. This is evident with zero deaths reported in months with high cases, such as September 2024. Overall, the data reflect both seasonal patterns and longitudinal trends indicating progress in managing the disease over time.
