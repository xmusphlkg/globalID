# Japanese encephalitis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral disease caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted to humans through the bite of an infected Culex mosquito. Predominantly found in rural and agricultural areas of Asia and the Western Pacific, the disease is a major cause of viral encephalitis in these regions. Most JEV infections are asymptomatic, but a small percentage can cause severe symptoms, including fever, headache, seizures, and inflammation of the brain, leading to potentially fatal outcomes. There is no specific treatment for Japanese encephalitis, but vaccination effectively prevents the disease, reducing human cases substantially.

## Highlights

1. **Seasonal peaks**: Japanese encephalitis cases in China show consistent summer peaks, especially from June to September. In 2024, September recorded 71 cases. <br/>
2. **Decreasing mortality**: Mortality has declined in recent years. For example, November 2024 reported 13 cases with no deaths. <br/>
3. **Annual fluctuations**: Cases fluctuate annually, with significant spikes in 2015, 2017, and 2018. Numbers have stabilized in 2023 and 2024. <br/>
4. **Rural impact**: While specific regions are not detailed, outbreaks typically occur in rural areas where mosquito vectors are more common. <br/>

The data on Japanese encephalitis (JE) in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows fluctuating case numbers, with peaks typically occurring during the summer months (June to August), when mosquito activity is highest. Notable outbreaks occurred in 2015, 2016, and 2018, with cases reaching up to 904 in August 2018 and 580 in August 2017. These spikes are often followed by a sharp decline in cases during cooler months, suggesting a strong seasonal pattern influenced by environmental factors.

Mortality data reveals a variable but relatively low case-fatality rate overall. In years with large outbreaks, such as 2017, deaths were more pronounced, with 36 fatalities in August 2017 and 55 in August 2018. However, in years like 2021 and 2022, the number of cases and deaths was much lower, indicating that while JE remains a recurrent threat, the impact is not uniform. The data suggests that public health measures, such as vaccination and mosquito control, may help reduce the severity of outbreaks, as mortality rates do not directly correlate with the number of cases. Despite significant fluctuations, JE remains a serious concern, especially during peak transmission periods.

The data for Japanese encephalitis (JE) in China from 2014 to 2024 shows distinct seasonal patterns in both cases and deaths, with the highest incidence and mortality occurring during the late summer and early autumn months, particularly from July to September. The most significant spikes in both cases and fatalities occurred in 2017 and 2018, with 904 cases and 55 deaths reported in August 2018 alone. September 2017 also saw a notable surge, with 361 cases and 32 deaths, highlighting a seasonal peak likely linked to increased vector activity.

Case fatality rates (CFR) fluctuated over the years but were particularly high during these seasonal peaks. In 2017 and 2018, the CFR reached as high as 6%, reflecting the severity of outbreaks during these months. In contrast, years like 2021 and 2022 saw lower numbers of both cases and deaths, suggesting potential improvements in control measures or a natural decline in transmission.

Despite fluctuations, the data highlights the persistent risk of fatal outcomes during peak transmission months. The trends emphasize the importance of continued vector control and vaccination efforts to mitigate the disease burden during these high-risk periods.
