# Japanese encephalitis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. It is prevalent in rural and agricultural areas of Asia and the Western Pacific. The disease mainly affects children and can lead to severe neurological complications, including encephalitis, which is inflammation of the brain. Symptoms range from mild fever and headache to severe cases involving high fever, neck stiffness, seizures, and coma. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but vaccination is effective in preventing the disease. Public health measures focus on mosquito control and vaccination programs.

## Highlights



- **Seasonal Trends**: Japanese encephalitis cases peak in summer, especially in July and August, with a decline in winter months. This pattern is consistent annually. <br/>

- **Yearly Fluctuations**: The highest cases were in August 2018, with 904 cases. Recent years show a decline in cases and deaths, indicating improved control measures. <br/>

- **Mortality Rates**: Despite fewer cases, mortality spikes during peak months, notably in August 2018 and September 2016, remain a concern. <br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of February 2025, no new cases or deaths have been reported, suggesting effective disease management and prevention strategies. <br/>

The data on Japanese encephalitis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a distinct seasonal pattern, with the majority of cases occurring between June and October each year. This pattern aligns with the known epidemiology of Japanese encephalitis, which is transmitted by mosquitoes that are more active during the warmer months. The peak in cases typically occurs in August, with a notable spike in 2018 when 904 cases were reported. This seasonal trend suggests that public health interventions, such as vaccination campaigns and mosquito control efforts, should be intensified during these months to effectively reduce transmission.

Over the years, there is a noticeable fluctuation in the number of cases and deaths. For instance, the year 2018 saw a significant increase in both cases and deaths, with 904 cases and 55 deaths in August alone. In contrast, the subsequent years show a general decline in both metrics, with 2021 and 2022 reporting significantly fewer cases and deaths. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased vaccination coverage, or changes in environmental factors affecting mosquito populations. However, the data also indicates occasional spikes, such as in 2023, which underscores the need for continuous monitoring and adaptive public health strategies to address potential outbreaks.

The data on Japanese encephalitis in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends in the incidence and mortality of the disease. Notably, the number of cases and deaths fluctuates significantly across the years, with a pronounced seasonal pattern. The peak incidence typically occurs in the summer months, particularly from July to September, which aligns with the mosquito breeding season, as mosquitoes are the primary vectors for the virus. For instance, in August 2018, there were 904 cases and 55 deaths, marking one of the highest peaks in the dataset. This seasonal trend underscores the importance of vector control and public health interventions during these months to mitigate the spread of the disease.

In terms of mortality, the data shows variability in the case fatality rate over the years. While some months report a high number of cases with relatively few deaths, indicating effective clinical management or less virulent strains, other months show a higher proportion of deaths relative to cases. For example, in April 2019, there were only 6 cases but 26 deaths, suggesting possible reporting anomalies or a particularly severe outbreak. Overall, the data highlights the need for continuous surveillance and targeted public health strategies to address the seasonal and annual fluctuations in Japanese encephalitis cases and deaths in China.
