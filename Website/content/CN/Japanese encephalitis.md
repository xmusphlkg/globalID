# Japanese encephalitis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis (JE) is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus, primarily transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. It is prevalent in rural and agricultural regions of Asia and the Western Pacific. Pigs and wading birds serve as important amplifying hosts. Most infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can result in encephalitis, characterized by fever, headache, seizures, and neurological damage. There is no specific treatment for JE; preventive measures include vaccination and mosquito control. Japanese encephalitis remains a significant public health concern in endemic areas.

## Highlights

- Japanese encephalitis cases in Chinese mainland have sharply decreased since 2018, with the highest annual incidence recorded in 2018 (peak in August: 904 cases, 55 deaths), and significant reduction thereafter.<br/>
- Since 2021, annual case counts remain low (mostly under 100), with minimal deaths, indicating successful disease control efforts and possibly strengthened vaccination coverage.<br/>
- Seasonality persists, with most cases reported between July and October each year, although outbreaks are much less severe than previous years.<br/>
- By December 2025, Japanese encephalitis maintains a low endemic level (only 1 case and 0 deaths reported), reflecting effective ongoing public health interventions.<br/>

Japanese encephalitis in the Chinese mainland demonstrates marked seasonality, with the majority of cases occurring between July and October each year. The case numbers typically surge in late summer, peaking in August or September—2018 is especially notable, with 904 cases in August and 528 in September—and then declining sharply in winter and early spring months. There is also inter-annual variability in intensity; while 2017 and 2018 show pronounced epidemics, subsequent years—particularly after 2020—see a downward trend both in total cases and deaths, possibly reflecting improved vaccine coverage, vector control, or changes in surveillance and reporting practices.

While deaths tend to follow the curve of reported cases, their proportionality varies. For instance, the case fatality ratio was significantly higher in sporadic months with few cases (such as April 2019, with 6 cases and 26 deaths recorded, which may indicate misclassification or reporting delays), but generally, mortality rises with caseload during epidemic months—August and September are consistently the most lethal periods. Since 2021, both cases and deaths have diminished sharply, with most deaths presenting in isolated months and none of the large fatality clusters seen previously, which could be linked to transitions in public health policy, novel interventions, or demographic shifts in vulnerable populations. The consistent seasonal clustering indicates ongoing transmission cycles aligned with vector ecology, highlighting areas for continued vigilance and adaptive public health strategy.

Analysis of Japanese encephalitis deaths in mainland China from late 2015 to 2025 shows peaks aligning with summer–autumn case surges, especially between 2017 and 2019 when monthly deaths reached as high as 55. During this interval, August and September accounted for most fatalities, reflecting seasonality driven by vector activity. Despite high case numbers, some months with modest incidence (e.g., April 2019: 6 cases, 26 deaths) show anomalously elevated deaths, pointing to possible reporting delays, misclassification, or missed mild cases, which complicate interpretation of the true case-fatality ratio.

From 2020 onward, both cases and deaths declined, with most months reporting single-digit fatalities even during peak transmission periods. This reduction may indicate improved vaccination, clinical care, or public health interventions, but can also reflect COVID-19 pandemic impacts such as altered medical-seeking behaviour or disruptions in surveillance. Sporadic deaths continue to be reported each year regardless of overall case trends, suggesting ongoing risk for severe outcomes in susceptible populations. While seasonal patterns persist, the data points to progress in Japanese encephalitis control, yet also highlights the necessity of vigilance in surveillance and healthcare response to sustain these gains and further reduce mortality.
