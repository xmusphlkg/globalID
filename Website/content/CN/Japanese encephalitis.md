# Japanese encephalitis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis (JE) is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), a flavivirus related to dengue and West Nile viruses. It is transmitted primarily by Culex mosquitoes, with pigs and wading birds serving as amplifying hosts. JE is the leading cause of viral encephalitis in Asia, with most cases occurring in rural or agricultural areas. Although most infections are asymptomatic, severe cases can cause high fever, seizures, and neurological complications. No specific treatment exists; prevention relies on vaccination, mosquito control, and avoidance of bites. The disease has a high fatality and disability rate.

## Highlights

- Japanese encephalitis in the Chinese mainland has shown a significant decline since its peak years (2017â€“2018), with only 1 case and no deaths reported in May 2025. <br/>
- Seasonality remains evident, with most cases consistently occurring between June and October, emphasizing the importance of mid-year preventive measures. <br/>
- From 2022 to 2025, annual case counts have remained low with minimal fatalities, indicating sustained control and possible vaccination effectiveness. <br/>
- Compared to the high-burden years (e.g., 2018 with 904 cases in August), the current declining trend suggests continued epidemiological stability and low transmission risk. <br/>

Fatality patterns vary more irregularly. Some high-death periods occurred despite relatively few cases, such as April 2019 (6 cases, 26 deaths) and early 2019 overallâ€”a likely reporting anomaly or indicative of severe underreporting of cases. In contrast, years with high incidence like 2017 and 2018 had proportionally lower case fatality rates, possibly reflecting improved diagnosis and care quality. After 2020, fatalities became sparse and more evenly distributed, with few exceptions.

Overall, the data suggest annual seasonality with a notable decline in burden post-2018. This may reflect expanded vaccination, effective mosquito control, improved clinical management, or external factors such as reduced exposure during COVID-19. However, sporadic spikes in deaths during low-incidence months highlight gaps or inconsistencies in surveillance or classification that warrant continued attention.
