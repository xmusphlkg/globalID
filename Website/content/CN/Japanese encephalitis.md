# Japanese encephalitis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily affecting the central nervous system. It is transmitted through the bite of infected Culex mosquitoes, prevalent in rural and agricultural regions of Asia and the western Pacific. While most JEV infections are asymptomatic, a small percentage can progress to severe neurological disease, leading to high morbidity and mortality rates. Symptoms include fever, headache, seizures, and neurological deficits. There is no specific antiviral treatment, but preventive measures include vaccination, mosquito control efforts, and protective clothing to minimize exposure risk.

## Highlights

- There has been a marked decline in Japanese encephalitis cases in Chinese mainland since 2018, showing effective control measures or vaccination programs. <br/>

- Seasonal peaks continue to appear, with the highest case numbers consistently occurring from July to October, suggesting this period remains critical for surveillance and prevention efforts. <br/>

- The year 2023 shows a notable reduction in cases and deaths compared to previous years, with continued low counts into 2025, reflecting sustained management success. <br/>

- Mortality rates have substantially decreased, indicating improvements in healthcare response and possibly better clinical management. <br/>

The data from October 2015 to October 2025 for Japanese encephalitis in the Chinese mainland reveals a clear seasonal pattern, with peaks typically occurring in the summer months of July, August, and September. This coincides with the rainy season, which supports mosquito breeding, crucial for the transmission of the virus. Notably, the years 2016, 2017, and 2018 exhibit heightened cases, with August 2018 reaching a peak of 904 cases and 55 deaths, indicating a severe outbreak or improved detection methods. The mortality rates fluctuate, with significant spikes in September and October 2018, possibly due to management challenges or a more virulent virus strain.

From 2019 onwards, a decline in cases and deaths is evident, suggesting the efficacy of public health interventions, vaccination drives, or environmental changes affecting mosquito populations. Although 2020 and 2021 display fewer cases, the late-summer peak remains, albeit with lower numbers. By 2025, the incidence appears to stabilize at a lower level, indicating better control over the disease. Yet, sporadic increases, such as in August and September 2025, highlight the ongoing risk of localized outbreaks. Effective strategies, including mosquito control, vaccination, and public health education, remain vital in managing Japanese encephalitis. These efforts aim to sustain the declining trend in incidence and fatalities within the region.

Year-on-year fluctuations in death rates are evident, suggesting changes in outbreak severity or effectiveness of public health interventions. Subsequent years, such as 2020 and later, show reduced cases and deaths, possibly due to better vaccination efforts or decreased mosquito activity.
