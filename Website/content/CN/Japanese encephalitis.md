# Japanese encephalitis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis (JE) is a mosquito-borne viral infection predominantly found in rural and agricultural areas of Asia and the Western Pacific. It is caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), which is transmitted mainly by the Culex species of mosquitoes. Most infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can lead to encephalitis, characterized by fever, headache, seizures, and, in some instances, permanent neurological damage or death. Effective prevention includes vaccination, mosquito control, and public health education. JE represents a significant public health challenge in endemic regions, particularly affecting children.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal pattern**: Japanese encephalitis cases peak during the summer months, particularly from July to September, with a significant drop in cases from October to May.<br/>
- **Declining trend**: Overall, the number of cases has gradually decreased from the high peaks of 2018-2019, showing a steady decline through 2020-2024.<br/>
- **Mortality rate**: Although the number of deaths fluctuates, the mortality rate remains relatively low compared to the total number of cases. Deaths are concentrated in specific years and months, with notable peaks in 2018 and 2019.<br/>
- **Recent situation (July 2024)**: As of July 2024, cases and deaths remain low, suggesting improved control measures and vaccination efforts.

The data on Japanese encephalitis (JE) cases in mainland China from July 2014 to July 2024 reveals significant seasonal and annual fluctuations in both reported cases and deaths. The incidence of JE typically peaks during the summer months, particularly from July to September, with August consistently showing the highest number of cases each year. For instance, August 2018 saw the highest number of cases at 904, accompanied by 55 deaths. Conversely, the winter months, particularly from December to April, report significantly fewer cases, often in single digits or even none, reflecting the seasonal nature of JE transmission, likely due to vector activity patterns.

Over the years, while the overall trend in the number of cases during peak months fluctuates, a notable decrease is observed in the severity and frequency of outbreaks in later years, especially post-2019. This trend is particularly evident in the lower number of cases and deaths from 2020 onwards, which might be attributed to increased public health interventions, vaccination efforts, or changes in vector control strategies. However, isolated spikes, such as in August 2023 with 80 cases, indicate that the disease remains a public health concern requiring ongoing vigilance, especially during peak transmission periods.

An analysis of Japanese encephalitis deaths in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals notable trends and variations over the years. The highest number of deaths was reported in August 2018, with 55 fatalities out of 904 cases, followed by September 2018 with 36 deaths from 528 cases. The year 2018 consistently recorded the highest mortality rates, with elevated death counts in August, September, October, and December, indicating a particularly severe outbreak during that period. The sharp increase in both cases and deaths in these months suggests heightened transmission, likely influenced by environmental or epidemiological factors.

In contrast, the years following 2018 show a general decrease in both cases and deaths. From 2019 onwards, deaths tend to be lower, often with single-digit fatalities per month, even as case numbers fluctuated. By 2021 and 2022, the overall numbers of cases and deaths had significantly decreased, indicating improved control efforts or lower transmission levels. However, periodic spikes in deaths, such as the 26 deaths from just six cases in April 2019, underscore the disease's potentially fatal impact despite fewer reported cases, highlighting the importance of continuous vigilance and public health interventions.
