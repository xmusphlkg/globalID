# Japanese encephalitis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Japanese encephalitis is a viral infection caused by the Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), primarily transmitted by Culex mosquitoes. It is endemic to parts of Asia and the Western Pacific, where it is a leading cause of viral encephalitis. Most human infections are asymptomatic, but severe cases can result in high fever, headache, vomiting, seizures, and inflammation of the brain, potentially leading to neurological damage or death. The disease predominantly affects rural and agricultural areas, particularly during the rice-growing season. Prevention includes vaccination, mosquito control, and personal protection measures such as using repellents and nets.

## Highlights

- **Recent Decline in Cases**: As of June 2025, Japanese encephalitis cases have significantly declined compared to peak years, showing only 1 case per month for May and June. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Deaths have consistently remained low, with recent months indicating zero fatalities, suggesting improvement in case management and disease control strategies. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variation**: Historical data shows a pattern of higher incidences between July and October, highlighting the need for heightened vigilance during these months. <br/>
- **Effectiveness of Control Measures**: The continued decrease in cases and deaths suggests control measures taken over the years have been effective, contributing to the overall reduction in disease burden. <br/>

The data on Japanese encephalitis in Mainland China reveals both annual trends and seasonal patterns typical for this mosquito-borne viral disease. From 2015 to 2025, there is a clear pattern where cases rise significantly from June to September, aligning with warm and rainy seasons that facilitate mosquito activity. Peaks in cases are particularly evident in 2016, 2017, and 2018, with August consistently showing the highest numbers (425 cases in 2016, 580 in 2017, 904 in 2018). From 2020 onward, there is a marked decrease in case numbers, likely indicating effective public health interventions, or shifts in environmental or social factors impacting transmission.

The reduction in both cases and deaths starting in 2019 might be due to enhanced vaccination efforts, better vector control measures, or potential underreporting during the COVID-19 pandemic. The mortality data also shows times when case fatality rates were unusually high, such as in March and April 2019, suggesting potential anomalies or challenges in medical response or accuracy of reporting during those periods. Overall, the data suggests significant progress in controlling Japanese encephalitis in China, yet highlights the crucial need for continued surveillance and vector control efforts to prevent future outbreaks and ensure public health safety.

Analyzing the data on Japanese encephalitis deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025, a discernible trend highlights a seasonal pattern with peak mortality occurring predominantly in late summer and early autumn, coinciding with the rainy season, which enhances breeding conditions for mosquito vectors. Notably, during the period from 2015 to 2018, mortality numbers were significantly high, with frequent peaks—August consistently exhibited the highest mortality. For instance, in August 2017, there were 36 deaths, increasing to 55 by August 2018, while September of these years also showed elevated numbers with 32 and 36 deaths respectively. These high mortality rates in conjunction with high case numbers underline the disease's catastrophic impact during these months.

From 2019 onward, the data reflects a decrease in both the number of cases and deaths. This decline can likely be attributed to improved public health interventions, vaccination programs, or other vector control measures that have been working effectively over time. In 2019 and 2020, although occasional spikes in deaths were seen—like the uncharacteristic peak of 26 deaths in April 2019—the overall mortality rate remained lower compared to earlier years. Furthermore, the period from 2021 onwards exhibits a substantial decrease in mortality, maintaining single-digit figures with several months registering zero deaths, particularly during what were once peak periods—indicative of possibly sustained control measures or changing environmental or social factors impacting the transmission dynamics.
