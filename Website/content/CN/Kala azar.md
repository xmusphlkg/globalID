# Kala azar

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by Leishmania parasites, transmitted through the bites of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. It predominantly affects rural and impoverished regions in countries such as India, Sudan, and Brazil. Characterized by prolonged fever, substantial weight loss, swelling of the liver and spleen, and anemia, kala azar is potentially fatal if untreated. Diagnosis typically involves serological and molecular tests, while treatment includes antimonial drugs or amphotericin B. Control and prevention focus on vector control strategies and early case detection and management to curb transmission.

## Highlights

- Kala azar cases in Chinese mainland showed significant fluctuations over the years, with notable peaks in late 2015 and early 2025. <br/>
- The number of deaths remained low, though there was a slight increase in fatalities from 2020 onwards, with multiple months recording isolated deaths. <br/>
- Recent data indicates a resurgence in cases from late 2024 to early 2025, suggesting potential outbreaks or increased detection efforts. <br/>
- The trend of cases indicates a higher incidence in the first and last quarters, possibly reflecting seasonal variations or reporting delays. <br/>

From 2015 to early 2025, the reported Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis) cases in mainland China demonstrate a significant shift in temporal distribution and disease intensity. The highest concentration of cases occurred in late 2015 through early 2016, with a peak in October and November 2015 (113 and 123 cases respectively), marking the most severe period in the dataset. This initial surge was followed by a steep and sustained reduction in annual totals from 2016 through 2019, when monthly counts stabilized mostly between 10–20 cases. This phase likely reflects either effective public health interventions, changes in surveillance sensitivity, improved vector control, or decreasing transmission intensity.

Beginning in 2020, a modest resurgence trend is apparent, particularly noticeable from 2021 onward as case numbers began to gradually rise again. By 2023 and 2024, monthly values frequently exceeded 25, with some months reaching into the 30s. Importantly, unlike the earlier years with zero or rare deaths, Kala-azar fatalities reemerged from 2016 and became more pronounced in 2023 with one death in four separate months. This pattern may indicate either widening geographic transmission to areas with delayed diagnosis or vulnerable populations, or the appearance of treatment resistance. The underlying cause of this recent resurgence deserves further investigation with regional disaggregation and migration, vector ecology, and treatment efficacy data.
