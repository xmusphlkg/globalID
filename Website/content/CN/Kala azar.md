# Kala azar

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the Leishmania donovani parasite. It is transmitted through the bite of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. The disease primarily affects the liver, spleen, and bone marrow, leading to symptoms such as prolonged fever, weight loss, anemia, and an enlarged spleen and liver. If left untreated, Kala azar can be fatal. It predominantly occurs in parts of Asia, East Africa, and South America, with India, Bangladesh, Sudan, South Sudan, Ethiopia, and Brazil bearing the highest burden.

## Highlights

- **Consistent Low Case Numbers:** Kala azar cases have remained generally low, averaging around 20-30 cases per month since 2019. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate:** The disease has a low mortality rate, with sporadic deaths reported in February 2016, November 2020, February 2022, February 2023, April 2023, August 2023, and October 2023. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends:** A notable increase in cases is observed in October and November 2015, potentially indicating seasonal variability. <br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of June 2024, there were 24 cases with no reported deaths, reflecting a stable epidemiological situation. <br/>

The reported cases of Kala azar in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 exhibit notable fluctuations, with a peak in reported cases occurring in late 2015 and early 2016. Specifically, the number of cases surged dramatically from September 2015, reaching a peak of 123 cases in November 2015. Following this peak, the number of cases gradually decreased, stabilizing to lower levels by mid-2017. A secondary, smaller peak is observed in early 2022, with the highest number of cases in March 2022 at 30 cases. Throughout this period, the majority of months report cases ranging between 10 to 30, indicating endemic transmission with periodic outbreaks.

The data also reveal the occurrence of deaths associated with Kala azar, albeit infrequent. There are five reported deaths over the decade: one each in February 2016, November 2020, February 2022, April 2023, and August 2023. These fatalities underscore the potential severity of the disease despite the relatively low case fatality rate. The sustained number of cases and periodic peaks suggest ongoing challenges in controlling the disease, possibly related to environmental, epidemiological, or healthcare factors that influence the transmission and management of Kala azar in the region.
