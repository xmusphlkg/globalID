# Kala azar

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the Leishmania donovani complex. It is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. The disease primarily affects the spleen, liver, and bone marrow, leading to symptoms such as prolonged fever, weight loss, anemia, and swelling of the spleen and liver. Kala azar is endemic in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America. Without treatment, it can be fatal, but it is treatable with antileishmanial drugs. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial for recovery.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, Kala azar cases have increased, peaking at 39 in February 2025, indicating a rising trend. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Cases show seasonal fluctuations, with higher numbers typically from July to October. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite rising cases, the mortality rate remains low, with sporadic deaths over the years. <br/>

- **Recent Increase**: A surge in cases is observed in recent months, suggesting a potential outbreak or increased transmission leading up to February 2025. <br/>

Following this peak, the number of cases generally declines, with some fluctuations, until another notable increase in 2020. From 2021 onwards, the data shows a relatively stable pattern with minor fluctuations in the number of cases each month.
