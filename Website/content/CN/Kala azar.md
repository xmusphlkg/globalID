# Kala azar

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the protozoa Leishmania, transmitted by the bite of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. It primarily affects internal organs such as the spleen, liver, and bone marrow, leading to symptoms like prolonged fever, weight loss, anemia, and enlargement of the spleen and liver. Without timely treatment, kala azar can be fatal. The disease is endemic in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America, often affecting the poorest and most vulnerable populations.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in Cases:** From a peak of 123 cases in November 2015, the number of Kala-azar cases has steadily declined, with the most recent data for May 2024 showing 27 cases. <br/>
- **Occasional Deaths:** There have been intermittent deaths, with 1 death reported in 2024 so far, illustrating intermittent but continuing mortality risk. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Cases show seasonal variation, with some months consistently experiencing higher case counts, suggesting potential environmental or behavioral factors at play. <br/>
- **Recent Increase:** There was a noticeable increase in cases in early 2024 compared to late 2023, with 33 cases reported in both January and April 2024. <br/>

The data for Kala azar in mainland China shows a fluctuating pattern of reported cases from 2014 to 2024, characterized by periodic peaks and troughs. Early on, the number of cases generally increased from mid-2014, reaching a significant peak in the latter months of 2015, specifically in October and November, with 113 and 123 cases, respectively, indicating a possible outbreak. This trend suggests either seasonal factors, increased transmission efficiency, or perhaps changes in reporting practices or population movements during this period. 

Post-2015, the number of cases shows less dramatic peaks but fluctuates with moderate amplitude. Notably, months such as February 2016 and November 2020 show notable mortality despite fewer cases, pointing to possible challenges in disease management during these times. The data also suggest potential undercurrents of endemicity with yearly trends showing sharper rises during specific months, which may be indicative of seasonal transmission patterns. By 2023 and 2024, there's another apparent rise in cases, most prominently in January and April 2024, highlighting the continued presence and perhaps resurgence of the disease. The consistent reporting of cases with intermittent deaths underscores the persistent public health challenge Kala azar poses, suggesting the necessity of ongoing surveillance, preventive strategies, and adequate healthcare responses.
