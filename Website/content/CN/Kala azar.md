# Kala azar

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Kala-azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the protozoan Leishmania parasites, transmitted through the bite of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. It primarily affects the liver, spleen, and bone marrow, leading to symptoms like prolonged fever, weight loss, an enlarged spleen and liver, and anemia. Without timely treatment, kala-azar can be fatal, particularly in regions with limited healthcare resources. The disease is endemic in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America. Control measures focus on vector management, early diagnosis, and antiparasitic treatment to reduce mortality and transmission.

## Highlights

- **Case Fluctuation**: Kala azar cases in China demonstrate an overall fluctuating trend, with peaks and troughs observed across the years. Notably, cases spiked in early 2023 and again in early 2025.<br/>
- **Mortality Rate**: Although deaths are infrequent, they were reported sporadically, with a noticeable increase in 2023 compared to previous years.<br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns**: No consistent seasonal pattern emerges from the data, suggesting that multiple factors may affect transmission.<br/>
- **Current Status**: As of October 2025, case numbers are relatively stable compared to earlier fluctuations, and efforts should continue to further understand transmission dynamics and reduce mortality.<br/>

The data on Kala azar in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals key trends. Initially, there was a peak in cases in late 2015, reaching 123 in November, which then declined through 2016 to a stable range of 10 to 30 cases monthly. From 2017 to 2019, the numbers remained relatively stable, suggesting control over the disease without significant outbreaks.

However, starting in 2020, there was a slight increase in case variability, with periodic peaks exceeding 30 cases in months like March 2020 and May 2021, marking a deviation from previous stability. Deaths remained minimal but occurred sporadically in months such as February 2016, November 2020, and several months in 2022 and 2023, indicating occasional spikes in disease severity or vulnerable populations affected.

Post-2023, case variability increased, with months reporting over 30 cases, peaking at 39 in February 2025. The consistent reporting of zero deaths in many months suggests effective treatment or health responses, though the periodic fatalities highlight potential gaps in handling severe cases. Overall, the Kala azar situation in mainland China appears largely controlled, with occasional inconsistencies in case numbers and rare deaths. Targeted public health interventions may be needed to further mitigate incidence and improve outcomes, maintaining the generally stable condition while addressing occasional rises and fatalities.
