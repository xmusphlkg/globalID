# Kala azar

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by Leishmania donovani and transmitted through the bite of infected female Phlebotomus sandflies. The disease primarily affects the spleen, liver, and bone marrow, leading to fever, weight loss, anemia, and enlargement of the spleen and liver. Kala azar is most prevalent in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America. If left untreated, the condition is typically fatal. Control measures focus on early diagnosis, prompt treatment, and vector control to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- Kala azar case numbers in the Chinese mainland have remained stable, fluctuating between 20 and 35 cases per month since 2023, without marked increases or decreases.<br/>
- Mortality remains extremely low, with only a few sporadic deaths (one death each in February, April, August, and October of 2023, and one in July 2025), indicating improved disease management.<br/>
- Seasonal trends show slightly higher case counts in spring, summer, and early autumn each year.<br/>
- As of December 2025, Kala azar remains a low-incidence, well-contained disease in China, with no evidence of outbreaks or significant escalation in transmission.<br/>

Analysis of reported Kala azar cases in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 to December 2025 shows consistently low numbers, generally between 10 and 35 cases per month, with only occasional minor peaks. The annual baseline fluctuates but there is a slight upward drift in monthly cases from 2021 onwards, with more months exceeding 30 cases. There is no clear evidence of seasonality, though some higher counts are noted in late spring and summer. Mortality remains very rare—only seven deaths in the entire 10-year period—with no clustering, suggesting effective detection and treatment efforts.

The stable case count and sparse fatalities point to strong public health control, with ongoing vigilance likely holding the disease in check. The periodic rise in cases, especially in later years, may merit further investigation to rule out new transmission foci, changes in vector ecology, or possible underreporting in previous years. While Kala azar remains endemic at low levels, the data do not indicate escalating risk or sustained outbreaks. However, continued surveillance and rapid response capacity are important to maintain these gains, especially as environmental or demographic changes could alter the epidemiological landscape and risk of transmission. Regular review of diagnostic, reporting, and vector control activities should underpin this stability and guard against future increases.
