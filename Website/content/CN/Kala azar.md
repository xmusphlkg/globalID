# Kala azar

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a parasitic disease caused by *Leishmania* species, primarily transmitted through the bite of infected female sandflies. It affects internal organs such as the spleen, liver, and bone marrow. Symptoms include fever, weight loss, anemia, and enlargement of the spleen and liver. If untreated, kala azar can be fatal. The disease is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions of Asia, Africa, and parts of South America. Diagnosis is based on clinical signs, blood tests, and bone marrow examination, with treatment typically involving antileishmanial drugs.

## Highlights

1. **Declining number of cases**: Kala azar cases have generally decreased since 2015, with fluctuations, peaking at 34 in August 2024 and dropping to 6 in October. <br/>

2. **Zero mortality**: No deaths have been reported since February 2016, indicating effective management and treatment. <br/>

3. **Seasonal variations**: Cases tend to be higher in warmer months, particularly from April to August. <br/>

4. **Sustained low incidence**: The disease remains under control in the Chinese mainland, with most months reporting fewer than 40 cases, including 24 cases in November 2024. <br/>

Over the past decade, Kala azar (visceral leishmaniasis) in the Chinese mainland has been characterized by fluctuating case numbers, with occasional outbreaks followed by a general decline. From November 2014 through early 2015, a notable peak in cases was observed, with monthly figures reaching over 100 cases, suggesting a localized outbreak or a response to improved surveillance or reporting mechanisms. However, despite these fluctuations, mortality remained exceptionally low, with only a few sporadic deaths reported, typically confined to the months of February 2016, November 2020, and early 2023. The consistently low mortality rate suggests effective management or a lack of advanced-stage cases that are typically fatal without intervention.

Looking at the trends in subsequent years, it appears that case numbers generally declined post-2015, reaching their lowest in the early months of 2023 and 2024. This decrease could indicate the success of control measures, such as vector control, improved diagnostic capabilities, or better healthcare access. However, there are still occasional spikes, particularly in the winter and spring months, which may reflect seasonal variations in vector populations or changes in environmental conditions favorable to the disease. The presence of deaths in some of these years, albeit few, underscores the importance of continued vigilance, particularly in areas where Kala azar persists as a risk, despite the overall decline in cases.
