# Kala azar

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the Leishmania parasites, transmitted through the bites of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. It primarily affects the spleen, liver, and bone marrow, leading to fever, weight loss, anemia, and enlargement of the spleen and liver. The disease is prevalent in parts of Africa, Asia, and Latin America, with significant impacts in resource-limited regions. Without timely treatment, kala azar can be fatal. Current treatments include antimonial compounds, amphotericin B, and miltefosine, but challenges such as drug resistance and side effects remain.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Cases in Recent Years**: Since 2022, there has been a noticeable rise in cases, with peak monthly cases reaching 39 in February 2025. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite the increase in cases, the mortality rate remains low, with only a few deaths reported sporadically from 2016 onwards. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends**: A pattern of higher cases in the spring months (April to May) compared to other months is evident, indicating potential seasonal influence on transmission. <br/>
- **Efforts Needed for Decline**: The persistently high number of cases since 2022 suggests a need for enhanced surveillance and intervention strategies to curtail transmission effectively. <br/>

The data shows a sharp surge in kala-azar cases during late 2015, peaking in November (123 cases), followed by a substantial decline in 2016 and stabilization thereafter. From 2017 onward, the annual monthly distributions became markedly flatter, with low to moderate fluctuations that indicate improved control and surveillance, likely due to strategic public health interventions. The total death toll remained minimal, with very sporadic deaths—only one each in 2016, 2020, 2022, and four in 2023—underscoring the generally low fatality rate, although 2023's four deaths suggest the need to monitor potential gaps in case management or delayed detection.

From 2021 to 2025, there is a mild upward trend in case numbers, notably in 2023 and 2024, with higher and more sustained monthly reports than previous years. Seasonal variation is less pronounced compared to early years, indicating possibly altered transmission dynamics or a wider geographical spread reducing temporal clustering. February continues to show occasional surges (notably in 2023 and 2025), suggesting the importance of tracking winter–spring transitions and understanding whether sociocultural, ecological, or vector factors contribute to this periodicity. While mortality remains rare, the scattered deaths across recent years necessitate examining access to diagnosis and treatment in particular sub-regions or populations.
