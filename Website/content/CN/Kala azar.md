# Kala azar

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the Leishmania donovani complex, primarily transmitted through the bites of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. It is endemic in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America. The disease manifests with prolonged fever, weight loss, splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, and anemia. Without treatment, it can be fatal. Diagnosis is typically confirmed through parasitological methods, serological tests, or molecular techniques. Current treatments include liposomal amphotericin B, miltefosine, and antimonial drugs, though resistance and access remain significant challenges. Control strategies focus on vector control and early diagnosis.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers:** Kala azar cases in the Chinese mainland show seasonal variation, with peaks observed around September to December and lower numbers from April to June. Recent data for 2024 indicates an increase in cases. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Deaths remain low, with only occasional fatalities reported over the years; no deaths have been reported in 2024. <br/>

- **Recent Uptick:** After a steady period, there has been a slight rise in cases from early 2024, suggesting a potential resurgence that requires monitoring. <br/>

- **Stable Long-Term Trend:** Overall, the number of cases has shown stability over the last few years, with minor fluctuations suggesting effective control measures. <br/>

The incidence of Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis) in mainland China exhibits notable fluctuations over the ten-year period analyzed, with varying peaks and troughs. Initially, from 2014 to early 2015, the cases were moderate, but by late 2015, there was a pronounced spike, peaking at 123 cases in November 2015. This surge was followed by a gradual decline over the subsequent years. By 2017, the number of cases had fallen significantly, with monthly counts often remaining below 20. However, there was a noticeable uptick in 2020 and 2021, likely influenced by pandemic-related disruptions to healthcare access or vector control measures, although still generally lower than 2015.

From 2022 onwards, the cases show a general trend of stabilization, though there are sporadic increases, notably in mid-2023 and September 2024. Deaths have remained extremely rare, with only a few reported over the entire period, suggesting that despite occasional spikes in case numbers, the overall clinical management of the disease is effective. The fluctuating case patterns, especially during the last three years, highlight potential areas where environmental or public health factors, such as changes in vector distribution or healthcare access, might be influencing transmission.
