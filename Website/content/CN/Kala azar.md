# Kala azar

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by protozoa of the genus Leishmania, transmitted through the bite of infected female sandflies. Predominantly affecting tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in parts of India, East Africa, and Brazil, it poses a significant public health challenge. The disease is characterized by prolonged fever, weight loss, enlargement of the spleen and liver, and anemia. Without treatment, it can be fatal. Diagnosis is primarily through serological tests, while management includes antileishmanial drugs like liposomal amphotericin B. Prevention relies on vector control and minimizing sandfly contact.

## Highlights

- Kala azar cases have fluctuated substantially since 2015, with spikes in 2022, 2023, and 2025 indicating potential outbreaks or heightened transmission periods. <br/>
- Mortality remains low, although occasional deaths are recorded, emphasizing the need for consistent medical intervention and monitoring. <br/>
- The consistent appearance of cases year-round suggests endemicity, necessitating ongoing public health initiatives for control and education within affected communities. <br/>
- Seasonal trends are not heavily evident, but certain months show increased cases; continuous surveillance is critical to understanding and mitigating these patterns. <br/>

An analysis of Kala azar cases in mainland China from September 2015 to September 2025 reveals significant fluctuations and generally low mortality rates. Initial data from late 2015 show high case numbers, with 113 and 123 reported in October and November, respectively, but no deaths. Throughout 2016 and 2017, cases decreased, likely indicating effective interventions or improved disease management.

Mortality remains low with isolated deaths reported in February 2016, November 2020, February 2022, and several months in 2023 and 2025, suggesting a minimal impact on life expectancy due to advancements in medical care and public health efforts. Notably, spikes appear in March 2020 and March 2022 with 27 and 30 cases, respectively, followed by reductions. These fluctuations hint at regional outbreaks or changes in reporting accuracy.

Seasonal trends are visible, with higher case counts often in spring and summer, indicating environmental factors affecting vector ecology. By 2025, cases stabilize, ranging in the mid to high twenties, except for a spike in February with 39 cases. This stability indicates ongoing yet managed endemicity, emphasizing continuous surveillance and adaptive public health strategies.

The increase in cases in certain months demands further investigation into socio-environmental conditions driving these rises. It also highlights the need for robust medical infrastructure and early detection systems to mitigate periodic surges and prevent disease expansion into non-endemic areas.
