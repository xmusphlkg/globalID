# Kala azar

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Kala-azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the protozoa Leishmania donovani. It is transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. The disease predominantly affects the spleen, liver, and bone marrow, leading to symptoms such as prolonged fever, weight loss, anemia, and swelling of the liver and spleen. Kala-azar is endemic in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America, with India, Brazil, and Sudan being the most affected countries. Without timely treatment, it can be fatal, emphasizing the need for early diagnosis and care.

## Highlights

- Kala-azar cases in mainland China have shown a fluctuating but generally increasing trend since 2019, with monthly cases in 2025 higher than early years like 2018 and 2019.  
<br/>
- Mortality remains low, with only sporadic deaths reported—six total between February 2016 and October 2023—and no deaths recorded since late 2023.  
<br/>
- Seasonal variation is observed, with a tendency for higher case numbers from spring through early autumn, peaking around April to August.  
<br/>
- As of May 2025, 35 new cases were reported with zero deaths, indicating persistent endemic transmission but relatively good control over fatal outcomes.  
<br/>

The case data for Kala-azar in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveal distinct temporal trends. The highest spike was observed from September to December 2015, with a peak of 123 cases in November. This cluster could be due to an outbreak or heightened detection during that period. Following this, a clear decline began in 2016, with overall numbers stabilizing between 10 to 30 cases per month until around 2020. The case numbers remained relatively consistent, with minor fluctuations across seasons and years, though some seasonal concentration appears during late spring to autumn in several years, particularly from 2016 onward.

A notable resurgence in cases emerged from 2021, with increased monthly variation and a rise in absolute case counts — peaking at 37 cases in March 2023. While the disease remained mostly non-fatal, deaths were sporadically recorded across a few years: once each in 2016, 2020, 2022, and four times in 2023. This slight uptick in mortality may indicate delayed case discovery or comorbidity complications. From 2024 into early 2025, monthly cases remained in the 20–35 range with no additional deaths, reflecting an ongoing but non-aggressive transmission trend, possibly reflecting improvements in case management but also persistence of transmission in endemic zones.
