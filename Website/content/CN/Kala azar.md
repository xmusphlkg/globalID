# Kala azar

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by protozoa of the Leishmania genus, primarily Leishmania donovani. It is transmitted by the bite of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. The disease is characterized by prolonged fever, weight loss, enlargement of the spleen and liver, and anemia. Kala azar is endemic in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and Latin America. Without treatment, it can be fatal. Diagnosis typically involves serological tests, along with parasitological confirmation through tissue samples. Control measures focus on vector control, early diagnosis, and effective treatment using antileishmanial drugs.

## Highlights

- **Steady case numbers:** Kala-azar cases in China have remained stable between 10 to 34 cases per month from 2018 to 2024, with fluctuations around seasonal and annual patterns. <br/>
- **Increased deaths recently:** From 2022 to August 2024, there have been occasional deaths recorded, peaking in months such as February 2023 and April 2023. <br/>
- **Seasonal variation:** Case peaks are often observed from July to November, with lower cases in spring, reflecting possible environmental or vector factors. <br/>
- **Current trend:** August 2024 shows a slight rise in cases (34), though no deaths were recorded, maintaining recent stability in disease control. <br/>

The reported data for Kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis) in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows periodic fluctuations in the number of cases, with notable peaks and declines over the years. Between 2014 and 2016, there is a general increase in cases, peaking in late 2015 with a high of 123 cases in November. After this, cases gradually decline but fluctuate across the following years, reaching a second significant peak in 2022 and 2023, where cases rise again, particularly in the summer months. A pattern emerges where cases typically increase in autumn and early winter months and are lower in spring and early summer. Deaths are rare but are sporadically reported in 2016, 2020, 2022, and 2023, with a maximum of one death per month during these periods.

The general trend suggests cyclical spikes in Kala-azar cases every few years, potentially indicating environmental, climatic, or socio-economic factors that could be influencing transmission. For example, the months leading up to the cooler seasons, such as autumn, often show an increase in cases, possibly due to the heightened transmission from vector activity during warmer periods. Despite the recurring incidence of the disease, the mortality rate remains low, suggesting that while transmission continues, medical interventions or disease management in China are effective at preventing deaths in most cases.
