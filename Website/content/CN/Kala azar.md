# Kala azar

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Kala azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a severe parasitic disease caused by the protozoan parasites of the Leishmania genus. It is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. Predominantly affecting the world's poorest regions, the disease is prevalent in parts of South Asia, East Africa, and South America. Symptoms include prolonged fever, weight loss, enlargement of the spleen and liver, and anemia. Without treatment, kala azar is almost always fatal. Control and preventive measures include early diagnosis, effective treatment, vector control, and reducing human-sandfly contact.

## Highlights

- Kala azar cases in mainland China have varied significantly from 2014 to 2024, reaching a high of 123 cases in November 2015, indicating periodic spikes in infection rates. <br/>
- Recent data from 2024 shows a downward trend with just 6 cases reported in October, suggesting effective control measures or seasonal variations affecting transmission. <br/>
- Mortality due to Kala azar remains low, with sporadic deaths recorded, notably a single death in several months from 2016 to 2023, highlighting successful treatment and management strategies.<br/>
- The consistent incidence of cases between 2023 and 2024 signals the persistence of the disease, requiring ongoing surveillance and sustained public health interventions.<br/>

Seasonal patterns emerge from the data, with cases rising in later months of the year, suggesting environmental or climatic influences on transmission.

This low mortality rate may reflect effective treatment protocols, public health interventions, or less virulent strains. In recent years, from 2022 to 2024, there is a more stable trend with fewer dramatic shifts in case numbers, suggesting consistent public health efforts and better healthcare access.
