# Kala azar

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Kala-azar, also known as visceral leishmaniasis, is a disease caused by the Leishmania parasites, transmitted through bites of infected female phlebotomine sandflies. Predominantly found in parts of Asia, East Africa, and South America, it affects the internal organs, particularly the spleen, liver, and bone marrow. Symptoms include prolonged fever, weight loss, fatigue, anemia, and swelling of the liver and spleen. If left untreated, it can be fatal. Diagnosis typically involves parasitological tests, serological assays, or molecular methods. Treatment is available but can be complex, requiring a combination of antileishmanial drugs tailored to the region and specific Leishmania species.

## Highlights

- Kala azar cases have shown fluctuations over the years, with peaks around late months of 2015, and a more stable trend from 2019 onwards, averaging around 20-30 cases per month. <br/>
- Sporadic deaths have occurred each year since 2016, with a slight increase in mortality observed in 2023. As of July 2025, one death is recorded, indicating an ongoing risk. <br/>
- The data indicates a recurring pattern of higher cases during early spring to summer months, suggesting a potential seasonal influence on disease transmission. <br/>
- The disease appears to be under control but warrants continued surveillance, especially given the observed yearly fluctuations in case numbers and occasional deaths. <br/>
