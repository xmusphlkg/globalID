# Leprosy

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansen's disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium leprae. It primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosal surfaces of the upper respiratory tract, and the eyes. Leprosy is known for its long incubation period, often taking years to manifest symptoms. It is transmitted via droplets from the nose and mouth during close and frequent contact with untreated cases. Early diagnosis and treatment with multidrug therapy (MDT) can prevent disability. Despite its historical stigma, leprosy is curable, and efforts continue to reduce its prevalence worldwide.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** Leprosy cases have significantly decreased from 73 in December 2014 to 19 in December 2024, indicating effective disease control. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate is very low, with only three deaths reported over the decade, highlighting successful treatment and management. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** Monthly case numbers show fluctuations, with peaks often in March and April, suggesting possible seasonal transmission factors. <br/>

- **Recent Stability:** In the past two years, cases have stabilized at lower levels, consistently below 40 per month, reflecting sustained public health efforts. <br/>

The data on leprosy cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a general decline in the number of reported cases over the years. Initially, the monthly cases fluctuated significantly, with peaks such as 108 cases in March 2016 and 84 cases in March 2015. However, from 2018 onwards, there is a noticeable downward trend, with the number of cases generally decreasing each year. By 2024, the monthly cases have reduced to as low as 10 in September, indicating a significant reduction in the incidence of leprosy.

Throughout the period, the number of deaths reported is extremely low, with only three deaths recorded over the entire span of ten years. This suggests that while leprosy cases were present, the disease was largely non-fatal, likely due to effective treatment and management strategies. The consistent reduction in cases could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, and better access to medical care, which have collectively contributed to controlling the spread of leprosy in the region.
