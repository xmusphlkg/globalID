# Leprosy

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansen’s disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by the bacteria Mycobacterium leprae. It primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosa of the upper respiratory tract, and eyes. Transmission likely occurs through prolonged close contact with an untreated person, usually via respiratory droplets. Symptoms include skin lesions, numbness, muscle weakness, and thickened nerves. If untreated, leprosy can lead to severe disfigurement and disability. Early diagnosis and multidrug therapy are effective for cure and preventing complications. The disease remains endemic in several low-income countries.

## Highlights

- Leprosy incidence in the Chinese mainland has shown a steady decline from 2015 to December 2025, with monthly cases dropping from over 60 to around 15 by the end of 2025.<br/>
- Mortality associated with leprosy is extremely low; only three deaths were reported over the entire decade, highlighting effective case management and treatment.<br/>
- The majority of months in recent years report less than 40 cases, with sporadic minor increases, reflecting sustained control and surveillance.<br/>
- As of December 2025, leprosy remains a rare disease in China, and transmission is limited, suggesting that elimination goals are well within reach.<br/>

Reported monthly leprosy cases in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 through December 2025 display a marked decline over the decade. Early years (2016–2018) averaged 50–70 cases per month, with occasional spikes, such as 108 cases in March 2016. By 2020, case counts consistently dropped to 20–40 monthly, and the trend continued; 2022 and 2023 saw several months below 30 cases. The most recent complete year shows frequent months with cases in the teens, indicating a sustained reduction in new diagnoses. Deaths have been rare throughout the dataset, with only four confirmed over the entire period.

No pronounced seasonal pattern emerges, but occasional minor peaks occur in March and May. The consistent downward trajectory suggests effective disease control measures, as well as possible saturation among susceptible populations. The low mortality rate reflects successful management and access to multidrug therapy. By late 2024 and 2025, months with only a dozen or fewer cases are common, marking a historic low and demonstrating durable progress in leprosy containment across China.

Over the observed decade in the Chinese mainland, leprosy mortality has remained extremely low, with only four deaths reported: in January 2016, August 2019, and December 2021. Notably, these deaths are isolated events and do not coincide with peaks in case numbers, as periods of higher incidence, such as March 2016 or March 2017, did not yield corresponding fatalities. The overall case fatality rate is substantially below 1%, reflecting the effectiveness of public health interventions, early case detection, and standardized treatment protocols.

The absence of clustering or recurring deaths, alongside persistent fluctuations in reported cases, further supports the likelihood that these rare mortality events are due to individual factors such as patient comorbidities or delayed health-seeking, rather than systemic clinical or reporting issues. Moreover, a gradual decline is observed both in case counts and deaths towards the latter years, which may indicate a falling disease burden or improvement in surveillance and management strategies. The data thus imply that leprosy is now a highly manageable condition with virtually no mortality under current Chinese public health frameworks, and deaths are rare exceptions rather than expected sequelae of the disease.
