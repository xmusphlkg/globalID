# Leprosy

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansenâ€™s disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by *Mycobacterium leprae*. It primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosa of the upper respiratory tract, and eyes. The disease spreads through prolonged close contact with an untreated infected person, likely via respiratory droplets. Leprosy is curable with multidrug therapy (MDT), which the World Health Organization provides free of charge. Early diagnosis and treatment prevent disability and transmission. Although largely eliminated as a public health problem in many countries, leprosy continues to affect thousands annually, particularly in tropical and subtropical regions.

## Highlights

- Leprosy incidence in mainland China has shown a steady decline from monthly peaks above 80 cases (e.g., March 2016) to around 30 cases per month in 2025.<br/>
- Since 2021, the monthly case count has remained low and stable, with no new deaths reported since December 2021, indicating effective disease control.<br/>
- The seasonal variation observed in earlier years has diminished, suggesting improved surveillance and consistent public health intervention.<br/>
- As of May 2025, leprosy remains a rare disease in China, with only 31 new cases and no deaths that month, reflecting sustained success in national eradication efforts.<br/>

During this period, several months averaged above 60 cases, suggesting higher disease activity or expanded detection. From 2018 onward, the data reflect a gradual decline, with most months falling in the 30â€“60 range, and seasonal increases still visible in March and May.

The decline becomes more pronounced from 2022 to 2025. Monthly counts regularly drop below 30, with some months reporting as few as 10 to 14 cases. The consistent drop in incidence over time suggests progress toward control, though the continuing presence of new cases indicates ongoing transmission risks in localized regions. From the surveillance data spanning May 2015 to May 2025 in mainland China, reported leprosy deaths have been exceedingly rare, with only three deaths registered: one each in January 2016, August 2019, and December 2021. This represents an exceptionally low case-fatality ratio relative to the total number of reported cases, which exceeds 3,000. The rarity of deaths underscores the effectiveness of early diagnosis, multidrug therapy, and public health outreach in minimizing mortality, even as the disease continues to persist at low incidence rates across the country. These deaths are isolated and do not follow any clear geographic, seasonal, or trend-based clustering, suggesting they may be due to individual clinical complications rather than systemic health service inadequacies.

Monthly variability in new cases is apparent, but deaths show no correlation with case surges. For instance, March 2016 saw a spike to 108 cases with no associated mortality, further affirming that increased detection does not imply greater lethality. The three fatal months did not have anomalously high case counts, possibly indicating that deaths were not necessarily tied to outbreak conditions but rather to unique individual factors such as late diagnosis or comorbidities. The data demonstrate that while leprosy remains endemic at low levels, it is controllable, and fatal outcomes under the current health system remain exceedingly uncommon.
