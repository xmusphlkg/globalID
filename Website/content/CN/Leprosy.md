# Leprosy

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansen's disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium leprae. It primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosal surfaces of the respiratory tract, and the eyes. Leprosy is characterized by lesions, numbness, and muscle weakness, which can lead to severe disfigurement and disability if not treated. The disease is transmitted via droplets from the nose and mouth during close and frequent contact with untreated cases. Despite its historical stigma, leprosy is now treatable with a multidrug therapy regimen recommended by the World Health Organization, significantly reducing its prevalence globally.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend**: Leprosy cases in China have shown a gradual decrease from 48 in October 2015 to 14 in October 2025, indicating successful control measures. <br/>

- **Mortality**: Deaths from leprosy have been rare, with only three recorded between 2015 and 2025, reflecting effective treatment and healthcare strategies. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variation**: A peak in cases is often observed in March, while the lowest numbers are typically seen in October and other late-year months. <br/>

- **Recent Fluctuations**: Despite the overall downtrend, slight fluctuations in case numbers are observed, with minor increases in cases like 53 in March 2024, suggesting continued vigilance is necessary. <br/>

The data for leprosy cases in mainland China from 2015 to October 2025 reflects a general decline in the number of reported cases, with some fluctuations over the years. From late 2015 to 2016, there was an inconsistent pattern, with a peak in March 2016 at 108 cases and other months showing variable numbers. In subsequent years, there was noticeable variability, yet generally, the number of monthly cases stayed under 100. The year 2018 marked the beginning of a more steady decline, with most months reporting fewer than 50 cases and continuing downwards.

From the years 2020 onwards, there is a more coherent trend of reduced incidence. Notably, by 2022, there is a significant decline in reported cases, with monthly figures mostly below 40, and decreasing further to as low as 13 cases in October 2022. The years 2023 and 2024 continue this downward trend, albeit with some small fluctuations, maintaining minimal monthly cases. This consistent decline over years may be attributed to enhanced public health efforts, increased access to medical treatment, or social and environmental factors reducing transmission. Deaths have been rare throughout these years, with only a handful occurring, indicating effective management of the disease among reported cases.
