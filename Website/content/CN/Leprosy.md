# Leprosy

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansen's disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium leprae. It primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosa of the upper respiratory tract, and eyes. Symptoms include skin lesions, numbness, muscle weakness, and thickened nerves, which can lead to severe disfigurement and disability if untreated. Leprosy is transmitted through prolonged close contact with untreated individuals via nasal droplets. It has a long incubation period, ranging from a few months to several years. Early diagnosis and multidrug therapy (MDT) are crucial for effective treatment, helping to prevent transmission and manage the disease.

## Highlights

- There has been a steady decline in leprosy cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025. <br/>
- Peak cases were observed in 2016 March with 108 cases, while in 2024 September the lowest at 10 cases. <br/>
- Deaths have remained minimal, with only a few recorded over the entire period until 2025. <br/>
- Recent data from January to March 2025 shows a slight increase in case numbers, averaging around 34 cases per month. <br/>

From 2015 to early 2025, leprosy cases in mainland China have generally declined, though there are fluctuations. In 2015, the monthly case count was consistently above 40, peaking at 84 in March. By 2018, these numbers had decreased, with some months recording fewer than 30 cases, indicating successful management efforts in controlling leprosy spread. Despite this, periodic spikes occurred, such as 108 cases in March 2016 and 83 in March 2017, which may have been influenced by seasonal patterns or specific regional surges.

The death toll has been extremely low, with only three deaths reported over the entire period: in January 2016, August 2019, and December 2021. This suggests effective treatment and timely diagnosis, keeping mortality to a minimum. By 2022, monthly cases had halved compared to earlier years, with a record low of 13 in October. The trend of low incidence continues into 2023, with minor month-to-month fluctuations, reflecting ongoing success in disease control.

Overall, this declining trend suggests that public health measures have been effective. Sustained vigilance in detection and expanded public health initiatives are likely key factors in preventing a potential resurgence, which is critical for maintaining control over the disease spread. Continuing efforts are essential to support affected individuals and ensure that leprosy remains under control.
