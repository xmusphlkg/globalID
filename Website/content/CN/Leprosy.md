# Leprosy

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansen's disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by the bacteria *Mycobacterium leprae*. It primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosal surfaces of the upper respiratory tract, and the eyes. Transmission occurs through prolonged close contact with untreated cases, typically via respiratory droplets. Symptoms include skin lesions, numbness, and muscle weakness. Early diagnosis and treatment with multi-drug therapy (MDT) can cure the disease and prevent disabilities. Despite its historical stigma, leprosy is now curable, and efforts continue to eliminate it as a public health issue globally.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend in Cases:** There has been a noticeable decline in reported leprosy cases from 2014 (average 65 cases/month) to 2024 (average 33 cases/month).<br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** Deaths due to leprosy remain rare, with only four reported deaths from 2014 to June 2024.<br/>
- **Seasonal Variability:** The data show fluctuations in case numbers, with peaks in some months, particularly in early spring and late summer.<br/>
- **Stable Recent Trends:** The recent data for 2024 indicates a relatively stable situation, with cases consistently lower compared to earlier years.<br/>

The reported cases of leprosy in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 show a general downward trend with some periodic fluctuations. In the early years of the data, monthly cases were relatively higher, with peaks such as 84 cases in March 2015 and 108 cases in March 2016. Following these peaks, there appears to be a steady decline, particularly from mid-2018 onwards, where cases frequently fall below 50 per month. Notably, the lowest monthly case count is observed in October 2022, with only 13 cases.

Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, the data indicates that deaths from leprosy are extremely rare, with only three reported deaths over the ten-year period: one in January 2016, one in August 2019, and one in December 2021. This suggests effective treatment and management of the disease. However, the presence of these few deaths also underscores that while rare, mortality from leprosy can still occur, indicating a need for continued vigilance in disease management and patient care. The overall trend points towards successful control measures, but the consistent, albeit low, number of new cases each month highlights that leprosy remains a public health issue in the region.
