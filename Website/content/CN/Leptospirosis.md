# Leptospirosis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a zoonotic bacterial infection caused by the Leptospira species, affecting humans and animals. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions and often associated with heavy rainfall or flooding. Humans typically acquire the disease through direct contact with contaminated water or soil, or via the urine of infected animals such as rodents, dogs, and livestock. Symptoms range from mild flu-like illness to severe manifestations like Weil's disease, characterized by jaundice, kidney damage, and bleeding. Early diagnosis, through serological tests, and antibiotic treatment are vital for management, as delayed treatment can lead to complications and increased mortality.

## Highlights

- Leptospirosis trends in mainland China exhibit seasonal variation, with higher cases typically from July to October.<br/>
- A notable increase in cases was observed in August and September of 2024, with 91 and 128 cases respectively, highlighting an alarming rise.<br/>
- Deaths have been sporadic over the years, peaking during high-incidence months, though no deaths have been reported in 2024 thus far.<br/>
- The high number of cases in 2024, especially in September, suggests potential changes in environmental or epidemiological factors, necessitating further investigation and public health interventions.<br/>

The data on leptospirosis in mainland China from October 2014 to October 2024 reveals notable seasonal and annual patterns. A significant spike in cases occurs in December 2014, setting a precedent for similar annual increases, particularly from September to December. This trend suggests a potential link with seasonal factors like post-monsoon conditions that can escalate disease transmission due to environmental aspects, such as flooding or increased human-animal interactions, commonly associated with leptospirosis.

While mortality rates remain relatively low, there are occasional spikes, notably in September 2020 and 2021, and July 2019. These instances may highlight areas for reviewing healthcare interventions during such periods. Even though early years show sharp peaks in case numbers, a decline in annual peak values is noted in subsequent years, although late 2023 to 2024 sees a resurgence, indicating potentially increased transmission or improved detection.

Zero mortality months despite high case numbers suggest better management and treatment improvements over time. The continued variability highlights the necessity for sustained surveillance and adaptable response strategies to effectively control leptospirosis' impact in the region. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for public health planning, especially in preparing for seasonal outbreaks and addressing potential resurgences of the disease, ensuring robust healthcare responses and mitigation strategies.
