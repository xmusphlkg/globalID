# Leptospirosis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by the Leptospira species, commonly found in tropical and subtropical regions. It is transmitted to humans through direct contact with water, soil, or food contaminated with the urine of infected animals, such as rodents, cattle, and dogs. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe illness, including jaundice, kidney damage, and bleeding. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial to prevent complications. Preventive measures include avoiding contaminated water, wearing protective clothing, and controlling rodent populations. Leptospirosis is a significant public health concern due to its potential for outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Leptospirosis cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit seasonal peaks, particularly from August to October. <br/>

- **2024 Surge:** In 2024, there was a significant rise in cases, with September reaching 128 cases, the highest in the dataset, suggesting a potential outbreak. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** Deaths have remained low, with occasional spikes, notably in 2020, where September and October had higher mortality rates. <br/>

- **Long-term Decline:** Despite recent increases, the overall trend from 2014 to 2024 shows a decline in cases and deaths, indicating improved control measures or reporting. <br/>

The data on Leptospirosis cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring in the late summer and early autumn months, particularly from July to October. This pattern is consistent with the environmental conditions favorable for the transmission of Leptospirosis, which is often associated with increased rainfall and flooding, leading to higher exposure to contaminated water. For instance, the highest number of cases in a single month was recorded in September 2024, with 128 cases, aligning with this seasonal trend.

Over the years, there is a general increase in the number of reported cases, particularly from 2020 onwards. This could be attributed to several factors, including improved surveillance and reporting systems, changes in environmental conditions, or increased human activities that lead to greater exposure to the bacteria. The number of deaths, however, remains relatively low compared to the number of cases, suggesting that while the disease is prevalent, it may not be as lethal, or that medical interventions have been effective in reducing mortality. Notably, the year 2020 saw a spike in deaths, with a peak in October, which could indicate a particularly severe outbreak or challenges in healthcare response during that period.
