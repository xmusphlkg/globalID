# Leptospirosis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by *Leptospira* species, often transmitted through contact with water or soil contaminated by the urine of infected animals. Common symptoms include fever, headache, muscle aches, and vomiting, with severe cases potentially leading to liver damage, kidney failure, or meningitis. The disease is more prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in areas with poor sanitation. Human infection is commonly linked to outdoor activities in contaminated environments, including swimming, farming, and flooding. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are critical for effective management.

## Highlights

1. **Seasonal Peaks**: Leptospirosis cases in China peak during summer and fall, especially between June and September, with 128 cases in September 2024. <br/>

2. **Low Mortality**: Deaths remain rare, with only 3 fatalities in 2024 as of November. <br/>

3. **Fluctuating Incidence**: Leptospirosis incidence shows variability, with notable increases in 2019, 2020, and 2024, although overall cases have decreased since 2016. <br/>

4. **Rainfall and Flooding**: Case spikes are often linked to seasonal rains and flooding, which likely enhance transmission, though regional patterns were not specified. <br/>

The data on Leptospirosis cases in mainland China reveals notable seasonal fluctuations, with peaks typically occurring in the summer and autumn months, such as August and September. For instance, in 2020, there were significant spikes in cases—110 in September and 53 in October—likely linked to increased flooding and environmental exposure to contaminated water, which facilitates the spread of *Leptospira* bacteria. These seasonal surges suggest that climatic factors, particularly the rainy season, play a key role in the transmission of the disease.

Despite these periodic spikes, the mortality rate remains consistently low over the years. The highest number of deaths occurred in 2019, with two fatalities in July. This suggests that while Leptospirosis remains a health concern, it is generally not highly lethal, possibly due to improved medical interventions and timely treatment. The low fatality rate across most months indicates effective public health responses and diagnostic improvements. 

Overall, while case numbers fluctuate, particularly in flood-prone months, the low death toll and seasonal patterns highlight the ongoing need for monitoring and prevention, especially in high-risk regions. Continued public health efforts are crucial to reduce exposure and mitigate outbreaks, especially during the rainy season.
