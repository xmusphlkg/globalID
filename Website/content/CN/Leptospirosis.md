# Leptospirosis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by the Leptospira species, commonly found in tropical and subtropical regions. It is transmitted to humans through direct contact with water, soil, or food contaminated with the urine of infected animals, such as rodents, cattle, and dogs. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe illness, including jaundice, kidney damage, and bleeding. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial to prevent complications. Preventive measures include avoiding contaminated water, wearing protective clothing, and controlling rodent populations. Leptospirosis is a significant public health concern due to its potential for outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Leptospirosis cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit a seasonal pattern, peaking between July and October, indicating a link to environmental factors. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers:** Although there is a general increase in cases from 2015 to 2024, January 2025 shows a low number of cases, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** Deaths from Leptospirosis remain low, with occasional spikes, indicating that while the disease is prevalent, it is not highly lethal. <br/>

- **Recent Increase:** The year 2024 experienced a notable rise in cases, especially in September, warranting further investigation into potential causes. <br/>

The data on Leptospirosis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a distinct seasonal pattern, with a notable increase in cases during the late summer and early autumn months, particularly from July to October. This trend is consistent with the epidemiological understanding of Leptospirosis, which is often associated with increased rainfall and flooding, conditions that facilitate the spread of the bacteria through water contaminated by animal urine. The peak in cases typically occurs in September, with the highest number of cases recorded in September 2024 (128 cases). This seasonal surge suggests a strong environmental component to the transmission dynamics of Leptospirosis in the region.

The data also indicates a relatively low mortality rate, with sporadic deaths reported across the years. Notably, the highest number of deaths in a single month was four, recorded in October 2020. The overall low mortality rate could be attributed to improved healthcare access and effective treatment protocols. However, the presence of deaths, albeit few, underscores the potential severity of the disease and the need for continued public health efforts to mitigate its impact. The fluctuations in case numbers and the occasional spikes in mortality highlight the importance of ongoing surveillance and targeted interventions, particularly in high-risk periods and regions.
