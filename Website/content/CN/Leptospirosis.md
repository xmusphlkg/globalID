# Leptospirosis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial zoonotic disease caused by pathogenic *Leptospira* species. It is transmitted to humans through direct contact with the urine of infected animals or exposure to contaminated water, soil, or food. Common hosts include rodents, livestock, and dogs. Symptoms range from mild flu-like illness to severe complications such as Weilâ€™s disease, characterized by liver and kidney failure. The disease is more prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, especially following heavy rainfall or flooding. Early diagnosis and antibiotic treatment are critical for effective management and reducing serious health outcomes.

## Highlights

- Leptospirosis in mainland China shows a clear seasonal pattern, with peak case numbers typically occurring between July and October each year. <br/>
- The disease burden remains low overall, with an annual case count rarely exceeding 600 and mortality staying minimal; no deaths were reported from January 2022 to April 2025. <br/>
- From late 2020 to 2021, there was a short-term increase in both cases and deaths, especially in September-October 2020, suggesting a possible outbreak or enhanced surveillance. <br/>
- As of April 2025, the situation appears stable with low case numbers (7 cases) and no reported deaths, continuing a downward trend in disease severity observed since 2021. <br/>
