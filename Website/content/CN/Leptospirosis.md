# Leptospirosis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by the Leptospira species. It is a zoonotic disease, meaning it can be transmitted from animals to humans, often through contact with water contaminated by animal urine. Common carriers include rodents, cattle, and dogs. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe illness, including kidney damage, liver failure, or meningitis. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, especially after heavy rainfall or flooding. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial to prevent complications. Preventive measures include avoiding contaminated water and vaccinating pets.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Peaks:** Leptospirosis cases in mainland China show a seasonal pattern, peaking between July and October annually. <br/>

- **Recent Decline:** As of February 2025, cases have declined to 2, indicating reduced transmission or improved control measures. <br/>

- **Low Mortality:** The disease has a low mortality rate, with sporadic deaths, mostly during peak months. The highest monthly deaths were 4 in October 2020. <br/>

- **Yearly Variability:** Despite seasonal peaks, case numbers vary yearly, suggesting fluctuating environmental or social factors affecting transmission. <br/>

The data on Leptospirosis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a distinct seasonal pattern, with a notable increase in cases during the late summer and early autumn months, particularly from July to October. This trend is consistent across the years, suggesting a correlation with environmental factors such as increased rainfall and agricultural activities, which are known to facilitate the spread of Leptospira bacteria. The peak in cases often occurs in September, as seen in 2020 and 2021, with 110 and 116 cases respectively, indicating a potential need for targeted public health interventions during these months to mitigate the spread.

The mortality rate associated with Leptospirosis in this dataset is relatively low, with sporadic deaths reported in some years. For instance, 2020 saw the highest number of deaths, with a total of 9, which coincided with a significant spike in cases. This suggests that while the disease can be widespread, the fatality rate remains low, possibly due to effective medical interventions or the mild nature of most infections. However, the presence of any fatalities underscores the importance of continued surveillance and public health efforts to prevent outbreaks and manage severe cases effectively.
