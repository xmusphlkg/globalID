# Leptospirosis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by the Leptospira species. It is a zoonotic disease, meaning it can spread from animals to humans. Humans typically contract it through contact with water or soil contaminated with the urine of infected animals, such as rodents, livestock, and dogs. Symptoms range from mild flu-like signs to severe illness, including kidney damage, liver failure, and meningitis. Leptospirosis is most common in tropical and subtropical regions, especially after heavy rainfall or flooding. Preventive measures include avoiding contact with contaminated water and soil and using protective clothing.

## Highlights

- Leptospirosis cases show seasonal peaks, particularly in late summer and early autumn, with the highest incidence typically in August and September. <br/>
- The number of cases has varied over the years, with notable increases in August 2016 (68 cases), September 2020 (110 cases), and September 2021 (116 cases). <br/>
- Mortality rates are generally low, with sporadic deaths recorded. The highest death toll was in October 2020, with four deaths. <br/>
- As of June 2024, the situation appears stable with 21 cases reported, reflecting a consistent pattern compared to previous years. <br/>

The reported cases of leptospirosis in mainland China from mid-2014 to mid-2024 exhibit notable seasonal variations and fluctuations over the years. The highest number of cases typically occurs between June and October each year, suggesting a seasonal pattern likely influenced by climatic and environmental factors conducive to the spread of the Leptospira bacteria. For instance, December 2014 saw a significant spike with 207 reported cases, while other notable peaks include August 2016 with 68 cases, and August 2021 with 81 cases. Conversely, the periods from January to May usually report lower case numbers, indicating a possible lull in transmission during colder months.

In terms of fatalities, the data indicates sporadic occurrences of deaths, with certain months experiencing higher mortality. September and October of 2014 recorded five deaths collectively, which is significant given the otherwise low mortality rate throughout the dataset. Noteworthy are also the years 2020 and 2021, which show several months with one or more deaths, aligning with the peaks in case numbers. Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, the fatality rate remains relatively low overall, with occasional months like September 2020 (three deaths) and October 2020 (four deaths) standing out as exceptions. The data highlights the importance of monitoring and responding to seasonal outbreaks to mitigate the impact of leptospirosis.
