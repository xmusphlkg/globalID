# Leptospirosis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial zoonotic disease caused by pathogenic spirochetes of the genus *Leptospira*. It commonly affects humans and animals, with transmission primarily occurring through contact with water or soil contaminated by urine from infected animals, such as rodents. Symptoms range from mild flu-like manifestations to severe illness involving kidney or liver failure, meningitis, or pulmonary hemorrhage. Leptospirosis is widespread in tropical and subtropical regions, especially where sanitation is poor. Early recognition and antibiotic treatment improve prognosis, while preventive measures focus on minimizing exposure to contaminated environments.

## Highlights

- Leptospirosis cases in Chinese mainland show a pronounced seasonal pattern, peaking in late summer and early autumn (July–October) annually.<br/>
- 2025 saw a significant surge in cases, with monthly counts reaching the highest levels (August: 156, September: 176, October: 169) over the past decade, indicating an upward trend.<br/>
- Mortality remains low, with only 2 deaths reported in September 2025 and sparse fatalities throughout the data period.<br/>
- Outside peak months, incidence is consistently minimal, suggesting sporadic transmission during winter and spring; overall, leptospirosis persists as a seasonal public health concern needing close monitoring.<br/>

Analysis of the reported monthly cases of leptospirosis in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 to December 2025 reveals consistent seasonal surges, primarily during the late summer and early autumn months. Each year, there is a low baseline of reported cases from January through May, with increases beginning in June and peaking between August and October before declining towards year's end. This pattern suggests a connection to climatic or environmental factors, likely increased rainfall and flooding during the monsoon season, which is known to facilitate leptospira transmission via increased contact with contaminated water.

While the overall case numbers fluctuate from year to year, there is a marked increase in 2025, peaking at 176 cases in September—the highest monthly value in the ten-year span. Despite these annual spikes, the number of deaths remains low and sporadic, with most years having only a handful of fatal cases, and several months with zero deaths even during peak incidence. The low lethality relative to cases may indicate improvements in clinical recognition and treatment, or possibly shifts in the demographics or exposures of affected populations. Nevertheless, the persistent and occasionally escalating seasonality underscore an ongoing public health challenge in managing the environmental and occupational risks that underpin leptospirosis transmission in this setting.

Reported leptospirosis deaths in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 to December 2025 are markedly low in relation to total case numbers, with most months and years showing zero deaths. Fatalities—when they do occur—are sporadic and largely concentrated in late summer and autumn, coinciding with seasonal peaks in case counts. Years such as 2020 demonstrate this pattern clearly: October recorded four deaths among 53 cases, while September saw three deaths from 110 cases. Similarly, isolated deaths appear in some months of 2019, 2021, 2022, and 2025, generally aligning with moderate or high incidence periods. 

Despite these periodic clusters, there is no indication of a sustained increase in mortality across the years, even amid significant variation in case numbers. Extended stretches, including the entirety of 2023 and nearly all of 2024, registered no deaths despite frequent diagnoses, suggesting improvements in clinical management, public awareness, or perhaps shifts in virulence or exposure setting. The infrequent and sporadic nature of deaths, even during high-transmission months, points to successful disease control and treatment strategies mitigating severe outcomes. In sum, while annual peaks in cases do loosely coincide with the rare occurrence of deaths, the case fatality rate remains consistently low, highlighting that the majority of leptospirosis cases are non-lethal under current surveillance and healthcare conditions.
