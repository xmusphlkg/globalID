# Leptospirosis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a zoonotic bacterial infection caused by pathogenic strains of the genus Leptospira, affecting both humans and animals. It is prevalent worldwide, especially in tropical and subtropical regions. Transmission occurs through contact with water or soil contaminated by the urine of infected animals, commonly rodents, cattle, and dogs. Humans can become infected through open wounds or mucous membranes. Symptoms range from mild flu-like effects to severe manifestations, including Weil's disease and pulmonary hemorrhage. Early diagnosis and antibiotic treatment improve outcomes. Preventative measures include sanitation and avoiding contact with contaminated environments.

## Highlights

- Seasonal Variation: Leptospirosis cases exhibit a clear seasonal pattern, peaking from July to October each year, with spikes notably in September and October. <br/>

- Recent Trends: There is a general trend of increasing cases particularly from 2023 to 2024, with 2024 September recording the highest monthly cases (128) in the dataset. <br/>

- Mortality: The disease's mortality rate appears relatively low with sporadic deaths over the years, as seen in months like September 2020 with three deaths. <br/>

- Current Situation: As of June 2025, 18 new cases have been reported without any fatalities, indicating a modest incidence with effective management at this time. <br/>

The data for Leptospirosis cases in mainland China indicates a seasonal pattern, with peaks from July to October each year. This suggests environmental or behavioral factors contributing to increased transmission during these months. Over the years, the data shows an overall rise in cases, with notable spikes in 2021 and 2024 compared to earlier years. Conversely, lower case numbers from December to April might be due to cold weather conditions that hinder the bacterium's spread.

Fatalities are relatively rare and sporadic, indicating a low case-fatality rate for Leptospirosis in this region. However, months like September and October 2020 show increased deaths, potentially reflecting periods of heightened clinical severity or reporting discrepancies. The inconsistent pattern of deaths compared to cases points to various contributory factors, possibly including alterations in clinical management or pathogen virulence.

The analysis suggests focusing on prevention strategies during months with higher incidence rates, such as environmental interventions or awareness campaigns to mitigate risk factors. Additionally, as episodes of increased mortality are infrequent but significant, improving diagnostic capacity and timely treatment protocols could further reduce the disease's public health impact. Targeted efforts during the peak months could potentially control the spread and reduce the number of cases and fatalities experienced in subsequent years.

Analyzing the data on leptospirosis-related deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals some critical patterns and insights. The total number of deaths reported over this period is relatively low, with only sporadic incidents in certain months and years. Specifically, there have been noticeable peaks in mortality during certain months, such as July 2019, September and October 2020, September 2021, and December 2018. These death occurrences often do not correlate linearly with the number of reported cases, indicating potential factors influencing lethality, such as the virulence of particular outbreaks, healthcare response effectiveness, or environmental conditions that might exacerbate the disease severity.

A more profound look into the temporal distribution of these deaths suggests potential seasonal or environmental triggers that might impact the severity of leptospirosis outbreaks in certain years. The noted increase in deaths in late 2020, for instance, could be aligned with unusual environmental patterns or concurrent health system pressures, particularly given that this period coincides with the COVID-19 pandemic. The data also highlight an absence of deaths in several years, suggesting improvements in disease management or shifts in the environmental or epidemiological landscape that favor reduced mortality. Understanding these patterns requires considering numerous factors, including changes in public health policies, awareness campaigns, tick or rodent populations (which are vectors), and climatic changes impacting these vectors' behavior and distribution.
