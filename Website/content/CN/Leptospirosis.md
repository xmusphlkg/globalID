# Leptospirosis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a widespread zoonotic disease caused by bacteria of the genus Leptospira. This infectious disease primarily targets animals but can be transmitted to humans through direct contact with contaminated water, soil, or surfaces. Human infection often occurs through touching or ingesting contaminated water or food. Symptoms vary from mild flu-like signs such as fever, headache, and muscle pain to severe illness involving liver and kidney damage, meningitis, or respiratory distress. Leptospirosis is more common in tropical and subtropical regions due to favorable environmental conditions. Early diagnosis and antibiotic treatment are crucial for effective management and prevention of severe outcomes.

## Highlights

**Seasonal peaks:** Leptospirosis cases in Chinese mainland exhibit a clear seasonal pattern, typically peaking between August and November each year, with the highest numbers noted in August 2025.  
<br/>
**Mortality rate:** Though there was a notable increase in cases in certain months, the mortality rate remains relatively low, with sporadic deaths occurring mainly in the latter half of the year.  
<br/>
**Recent trends:** The case numbers have risen substantially in August 2025 compared to previous years, indicating a possible heightened transmission.  
<br/>
**Long-term patterns:** Overall, leptospirosis incidence shows fluctuating trends across years, but the recent uptick signals a need for heightened surveillance and preventive measures.

The data on Leptospirosis in mainland China shows marked seasonal variation, with cases generally peaking in the latter half of each year, particularly between August and October. This pattern might be associated with environmental factors, such as monsoon season or increased agricultural activities during these months, which can amplify human exposure to Leptospira bacteria often found in water contaminated by animal urine. The seasonal peak is observed consistently across the years in the dataset, although the magnitude varies, indicating annual fluctuations potentially influenced by climatic conditions or efficacy of public health interventions.

During the examined years, the month of December 2015 stands out with an unusual spike in cases, reaching over 100 without any recorded deaths. Such peaks might signify temporary changes in local environmental conditions or reporting practices. The data also includes scattered instances of fatalities, generally limited to a few months each year, indicating the sporadic but serious risk posed by severe cases, which can lead to complications such as renal failure, meningitis, or respiratory distress. A notable year for fatal cases was 2020, with a higher-than-usual number of deaths distributed primarily toward September and October, suggesting either the increased virulence of strains circulating that year or potential lapses in timely medical intervention.
