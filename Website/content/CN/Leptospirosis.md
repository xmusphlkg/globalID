# Leptospirosis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by Leptospira species, typically found in water or soil contaminated by the urine of infected animals. It affects both humans and animals, with symptoms ranging from mild flu-like signs to severe illness, including liver and kidney damage, meningitis, respiratory distress, and, in rare cases, death. The disease is more prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions and is often associated with activities involving contact with contaminated water, like swimming or farming. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial to prevent complications and improve outcomes.

## Highlights

- Leptospirosis cases in Chinese mainland show a seasonal pattern with higher incidences in the summer and autumn months, peaking particularly in September and October. <br/>
- The number of cases has seen significant fluctuations over the years, with notable spikes in 2015, 2020, 2021, and 2024. <br/>
- Deaths due to Leptospirosis remained relatively low overall, with occasional increases, particularly notable in the years 2015, 2020, and 2021. <br/>
- As of March 2025, the cases have significantly decreased, showing a downward trend in the last few months, indicating effective control measures or seasonality. <br/>

Analyzing the data from 2015 to early 2025, Leptospirosis cases in mainland China exhibit distinct seasonal surges, primarily reaching their peaks between July and December of each year. These spikes likely correlate with seasonal patterns and weather conditions that facilitate the spread of this bacterial disease, such as increased rainfall leading to more human contact with contaminated water sources. For example, a notable rise in cases is observed each year from July, peaking typically in September or October before declining towards the beginning of the following year. For instance, 2024 recorded a high of 128 cases in September and 65 cases in October, while previous years followed similar trends with 116 cases in September 2021 and 110 cases in September 2020.

Despite the seasonal variations in case numbers, mortality rates have remained relatively low with sporadic fatal incidents. Notable spikes in mortality occurred in selected months, such as October 2020 with 4 deaths and September 2021 with 2 deaths, indicating that while some months experienced significant upticks in mortality, the overall lethality of the disease remained controlled. This could reflect potentially effective healthcare responses or seasonal interventions to mitigate mortality despite rising case numbers in higher-risk months. Nonetheless, continuous monitoring and improved prevention strategies during peak seasons are crucial to managing and reducing both incidence and fatality rates of leptospirosis.

Across the period from March 2015 to March 2025, the mortality from leptospirosis in mainland China has remained consistently low relative to case counts, with only sporadic deaths reported. Most months, even those with elevated caseloads, reported zero deaths. Notable exceptions include isolated incidents in June 2015 and 2016 (one death each), July 2019 (two deaths), and several between 2020 and 2022, particularly September and October 2020, which had three and four deaths respectively despite relatively moderate case counts compared to later years. These suggest that while the incidence of leptospirosis can increase seasonally (notably in late summer/early autumn), mortality is not linearly correlated and instead reflects sporadic severity or delays in treatment.

The years 2020 to 2022 show a temporary rise in deaths, totaling 13 out of the 20 overall deaths recorded in the decade covered. Notably, September and October remain high-risk months both in incidence and fatalities, suggesting a seasonal or environmental factor. Yet, from 2023 onward, despite increasing case numbers (e.g., 128 in September 2024), the absence of death reports may hint at improvements in diagnostics, treatment delivery, or public health interventions during peak periods. The low case fatality ratio through the dataset further reflects strong clinical management despite seasonal surges.
