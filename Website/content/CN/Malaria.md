# Malaria

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, especially sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Symptoms typically include fever, chills, headache, and muscle aches, which can progress to severe anemia, respiratory distress, or organ failure if left untreated. Diagnosis is primarily through blood tests, and treatment involves antimalarial medications such as artemisinin-based combination therapies. Preventive measures include insecticide-treated nets, indoor residual spraying, and prophylactic antimalarial drugs.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases Over Time**: Malaria cases reduced significantly after 2020, although a resurgence is seen in 2023 and 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variability**: From 2014 to 2024, higher incidences typically occurred from May to September, suggesting seasonal influences. <br/>
- **Deaths Correlate with Cases**: Deaths generally follow the trend in cases, peaking when cases are high, emphasizing effective prevention and treatment. <br/>
- **Current Situation, July 2024**: As of July 2024, malaria cases have risen to 306 with two deaths, indicating an increase from previous months, necessitating close monitoring and intervention. <br/>

From July 2014 to July 2024, malaria cases in mainland China fluctuated, with a notable decline from 2014 to 2021, followed by a resurgence starting in 2022. Initially, cases were consistently above 200, peaking at 386 in June 2015. A seasonal pattern was evident, with higher cases during warmer months, but this trend diminished in later years. By 2020, cases dropped significantly, likely due to public health measures related to the COVID-19 pandemic. During 2020, monthly cases were much lower compared to previous years, with some months recording fewer than 100 cases.

Post-2020, cases remained low throughout 2021, rarely exceeding 100 per month. However, from August 2022, cases began increasing again, surpassing 200 by early 2023 and continuing this upward trend into 2024. Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, the death toll remained relatively low, with monthly deaths seldom exceeding three, indicating effective case management. The sharp decline up to 2021 suggests successful malaria control efforts, though the subsequent increase in cases from 2022 highlights potential challenges in maintaining these gains. The data underscores the need for sustained vigilance in malaria control to prevent a resurgence of cases.
