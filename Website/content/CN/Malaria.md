# Malaria

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. Predominantly found in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and Latin America, the disease manifests through symptoms including fever, chills, headache, and, if untreated, can lead to severe illnesses like anemia and cerebral malaria. Although preventable and curable, malaria poses significant public health challenges, with numerous cases and deaths annually. Efforts to combat malaria involve a combination of strategies, including insecticide-treated mosquito nets, indoor spraying, antimalarial medications, and ongoing research for effective vaccines.

## Highlights

1. Malaria cases in mainland China have decreased significantly from nearly 400 per month in 2015-2016 to just over 200 in October 2024, highlighting successful control efforts. <br/>

2. Throughout the period, the mortality rates remained low, generally staying below a few deaths per month, reflecting effective treatment and management strategies. <br/>

3. A resurgence in cases since late 2022 is notable, with numbers reaching over 300 monthly in some periods, pointing to new challenges that need addressing to prevent further increases. <br/>

4. Case numbers typically rise in the summer, indicating that climatic factors play a significant role in transmission, necessitating enhanced vector control during these periods. <br/>

The data from 2014 to 2024 on malaria in Chinese mainland reflects changes in disease prevalence and control efforts. Initially, from 2014 to 2015, there are notable fluctuations in malaria cases, with peaks during May and June, which align with the mosquito breeding season. Despite the prevalence, relatively low death rates suggest effective clinical interventions. However, outbreaks like those in May and June 2015 correlate with higher mortality, indicating possible lapses in preventive measures or vector control.

From 2016 to 2020, there is a gradual reduction in cases, especially from early 2020 onwards. This decline may be due to improved public health measures such as enhanced vector control, greater access to insecticide-treated nets, and better antimalarial treatments. The significant drop in cases from 2020 to 2021 could partly be due to COVID-19 restrictions, which reduced human-vector interactions. After 2021, there is a slight increase in cases by mid-2023, but death rates remain low, suggesting effective disease management.

The data from 2024 indicates a continuing battle with fluctuating cases, reflecting progress in reducing the burden of malaria, but also emphasizing the need for ongoing public health vigilance and interventions. These trends highlight the importance of sustained efforts in malaria control and prevention to maintain low transmission rates and mortality.
