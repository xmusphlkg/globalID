# Malaria

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, with the highest burden in sub-Saharan Africa. Symptoms include fever, chills, headache, and muscle aches, which can progress to severe complications if untreated. Malaria prevention focuses on mosquito control measures, such as insecticide-treated bed nets and indoor spraying, alongside antimalarial medications. Despite significant progress in reducing cases and deaths, malaria remains a major public health challenge, necessitating ongoing efforts in research, prevention, and treatment strategies.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases (2015-2020):** Malaria cases in the Chinese mainland dropped significantly from 250 in January 2015 to 68 in January 2021, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Resurgence in Cases (2021-2025):** After reaching a low in 2021, cases increased to 295 by January 2025, suggesting challenges in maintaining control or emerging resistance. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Mortality Rates:** Deaths have varied, with a decrease in 2020 and a slight increase afterward, indicating changes in treatment effectiveness or healthcare access. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** Higher cases in warmer months highlight the need for targeted interventions during these periods. <br/>

The data on malaria cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and fluctuations. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, there is a noticeable decline in the number of cases, with a peak in 2015 and a gradual decrease towards 2019. This period also shows a relatively stable number of deaths, with occasional spikes, such as in December 2019. The decline in cases could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, and possibly the implementation of effective malaria control programs.

From 2020 onwards, there is a significant drop in malaria cases, particularly in 2020, which could be linked to the global COVID-19 pandemic and the associated travel restrictions and public health interventions that inadvertently reduced malaria transmission. However, starting in 2022, there is a resurgence in cases, peaking in 2023, before stabilizing somewhat in 2024. This resurgence might be due to the relaxation of COVID-19 restrictions, changes in vector control strategies, or environmental factors favoring mosquito breeding. The data also shows a consistent pattern of low mortality rates, suggesting effective treatment protocols and healthcare access for those affected by malaria.

Initially, from 2015 to 2019, there is a noticeable fluctuation in both cases and deaths, with a peak in cases around mid-2015 and a gradual decline towards the end of 2019. The number of deaths during this period also shows variability, with occasional spikes, such as in December 2019, where deaths reached four. This period reflects a relatively high burden of malaria, with consistent monthly cases often exceeding 200.

From 2020 onwards, there is a significant decline in both malaria cases and deaths, particularly noticeable in 2020 and 2021. This reduction could be attributed to enhanced public health measures, increased awareness, or other interventions. By 2022, the number of cases begins to rise again, but the death toll remains relatively low, suggesting improved management and treatment of malaria cases. The data for 2023 and 2024 shows a resurgence in cases, yet the death rate remains controlled, indicating that while malaria transmission may have increased, the healthcare system's ability to manage and treat the disease has improved, preventing a corresponding rise in mortality.
