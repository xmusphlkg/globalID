# Malaria

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, which are transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It occurs predominantly in tropical and subtropical regions, especially sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of South America. Common symptoms include fever, chills, headache, and flu-like illness. Severe cases can result in anemia, organ failure, and death. Malaria is preventable and treatable, with diagnosis based on blood tests and management typically involving antimalarial medications, vector control measures, and personal protection strategies to reduce transmission risk.

## Highlights

- Malaria cases in mainland China saw a drastic decline from over 300/month in 2015-2019 to less than 100/month in 2021-2022, before rising sharply again to over 300/month by late 2024 and throughout 2025.<br/>
- Deaths remained low overall, with most months reporting 0–2 fatalities; occasional spikes up to 4 deaths were observed, indicating generally effective case management.<br/>
- In 2025, reported monthly cases surged, peaking at 437 in June and 393 in December, suggesting resurgence or increased detection.<br/>
- The overall trend reveals successful earlier control, but recent increases highlight ongoing transmission and a need for strengthened surveillance and intervention.<br/>

Analysis of malaria data from 2015 to 2025 in Chinese mainland shows notable shifts in incidence and seasonality. Cases were relatively stable and moderate between 2015 and 2019 (typically 180–370 monthly), with low but persistent mortality. A steep decrease in cases began in early 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic; monthly totals dropped to as low as 40, and deaths became rare or absent, which suggests impact from pandemic-related movement restrictions and health system shifts rather than true elimination or interruption of transmission.

From late 2022, malaria cases rebounded sharply, with monthly incidence returning to pre-pandemic levels and exceeding previous peaks in 2025 (over 400 cases per month). Throughout the decade, deaths remain sporadic and generally very low, suggesting stable case fatality despite fluctuations in incidence. Seasonality is evident, with higher numbers in the summer months, consistent with vector activity cycles. The post-pandemic surge, especially evident in 2023–2025, likely reflects resumed travel, vector dynamics, or changes in reporting and surveillance rather than abrupt changes in local transmission alone. Overall, while malaria control improved during the COVID-19 era, the resurgence highlights persistent vulnerabilities and the need for strengthened, sustained efforts to prevent re-establishment, with ongoing monitoring vital to respond to dynamic transmission risks.

Analysis of malaria mortality in the Chinese mainland from late 2015 through 2025 shows consistently low monthly deaths, usually ranging between zero and three, even during periods of higher case counts. Sporadic spikes—such as four deaths in December 2019 and November 2024—are not tightly aligned to monthly case surges, suggesting that factors like delayed diagnosis, imported severe cases, or variation in case management may drive mortality more than sheer incidence. The clustering is minor, with single months standing out rather than recurring patterns, and there is no substantial seasonality in fatality relative to malaria notifications.

These trends reflect strong public health control, especially as indigenous transmission has been interrupted and most cases are imported. The sharp decline in both cases and deaths during 2020-2021 likely relates to COVID-19 travel restrictions and healthcare system shifts, with subsequent years returning to pre-pandemic trends. While the monthly case fatality rate is low, the persistence of infrequent deaths indicates ongoing vulnerability among specific groups—possibly travelers, individuals with comorbidities, or those with delays in seeking care. Overall, the pattern demonstrates successful containment but ongoing need for vigilance, particularly in rapid identification and management of malaria among high-risk individuals and in preventing reestablishment of local transmission.
