# Malaria

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of South America. Key symptoms include fever, chills, headache, muscle pain, and fatigue, which can progress to severe illness or death, especially in children and pregnant women. The disease poses a significant global health challenge, with efforts focused on prevention, control, and treatment through bed nets, insecticides, diagnostic testing, and antimalarial medications. Continuous research is vital to developing more effective vaccines and therapies.

## Highlights

- **Resurgence in Cases:** Malaria cases have significantly increased in 2025, with June recording 437 cases, the highest monthly count since 2015, indicating a resurgence in the disease. <br/>

- **Stable Mortality:** Despite increased cases, deaths have remained stable, with zero reported in June 2025, suggesting improvements in treatment effectiveness or healthcare access. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns:** Higher cases during warmer months show consistent seasonal trends, highlighting the influence of climatic factors on malaria transmission dynamics. <br/>

- **Long-term Variation:** A decline from 2020 to 2022 followed by a rise after 2022 may reflect shifts in underlying factors or changes in intervention efficacy. <br/>

An analysis of malaria cases in the Chinese mainland reveals fluctuating patterns over the specified years. From 2015 to 2016, monthly cases varied between 210 and 386, with occasional spikes in deaths, peaking at three in certain months. This period suggests a correlation between malaria transmission and environmental factors such as climate. The inconsistent alignment between case counts and mortality rates may indicate variability in disease severity or healthcare access.

Beginning in 2017, there was a noticeable decline in case numbers, with a significant drop in 2020, when cases fell below 100 monthly while mortality rates decreased substantially. Though case fluctuations persist, as seen in 2022, the overall downward trend over subsequent years reflects effective malaria control measures, potentially including improved vector control, health services, and increased public health awareness. Notable spikes in specific months, such as August 2022, may be attributed to external factors like changes in weather, travel, or reporting practices. By mid-2025, an uptick in cases with stable mortality rates suggests ongoing adaptation in healthcare strategies to manage malaria outcomes.

Further analysis is essential to understand recent case increases, evaluating if they signal new challenges or deviations from previous successful efforts. Continued improvements in surveillance and healthcare interventions are critical to maintaining progress against malaria, necessitating future studies to explore factors influencing these epidemiological trends.
