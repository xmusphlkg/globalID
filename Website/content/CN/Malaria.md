# Malaria

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening infectious disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. The disease is characterized by symptoms such as fever, chills, fatigue, and anemia. There are five species of Plasmodium that cause malaria in humans, with *Plasmodium falciparum* being the most severe. Malaria primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and parts of Latin America. Effective prevention methods include insecticide-treated nets, indoor spraying, and antimalarial drugs, while treatment involves antimalarial medications tailored to the type of infection.

## Highlights

- **Declining Malaria Cases**: There has been a significant reduction in malaria cases over the past decade, with monthly cases consistently dropping after 2019, reaching fewer than 100 cases per month in 2020-2021.<br/>
- **Increase in Cases in 2024**: A slight resurgence of malaria cases occurred in 2024, with cases rising to 282 in November, the highest recorded in the past year.<br/>
- **Low Mortality**: Malaria-related deaths have been generally low, with a few months in 2024 showing fatalities (e.g., 4 deaths in November).<br/>
- **Seasonal Variation**: Malaria cases show seasonal peaks, with higher numbers typically observed in the warmer months (June–August).<br/>

Malaria cases in mainland China have shown a general decline, particularly after 2016, though some monthly fluctuations remain. From 2014 to 2016, case numbers were consistently high, often exceeding 200 per month, with peaks such as June 2015 (over 350 cases). Mortality, while low, was notable in certain months, such as January 2015, with four deaths. This period likely reflects ongoing transmission and less efficient control measures.

From 2017 onward, the situation improved significantly, with a steady decline in both cases and deaths, especially from 2020. By 2021, cases dropped below 100 per month in many instances, with occasional spikes during summer. Mortality remained low, with very few deaths reported in these years. By 2024, despite slight increases in case numbers, the death rate remained minimal, indicating improved malaria control. Notably, case numbers remained low even during higher transmission months.

This downward trend can be attributed to strengthened malaria control efforts, including better surveillance, diagnostics, and treatment. The reduction in cases and mortality over time suggests that China is successfully managing malaria transmission, with recent years showing marked success in controlling the disease and bringing the country closer to its goal of malaria elimination.

This trend continued into 2017 and 2018, where deaths remained minimal, with a few months (e.g., August 2017 and November 2019) recording 2-4 deaths. Overall, the death toll averaged fewer than five per month during these years.

By 2020, as case numbers decreased, the mortality rate dropped further. From 2021 onward, deaths became rare, with many months showing zero fatalities, even when cases fluctuated. Despite the overall success in reducing malaria-related mortality, ongoing surveillance and control measures remain essential to prevent resurgence.
