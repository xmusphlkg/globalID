# Malaria

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. Predominantly found in tropical and subtropical regions, malaria symptoms include fever, chills, and flu-like illness. If untreated, it can lead to severe complications and death. The five Plasmodium species infecting humans are P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae, and P. knowlesi, with P. falciparum being the most dangerous. Prevention focuses on mosquito control, bed nets, and antimalarial drugs. Treatment involves prompt diagnosis and antimalarial medications, with ongoing research aimed at vaccine development.

## Highlights

- Malaria cases in mainland China have shown a steady decline, from monthly peaks of 386 cases in mid-2015 to consistently fewer than 100 cases per month by 2021, indicating significant progress in control efforts.<br/>
- After a low point in 2021â€“2022, there has been a resurgence in cases since mid-2022, with monthly counts reaching over 300 in some months of 2024.<br/>
- Mortality has remained low throughout the period, with only sporadic spikes, never exceeding 4 deaths per month.<br/>
- The recent data in 2024 reflects increased vigilance but suggests ongoing transmission, with September recording 242 cases and no deaths.<br/>

From 2014 to 2024, malaria cases in mainland China show a marked decline, especially noticeable from 2020 onwards. Prior to 2020, monthly cases fluctuated between 200 and 400, peaking in months such as June 2015 (386 cases) and May 2016 (369 cases). Deaths during this period were low but consistently present, usually between 1 and 3 per month, indicating an ongoing but controlled health risk. However, starting in 2020, cases sharply decreased, likely due to intensified public health interventions or external factors such as travel restrictions due to COVID-19. By 2021, cases dropped to below 100 per month, suggesting a significant improvement in malaria control efforts.

The resurgence of cases in 2022 and 2023, with spikes in months like August 2022 (217 cases) and June 2023 (264 cases), points to possible seasonal influences or shifts in public health measures. Nevertheless, the numbers remain well below pre-2020 levels, reflecting sustained progress. Mortality also remained consistently low throughout this period, generally not exceeding 2 or 3 deaths per month, underscoring the effectiveness of clinical management despite case fluctuations.
