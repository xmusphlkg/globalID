# Malaria

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. The disease primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, with the highest prevalence in sub-Saharan Africa. Symptoms include fever, chills, headache, and muscle aches, which can progress to severe complications like anemia, respiratory distress, and organ failure if not treated promptly. Malaria is preventable and treatable, yet it remains a major public health challenge, especially in low-resource settings. Prevention strategies include vector control measures, such as insecticide-treated nets and indoor residual spraying, as well as prophylactic antimalarial medications.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases**: Malaria cases in the Chinese mainland have significantly declined from a peak of 386 cases in June 2015 to just 236 cases in May 2024. <br/>

- **Mortality Reduction**: Deaths from malaria have decreased, with several months reporting zero deaths in recent years, including May 2024. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Higher malaria cases were observed during summer months (June to August) historically, but this seasonality has become less pronounced. <br/>

- **Effective Control Measures**: Consistent decline in both cases and fatalities indicates that malaria control measures in Chinese mainland have been effective over the years. <br/>

An analysis of malaria cases in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals fluctuating trends, with a notable decrease in cases beginning in early 2020. Initially, monthly cases ranged between 200 to 400, peaking during summer months, likely due to seasonal vector activity. A pronounced reduction was observed starting February 2020, which coincides with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, potentially impacting disease transmission dynamics and healthcare priorities. Malaria cases drastically dropped to single and double digits for several months in 2020 and early 2021, before slowly increasing again but remaining lower than pre-2020 levels.

Deaths attributed to malaria also reflected these trends, although they did not decrease as sharply as cases. Fatalities fluctuated between 0 to 5 monthly, with no clear seasonal pattern and occasional spikes not correlating directly with the number of cases. This could indicate variable healthcare quality, reporting practices, or other socio-environmental factors affecting mortality. The resurgence of cases in late 2022 and throughout 2023 demonstrates a partial rebound but still did not reach the pre-2020 figures, suggesting possible lasting impacts of the interventions or changes in vector control and public health responses post-pandemic.

Analysis of the mortality trends for malaria in mainland China reveals several key patterns. From 2014 to 2017, monthly deaths fluctuated modestly, generally oscillating between 0 to 4 deaths, with a total count gradually decreasing toward the latter part of this period. This is notable considering a concurrent slight decline in the reported cases. However, the data from 2018 onward indicates a discernible reduction in both the number of cases and fatalities, suggesting improving management or environmental factors reducing transmission risk. Despite fluctuating monthly case numbers, a sustained reduction in mortality rates can be seen, with many months reporting zero deaths.

A significant reduction in the number of cases is apparent in 2020, which interestingly corresponds to the COVID-19 pandemic period, possibly due to improved public health measures, such as enhanced hygiene practices and movement restrictions. Post-2020 data shows a persistent low number of malaria cases and deaths, albeit with occasional spikes. For instance, January 2020 had one of the highest death counts (2 deaths) compared to subsequent years with infrequent and isolated increments. This consistent low mortality rate post-2020 might reflect ongoing public health initiatives, high malaria awareness, and better healthcare responses or environmental changes that have effectively decreased malaria transmission and improved patient outcomes. Overall, while malaria cases continue to fluctuate, there is a clear trend towards decreased fatalities over the observed period.
