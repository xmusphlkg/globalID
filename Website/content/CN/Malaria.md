# Malaria

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female *Anopheles* mosquitoes. There are five parasite species that cause malaria in humans, with *Plasmodium falciparum* being the most deadly. Symptoms typically include fever, chills, headache, and vomiting, appearing 10–15 days after the infective bite. If untreated, malaria can lead to severe illness and death. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. Control measures include insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor spraying, prophylactic medications, and prompt diagnosis and treatment to reduce transmission and mortality.

## Highlights

- Malaria incidence in mainland China has markedly decreased since 2015, reaching a low in 2020–2022, followed by a moderate resurgence, with 257 cases reported in April 2025. <br/>
- The disease showed seasonality, peaking between May and August in most years, but annual totals from 2020 onward have remained significantly below pre-2020 levels. <br/>
- Mortality has stayed low throughout the observation period, with annual deaths typically under 10; 2024 saw a slight rise (16 deaths), though 2025 remains within normal range so far. <br/>
- Surveillance and control measures remain effective overall, with no signs of widespread outbreaks or uncontrolled transmission as of April 2025. <br/>

From 2015 to early 2025, malaria case counts in mainland China generally show a declining trend, with notable fluctuations and several low points from 2020 to 2022. Early years like 2015 and 2016 typically had monthly cases ranging from 200 to nearly 400, but by 2020 the numbers fell sharply—April to December 2020 saw a consistent drop in monthly cases, with many months recording fewer than 100. This sharp fall in reported cases possibly reflects the indirect effects of COVID-19-related travel restrictions and public health interventions, which limited vector-borne disease exposure and importation. However, 2023 and especially 2024 witnessed a resurgence, with monthly counts returning to and even exceeding the pre-2020 levels in several months, suggesting a potential rebound in malaria importations or surveillance recovery.

Mortality remained low throughout the period, with most months reporting between 0–2 deaths and sporadic peaks of up to 4 deaths. Death counts did not consistently correlate with case severity or volume—some higher case months had no reported deaths, while some lower case months had multiple fatalities. After the decline in 2020–2022, the slight uptick in malaria-associated deaths post-2023, including a few months with multiple fatalities (e.g., November 2024 with 4 deaths), raises concerns about clinical outcomes or delays in diagnosis. The combined trends point to successful case suppression in pandemic years, followed by a resurgence needing renewed surveillance and prevention strategies.

Between 2015 and 2017, malaria deaths in mainland China appeared sporadically, with several months recording up to three fatalities. Monthly case loads ranged from about 190 to 390, indicating ongoing transmission. Though deaths were not consistently high, their persistent occurrence suggested gaps in early diagnosis or treatment accessibility. A shift is noticeable starting in 2018, as both overall cases and deaths showed a downward trend, culminating in 2020, which had some of the lowest case numbers and only a handful of deaths. This period likely reflects the impact of strengthened public health measures or external factors affecting disease mobility.

From 2021 onward, cases gradually rose again, but deaths mostly remained isolated, with many months recording none. This suggests improved clinical management, where prompt diagnosis and treatment may have reduced fatal outcomes despite continued transmission. However, between 2022 and 2024, scattered months still saw spikes up to four deaths, possibly reflecting localized outbreaks or challenges in healthcare access in specific regions. The generally low case-fatality ratio through recent years highlights progress in malaria control, but the periodic resurgence of deaths indicates that elimination goals must be supported by sustained interventions. Continued surveillance and targeted response remain essential to mitigate mortality as malaria transitions from endemic to low-incidence status in the region.
