# Malaria

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It predominantly occurs in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Asia, and Latin America. The disease manifests through symptoms such as fever, chills, headache, and vomiting, appearing typically 10-15 days post-bite. Malaria can lead to severe complications like anemia, respiratory distress, and cerebral malaria if untreated. Efforts to combat malaria include preventive measures like insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, and antimalarial medications, alongside ongoing research for effective vaccines.

## Highlights

- Malaria cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant decline from 2015 to 2020, with the lowest case numbers recorded in 2020. <br/>
- After 2020, cases started to rise again, with notable surges in 2023 and a marked increase through 2025, peaking at 437 cases in June 2025. <br/>
- Deaths have remained relatively low throughout this period, with sporadic increases, such as 4 deaths in November 2024 and 3 in February 2025. <br/>
- The resurgence observed in 2025 calls for strengthened malaria control interventions to prevent further escalation and protect public health. <br/>

The trajectory of malaria cases in the Chinese mainland from mid-2015 to mid-2025 highlights significant fluctuations and trends. From 2015 to 2019, case numbers varied with notable peaks, such as in January and February 2016, surpassing 300 each month. During these years, deaths remained relatively stable, reflecting surges inconsistently, as seen with December 2019's unusual spike of four deaths amid decreasing cases. By early 2020, a dramatic drop occurred, reaching a low of 40 cases by May 2020, suggesting effective control measures or environmental impacts on transmission.

From 2021 onwards, there was a modest increase in cases, particularly noticeable from May 2023 onwards, indicating factors like vector adaptations or socioeconomic changes might be influencing transmission. Deaths persisted at a low but steady number, potentially highlighting improvements in healthcare or malaria management strategies. However, 2025 saw a resurgence, notably with June and July peaks almost doubling compared to the previous year, which could raise concerns and prompt reevaluation of public health strategies.

This analysis underscores the challenges of malaria control, pointing to the necessity for continuous surveillance and adaptive strategies to address the evolving patterns and prevent future outbreaks effectively. The data reflect both successes in temporary suppression and the ongoing complexity of achieving sustained malaria control.
