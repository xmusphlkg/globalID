# Malaria

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Symptoms include fever, headaches, chills, and, if untreated, can lead to severe complications or death. Malaria prevention strategies involve insecticide-treated mosquito nets, indoor residual spraying, and antimalarial medications. Despite significant global efforts to control and reduce malaria transmission, challenges such as drug resistance, insecticide resistance, and lack of healthcare access continue to hinder eradication efforts.

## Highlights

- **Recent Rise in Cases:** Since early 2023, malaria cases in the Chinese mainland have surged, peaking at 437 in June 2025. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite more cases, deaths are stable and few, with a maximum of four in a month since 2015. <br/>

- **Seasonal Pattern:** Malaria cases typically rise between June and August, showing a clear seasonal trend. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Trends:** After a significant drop in 2020 and 2021, cases increased again in late 2022, underscoring the need for continued vigilance and preventive strategies. <br/>

From August 2015 to August 2025, malaria cases in the Chinese mainland exhibit fluctuations, revealing important epidemiological patterns. In 2016, cases peaked with a high of 369 in May, marking a critical period in malaria transmission. However, from 2017 onward, there is a discernible decline, reaching a significant low by early 2022, particularly in January with only 26 cases. This decreasing trend likely reflects effective public health interventions and improved malaria control measures.

Interestingly, from 2023 onwards, there is a resurgence of cases, peaking in mid-2025 with 437 cases in June. This increase might be attributed to changes in vector control efforts, environmental factors, or climate variations impacting malaria transmission cycles. Although the number of cases varies, deaths remain relatively low throughout the years, indicating effective treatment and management strategies in place, thereby reducing mortality despite high case numbers at times.

The data suggest a seasonal pattern, with higher cases often recorded in the warmer months, highlighting the influence of environmental conditions such as temperature and rainfall on mosquito breeding and malaria transmission dynamics. These observations emphasize the continued need for vigilance in malaria control efforts and adaptation of strategies in response to changing transmission patterns to maintain effective disease management and minimize potential outbreaks.

The data on malaria in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 reflects fluctuations in case numbers and deaths, indicating an interplay of disease control efforts and potential seasonal variations. Early in the timeline, from 2015 to 2016, monthly malaria cases ranged between 195 and 369, with deaths mostly between 0 and 3. This period suggests relatively stable but persistent malaria transmission and mortality.

By 2020, there was a significant reduction in both malaria cases and deaths. Cases rarely exceeded 100 per month, and deaths decreased considerably. This decline coincides with the COVID-19 pandemic, where enhanced public health measures may have indirectly curbed malaria transmission.

From 2021 onward, cases remained low until mid-2022, when instances began to rise again, potentially due to public health measure relaxations or changes in environmental conditions favoring mosquito breeding. In 2024, a noticeable increase in deaths occurred, particularly in November, despite a resurgence to higher monthly cases.

The increase in cases and deaths in 2023 and 2024 hints at fluctuating control efforts and challenges, but improvements in medical treatments are suggested by low mortality despite high case counts in 2025. Collectively, this data underscores the shifting dynamics of malaria risk in China, influenced by public health actions, environmental factors, and healthcare accessibility over the reviewed period.
