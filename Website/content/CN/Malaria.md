# Malaria

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, with Africa bearing the highest burden. Symptoms typically include fever, chills, headache, and muscle aches, manifesting 10 to 15 days after the mosquito bite. Severe cases can lead to complications such as anemia, respiratory distress, and organ failure. Diagnosis often involves blood tests, while treatment relies on antimalarial medications like Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs). Efforts for control and prevention focus on vector management, bed nets, and vaccines.

## Highlights

- Steady Increase in Cases: Malaria cases have increased significantly in recent years, with the most recent months showing case numbers reaching above 400, signaling an upward trend. <br/>
- Fluctuating Mortality Rate: While deaths have fluctuated over the years, they generally remain low compared to the case numbers, indicating improved treatment or reporting methods. <br/>
- Seasonal Variation: Reflecting previous patterns, case numbers rise in warmer months, hinting at continued seasonality in malaria transmission. <br/>
- Recent Surge: The marked increase in cases from early to mid-2025 warrants heightened surveillance and control measures to prevent further escalation. <br/>

The reported malaria cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 exhibit a fluctuating trend with noticeable changes in both cases and deaths. From 2015 to 2019, monthly cases ranged from approximately 200 to 356, with deaths occurring sporadically. A significant drop in cases is observed in 2020, reducing to as low as 40, likely due to public health interventions influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have affected malaria transmission.

From 2021, malaria cases remained consistently low, mostly below 100 per month, likely due to ongoing control measures. However, in August 2022, a resurgence is apparent, with the number of cases increasing to levels similar to those seen earlier in the dataset. By 2025, a peak is reached with 437 cases in June. The sporadic nature of deaths during this entire period suggests some improvement in healthcare or treatment despite the peaks in case numbers, possibly due to better access to medical facilities or more effective treatments.

Overall, this trend reflects variability influenced by factors like public health measures, climate changes, vector adaptations, or shifts in reporting practices. The resurgence post-2020 highlights potential lapses in control strategies or vector resistance. Continuous monitoring and targeted interventions are vital to sustaining low transmission rates and minimizing malaria's public health impact.

Examining Malaria death data from 2015 to 2025 in the Chinese mainland shows fluctuating case numbers but a consistent low in deaths, indicating successful disease management. The data reflects a reduction in fatality rates despite persistent cases, pointing to effective interventions over the decade. In the early years, from 2015 to 2017, deaths were minimal, with most months recording zero or one fatality, demonstrating the effectiveness of initial public health responses.

From 2018 onward, sporadic peaks in deaths were observed, particularly in months like December 2019 and November 2024, with up to four fatalities, highlighting challenges in certain periods despite lower case counts. This suggests periods of either increased virulence or potential lapses in treatment efficacy. Continuing this trend, by 2025, although there were months with high Malaria cases, deaths remained relatively low, showing the resilience of public health strategies implemented over the years.

This sustained low mortality amidst fluctuating incidences of Malaria suggests improved healthcare delivery and disease management, which may include better access to medical care, enhanced diagnostic capabilities, and more effective treatment protocols. Overall, the pattern underscores the prioritization of healthcare quality and the effectiveness of policy measures in reducing Malaria's fatal impact in the region despite its continued prevalence.
