# Malaria

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, and South America. Key symptoms include fever, chills, headache, and muscle aches, appearing 10-15 days after the infective mosquito bite. If not treated promptly, malaria can lead to severe complications and death. Efforts to control malaria include insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, prophylactic antimalarial drugs, and rapid diagnostic testing. Despite significant progress, malaria remains a major public health challenge globally.

## Highlights

- Malaria incidence in China drastically declined after 2019, reaching a low in 2022, but has shown a modest resurgence through 2023–2025, with 213 cases reported in March 2025. <br/>
- Deaths remained low overall, with occasional spikes (e.g., 4 deaths in November 2024 and 3 in February 2025), indicating localized severity or delays in treatment. <br/>
- Monthly fluctuations show seasonal patterns, with higher case counts generally from June to August, suggesting vector activity aligns with warmer months. <br/>
- Compared to peak years like 2015–2016, the current burden is significantly reduced, reflecting sustained control efforts, though the recent uptick highlights the need for continued vigilance. <br/>

An analysis of the reported malaria data in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several notable trends. Initially, from 2015 to 2017, monthly case numbers were moderately high, peaking at 386 in June 2015. Deaths varied monthly, indicating challenges in malaria management or treatment effectiveness at the time. The substantial number of cases during these years likely reflects ongoing transmission dynamics, potentially influenced by climatic factors or seasonal changes affecting mosquito breeding.

Starting in 2020, a dramatic reduction in monthly case numbers is observed, reaching a low of 26 cases in January 2022. This decline could indicate successful malaria elimination strategies, improved public health interventions, or altered environmental conditions reducing vector habitats. The decrease in cases and sustained low death rates suggest effective control measures, increased public awareness, and better access to treatment.

However, from 2023 onwards, there is a slight resurgence, with monthly cases increasing to levels seen in earlier periods, hinting at potential re-establishment of transmission cycles. This underscores the need for continuous monitoring and dynamic strategies to prevent re-emergence. Despite the challenges, death rates have mostly remained low since 2021, suggesting effective case management. Nonetheless, the late 2024 reports show an increase in mortality, raising concerns around treatment access or resistance. Continuous surveillance and adaptive responses remain critical to sustaining advancements in malaria control on the Chinese mainland.

Despite fewer cases, deaths remained consistent, rarely exceeding two per month.

In 2020, a significant drop in cases was noted, likely due to global health measures affecting disease transmission. Case numbers started to increase again from 2021 onwards, with notable peaks in warmer months. This resurgence in cases was paralleled by deaths, which also peaked around these periods. Overall, the case fatality rate remained modest, with deaths rarely exceeding three in a month, reflecting possible advancements in treatment and management strategies over the years. The data highlights the seasonal nature of malaria and the ongoing efforts to control the disease, despite occasional spikes in both cases and fatalities.
