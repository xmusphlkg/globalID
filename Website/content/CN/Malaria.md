# Malaria

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected Anopheles mosquitoes. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, with the highest burden in sub-Saharan Africa. Symptoms include fever, chills, headache, and muscle aches, which can progress to severe complications if untreated. Malaria is preventable and curable, with prevention strategies including insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, and antimalarial drugs. Efforts to control and eliminate malaria focus on reducing transmission, improving access to diagnosis and treatment, and developing vaccines.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases (2015-2020):** Malaria cases decreased significantly from 236 in February 2015 to 126 in February 2020, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

- **Resurgence in Cases (2022-2025):** After a low in 2022, cases rose again, with 212 reported in February 2025, suggesting a resurgence needing renewed public health efforts. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Mortality Rates:** Deaths remained low but spiked occasionally, such as 4 deaths in November 2024, indicating the need for continuous monitoring. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** Higher cases in warmer months highlight the importance of targeted interventions during these periods. <br/>

The data on malaria cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and fluctuations. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, there is a noticeable seasonal pattern with higher cases typically occurring in the warmer months, such as May through August. This pattern aligns with the lifecycle of the Anopheles mosquito, which thrives in warmer climates. During this period, the number of cases generally fluctuates between 200 and 400 per month, with occasional spikes, such as in June 2015 and January 2016. The number of deaths per month remains relatively low, often not exceeding three, indicating effective management and treatment strategies during this period.

From 2020 onwards, there is a significant decline in malaria cases, particularly noticeable in 2020, where monthly cases drop to double digits, a stark contrast to previous years. This decline could be attributed to intensified public health measures, possibly influenced by the global COVID-19 pandemic, which may have led to increased health surveillance and mosquito control efforts. However, from 2022 onwards, there is a gradual increase in cases, peaking again in the warmer months, though not reaching the levels seen in the earlier years. The death toll remains low throughout, suggesting continued effective treatment protocols. The data indicates a successful reduction in malaria transmission, though the slight resurgence in later years suggests a need for sustained vigilance and control measures.
