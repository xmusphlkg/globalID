# Measles

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, predominantly affecting children. Transmitted through respiratory droplets from coughs and sneezes, it presents initial symptoms like high fever, runny nose, cough, red eyes, and sore throat. A red rash follows, starting on the face and spreading. Complications can include diarrhea, ear infections, pneumonia, encephalitis, and death, particularly in young children and the immunocompromised. While vaccine-preventable, measles remains a global health issue in areas with low vaccination rates. The MMR vaccine (measles, mumps, rubella) is highly effective in preventing this disease.

## Highlights

- Significant decrease in measles cases was observed from 2014 to 2020, with an initial high of over 8,000 cases in April 2015 dropping to just double-digit figures by early 2020. <br/>

- A consistent low incidence was maintained from 2020 to 2023, averaging below 100 cases per month, highlighting successful control measures. <br/>

- A slight increase in measles cases has been noted in 2024, from 51 cases in January to 201 in June, indicating a potential resurgence or seasonal influence. <br/>

- Despite rising cases in 2024, no measles-related deaths have been reported in recent years, suggesting effective treatment and vaccination strategies remain in place. <br/>

The reported data on measles cases in mainland China shows a notable fluctuation in incidence from 2014 to 2024, with a peak in cases observed between 2014 and 2016, followed by a gradual decline in subsequent years. The number of cases began to rise in early 2014, reaching a high in April 2015 with 8,265 cases, before starting a downward trend. This peak was likely driven by multiple factors, including potential gaps in vaccination coverage or public health measures. By 2017, the number of measles cases had decreased significantly, with annual totals continuing to decline through 2021, which suggests improved vaccination rates or stronger public health interventions.

From 2018 onwards, the data reveals a sustained period of low measles incidence, with monthly cases frequently remaining below 500. Notably, there were zero deaths recorded from 2018 to 2024, indicating a reduction in the disease's severity or improved clinical management of cases. The sharp reduction in measles cases from 2020 onwards may reflect the impact of broader public health measures, such as increased vaccination coverage, public awareness campaigns, or policies implemented during the COVID-19 pandemic that limited disease transmission. However, a slight increase in cases was observed in 2024, which may require further monitoring to understand if this represents a transient fluctuation or a potential resurgence.

The data on measles cases and deaths in mainland China from mid-2014 to mid-2024 indicates a notable pattern of fluctuating case numbers with declining mortality over time. Initially, there was a relatively high incidence of measles with significant numbers being recorded between 2014 and 2016, peaking at 8,265 cases in April 2015. Correspondingly, deaths during this period were observed in varying numbers, notably reaching a maximum of seven deaths also in April 2015. However, from 2017 onward, there is a clear and steady decline in both the number of cases and associated deaths. By 2020, the cases plummet to double digits, with no reported deaths from 2018 onward.

The data from 2020 through 2024 shows a significant reduction in measles cases, maintaining mostly double-digit figures, and a striking absence of reported measles-related deaths. This trend suggests that public health interventions, such as vaccination campaigns, were likely effective in reducing both the incidence and severity of measles cases. The reduction in measles mortality, particularly post-2018, underscores successful public health measures and improvements in healthcare access and quality, which may have contributed to better management of both measles prevention and treatment in the Chinese mainland.
