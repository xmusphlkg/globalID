# Measles

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus. Transmission occurs primarily through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Typical symptoms include high fever, cough, runny nose, and conjunctivitis, followed by a characteristic red rash that spreads from the face downward. Measles can lead to severe complications such as pneumonia, encephalitis, and death, especially in young children and immunocompromised individuals. Vaccination with the measles-containing vaccine is effective in preventing infection and controlling outbreaks.

## Highlights

- Measles incidence in the Chinese mainland peaked in early 2016, followed by a sustained and dramatic decline through subsequent years. <br/>
- Since mid-2020, annual case counts have remained low, consistently below 350 per month, with further reductions noted in 2023 and 2024. <br/>
- No measles-related deaths have been reported since 2018, indicating strong disease control and improved clinical management. <br/>
- As of December 2025, measles activity remains very low (90 cases), suggesting that China maintains effective surveillance and high coverage of immunization programs, with no current evidence of outbreaks or mortality. <br/>

Analysis of the measles case data in the Chinese mainland from late 2015 through 2025 reveals a pronounced multi-year downward trend in incidence, punctuated by marked seasonality and several abrupt inflection points. Cases surged sharply in early 2016, peaking at nearly 5,500 per month in March and April before dropping consistently through the year and into subsequent years. By 2018 and 2019, monthly cases were consistently below 700 and continued to fall further, with months in late 2020 through 2025 rarely exceeding 200 cases outside brief periodic upswings. This improvement in control is underscored by the complete elimination of reported deaths after 2019, indicating progress both in prevention through immunization and in clinical management.

The dramatic decline post-2019, particularly the unusually low monthly figures in 2020 and 2021, strongly suggests sizable impacts from broader public health interventions linked to the COVID-19 pandemic, including travel restrictions, social distancing, and heightened surveillance. As these controls eased, cases gradually increased from early 2024 through early 2025, but without corresponding mortality. The persistence of a seasonal pattern, with higher notifications in spring, reflects the underlying transmission dynamics but at a much suppressed baseline. Taken together, the data point to successful long-term suppression of measles transmission and near-elimination of fatal outcomes; the low-level, residual case numbers in recent years likely represent isolated outbreaks or importations rather than ongoing endemic transmission.

Analysis of measles death data in Chinese mainland from late 2015 to the end of 2025 reveals a striking and sustained reduction in mortality over the observed decade. In the 2015–2017 period, measles deaths averaged a few fatalities per month, with the highest concentration in early 2016 (peaking at 5 in March). The case fatality rate during these months, though low, underscored the persistent risk associated with measles outbreaks. However, beginning in 2018, the dataset demonstrates a sharp decline, with several months consistently reporting zero deaths despite ongoing transmission and hundreds of reported cases, a trend that persists unwaveringly from 2019 onward.

This pronounced decline points to a pivotal shift in measles control and case management, likely driven by improvements in vaccination coverage, prompt diagnosis, and enhanced clinical care. Even as case numbers continue on modest seasonal cycles, especially in recent years (with mild rebounds in 2024–2025), mortality is virtually eliminated, suggesting that the epidemic curve is effectively uncoupled from deaths. The last death was recorded in January 2018, and for more than seven years, no deaths occurred despite continued reporting of cases, including episodic increases. Such sustained zero mortality signals breakthroughs in public health measures and sharply reduced disease severity, although the persistence of cases highlights ongoing transmission risk and the need for vigilance in maintaining immunization and rapid response systems.
