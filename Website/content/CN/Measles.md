# Measles

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral infection caused by the measles virus, a member of the paramyxovirus family. It primarily affects children and is spread through respiratory droplets from coughs or sneezes of infected individuals. Initial symptoms include high fever, cough, runny nose, and conjunctivitis, followed by a characteristic rash that starts on the face and spreads across the body. Measles can lead to severe complications, such as pneumonia, encephalitis, and death, especially in young children and immunocompromised individuals. Vaccination is highly effective in preventing the disease and is a critical public health tool in reducing measles outbreaks globally.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in Cases**: From a peak in 2016, measles cases in China have since shown a consistent downward trend, decreasing from thousands to just 55 by September 2025. <br/>

- **Recent Uptick Followed by Decline**: A brief rise in cases from early 2025 was observed, followed by a decline, suggesting fluctuations before stabilizing at lower numbers in September. <br/>

- **Zero Deaths**: Since 2018, no measles-related deaths have been reported, indicating successful management of severe outcomes despite fluctuating case numbers. <br/>

- **Effective Control Measures**: The overall decline in cases suggests improved vaccination coverage and effective public health interventions have been crucial in controlling measles transmission in the region. <br/>

Between September 2015 and February 2016, measles cases in mainland China surged from 1100 to a peak of 5488 cases in April 2016. This rise suggests potential seasonal trends, reduced immunity, or healthcare gaps, but with relatively low mortality, as deaths ranged from zero to five per month during this peak period, indicating some effectiveness in disease management and control measures.

Following the 2016 peak, reported measles cases significantly declined, reaching lower levels each year through 2017 and further dropping in 2020 to the lowest since 2015. This steady decline may be attributed to improved vaccination coverage and public health interventions post-outbreak. Remarkably, reported deaths fell to zero during early 2017 through late 2025, suggesting effective healthcare responses, possibly a low case fatality ratio, or insufficient death reporting.

From 2020 onwards, cases remained consistently low, despite a slight uptick in 2025. This trend indicates successful community immunity and heightened public health awareness. Variations in monthly cases might reflect minor regional outbreaks or impacts from global crises like COVID-19, which could have disrupted healthcare access and vaccination efforts. The dataset showcases successful public health efforts while highlighting the necessity for ongoing surveillance and immunization strategies to maintain reduced incidence levels.
