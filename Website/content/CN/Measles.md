# Measles

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, characterized by symptoms including high fever, cough, runny nose, and a distinctive red rash. It spreads through respiratory droplets from coughs and sneezes. Complications can include pneumonia, encephalitis, and even death, particularly in young children and immunocompromised individuals. The disease can be effectively prevented through the measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine. Despite global vaccination efforts, measles outbreaks still occur, especially in areas with low vaccination coverage.

## Highlights

- Significant decline in measles cases from a peak of 10,563 in May 2014 to 162 in May 2024, indicating enhanced control measures. <br/>
- No deaths reported since January 2019, highlighting improved clinical management and healthcare preparedness. <br/>
- Cases have remained below 200 per month since January 2020, suggesting sustained low transmission rates. <br/>
- A slight uptick in cases observed in April and May 2024 may warrant investigation into potential outbreaks or surveillance system enhancements. <br/>

Analyzing measles cases in mainland China from 2014 through mid-2024, we observe a drastic decline in both cases and fatalities. The initial surge in cases—peaking at 10,563 in May 2014—rapidly diminishes over the years following strategic public health interventions and widespread immunization. From a high incidence observed in the earlier years, with some variability in the number of reported cases and occasional spikes in mortality, there is a steep reduction in cases post-2016, with monthly figures frequently falling below 1,000.

The reduction becomes even more pronounced from 2018 onwards, with cases consistently under 1,000 and no subsequent deaths recorded, signaling effective control measures. The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic appears to correlate with a notable decrease in measles reporting, as seen with the dip to 66 cases in February 2020. This reduction suggests possible impacts of health service disruptions or reporting changes during the pandemic. The trend of low case numbers continues into the ensuing years, not surpassing 200 cases per month, indicative of the success in sustained disease management. Even sporadic increases in cases, like the 162 recorded in May 2024, remain minor compared to the initial years in the dataset, reinforcing the effectiveness of the ongoing efforts to maintain measles suppression.

However, as the timeline progresses, there's a notable downward trend in cases, accompanied by an equally significant decline in death rates. By 2015, although case numbers fluctuated, fatalities decreased, suggesting improved control measures.

From 2017 onward, measles cases consistently dropped, with death counts falling to zero from 2018 onwards, a striking trend that implies highly effective vaccination strategies and stronger public health responses. Despite the occasional rise in case numbers, which could align with seasonal patterns, the sustained absence of measles-related mortality throughout the last six years of the dataset underscores the success of intervention programs in the Chinese mainland. Overall, the data paints a picture of effective measles management and the capacity of health systems to prevent related deaths.
