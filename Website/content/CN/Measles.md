# Measles

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, primarily affecting children. It spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing and can survive in the air for up to two hours. Symptoms typically appear 10–12 days after exposure and include high fever, cough, runny nose, conjunctivitis, and a characteristic rash. Measles can lead to serious complications such as pneumonia, encephalitis, and death, particularly in malnourished children or those with weakened immune systems. Vaccination is highly effective and is the main strategy for prevention and outbreak control worldwide.

## Highlights

- Measles incidence in mainland China has declined dramatically from over 7,000 monthly cases in early 2015 to fewer than 300 per month in 2025, indicating sustained control efforts. <br/>
- Since mid-2020, the monthly case count has remained consistently low, averaging below 300 cases, with 2025 data showing continued low transmission. <br/>
- No measles-related deaths have been reported since January 2019, suggesting a significant reduction in measles severity and/or improved clinical management. <br/>
- There is a mild seasonal increase in early spring and summer months, but overall surveillance indicates that the disease remains well-contained as of May 2025. <br/>

The reported measles cases in the Chinese mainland show a clear pattern of significant decline over the years from 2015 through 2025. Peak monthly cases were recorded in the first half of 2015, particularly in May with over 7,600 cases, after which a marked downward trend began. From 2016 onward, seasonal fluctuations persisted, but the overall case loads decreased sharply, with 2019 and 2020 showing monthly figures mostly below 500. This decline continued into 2021 and beyond, where consistent single- and double-digit monthly reports became common, suggesting sustained suppression of transmission or successful public health interventions.

Following the early 2020 onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, a substantial drop in measles incidence is observed, which likely correlates with lockdowns, enhanced hygiene, mask mandates, and reductions in population mobility—all of which disrupt measles transmission routes. Post-2020 data confirm stable suppression of outbreaks without resurgence, reflected in the near elimination of measles-related deaths after 2018. Even with gradual case elevation in 2024–2025 peaking at 344 cases in January 2025, totals remain far below historical levels and mortality remains at zero, underscoring effective measles control measures and likely high vaccination coverage or improved health surveillance infrastructure.

From mid-2015 through early 2016, measles fatalities in mainland China remained relatively rare but non-negligible—the highest burden falling within this early interval. A total of 30 deaths occurred in the 12 months between May 2015 and April 2016, corresponding to 44,195 reported cases, representing a crude case fatality rate (CFR) of roughly 0.068%. Following that period, both cases and deaths declined markedly. Between mid-2016 and the end of 2017, deaths were sporadic, never exceeding one per month from January 2017 onward, even as monthly case counts occasionally exceeded 1,000, suggesting possible intervention effectiveness or improved case management.

Beyond 2018, the national profile shifted further with an extremely low or near-zero death toll despite continuous—albeit low-level—virus transmission. From January 2018 through May 2025, more than 10,000 measles cases were recorded without a single death. This reflects significant improvements in public health performance, likely involving enhanced vaccination coverage, rapid case identification, and better healthcare access. The decoupling of incidence and mortality suggests that though transmission may persist in small pockets, the most severe outcomes of measles are now largely preventable in the current health system context.
