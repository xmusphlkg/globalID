# Measles

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, a member of the paramyxovirus family. It primarily affects children and is transmitted through respiratory droplets from coughs and sneezes. Symptoms typically appear 10-12 days after exposure and include high fever, cough, runny nose, and a characteristic red rash that spreads across the body. Complications can be severe, leading to pneumonia, encephalitis, and even death. Vaccination with the measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine is highly effective in preventing the disease and is crucial for maintaining herd immunity and preventing outbreaks.

## Highlights



- **Decline in Cases:** From 2015 to 2020, measles cases dropped significantly from thousands to under 100 per month, indicating effective public health measures and vaccination efforts. <br/>

- **Stable Low Mortality:** Between 2020 and 2023, cases remained low with no deaths, suggesting successful control and high vaccination coverage. <br/>

- **Recent Case Increase:** Since 2024, cases have risen, peaking in early 2025, which may indicate a need for renewed public health focus or changes in population immunity. <br/>

- **No Recent Deaths:** Despite the rise in cases, no deaths have been reported since 2019, highlighting effective medical management and possibly milder outbreaks. <br/>

The data on measles cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals a significant decline in the number of reported cases over the years. In 2015, the number of cases was notably high, peaking in April with 8,265 cases. This period also saw a relatively higher number of deaths, with a total of 29 deaths reported throughout the year. However, from 2016 onwards, there is a clear downward trend in both the number of cases and deaths. By 2019, the number of cases had decreased significantly, with no deaths reported throughout the year, indicating improved control measures and possibly higher vaccination coverage.

From 2020 onwards, the number of cases continued to decline, reaching a low point in 2021 with monthly cases often below 100. This trend suggests effective public health interventions and possibly the impact of COVID-19 related measures, such as increased hygiene practices and reduced travel, which may have contributed to the reduced transmission of measles. However, a slight increase in cases is observed in 2024, with a peak in June at 201 cases, but still with no reported deaths. This resurgence could indicate a need for renewed focus on vaccination campaigns and public health strategies to prevent potential outbreaks. Overall, the data reflects a successful reduction in measles cases over the decade, highlighting the importance of sustained public health efforts.
