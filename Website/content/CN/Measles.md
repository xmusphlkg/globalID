# Measles

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, which belongs to the Paramyxoviridae family. It is primarily spread through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. The disease is characterized by a high fever, cough, runny nose, inflamed eyes, and a distinctive red rash that typically starts on the face and spreads downward. Measles can lead to serious complications, particularly in young children, such as pneumonia, encephalitis, and death. Vaccination with the measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine is the most effective way to prevent the disease and its spread.

## Highlights

- **Declining Measles Cases**: The number of measles cases has steadily decreased over the past decade, with cases remaining very low from 2019 onwards, often below 100 monthly.  
<br/>
- **Low Mortality**: There have been no deaths from measles since 2019, reflecting effective control measures and vaccination coverage.  
<br/>
- **Recent Trends**: In 2024, monthly cases have been under 200, with November 2024 reporting 116 cases, indicating a slight increase compared to earlier in the year, but still at very low levels.  
<br/>
- **Sustained Low Incidence**: The sustained low incidence suggests continued success of public health strategies, including vaccination programs.  
<br/>

The reported data for measles cases in mainland China reveals significant fluctuations in case numbers over the years, with notable peaks during the first half of 2015. In particular, the months of January to May 2015 show a sharp rise, peaking at 8,265 cases in April before tapering off in subsequent months. This increase could be indicative of a local outbreak or lapses in vaccination coverage at that time. However, from 2016 onward, the case counts generally exhibit a steady decline, reaching consistently low levels by 2018 and maintaining these levels through the following years, with monthly cases often fewer than 200.

The low number of deaths across this period is striking, especially considering the higher case numbers in 2015. The recorded deaths, mostly concentrated in the first few months of 2015, appear to have been effectively managed through improvements in healthcare interventions and public health responses. By 2020 and beyond, the number of measles cases reported each month is notably reduced, often fewer than 100, which could reflect the success of vaccination campaigns, the wider adoption of immunization practices, and potentially changes in the reporting or surveillance systems. Notably, no deaths have been recorded after 2015, indicating an effective control of the disease's fatal outcomes. The overall trend suggests a successful reduction in measles incidence, which aligns with global efforts to eliminate measles through vaccination.
