# Measles

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, a member of the paramyxovirus family. It primarily affects children but can occur at any age. The disease is transmitted through respiratory droplets from coughs or sneezes of infected individuals. Symptoms typically include high fever, cough, runny nose, inflamed eyes, and a characteristic red rash that spreads across the body. Complications can be severe, leading to pneumonia, encephalitis, and death, especially in malnourished children and those with weakened immune systems. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, with the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine offering high protection.

## Highlights

- **Declining Cases:** Measles cases showed a strong decline from 2015, with peaks over 8,000 cases in April 2015, to consistently below 100 cases per month from 2020 to early 2022. <br/>
- **Recent Increase:** Since late 2023, cases have risen modestly, peaking at 344 in January 2025, possibly due to reduced vaccination rates or enhanced disease transmission. <br/>
- **Stable Death Rate:** The death rate remains very low, with zero deaths monthly since 2023, suggesting effective clinical management and vaccination impact. <br/>
- **Current Trend:** Despite the recent rise in cases, the situation in 2025 remains controlled, with no fatalities and lower case numbers compared to historical levels. <br/>

From March 2015 to March 2025, measles cases in mainland China show a steep overall decline. Initial years saw high incidence, notably peaking at 8,265 cases in April 2015. A seasonal pattern is evident, with spikes generally occurring in the first half of each year and lower numbers toward the end. Deaths, while present early on, remained low relative to case counts and disappeared entirely after 2018, indicating improved treatment and control.

From 2016 through 2017, case numbers gradually dropped, though still exceeding 1,000 per month in peak seasons. Starting in 2018, cases consistently stayed below 700 monthly, falling further to double-digit monthly averages from 2020. This period coincided with COVID-19-related public health interventions, which may have contributed to decreased transmission.

This may reflect restored mobility and lowered vigilance post-pandemic, yet measles control remains strong. Importantly, no measles-related deaths were reported from 2019 onward, underscoring continued success in clinical and immunization efforts. The data reflects both sustained suppression of outbreaks and effective long-term public health strategies, with strong indications of nationwide herd immunity taking hold after 2020.
