# Measles

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Measles is a highly contagious viral disease caused by the measles virus, primarily affecting children. It spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Common symptoms include high fever, cough, runny nose, conjunctivitis, and a distinctive red rash that usually begins on the face and spreads downward. Complications can include pneumonia, encephalitis, and death, particularly in malnourished children or those with weakened immune systems. Measles can be prevented through vaccination with the measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine. Despite available vaccines, outbreaks can occur in unvaccinated populations.

## Highlights

- Measles incidence in China has seen a dramatic decline from over 8,000 monthly cases in April 2015 to 234 cases in April 2025, indicating sustained long-term control.<br/>
- No measles-related deaths have been reported since December 2019, highlighting significant improvements in case management and early detection.<br/>
- A slight uptick in monthly cases has been observed since early 2024, rising from 51 in January 2024 to a peak of 344 in January 2025, suggesting increased transmission or surveillance.<br/>
- Despite the recent rise, overall case numbers remain historically low, and the absence of fatalities underscores continued success in vaccination and public health response.<br/>

From 2015 to 2025, measles incidence in mainland China showed a dramatic decline, with the most significant drop occurring between 2015 and 2018. In 2015, the average monthly case count exceeded 4,700, peaking in April (8,265 cases), but this declined steadily, reaching fewer than 300 monthly cases by late 2017. This trend reflects the impact of intensified immunization campaigns, enhanced surveillance, and public health interventions. The mortality figures corroborate this success: measles-related deaths, although sporadically present in earlier years, vanished entirely after December 2018.

From 2019 onward, the monthly caseload stabilized at a low baseline, generally oscillating between 50 and 200 cases, with no reported fatalities over these six years. The year 2020 saw unusually low numbers, especially from February through April, likely influenced by COVID-19-related mobility restrictions and non-pharmaceutical interventions. A mild resurgence in 2024 and a more noticeable increase in early 2025 indicate potential waning vaccine coverage or surveillance sensitivities. However, the absence of deaths across recent years signals that health systems have maintained control over measles transmission and disease outcomes.

Between 2015 and early 2017, deaths from measles in the Chinese mainland showed notable fluctuation, peaking at seven in April 2015 and gradually declining with isolated spikes. The overall lethality, while relatively low in proportion to reported cases, was non-negligible — with approximately 45 deaths out of nearly 60,000 cases during this 27-month span, implying an average case fatality ratio of roughly 0.075%. Seasonal trends were evident early on, with death counts often following case surges in the spring months, though exceptions existed, such as five deaths in a low-case period like November 2015.

From mid-2017 onward, the epidemiological landscape changed markedly. Deaths from measles disappeared entirely after January 2018, despite recurring (though declining) monthly case numbers. This sustained zero-death trend over more than seven years strongly suggests the impact of improved outbreak response, wider immunization coverage, and possibly faster case identification and clinical management. The sharp drop in measles mortality, even as sporadic cases persisted, reflects a significant decoupling of incidence and mortality — a hallmark of successful public health intervention.
