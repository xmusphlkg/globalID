# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by *Neisseria meningitidis*, affecting the protective membranes around the brain and spinal cord. It spreads through respiratory droplets and close contact, primarily in crowded environments. Symptoms include sudden fever, headache, stiff neck, nausea, and sensitivity to light. Prompt diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are essential to prevent severe complications like brain damage, hearing loss, or death. Vaccines are available to protect against most common serogroups. High-risk populations, such as infants, adolescents, and those with weakened immune systems, are especially vulnerable.

## Highlights

### Key Epidemiological Trends and Current Situation of Meningococcal Meningitis in Mainland China (as of November 2024)

- **Decreased Case Frequency**: Monthly cases have decreased, with fewer than 10 cases reported in recent months (November 2024: 10 cases). <br/>
- **Stable Mortality**: Fatalities remain relatively consistent, with occasional spikes, such as July 2024 (2 deaths) and November 2024 (2 deaths). <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks**: Higher incidence is observed in winter and spring months. <br/>
- **Ongoing Monitoring**: Despite the overall decline, localized outbreaks and deaths still occur, emphasizing the need for continued surveillance. <br/>

The data on Meningococcal meningitis in mainland China reveals significant fluctuations in the incidence and mortality rates across the years, with notable peaks and valleys in both cases and deaths. From 2014 to 2024, the monthly case counts varied considerably, with notable surges in early months of some years—particularly in December 2015 (20 cases) and December 2017 (24 cases)—indicating potential seasonal or environmental factors influencing the disease's spread. Additionally, the death toll showed a pattern of being sporadically higher during certain months, especially in months with larger case numbers. For example, in December 2017, there were 24 cases, resulting in 6 deaths, underscoring a heightened mortality rate during that period.

Despite these fluctuations, there is a general trend of decreasing fatalities in recent years, with several months in 2023 and 2024 reporting zero deaths. This decline could be attributed to improvements in healthcare infrastructure, public health response strategies, and possibly vaccination coverage, which may have contributed to better management and outcomes. However, the persistence of occasional death tolls—such as in January 2024 (1 death out of 17 cases) and July 2024 (2 deaths out of 9 cases)—indicates that while the overall burden might be decreasing, the disease still carries a significant risk. Overall, the data reflects a complex, dynamic pattern of Meningococcal meningitis in mainland China, influenced by both preventable factors and challenges in disease management.
