# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, characterized by an inflammation of the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. It primarily affects infants, adolescents, and young adults. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or close contact with an infected person. Symptoms include sudden fever, headache, stiff neck, nausea, and altered mental status. Without prompt treatment, it can lead to serious complications or death. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent meningococcal disease, and early antibiotic treatment is crucial for those infected. Outbreaks are more common in certain parts of the world, particularly sub-Saharan Africa.

## Highlights

- Meningococcal meningitis cases have generally remained low from 2020 onward, with monthly counts below 20, indicating stable endemic levels in the Chinese mainland. <br/>
- Seasonal trends persist, with higher incidence typically observed from December to March, peaking in January 2025 with 26 cases. <br/>
- Mortality remains sporadic but low, with 1â€“2 deaths reported in several months; March 2025 recorded 1 death, consistent with past seasonal patterns. <br/>
- No signs of a major outbreak are present as of March 2025, and disease control efforts appear effective in maintaining a low case fatality rate. <br/>

Initially, the data demonstrates a generally stable trend in terms of both cases and mortality from 2015 through 2019, with slight fluctuations observed. This period shows periodic rise in cases notable in the beginning months of each year, likely correlating with seasonal changes or other environmental factors. The data also reveal variable mortality rates, with specific months indicating higher death rates, suggesting potential factors influencing either the disease severity or healthcare response efficiency.

A significant decline in reported cases occurs in 2020, which may correlate with broader global health interventions due to COVID-19 pandemic measures, leading to reduced transmission or shifts in healthcare resource allocation. Following this decreased activity in 2020, cases start to rise again post-2021, reaching certain peaks, notably in January 2025, where the highest number of cases (26) is observed. Factors influencing this surge could range from changes in population immunity, variations in pathogen virulence, or changes in preventative measures. Death rates present an intermittent rise alongside the case numbers, signifying ongoing challenges in managing the disease effectively. Overall, trends highlight the importance of continuous public health vigilance and adaptable strategies to address fluctuations in disease prevalence and outcomes.
