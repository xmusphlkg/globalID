# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, affecting the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. The disease can progress rapidly, leading to high mortality and morbidity if not treated promptly. It is spread through respiratory droplets or close contact with an infected person. Symptoms include sudden fever, headache, stiff neck, nausea, and, in severe cases, seizures or coma. Outbreaks are more common in areas known as the "meningitis belt" in sub-Saharan Africa. Vaccination is the primary preventive measure, and early antibiotic treatment is crucial for affected individuals.

## Highlights

- Meningococcal meningitis cases in the Chinese mainland show a fluctuating trend from 2014 to August 2024, with periods of increased incidence typically observed during winter and spring months. <br/>
- Recent data for 2024 indicates moderate activity, with 11 cases reported in August, reflecting a slight decrease from July (9 cases) but a consistent number of cases earlier in the year. <br/>
- Deaths remain relatively low, with no fatalities recorded since July 2024, suggesting stable management and treatment efforts. <br/>
- Overall, the disease's incidence appears controlled, with periodic spikes, necessitating continued surveillance and vaccination efforts to prevent outbreaks. <br/>

An analysis of the Meningococcal meningitis data in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals noticeable variability in both reported cases and deaths. The cases appear to exhibit seasonal fluctuations, with peaks often observed during the colder months, particularly between January and March. The highest number of cases within a single month occurred in December 2017, with 24 reported cases, while the lowest monthly case counts often occurred in the summer months, such as August 2015 and August 2022, with just two cases each. 

In terms of mortality, there are sporadic increases in deaths, especially during the first few months of the year, aligning with case surges. Notable peaks in deaths include January 2015 with four deaths, and December 2017 with six deaths, suggesting a possible association between seasonal trends and case severity. In contrast, many months, particularly in the summer and autumn periods, report few or no deaths despite ongoing cases, indicating variability in disease severity or timely intervention efforts. Overall, while cases have fluctuated, mortality has remained relatively low, with many months reporting no deaths at all.
