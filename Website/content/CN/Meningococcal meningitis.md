# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, affecting the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. It is transmitted through respiratory droplets and close contact, often spreading in crowded settings. Symptoms include sudden fever, headache, stiff neck, nausea, and sensitivity to light. Without prompt treatment, it can lead to severe complications or death. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, with several vaccines available targeting different strains. Outbreaks are common in the "meningitis belt" of sub-Saharan Africa, where vaccination campaigns are crucial in controlling the disease.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Numbers**: From 2023 to early 2025, there is a noticeable increase in cases, peaking at 26 in January 2025, indicating a rising trend. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite fluctuations, the mortality rate remains low relative to cases, suggesting improvements in treatment or early detection. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Higher case numbers in winter months suggest a seasonal pattern, necessitating targeted interventions during these periods. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: From 2020 to 2024, there is a general stabilization in cases and deaths, with occasional spikes, highlighting the need for ongoing monitoring and public health strategies. <br/>

A general decline is observed from 2017 to 2020, with a significant drop in 2020, likely due to COVID-19 public health measures like social distancing and increased hygiene, which may have reduced transmission.

From 2021 onwards, there is a gradual increase in cases, with spikes in December 2023 and January 2025, reporting 21 and 26 cases, respectively. The mortality rate remains relatively low, with most months reporting zero to two deaths. This suggests that while the disease persists, its impact in terms of mortality is limited, possibly due to effective medical interventions and public health strategies.
