# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by *Neisseria meningitidis*. It affects the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord, leading to sudden onset of fever, headache, neck stiffness, and often vomiting or altered mental status. It is transmitted through respiratory droplets, especially in close-contact settings. Several serogroups (A, B, C, W, X, and Y) are responsible for most cases worldwide. Outbreaks are common in the "meningitis belt" of sub-Saharan Africa. Prompt antibiotic treatment is essential, and vaccination is a key preventive measure against the disease.

## Highlights

- Meningococcal meningitis cases in mainland China have shown a mild seasonal pattern, with peaks typically occurring between December and April each year. <br/>
- Reported cases remained relatively low and stable from 2020 to 2023, but a notable increase has been observed in early 2025, reaching 24 cases in April alone. <br/>
- Despite fluctuating case numbers, mortality has remained limited; however, death counts have also risen slightly in 2025, suggesting a potential increase in disease severity or reporting. <br/>
- Continuous surveillance and prompt public health response are essential, especially during winter and spring, to prevent larger outbreaks. <br/>

The data on meningococcal meningitis cases from April 2015 to April 2025 on the Chinese mainland shows notable variability in both reported cases and deaths. Initially, from 2015 to 2017, the number of cases ranged from 2 to 24 monthly, with December 2017 seeing a significant peak of 24 cases and 6 deaths. Generally, minor spikes occurred in colder months, suggesting possible seasonal influences on transmission rates.

From 2018 to 2019, a relatively stable pattern is evident, though occasional fluctuations are present. However, 2020 witnessed a significant reduction in cases coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. The enforcement of public health measures such as social distancing likely contributed to fewer meningitis cases due to reduced person-to-person contact and heightened hygiene practices.

As the pandemic's impact lessened, data from 2021 onwards indicates a gradual return to pre-pandemic case numbers, with a resurgence noted in early 2025; the number of cases peaked at 26 in January 2025. Deaths throughout the entire period remained sporadic, with no alarming mortality rise despite occasional increased case numbers. The overall trend suggests intermittent outbreaks that may align with climatic influences or shifts in public health strategies affecting reporting and transmission without clear evidence of long-term significant increase or decline over the years.
