# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, affecting the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. It can lead to serious health complications, including brain damage, hearing loss, or learning disabilities. The disease is transmitted through respiratory droplets or close personal contact. Symptoms may include sudden fever, headache, stiff neck, nausea, and a characteristic skin rash. Rapid diagnosis and treatment are crucial, typically involving antibiotics and supportive care. Vaccination is a key preventive measure, especially in regions prone to outbreaks. Early detection plays a critical role in managing and controlling its spread.

## Highlights

1. **Rising Cases**: From 2015-2025, cases of meningococcal meningitis fluctuated, peaking at 26 cases in January 2025. The last two years have seen higher occurrences. <br/>
2. **Decreasing Deaths**: While the mortality rate remained low, periodic increases were noted, such as in December 2017 and July 2024. In 2025, fatalities decreased despite higher cases. <br/>
3. **Seasonal Trends**: A consistent pattern showed more cases in colder months (January to April) compared to warmer months, which saw fewer cases. <br/>
4. **Current Situation (October 2025)**: As of October 2025, 9 cases and no deaths were reported, indicating a stable but concerning public health scenario. <br/>

Analyzing the cases of Meningococcal meningitis across several years within the Chinese mainland reveals discernible patterns and fluctuations. Initially, from late 2015 through 2016, there were sporadic outbreaks with intermittent high case counts, such as in December 2015, which saw 20 reported cases. The trend during this period indicates a possible seasonal influence, with an apparent increase in cases during the colder months. Notably, mortality rates varied but notably peaked in December 2015, March 2016, and December 2017, reflecting potential challenges in disease management or a surge in more virulent strains during these months.

By examining the data from 2020 onward, we observe a significant decline in both cases and related deaths. From late 2020, the reports of cases reduced drastically, remaining relatively low compared to previous years. This downward trend continued until at least early 2022, with some months reporting as few as zero to two cases. This decline may suggest effective intervention measures or improved public health strategies, such as vaccination campaigns or heightened awareness. However, the increasing number of cases and fluctuating death tolls through 2024 and 2025, notably peaking in January 2025 with 26 cases, indicate a resurgence that could point to waning immunity, emergence of new strains, or lapses in preventive measures. The data throughout these years illustrate the dynamic nature of infectious diseases and the importance of consistent monitoring and adaptive health policies.

Mortality rates don't always directly align with case numbers, suggesting possible advances in healthcare delivery or vaccination efforts. Notably, higher winter mortality suggests the need for reinforcing health infrastructures and outbreak readiness during these months.

Variability in cases—like the spikes seen in January 2015—emphasizes the need for continuous disease surveillance and rapid public health interventions. This includes better diagnostic measures, timely treatment access, and possibly targeted vaccinations to mitigate regional peaks. Despite some successful reductions, the analysis highlights the necessity of adaptive public health responses, supporting both current efforts and future planning.
