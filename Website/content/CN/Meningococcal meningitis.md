# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, leading to inflammation of the membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. Rapid transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or close contact, affecting individuals between seasons, particularly in the "meningitis belt" across sub-Saharan Africa. Symptoms include fever, headache, neck stiffness, and altered mental status, progressing rapidly within hours. Diagnosis requires prompt medical evaluation, often through cerebrospinal fluid analysis. Vaccination is an effective preventive measure, reducing outbreaks in susceptible regions. Early antibiotic treatment is critical to mitigate complications and associated high mortality rates.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Case Trend**: Since 2023, there has been a noticeable increase in meningitis cases, peaking at 26 in January 2025 and maintaining higher numbers compared to previous years. <br/>
- **Mortality Increase**: The mortality rate has fluctuated but remained low. However, deaths have consistently accompanied higher case numbers since late 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variability**: The data shows a typical seasonal pattern with higher cases in winter and early spring, likely driven by environmental factors. <br/>
- **Recent High in Cases**: As of July 2025, cases remain elevated with 12 reported, suggesting ongoing transmission and the importance of continued surveillance and intervention. <br/>

The data from mid-2015 through mid-2025 for Meningococcal meningitis in mainland China illustrates fluctuating case numbers. The notable peaks in December 2015 and May 2016 were followed by a general decline in cases, especially after March 2020, which coincides with the global COVID-19 pandemic. This decline could be attributed to social distancing and hygiene measures that unintentionally reduced meningitis transmission. Nonetheless, the persistence of low-level transmission suggests that even during the pandemic, the disease did not vanish completely.

Mortality rates mirrored case fluctuation, with late 2015 and early 2016 showing higher deaths, such as four fatalities in March 2016 and six in December 2017. These figures indicate potential periods of heightened strain virulence or challenges in treatment access or effectiveness. From 2018 to early 2019, mortality reduced, suggesting possibly improved healthcare responses or changes in strain behavior that reduced the fatality rate. 

In later years, such as 2024 and 2025, case numbers rose again, with periodic increases in mortality. This resurgence points to the necessity for sustained public health strategies, including vaccination and surveillance, to curb transmission and manage outbreaks effectively. By understanding these periodic swings in case numbers and deaths, strategies can be adapted to prevent future peaks and reduce the overall burden of Meningococcal meningitis.
