# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by *Neisseria meningitidis*, affecting the membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord. It spreads through respiratory droplets and close contact, often in settings like schools or dormitories. Symptoms include sudden fever, headache, neck stiffness, nausea, and sensitivity to light. Without prompt treatment, the disease can lead to brain damage, hearing loss, or death. Several serogroups, such as A, B, C, W, and Y, are responsible for outbreaks. Vaccination is the most effective method of prevention, alongside early detection and antibiotic treatment to control spread.

## Highlights

- Meningococcal meningitis cases in the Chinese mainland show seasonal peaks in winter-spring months, with the highest recent monthly total in January 2025 (26 cases).<br/>
- Mortality has declined compared to earlier years, but deaths still occur sporadically, including 7 reported between January and May 2025.<br/>
- From 2020 to 2023, annual case numbers were relatively low, but a moderate rise is noted in early 2025, suggesting close monitoring is necessary.<br/>
- Long-term trends indicate a reduction in large outbreaks seen before 2018, reflecting possible improvements in surveillance, prevention, and control measures.<br/>

From 2015 to 2025, Meningococcal meningitis cases in mainland China display clear seasonality, with peaks typically from December to April. Notable spikes occurred in March 2016 (16 cases), December 2017 (24), and January 2025 (26). The winter–spring pattern aligns with known transmission dynamics for this pathogen. After 2020, reported cases dropped sharply, reaching a low of just one case in October 2020. This decline coincided with COVID-19 mitigation efforts, suggesting indirect control of other infectious diseases via public health interventions. Cases gradually rebounded through 2023 and into 2025, with a moderate increase in incidence but generally lower case fatality.

Death counts do not always scale with case numbers. Some months with relatively few cases saw higher fatalities, such as March 2016 (4 deaths) and July 2017 (2 deaths), indicating variable severity or delays in treatment. Mortality also tends to follow the same seasonal cycle, concentrated in winter and early spring. December 2017 showed the highest lethality (6 deaths) over the decade. Annual case totals remain under control overall, and while sporadic surges occur, endemic levels prevail without widespread outbreaks. The pattern of resurgence post-2020 underscores the importance of continuous surveillance and proactive vaccination, particularly before peak seasons, to maintain low transmission and mortality levels in the population.

Notably, certain months—specifically March, July, and December—repeatedly show clustering of deaths over multiple years, suggesting a possible seasonal component to mortality risk. These short-lived surges may point to localized outbreaks or shifts in strain virulence, case management, or healthcare access.

While the overall case-fatality rate remains below critical thresholds, the persistence of deaths even in years with low total incidence—particularly evident in 2020 through 2023—suggests that disease severity or delays in diagnosis and treatment play a role in individual outcomes. Closer scrutiny of regional healthcare response patterns and strain serogroup variation during high-fatality months may clarify whether systemic factors or pathogen evolution primarily contributed to mortality risk. Despite lower recent mortality rates compared to early years, the deaths continue sporadically, underscoring the importance of sustained surveillance and intervention readiness.
