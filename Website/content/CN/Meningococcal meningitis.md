# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a serious bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, which can lead to inflammation of the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. It is characterized by symptoms such as fever, headache, stiff neck, and sensitivity to light. The disease can progress rapidly, leading to severe complications or death if not treated promptly. Meningococcal meningitis is transmitted through respiratory droplets and close contact. Vaccination is available and recommended to prevent infection, particularly in high-risk populations. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial for improving outcomes.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases**: Meningococcal meningitis cases have increased, with January 2025 reporting 26 cases, the highest in the dataset, indicating a potential outbreak. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite fluctuations, the mortality rate remains low. January 2025 recorded 2 deaths, consistent with previous years. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Higher cases are observed in winter months, particularly from December to March, suggesting a seasonal transmission pattern. <br/>

- **Stability with Spikes**: While there are occasional spikes, such as in December 2023 and January 2025, the period from 2020 to 2024 shows relative stability in case numbers. <br/>

The data on Meningococcal meningitis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable fluctuation in the number of cases and deaths over the years. The year 2015 starts with relatively high numbers, with a peak in December (20 cases) and a significant drop in the middle of the year. This pattern of higher cases in the colder months and lower in the warmer months is consistent across the years, suggesting a possible seasonal influence on the incidence of the disease. The number of deaths, however, does not always correlate directly with the number of cases, indicating variability in the severity or management of the disease.

From 2020 onwards, there is a marked decrease in both cases and deaths, particularly in 2020, which could be attributed to public health measures related to the COVID-19 pandemic, such as increased hygiene practices and reduced social interactions. However, by 2023 and 2024, the cases begin to rise again, with a significant peak in December 2023 (21 cases) and January 2025 (26 cases). This resurgence could be due to the relaxation of pandemic-related restrictions or other epidemiological factors. Overall, while the data shows a general decline in mortality over the years, the fluctuations in case numbers highlight the need for ongoing surveillance and targeted public health interventions to manage and prevent outbreaks effectively.
