# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, primarily affecting the meninges, the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. It often presents with a sudden onset of fever, headache, and stiff neck, accompanied by symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, increased sensitivity to light, and altered mental status. The disease can lead to serious complications, including brain damage, hearing loss, or learning disabilities, and may be fatal if untreated. Transmission occurs via respiratory droplets. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure, while prompt antibiotic treatment is crucial for managing the infection.

## Highlights

- Meningococcal meningitis cases peaked during cooler months, particularly January and March, emphasizing a seasonal pattern in incidence. <br/>
- Between 2014 and 2024, the highest number of cases was observed in January 2015 with 18 cases, while July months consistently showed lower averages, supporting the seasonal trend. <br/>
- A downward trend in cases is evident between 2020 and 2023, with sporadic increases in late 2023 and early 2024; suggesting fluctuating control efforts and environmental influences. <br/>
- Death rates remained low despite some fluctuations over the years, indicating possibly effective medical management or accessibility to healthcare services. <br/>

The data on Meningococcal meningitis cases in mainland China from mid-2014 to mid-2024 shows fluctuating trends in both cases and deaths. Notably, there is a seasonal pattern, with higher case numbers typically reported in the first quarter of each year. For instance, in 2015, cases peaked in January and February with 18 and 17 cases, respectively, contributing to significant deaths. A similar surge occurred in early 2016 and early 2017, though deaths were fewer.

In 2020, there was a marked decline in cases and deaths, potentially due to public health interventions related to the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have influenced healthcare access and reporting. After 2020, the data shows a modest rebound in cases, with minor peaks in 2023 and early 2024, though fatalities remained limited. 

Overall, the reported figures reflect periodic increases in cases, particularly in the cooler months, and highlight the influence of external factors like pandemics on public health trends. Despite sporadic spikes in cases, deaths remained relatively low throughout the period, suggesting potential improvements in healthcare and disease management.
