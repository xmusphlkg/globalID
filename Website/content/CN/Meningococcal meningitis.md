# Meningococcal meningitis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Meningococcal meningitis is a severe bacterial infection caused by Neisseria meningitidis, affecting the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. It often begins with flu-like symptoms, such as fever, headache, and stiff neck, and can rapidly progress to severe complications, including brain damage and death, if untreated. The disease is spread through close contact, such as coughing or sneezing. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure, especially in high-risk populations and regions. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial for improving outcomes and reducing mortality rates.

## Highlights

- Meningococcal meningitis cases in Chinese mainland have shown seasonal peaks, particularly in colder months, with a notable spike in January 2025 at 26 cases.<br/>
- Mortality rates are low, generally under 2 deaths per month, although there were brief increases in late 2017 and early 2015.<br/>
- Recent years exhibit a decline in summer cases, suggesting improved seasonal control.<br/>
- The moderate rise in 2025 cases suggests a resurgence that necessitates careful monitoring and potentially proactive public health interventions.<br/>

The data shows higher incidence from December to April each year, with some peaks like in May 2016 (24 cases) and December 2017 (24 cases), suggesting links to environmental or social factors that could influence transmission rates, such as colder weather or increased indoor gatherings.

The mortality rate remains relatively low compared to the number of cases, with certain months showing higher deaths, as in December 2017 (6 deaths). Interestingly, thereâ€™s a decline in cases observed from 2020 onwards, except for occasional spikes, including March 2023 (15 cases). This general decline might be due to improved health measures or vaccination campaigns. Regardless of the apparent decrease, 2025 shows an increase in cases once more, highlighting the unpredictable nature of meningococcal transmission.

Overall, while the recent data reflects periods of effective control, which could be attributed to public health interventions or vaccination efforts, the occurrence of periodic spikes emphasizes the need for ongoing vigilance. Therefore, continuous surveillance and adaptive strategies are essential to effectively manage and mitigate potential outbreaks, reinforcing efforts in disease prevention, especially given the severity meningococcal disease can present if not adequately controlled.
