# Monkey pox

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first discovered in 1958 in monkeys kept for research, with the first human case recorded in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The disease is endemic to Central and West Africa, with occasional outbreaks elsewhere. Symptoms resemble those of smallpox, including fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes, but are generally milder. Transmission occurs through contact with infected animals, humans, or contaminated materials. Vaccination against smallpox offers some protection against monkeypox.

## Highlights

- **Initial Surge and Decline:** Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland initially surged in late 2023, peaking at 127 cases in October, before gradually declining throughout 2024. <br/>
- **Stabilization in 2024:** The number of cases stabilized in 2024, with monthly cases generally ranging between 38 and 51, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- **Recent Increase:** A slight increase in cases was observed in early 2025, with 56 cases reported in February, suggesting a need for continued vigilance. <br/>
- **No Reported Deaths:** Throughout the period from September 2023 to February 2025, there have been no reported deaths, highlighting the non-lethal nature of the current outbreak. <br/>

The reported data for Monkeypox cases in mainland China from September 2023 to February 2025 shows a fluctuating trend in the number of cases, with no reported deaths throughout the period. Initially, there is a noticeable increase in cases from September 2023 to October 2023, peaking at 127 cases. This is followed by a decline in November 2023, with cases dropping back to 80. The subsequent months show a gradual decrease in cases, reaching a low of 39 cases in April 2024. This decline could suggest effective public health interventions or natural disease dynamics leading to reduced transmission.

From May 2024 onwards, the number of cases stabilizes, fluctuating between 39 and 56 cases per month. This stabilization might indicate a new equilibrium in the transmission dynamics, possibly due to sustained public health measures or changes in population immunity. The absence of deaths throughout the period suggests that while the disease is present, it may not be causing severe outcomes, or that healthcare systems are effectively managing the cases. The data from November 2024 to February 2025 shows a slight increase in cases, which could warrant further investigation to understand the underlying causes, such as changes in reporting, new outbreaks, or seasonal variations.
