# Monkey pox

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the monkeypox virus, belonging to the Orthopoxvirus genus. It primarily occurs in Central and West Africa, particularly in tropical rainforest regions. The virus can spread from animals to humans through direct contact with infected animals, such as rodents or primates, or their bodily fluids. Human-to-human transmission occurs via respiratory droplets, direct contact with skin lesions, or contaminated materials. Symptoms resemble those of smallpox, including fever, headache, muscle aches, and a distinctive rash that progresses to pus-filled sores. Though generally less severe than smallpox, monkeypox can cause significant illness and requires preventive measures.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases:** Since January 2025, Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland have consistently increased, reaching 135 cases by July 2025. <br/>
- **Absence of Deaths:** Despite rising cases, no deaths have been reported, indicating effective management and potentially less virulent strains. <br/>
- **Comparative Analysis:** The period from November 2023 to December 2024 showed a downward trend in cases, suggesting previous effective control measures or changes in transmission dynamics. <br/>
- **Potential Resurgence:** The upward trend in 2025 may indicate renewed transmission or other epidemiological factors requiring further investigation and intervention. <br/>

The reported data for Monkey pox in mainland China show distinct fluctuations in the number of cases from September 2023 to July 2025. Initially, the number of cases was at moderate levels in late 2023, peaking at 127 in October before declining over the subsequent months, reaching a low of 39 cases in April 2024. This downward trend indicates successful control measures or seasonal factors that influence the transmission dynamics of Monkey pox. However, beginning in late 2024, there was a gradual resurgence in cases, culminating in a sharp increase from March 2025 onwards, peaking at 135 cases in July 2025. This rise suggests changes in public health strategies, variations in exposure risks, or the potential introduction of new transmission vectors.

Throughout the reported period, no deaths were attributed to Monkey pox, which suggests effective management of cases and symptomatic treatments available that prevent fatalities. The fluctuation in case numbers might reflect underlying factors such as public health intervention strategies, public awareness, and international travel policies that could influence transmission rates. Given the observed resurgence in cases in 2025, further investigation into the epidemiological factors contributing to this increase would be prudent. Such analysis could focus on whether demographic shifts, ecological changes, or new pathogen variants might be playing a role, or if vaccination and containment strategies need revisiting to adapt to the evolving situation.

Remarkably, the data shows that there have been no deaths attributed to Monkeypox, underscoring its relatively low case fatality rate in this region. Typically, Monkeypox presents with symptoms similar to smallpox, albeit usually less severe, and is managed effectively with supportive care. The absence of deaths from the reported data suggests several possibilities: either the strain circulating is less virulent, the healthcare system is responding effectively to cases, or there's a combination of both factors.

Analyzing the trend in the number of cases, a notable variation is observed in the incidence rates. For instance, the early months starting from September 2023 witnessed a higher number of cases compared to the middle months of 2024 where cases dropped significantly. It is possible that this decline reflects effective public health interventions or the natural ecology of the disease impacting transmission dynamics. Conversely, there's a noticeable increase in cases beginning in early 2025, suggesting either a resurgence in transmission due to relaxed public health measures or increased human activities potentially leading to more exposure. Despite these fluctuations in case numbers, the consistent absence of deaths points to effective management and control measures in place which are mitigating severe outcomes despite the recurring presence of the virus.
