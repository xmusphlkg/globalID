# Monkey pox

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first identified in 1958 in laboratory monkeys and later detected in humans in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of Congo. The disease is similar to smallpox but generally milder. Transmission occurs through direct contact with infected animals, humans, or contaminated materials. Symptoms include fever, rash, swollen lymph nodes, and lesions. The illness typically lasts 2â€“4 weeks. Endemic in parts of Central and West Africa, monkeypox can cause outbreaks, especially in areas with limited access to healthcare and diagnostics.

## Highlights

- Monkeypox cases in mainland China have shown a steady increase in early 2025, rising from 40 cases in January to 86 in April. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations, the overall trend since mid-2024 indicates a gradual resurgence, particularly notable after a low point of 16 cases in November 2024. <br/>
- No deaths have been reported across the entire observation period, suggesting effective clinical management and a low case fatality rate. <br/>
- Continued monitoring and public health response are crucial, especially given the upward trend seen in the first four months of 2025. <br/>

Between September 2023 and April 2025, the reported Monkeypox cases on the Chinese mainland demonstrated a conspicuous temporal fluctuation with no recorded deaths. Initially, cases surged in late 2023, peaking in October at 127 before declining towards early 2024. A nadir occurred in April and July 2024, with just 39 cases each, representing a reduction of over 60% from the peak. The low case counts persisted until November 2024, which had the lowest monthly incidence (16 cases), suggesting a sustained period of epidemic suppression or successful intervention.

However, a noticeable resurgence began in December 2024, with a gradual month-to-month increase peaking again in April 2025 at 86 cases. The absence of fatalities throughout the entire period may point to effective case management, a younger or healthier affected population, or a less virulent circulating strain. The data suggest a seasonal pattern, possibly driven by behavioral, climatic, or public health policy factors, although further epidemiological investigation would be required to confirm causality.
