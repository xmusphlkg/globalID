# Monkey pox

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first discovered in 1958 in monkeys kept for research, with the first human case recorded in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The disease is endemic to Central and West Africa, with occasional outbreaks elsewhere. Symptoms resemble those of smallpox, including fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes, but are generally milder. Transmission occurs through contact with infected animals, humans, or contaminated materials. Vaccination against smallpox offers some protection against monkeypox.

## Highlights

- **Initial Surge**: Cases peaked at 127 in October 2023, followed by a decline through 2024. <br/>

- **Stabilization**: In 2024, cases averaged 40-50 monthly, with no deaths, indicating effective control. <br/>

- **Recent Increase**: January 2025 saw a slight rise to 40 cases, highlighting the need for ongoing vigilance. <br/>

- **Effective Management**: The absence of deaths and reduced cases from the peak suggest Monkeypox is well-managed in the Chinese mainland as of January 2025. <br/>

The reported data for Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland from September 2023 to January 2025 shows a fluctuating trend in the number of cases, with no reported deaths throughout the period. Initially, there is a noticeable increase in cases from September to October 2023, peaking at 127 cases. This is followed by a decline in November, a slight rise in December, and a gradual decrease in the first few months of 2024. The data suggests a potential seasonal pattern or the impact of public health interventions that might have been implemented to control the spread of the disease.

From March 2024 onwards, the number of cases stabilizes, fluctuating between 39 and 51 cases per month until October 2024. A significant drop is observed in November 2024, with only 16 cases reported, followed by a slight increase in December and January 2025. The absence of deaths indicates effective clinical management and possibly a less virulent strain of the virus. The overall trend suggests that while the disease remains present, the situation is under control, with a declining trend in cases towards the end of the period. This could be attributed to increased public awareness, vaccination efforts, or other public health measures.
