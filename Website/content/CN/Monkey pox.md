# Monkey pox

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic infection primarily transmitted to humans through contact with infected animals, notably rodents and primates. It is caused by the monkeypox virus, part of the Orthopoxvirus genus, which also includes variola (smallpox) virus. Human-to-human transmission can occur via respiratory droplets, contact with body fluids, or lesions. Symptoms typically include fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes, evolving to pox-like lesions. Endemic to Central and West African regions, sporadic outbreaks worldwide garner attention due to its clinical similarities to smallpox, despite being less severe in most cases.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in Cases:** Monkeypox cases have gradually declined from a peak of 127 in October 2023 to 49 in May 2024.<br/>
- **Zero Mortality Rate:** No deaths have been reported throughout the entire period from September 2023 to May 2024, indicating effective management and treatment.<br/>
- **Recent Stability:** The case count for the last three months (March to May 2024) shows relative stability, with 51, 39, and 49 cases respectively.<br/>
- **Initial High Variability:** Early months exhibited fluctuation in case numbers, with highs and lows between 80 and 127 cases, suggesting initial outbreaks were more unpredictable.<br/>

The data on Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland from September 2023 to May 2024 shows fluctuating trends. Cases peaked at 127 in October 2023, then dropped to 80 in November 2023. A slight increase to 102 cases was reported in December 2023, followed by a steady decline through early 2024, reaching a low of 39 cases in April 2024. Despite these variations, no deaths were reported during this period, suggesting an effective clinical management and control of the disease.

The initial peak in October 2023 may indicate either increased virus spread or heightened detection and reporting. The subsequent decline in cases could reflect successful public health measures such as improved surveillance, education, vaccination campaigns, or behavioral changes among the population. The consistent zero mortality rate implies that while the disease spread was significant, the health system effectively managed to prevent fatalities, possibly due to timely medical interventions and supportive care.

Overall, the observed trends suggest that despite initial fluctuations, effective strategies might have been implemented to control the disease's spread and prevent fatalities. Continued monitoring is essential to ensure these trends persist and to adapt strategies as needed to maintain control over Monkeypox transmission in the region.
