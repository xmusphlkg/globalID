# Monkey pox

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a rare viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, part of the Orthopoxvirus genus, which also includes the variola virus responsible for smallpox. Discovered in 1958, monkeypox primarily affects Central and West African regions. It is a zoonotic disease, meaning it can be transmitted from animals—like rodents and primates—to humans. Human-to-human transmission occurs through direct contact with infectious sores, body fluids, or respiratory droplets. Symptoms resemble smallpox and include fever, headache, muscle aches, and a distinctive rash. There is no specific treatment, but smallpox vaccines provide some protection. Awareness and preventive measures are crucial for control.

## Highlights

- **Initial Decrease in Cases:** After an initial peak in late 2023, Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland decreased steadily from 127 in October 2023 to a low of 16 in November 2024. <br/>
- **Upward Trend in 2025:** A significant upward trend is observed in 2025, with cases rising from 40 in January to a high of 135 in July. <br/>
- **Stabilization in Late 2025:** The latter part of 2025 shows signs of stabilization, oscillating between 71 to 84 cases from August to October. <br/>
- **No Reported Deaths:** Importantly, throughout the entire period from September 2023 to October 2025, no deaths were reported, suggesting effective disease management. <br/>

The dataset of monkeypox cases in mainland China from September 2023 to October 2025 highlights several noteworthy trends. Initially, there is a notable peak in cases in October 2023, where cases rose to 127 from 80 in the preceding month. This surge was followed by a seesaw pattern of decreases and increases over the subsequent months, reflecting an unstable period with cases fluctuating due to varied factors such as public health interventions, awareness, and possibly seasonal variations. Interestingly, there were no reported deaths during this timeframe, which underscores an effective healthcare response and possibly the non-lethality of the infections detected.

In the timeline observed, from September 2023 to October 2025, a clear resurgence is evident beginning in early 2025. After a period of relative decrease in the latter half of 2024, the number of cases began to rise again, reaching another peak in July 2025 with 135 cases. This resurgence could point to potential factors such as changes in public behavior, waning immunity, or new introductions of the virus. The increase in the cases' number towards the middle of 2025 suggests possible seasonal, epidemiological, or social dynamics influencing disease transmission, emphasizing the necessity for continuous surveillance and investigation into the drivers of these fluctuations to ensure appropriate public health responses and minimize case numbers.

The data on monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland from September 2023 to October 2025 reveals an overall increase in the number of cases, particularly noticeable in 2025. Despite this upward trend in incidence, the data consistently reports zero deaths across all months and years considered. The lack of recorded fatalities suggests effective disease management or possibly a less virulent strain affecting the population. This outcome could be reflective of successful public health interventions, effective clinical management of cases, or inherent characteristics of the virus itself that make severe outcomes less likely.

Initially, the number of cases fluctuates, showing a peak in October 2023 with 127 cases, followed by a reduction through the next year with figures dipping as low as 16 cases in November 2024. However, 2025 sees a resurgence, climaxing in July with 135 cases. The absence of deaths during both rising and falling trends in case numbers may encourage a deeper investigation into the possible reasons behind zero mortality, such as vaccination coverage, public health strategies, early detection and isolation practices, and healthcare system effectiveness. Understanding these elements will be crucial for maintaining zero mortality as case numbers shift and potentially rise in the future.
