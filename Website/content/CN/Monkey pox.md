# Monkey pox

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a zoonotic viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It is primarily found in Central and West African regions where it is endemic, though occurrences have been reported globally. Monkeypox is transmitted to humans through direct contact with infected animals, humans, or contaminated materials. Symptoms typically include fever, headache, muscle aches, swollen lymph nodes, and a characteristic rash that progresses to pustules and scabs. While similar to smallpox, monkeypox is generally less severe. Preventive measures focus on minimizing exposure and include vaccination with newer vaccines developed against orthopoxviruses.

## Highlights

1. The data shows a decline in Monkeypox cases from late 2024, reaching a low point in November 2024 with just 16 cases. Since then, cases have gradually increased to 71 in March 2025. <br/>
2. Throughout the reported period, there have been no deaths attributed to Monkeypox, indicating effective clinical management and response measures. <br/>
3. Monthly fluctuations in case numbers are observable, likely reflecting seasonal or epidemiological patterns, with a recent trend upwards in early 2025. <br/>
4. Despite an increase in cases in early 2025, the Monkeypox situation in mainland China remains non-lethal, suggesting control measures are mitigating severe outcomes. <br/>

From September 2023 to March 2025, monkeypox cases in mainland China reveal an interesting trajectory, marked by initial oscillations followed by a general decline. The spike from 80 cases in September 2023 to 127 in October 2023 suggests increased transmission or improved detection efforts. This was followed by a decline to 80 cases in November, pointing to effective public health interventions or adjustments in disease surveillance. The consistent absence of deaths implies efficient clinical management or a less severe virus strain during this period.

Throughout 2024, cases generally trended downward, albeit with some fluctuations. The year began with 97 cases in January and decreased to a low of 39 by April, showing possibly effective containment measures. After minor fluctuations, including a peak of 51 cases in June, numbers continued a downward trend, reaching just 16 cases by November. This pattern indicates a generally successful control and mitigation strategy, limiting widespread outbreak resurgence.

In early 2025, a rise in cases is noted, with 56 in February and 71 in March, suggesting variables like seasonal impacts, policy shifts, or public behavior changes. Despite this uptick, the continued absence of fatalities underscores the non-lethal nature of the outbreaks or effective healthcare responses. This period of monitoring and response could provide valuable insights for future outbreak management strategies in similar contexts.
