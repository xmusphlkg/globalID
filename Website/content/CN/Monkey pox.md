# Monkey pox

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonosis caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first identified in 1958 in monkeys and later found in humans in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Transmission occurs through contact with infected animals or humans, as well as contaminated materials. Symptoms include fever, rash, swollen lymph nodes, and muscle aches. The disease is generally self-limiting but can be severe in some cases. Monkeypox is endemic in parts of Central and West Africa, with occasional outbreaks reported in other regions due to international travel or animal importation.

## Highlights

- Monkeypox cases in mainland China showed a decline throughout most of 2024, reaching a low of 16 cases in November before starting to rise again in early 2025. <br/>
- In 2025, there has been a notable resurgence, with monthly cases increasing steadily from January (40 cases) to May (112 cases), marking the highest count since October 2023. <br/>
- No deaths have been reported during the entire surveillance period, suggesting a low case fatality rate and effective clinical management. <br/>
- The increasing trend in early 2025 highlights a potential resurgence and suggests the need for renewed public health interventions and enhanced surveillance. <br/>

The reported data for Monkeypox cases in mainland China between September 2023 and May 2025 show notable fluctuations in incidence rates. The initial period, from September to December 2023, reflected a relatively high number of cases, peaking at 127 in October before declining slightly in November and December. The consistent absence of deaths indicates effective management and response during this period. Notably, from January to April 2024, there is a marked decline in cases, reaching a low of 39 in April. This downturn suggests potential temporal control measures or seasonal factors reducing transmission rates. 

However, beginning in May 2024, the cases exhibit a period of mild resurgence, fluctuating monthly but maintaining a downward trajectory through November 2024, when the lowest case count of 16 is recorded. The dramatic decline suggests successful implementation of interventions or public compliance with preventive measures. Conversely, in the early months of 2025, there is a steady rise in reported cases, culminating at 112 in May 2025, may indicate changes in endemicity, altered strain virulence, or possible exposure-related factors influencing transmission dynamics. This upward trend necessitates reassessment of control strategies and proactive steps to mitigate expanding case numbers while maintaining awareness of the consistently zero death rate throughout the period, indicative of effective treatment protocols.

This extended zero-case fatality rate, despite monthly fluctuations in incidence, strongly indicates that the detected cases have been consistently non-lethal. Such a trend suggests effective clinical recognition and possible containment, alongside potential attributes of the virus circulating that may be causing predominantly mild disease. Notably, even during months with increased incidence—such as May 2025 with 112 cases—mortality remained at zero, affirming a lack of progression to severe or fatal outcomes.

The persistent absence of fatalities across 21 consecutive months also indirectly implies access to adequate medical care and surveillance capacity. It may reflect prompt case management, early diagnosis, and targeted public health interventions. Furthermore, the gradual rise and fall in monthly cases without a corresponding rise in deaths points toward a stable endemic or low-transmission pattern, rather than a high-risk outbreak scenario with escalated clinical severity. Overall, this mortality data shapes an epidemiologic picture of monkeypox in the region as a low-fatality condition under current health system conditions.
