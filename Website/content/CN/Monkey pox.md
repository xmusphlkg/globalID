# Monkey pox

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the monkeypox virus, part of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first discovered in 1958 in monkeys, with the first human case reported in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Symptoms resemble smallpox but are milder, presenting with fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes. Transmission occurs via direct contact with infected animals or humans and through respiratory droplets. The disease is endemic to Central and West Africa, with occasional outbreaks outside this region. Vaccination and preventive measures remain crucial for control and management.

## Highlights

- **Peak in late 2023**: Cases spiked in October 2023 with 127 cases, followed by a steady decline into 2024.<br/>
- **Consistent low mortality**: No deaths were reported throughout the entire observation period, indicating low mortality in China.<br/>
- **Sustained decline in 2024**: Monthly cases decreased significantly from 97 in January 2024 to around 46 in September 2024, reflecting effective control efforts.<br/>
- **Stabilizing trend**: Case numbers in mid-2024 have remained relatively stable, ranging from 39 to 51 per month from April to September, signaling a possible stabilization of the outbreak.<br/>

The reported data on Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland from September 2023 to September 2024 indicates a temporal fluctuation in the case numbers without any associated fatalities. Initially, the number of cases peaked in October 2023 at 127 cases, which is the highest number in the dataset. After this peak, there was a significant decline in cases over the following months, hitting the lowest point in April 2024 with just 39 cases. Such fluctuations could potentially be influenced by seasonal changes, public health interventions, or variations in reporting practices.

Post-April 2024, there is a slight resurgence in cases, with numbers varying moderately but remaining below the initial peak. This pattern suggests some level of control over the outbreak, likely due to a combination of containment efforts and natural waning or other epidemiological factors. The consistent absence of deaths throughout this period is notable and might reflect effective clinical management, a less severe strain, or demographic factors influencing the outcome of the infections. Overall, the observed trends highlight the dynamic nature of the outbreak and the importance of continued surveillance and public health measures.
