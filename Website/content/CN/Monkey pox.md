# Monkey pox

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a zoonotic viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, part of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It primarily occurs in Central and West African regions, transmitted from animals such as rodents to humans, and through human-to-human contact. Symptoms resemble smallpox but are milder, including fever, muscle aches, swollen lymph nodes, and a characteristic vesicular rash. While monkeypox is generally self-limiting, severe cases can occur, especially in vulnerable populations. Diagnosis involves clinical assessment and laboratory confirmation. Control measures focus on surveillance, isolation, and vaccination to limit outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Rising Trend in 2025:** Monkeypox cases increased notably in early-to-mid 2025, peaking at 135 cases in July before a gradual decline towards December (43 cases). <br/>
- **Seasonal Variation:** The incidence exhibits seasonal fluctuations, with higher case counts observed from spring to summer months each year, and lower numbers in winter. <br/>
- **No Fatalities:** Throughout the entire reporting period (September 2023 to December 2025), no deaths were recorded, indicating low disease severity and effective clinical management. <br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of December 2025, monkeypox transmission persists but at reduced levels, highlighting sustained surveillance and control efforts on the Chinese mainland. <br/>

The reported data for monkeypox in the Chinese mainland from September 2023 through December 2025 displays marked temporal fluctuation in case counts, with a notable absence of recorded deaths throughout the entire period. The initial surge in the latter months of 2023, peaking in October at 127 cases, is followed by a gradual decline, reaching lower counts in mid-2024. This phase is characterized by generally subdued incidence, with the lowest point observed in November 2024 (16 cases). Subsequently, beginning in early 2025, there appears a renewed and sustained increase, culminating in a secondary peak in July 2025 at 135 cases. 

The overall epidemiological trend suggests an episodic pattern rather than a steady endemic presence, possibly reflecting environmental, behavioral, or reporting-related factors influencing transmission dynamics. The total absence of mortality is conspicuous and may be indicative of effective clinical management, low virulence in circulating strains, or the predominance of cases in lower-risk population segments. Additionally, fluctuations in case counts might correlate with public health interventions, changes in diagnostic criteria or healthcare-seeking behavior, or seasonality effects. Further elucidation would require exploration of demographic, geographic, and exposure-related data for these cases to establish underlying drivers of these observed patterns.
