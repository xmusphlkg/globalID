# Monkey pox

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a rare zoonotic viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, a member of the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first identified in laboratory monkeys in 1958 and reported in humans in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Symptoms often resemble smallpox, including fever, headache, muscle aches, and a characteristic rash. Human-to-human transmission occurs through respiratory droplets, contact with bodily fluids, or lesion materials. While typically less severe than smallpox, it can still cause significant illness, particularly among immunocompromised individuals. Vaccination for smallpox provides some cross-protection against monkeypox.

## Highlights

- The number of Monkeypox cases in the Chinese mainland peaked in October 2023 with 127 cases, followed by a gradual decline. <br/>
- From January to July 2024, cases steadily decreased from 97 in January to 39 in July, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- No deaths were reported throughout the entire period, suggesting a low fatality rate and potentially effective clinical management. <br/>
- The consistent decline in cases since February 2024 indicates successful public health interventions and increasing awareness. <br/>

The recorded data on monkeypox cases in mainland China from September 2023 to July 2024 shows a fluctuating trend with some significant variations month-to-month. Initial reports in September 2023 showed 80 cases, which saw a notable increase in October with 127 casesâ€”the highest throughout the span of the data. Conversely, November reported a decrease back to 80 cases, showing a potential monthly variance or fluctuation in transmission or reporting cycles. After a rise again in December to 102 cases, the number dwindles per month consistently until April 2024, reaching a low of 39 cases for that month, which is consistently observed again in July.

Despite the variance in monthly case numbers, it is noteworthy that no deaths have been reported in this dataset, indicating that while transmission and case numbers fluctuate, the fatality rate remains at zero. This consistent absence of mortality might suggest effective clinical management and treatment of infected individuals or predominant infection by a less virulent strain circulating within the population. The gradual decrease observed after January 2024 might hint at successful intervention measures being implemented, public health awareness improving, or natural decreases due to epidemic cycles. Overall, while the initial months show a relatively high level of cases, subsequent periods exhibit a downward trend, which could signal effective containment and management strategies.

This general decline might suggest improved public health interventions, natural waning of the outbreak, or seasonal effects impacting transmission patterns.

This could imply a number of underlying factors such as the efficacy of medical treatment for infected individuals, a potentially milder strain of the virus circulating in this region, or demographic and health characteristics of the population that result in lower fatality rates. Additionally, the healthcare infrastructure might be sufficiently robust, enabling effective management of cases and preventing fatalities.
