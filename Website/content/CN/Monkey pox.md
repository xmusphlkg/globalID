# Monkey pox

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, belonging to the Orthopoxvirus genus. It is primarily found in Central and West Africa and can spread through direct contact with infected animals, humans, or contaminated materials. Symptoms include fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes. The illness typically lasts 2-4 weeks, with most people recovering without treatment. However, severe cases can occur, especially in immunocompromised individuals. The disease is closely related to smallpox, but with a lower fatality rate. Vaccination for smallpox offers some protection against monkeypox.

## Highlights

- **Declining case numbers**: After a peak of 127 cases in October 2023, monthly cases have generally decreased, reaching a low of 16 cases in November 2024. <br/>
- **No reported deaths**: Throughout the entire period, there have been no reported fatalities related to monkeypox in the Chinese mainland. <br/>
- **Seasonal fluctuations**: The data shows some fluctuations in case numbers, but no significant seasonal pattern is evident, with cases remaining relatively stable year-round. <br/>
- **Control measures**: The overall decline in cases suggests that public health interventions may have effectively reduced the spread of monkeypox in the region. <br/>

The trend of Monkeypox cases in mainland China shows a fluctuating pattern over the past year, with a noticeable decrease in the number of cases since the peak in October 2023. In September 2023, cases increased sharply to 80, which was followed by a slight rise to 127 cases in October. However, after October, there was a steady decline in reported cases, dropping to 39 cases by April 2024. This reduction appears to stabilize around 40-50 cases per month in the following months, with the exception of November 2024, where cases fell to 16, suggesting a possible seasonal or reporting variation. 

Throughout the entire period, the data consistently reports no fatalities, indicating that while the disease remains present, it has not led to severe outcomes requiring hospitalization or resulting in death. The absence of mortality could suggest effective public health interventions or a relatively low virulence of the circulating strain. It is also noteworthy that despite these fluctuations, the number of cases has remained manageable, and China’s healthcare system has likely adapted to the disease’s presence. Given the absence of deaths, the focus for the coming months may shift toward surveillance and continued management to prevent resurgence, while also considering the potential influence of changing social behaviors or public health strategies.
