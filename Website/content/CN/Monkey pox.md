# Monkey pox

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the monkeypox virus, which is closely related to the variola virus that causes smallpox. First identified in humans in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, it primarily occurs in Central and West African countries. The virus is transmitted through direct contact with infected animals, humans, or contaminated materials. Symptoms resemble smallpox but are usually milder, featuring fever, headache, muscle aches, and a characteristic rash. While smallpox vaccination offers some cross-protection, there is growing concern about the disease due to increasing human-to-human transmission and potential outbreaks.

## Highlights

1. **Rising Cases in 2025:** The year 2025 shows a marked increase in Monkeypox cases, peaking at 135 in July, indicating a resurgence compared to the prior year. <br/>

2. **Fluctuation After Initial Decline:** Following an initial decrease in cases from late 2023 through 2024, the trend reversed with consistent monthly increases starting January 2025. <br/>

3. **No Reported Deaths:** Despite fluctuations in case numbers, no deaths have been reported across the entire period, suggesting effective disease management and treatment practices. <br/>

4. **Seasonal Variation:** Case numbers exhibit potential seasonal patterns, with lower counts generally observed during colder months and spikes as warmer weather ensues. <br/>

The Monkeypox case data from mainland China between September 2023 and September 2025 shows fluctuating monthly cases but no deaths. Initially, case numbers were higher, peaking at 127 in October 2023, possibly due to increased transmission or better reporting. The months following this peak saw a decline, reaching 39 cases by April 2024, hinting at effective public health interventions or seasonal factors affecting transmission.

From mid-2024, cases remained relatively low, fluctuating slightly month-to-month. November 2024 reported only 16 cases, suggesting a potential stabilization in transmission, possibly due to improved public awareness or vaccination. However, early 2025 experienced a notable increase in cases, peaking at 135 in July. This rise may indicate seasonal influences, emerging vulnerabilities, or lapses in control measures, highlighting the need for further analysis of socio-environmental factors affecting these trends.

The absence of deaths suggests effective management of disease severity and available treatment protocols, a positive indicator of the health system's capacity to control Monkeypox impacts. Despite ongoing transmission, these trends underscore the importance of continuous surveillance and adaptive strategies to sustain control efforts and prevent future outbreaks. Ensuring ongoing public health measures and adapting to changing circumstances remains critical in managing Monkeypox effectively in the long term.
