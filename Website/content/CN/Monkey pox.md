# Monkey pox

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Monkeypox is a zoonotic viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus, which belongs to the Orthopoxvirus genus. It was first identified in 1958 in laboratory monkeys, while the first human case was recorded in 1970 in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The disease presents symptoms similar to smallpox, including fever, headache, muscle aches, and a distinctive rash that progresses to pustules. Human-to-human transmission primarily occurs via direct contact with infectious sores, bodily fluids, or respiratory droplets. Although less severe than smallpox, monkeypox can lead to complications and is endemic to regions in Africa, with occasional outbreaks reported globally.

## Highlights

- The Monkeypox cases in mainland China have shown fluctuations in monthly case counts, with an apparent decline during early 2024 followed by an uptick in 2025. <br/>
- The highest number of cases reported was in June 2025, at 112 cases, indicating a potential increase in transmission or reporting. <br/>
- Notably, there have been no reported deaths since the first recorded cases, suggesting the disease may be less severe in this population or due to effective healthcare responses. <br/>
- Overall, though cases increased in mid-2025, the situation appears manageable, with trends warranting continued monitoring to prevent potential outbreaks. <br/>

The Monkeypox data from September 2023 to June 2025 in mainland China reveals fluctuating case numbers but consistently no deaths, indicating manageable disease severity. In late 2023, cases initially surged from 80 in September to 127 in October, before dropping to 80 in November. The year 2024 shows a declining trend, with a significant dip in cases, reaching its lowest of 16 cases in November. This decline could indicate successful public health interventions or natural declines in transmission rates.

Entering 2025, case numbers begin to rise steadily again, increasing monthly from January, peaking at 112 cases in May. This resurgence might suggest changes in virus transmission dynamics, population behavior, or public health response efficacy. The slight decrease in June to 102 cases could suggest stabilization after the temporary increase. Despite fluctuating case numbers, the absence of reported deaths throughout this period implies effective healthcare management, possibly due to strong symptomatic treatments, vaccination efforts, or the generally lower lethality of Monkeypox in this context.

The lack of fatalities suggests either a successful containment strategy, a robust healthcare response, or intrinsic factors related to the disease’s lower case-fatality ratio. Overall, the data illustrates fluctuating Monkeypox incidence with no mortality, reflecting ongoing management and potential changes influencing transmission dynamics in mainland China.
