# Mumps

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Mumps is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting the salivary glands, particularly the parotid glands located near the ears. It is caused by the mumps virus, a member of the Paramyxoviridae family. The disease is characterized by symptoms such as fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, and swollen, painful salivary glands. Mumps spreads through respiratory droplets or direct contact with an infected person's saliva. While generally mild, complications can include meningitis, encephalitis, and orchitis. Vaccination, typically with the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine, is the most effective prevention method, significantly reducing incidence rates.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** Mumps cases in the Chinese mainland have decreased significantly from a peak in 2019, with numbers dropping from over 37,000 in June 2019 to about 6,700 by December 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns:** Cases typically rise in spring and early summer, then decline towards the year's end, indicating a seasonal trend. <br/>
- **Low Mortality:** The mortality rate remains very low, with only a few deaths reported, reflecting effective management and treatment. <br/>
- **Stabilization:** Recent years show stabilization in case numbers, suggesting improved control measures and possibly increased vaccination coverage. <br/>

The data on mumps cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in the number of cases, with peaks typically occurring in the late spring and early summer months, particularly from April to June. This pattern is consistent with the seasonal nature of mumps, which often sees increased transmission during these months due to factors such as school terms and increased social interaction. The highest number of cases was recorded in June 2019, with 37,913 cases, indicating a significant outbreak during that period. 

Over the years, there is a general decline in the number of reported cases, especially noticeable from 2020 onwards. This decline could be attributed to several factors, including improved vaccination coverage, public health interventions, or changes in reporting practices. The data also shows a low mortality rate, with only a few deaths reported over the entire period, suggesting that while mumps can be widespread, it remains a relatively low-fatality disease in this region. The sharp decrease in cases from 2020 might also reflect the impact of COVID-19-related public health measures, such as social distancing and increased hygiene practices, which could have inadvertently reduced the transmission of mumps.
