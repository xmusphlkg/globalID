# Mumps

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Mumps is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting the salivary glands, notably the parotid glands located near the ears, leading to characteristic swelling. It is caused by the mumps virus, a member of the Paramyxoviridae family, and is transmitted through respiratory droplets or direct contact with an infected person. Symptoms may include fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, and loss of appetite, along with the gland swelling. While generally mild in children, complications such as meningitis, encephalitis, orchitis, and oophoritis can occur, particularly in adults. Vaccination, especially within the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine, remains the most effective preventive measure.

## Highlights

- **Reduction in Cases:** The number of reported mumps cases in Chinese mainland showed a significant decrease from peaks in 2017-2019, with a gradual decline continuing into 2025. <br/>
- **Death Incidence:** While deaths related to mumps were uncommon throughout the years, occurrences were sporadic, with zero reported fatalities since 2021. <br/>
- **Seasonal Pattern:** A seasonal trend of increased cases during spring and summer months was observed, aligning with historical patterns typical of mumps. <br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of September 2025, mumps cases are generally lower compared to previous years, indicating stable disease control efforts despite seasonal fluctuations. <br/>

The data indicates fluctuations in mumps cases in mainland China from 2015 through September 2025. Analyzing the trend, we notice variability in reported cases, with peaks observed in mid-year months and lower cases in the year's early months, aligning with seasonal patterns often seen in communicable diseases. In 2016 and subsequent years until 2018, there was a significant increase in cases, peaking around May and June each year, with a slight drop in August and September. This recurring pattern suggests possible increased transmission during warmer months, potentially linked to changes in social behavior, such as holidays and school activities, which can affect transmission dynamics.

From 2020 onwards, there was a notable decline in cases, plausibly impacted by public health interventions and societal changes due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Measures such as lockdowns, increased hygiene, mask-wearing, and school closures likely contributed to reduced mumps transmission, as seen by fewer cases in 2020 through 2022. As these measures eased, a gradual increase in cases is observed, yet still not reaching pre-2020 levels by 2025. The number of deaths remains minimal throughout the period, which aligns with the typically mild nature of mumps and effective management strategies in place. Overall, the dataset reflects the impact of external factors like seasonal shifts and broader health interventions on mumps incidence, showcasing effective control and prevention efforts that keep mortality low despite fluctuations in incidence rates.

During this period, mumps cases fluctuated significantly, reaching peaks in the mid-year months, particularly from May to June. However, despite this variability in case numbers, deaths remained exceedingly rare, with zero deaths reported in most months. Notably, mumps attributed deaths occurred in just a handful of instances: two deaths in August 2016, one death each in July 2017 and February 2018, four deaths in January 2020, one death in February 2020, and one in June 2021. These scant deaths indicate a low mortality rate relative to the number of reported mumps cases.

This pattern suggests that while mumps is considerably contagious, its lethality is minimal, potentially due to widespread vaccination programs and improved healthcare interventions that efficiently manage complications. The spike in deaths in early 2020 might correlate with the strain on healthcare systems due to the COVID-19 pandemic, possibly affecting the management of non-COVID diseases like mumps. Post-2020, no reported deaths accompanied the decrease in reported cases, indicating sustained control over the virus's mortality impact. Despite the undulating case numbers, the consistent pattern of low fatality aligns with mumps' general epidemiological profile, where most individuals recover fully with minimal intervention, underscoring the efficacy of public health strategies in place.
