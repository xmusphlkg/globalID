# Mumps

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Mumps is a contagious viral infection caused by the mumps virus, which belongs to the Paramyxoviridae family. It primarily affects the salivary glands, particularly the parotid glands located near the ears, leading to swelling and pain. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or direct contact with an infected person's saliva. Symptoms include fever, headache, muscle aches, tiredness, and loss of appetite, followed by swollen and tender salivary glands. While mumps cases have decreased due to vaccination, outbreaks can still occur, particularly in communities with low vaccination coverage. Complications, though rare, can include orchitis, oophoritis, and encephalitis.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trends**: Mumps cases peaked at 37,913 in June 2019, but have decreased significantly, reaching 9,406 by June 2025, indicating a downward trend in incidence.<br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Earlier data showed seasonal spikes in June and July, but recent years reflect more stable, flatter incidence patterns, suggesting changes in transmission dynamics.<br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Mortality remains extremely low, with only sporadic deaths and none since June 2022, highlighting improved clinical management and low disease severity.<br/>

- **Impact of Interventions**: The consistent decline in cases since 2020 implies successful public health interventions, likely vaccine campaigns and better awareness have lessened mumps transmission.<br/>

The analysis of mumps cases in mainland China from 2015 to mid-2025 highlights fluctuating infection rates. Initially, in 2015, monthly cases ranged from 12,000 to over 23,000, with a decrease noted in early 2016, possibly due to seasonal factors or interventions. However, cases rose again in the summer of 2016, peaking around 20,000 in June and July. The trend continued with a significant increase from 2017 through 2019, particularly during mid-year months, with numbers exceeding 33,000 cases in mid-2019, indicating persistent mumps transmission and potential immunity gaps.

With the onset of COVID-19 in 2020, a marked decline in mumps cases is evident, likely resulting from reduced contact and improved hygiene practices. This decline persisted into 2021 and beyond, with cases stabilizing at lower levels than pre-pandemic years, averaging around 10,000 monthly. Seasonal variation suggests a potential easing of restrictions or natural cycles. The reported deaths were sporadic and did not correspond to case peaks, indicating they may be outliers or due to other health complications.

By mid-2025, mumps cases stabilized at lower rates compared to the pre-pandemic era, suggesting changes in disease dynamics, effective public health measures, or lasting effects of altered social behaviors post-pandemic.
