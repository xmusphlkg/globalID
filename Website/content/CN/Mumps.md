# Mumps

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Mumps is a contagious viral disease caused by the mumps virus, primarily affecting the salivary glands, especially the parotid glands. It spreads through respiratory droplets from coughs, sneezes, or direct contact with an infected person's saliva. Symptoms typically include fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, and swollen, painful salivary glands. Most cases resolve without complications, but mumps can lead to serious issues such as meningitis, encephalitis, or infertility in males. A two-dose regimen of the measles-mumps-rubella (MMR) vaccine provides effective prevention. Mumps can occur in both children and adults, especially in unvaccinated populations.

## Highlights

- Mumps incidence in mainland China has declined significantly since the peak years (2017–2019), with April 2025 showing 8,641 cases compared to over 28,000 in April 2019. <br/>
- The seasonal trend persists, with higher case numbers typically from May to July, though 2021–2025 peaks remain notably lower than pre-2020 values. <br/>
- Mortality remains extremely low, with no deaths reported from August 2020 to April 2025, highlighting improved clinical outcomes. <br/>
- The current disease situation is stable and well-controlled, with sustained reductions in incidence and no recent fatalities, reflecting effective surveillance and immunization efforts. <br/>

From 2015 to 2019, mumps cases in mainland China exhibited strong seasonality, with peaks typically in late spring to early summer. Monthly cases often exceeded 30,000 between May and July, peaking at nearly 38,000 in June 2019. During this period, no deaths were recorded until August 2016, when the first two fatalities appeared. Sporadic deaths—one each in July 2017, February 2018, January 2020, and June 2021—suggest occasional complications, though overall mortality remained extremely low.

Beginning in early 2020, amid the COVID-19 pandemic, reported mumps cases sharply declined. From 2020 through 2025, peak monthly cases rarely surpassed 12,000, a stark contrast to pre-pandemic levels. Suppression measures such as masking, distancing, and school closures likely contributed to reduced mumps transmission. Seasonal trends persisted, but at a diminished scale. By 2023–2025, mumps incidence stabilized at lower levels year-round, and no deaths were reported. This sustained decline may reflect post-COVID behavioral shifts or immunization impacts, although further confirmation would require vaccination and surveillance data.
