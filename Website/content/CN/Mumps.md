# Mumps

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Mumps is a viral infection caused by the mumps virus, belonging to the Paramyxoviridae family. It primarily affects the salivary glands, causing swelling, pain, and fever. Although it can occur at any age, it is most common in children. Mumps can lead to complications such as orchitis, meningitis, and hearing loss. The disease is spread through respiratory droplets or direct contact with an infected person. Vaccination with the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine has significantly reduced its incidence worldwide.

## Highlights

### Key Epidemiological Trends of Mumps in China (as of November 2024)  
- **Decline in cases in recent years**: A notable decrease in mumps cases since 2019, with fewer than 10,000 cases reported monthly in 2024, compared to over 30,000 in 2017. <br/>  
- **Low mortality rate**: Deaths remain rare, with only a few recorded throughout the years, including one in 2014 and one in 2017. <br/>  
- **Seasonal fluctuations**: Mumps cases exhibit seasonal peaks, with elevated cases often occurring in late spring and summer months. <br/>  
- **General control trend**: Despite fluctuations, the overall trend shows a decline in the frequency of cases since the peak in 2017.

The reported data for mumps in mainland China reveals a consistent pattern of seasonal fluctuations in case numbers, with significant peaks typically occurring during the middle months of the year. The most pronounced surges were observed in May, June, and July, with the highest numbers recorded in 2019, where monthly cases ranged from 37,913 to 38,000. This suggests a seasonal component to the transmission dynamics of mumps, likely linked to school schedules and increased social interactions during these months. Despite the high volume of cases, fatalities remained exceptionally rare, with only a few deaths across several years, indicating that the disease's mortality rate is relatively low, especially in the context of a large number of infections.

From a broader perspective, the data also reflects a general downward trend in the number of reported cases from 2020 onwards, particularly after the initial peak in the first half of 2019. This could be attributed to various factors, including improvements in vaccination coverage and public health interventions, possibly related to the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent changes in social behaviors, which may have led to reduced transmission opportunities. Notably, the number of reported cases in 2023 and 2024 remained significantly lower than in earlier years, though still showing seasonal peaks, indicating that mumps continues to be a relevant public health concern, albeit with a reduced burden compared to prior years.
