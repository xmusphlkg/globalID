# Mumps

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Mumps is a contagious viral infection caused by the mumps virus, primarily affecting the salivary glands, particularly the parotid glands located near the ears. It commonly manifests with swelling of these glands, leading to puffy cheeks and a painful, swollen jaw. Mumps is transmitted through respiratory droplets, direct contact with an infected person, or contact with contaminated surfaces. In addition to glandular swelling, symptoms may include fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, and loss of appetite. While generally mild in children, complications such as meningitis, orchitis, and hearing loss can occur, particularly in adults. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method.

## Highlights

- Mumps incidence in Chinese mainland displayed a general increase from 2014 to a peak in 2019, reaching over 37,000 cases in June before declining significantly post-2020. <br/>
- A notable reduction in cases occurred in 2020, likely due to COVID-19 mitigation efforts; monthly cases dropped to as low as 6,000 in March 2020. <br/>
- From 2021 onward, Mumps cases stabilized at lower levels, with monthly figures fluctuating around 10,000 before further decreasing to below 10,000 in most months of 2024. <br/>
- Despite large fluctuations in case numbers during these years, the death toll remained minimal, with only a few deaths recorded, indicating effective case management and high recovery rates. <br/>

The mumps case data in mainland China from October 2014 to October 2024 reveals several notable trends. From the initial months in late 2014, there was a notable increase in cases, peaking significantly in 2019 with the highest monthly case count of 37,913 in June. This peak corresponds with typical seasonal trends often observed in mumps, where cases usually rise in early summer. However, following 2019, there is a sharp decline in cases coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, illustrating potential effects of public health measures like lockdowns, social distancing, and increased hand hygiene that may have inadvertently suppressed mumps transmission.

Examining the trend post-2019 pandemic onset, case numbers dramatically fell in 2020 and continued to stay low relative to pre-pandemic values up to late 2024. This suggests ongoing effects of pandemic-induced behavioral changes or health policies on mumps spread. Moreover, the data shows a few anomalies, such as the occurrence of deaths in specific months like December 2014, August 2016, and scattered singular deaths in later years not usually associated with mumps, traditionally known for low mortality. These anomalies may warrant further scrutiny into specific outbreaks or indicate concurrent healthcare challenges during those periods. Overall, the observed patterns align with the anticipated epidemic cycles of mumps but also emphasize the impact of broader public health dynamics imposed by global pandemics and interventions.

The data on mumps cases and deaths in mainland China from 2014 to October 2024 illustrates intriguing epidemiological trends. Mumps cases exhibit seasonal variations, with a pronounced peak typically around the mid-year months, especially noticeable from 2017 to 2019. Despite fluctuations in case numbers, the mortality attributed to mumps remains exceedingly low throughout the entire period. Deaths were notably recorded in December 2014, August 2016, July 2017, February 2018, January, and February 2020, and June 2021, demonstrating the sporadic occurrence of fatal cases amidst thousands of documented infections. The low fatality rate aligns with general mumps epidemiology, as the infection is known for its mild course in most patients, with severe outcomes being rare but documented.

In the context of public health, the data suggests effective management and possibly widespread immunization contributing to the control of severe outcomes, even when case numbers rise. Notably, no deaths have been reported in the most recent data stretching into late 2024, despite mumps' persistence in the population. This consistency reinforces the understanding of mumps as a low-mortality disease under adequate healthcare conditions and vaccination strategies. Nevertheless, the importance of maintaining high immunization coverage cannot be understated to prevent the escalation of complications that could increase fatality rates. Overall, the data reflects a successful public health infrastructure capable of mitigating the potential lethality of mumps in the Chinese population.
