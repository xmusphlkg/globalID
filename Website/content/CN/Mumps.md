# Mumps

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Mumps is a contagious viral infection primarily affecting the salivary glands, particularly the parotid glands located near the ears. It is caused by the mumps virus, a member of the Paramyxoviridae family. The disease is characterized by symptoms such as fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, and swollen, painful salivary glands. Mumps is transmitted through respiratory droplets or direct contact with an infected person. While it is generally a mild disease, complications can occur, including meningitis, encephalitis, and orchitis. Vaccination with the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine is the most effective way to prevent mumps.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** Mumps cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant decline from a peak in 2019, with 37,913 cases in June 2019, to just 4,116 cases in January 2025. <br/>

- **Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate remains extremely low, with only a few deaths reported sporadically over the years, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease. <br/>

- **Seasonal Trends:** Historically, higher case numbers were observed during the warmer months, particularly from April to July, but this trend has diminished in recent years. <br/>

- **Recent Stability:** Since 2020, the number of cases has stabilized at lower levels, suggesting improved public health measures and possibly increased vaccination coverage. <br/>

The data on mumps cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals significant fluctuations in reported cases, with a notable peak in 2017. The highest number of cases was recorded in May 2017, with 31,840 cases, indicating a potential outbreak during that period. This spike could be attributed to various factors, including vaccination coverage, population density, and public health interventions. The absence of reported deaths throughout this period suggests that while the disease was prevalent, it did not lead to severe outcomes in the majority of cases, possibly due to effective management and treatment protocols.

In the years following the peak, there was a general decline in cases, particularly from 2021 onwards, where numbers dropped significantly, with January 2023 reporting only 2,370 cases. This decline may reflect improved vaccination strategies, increased public awareness, and better healthcare access. However, the data also shows occasional increases, such as in May 2023, which could indicate localized outbreaks or lapses in vaccination coverage. Continuous monitoring and targeted vaccination campaigns will be essential to maintain control over mumps and prevent future outbreaks.
