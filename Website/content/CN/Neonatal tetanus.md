# Neonatal tetanus

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. It primarily affects newborns, leading to muscle spasms, rigidity, and difficulty feeding due to contaminated environments or instruments, such as unsterilized scissors in umbilical cord cutting. The bacteria produce a toxin affecting the nervous system, resulting in symptoms typically appearing within the first two weeks after birth. Poor maternal immunization contributes to risk, especially in developing regions. Prevention is achievable through immunizing pregnant women with tetanus toxoid and ensuring sterile birth practices. Without treatment, neonatal tetanus has a high mortality rate.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus incidence in China has shown a consistent downward trend from 2015 to May 2025, decreasing from monthly peaks of over 30 cases in 2015 to just 1 case in May 2025. <br/>
- Reported deaths have nearly ceased since 2020, indicating major progress in reducing lethality, with no fatalities reported since August 2022. <br/>
- The disease appears to be approaching elimination levels, with several months from 2021 onward registering zero new cases. <br/>
- Improved maternal immunization, safer delivery practices, and public health surveillance likely contributed to the sustained low incidence. <br/>

A review of neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China from May 2015 through May 2025 reveals a substantial and continuous decline in reported cases over time. In 2015, monthly cases ranged from 17 to 33, with a total of 248 cases for the year and 10 associated deaths. This represents the highest burden in the dataset. By contrast, from 2018 onwards, the annual case load decreased sharply, and after 2020, monthly reports typically include zero to five cases, with some months reporting no cases at all. This suggests a significant reduction in disease transmission and/or improved neonatal and maternal healthcare interventions, such as wider immunization coverage and improved hygiene during deliveries.

From 2021 onward, reported neonatal tetanus deaths became extremely rare, with only isolated occurrences. By 2023 and through to early 2025, deaths were entirely absent, and monthly case counts often hovered near zero. The long-term decline in both cases and fatalities, despite fluctuations in individual months, indicates possible success of sustained public health interventions and surveillance. However, low but persistent sporadic cases through 2024 and 2025 may reflect ongoing challenges in specific localized or underserved regions.

The mortality trend of neonatal tetanus in the Chinese mainland from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals a notable decline in both deaths and reported cases over time. From 2015 to 2016, the disease exhibited significant lethality, with monthly deaths ranging up to three, and a yearly cluster of fatalities averaging roughly eight per year. However, beginning in 2017, there was a noticeable shift: while the disease persisted sporadically, the number of deaths began to decrease, with some months still recording isolated spikes—such as November 2017 or October 2019—but these appeared less consistent and more isolated. Importantly, after 2020, the annual fatalities sharply dropped, with several years (notably 2021, 2023, and 2024) recording zero or only a single death, even as sporadic cases continued.

This pattern suggests an improvement in neonatal care, maternal vaccination programs, sanitation, and perhaps better surveillance and reporting systems. Despite the low incidence, a few outlier months in the latter part of the time series, such as March 2021 reporting a death with zero cases, might reflect discrepancies in registration or delayed death reporting. From 2022 onwards, fatal outcomes have become exceedingly rare, suggesting near-elimination conditions and sustained public health interventions that effectively suppress the fatality burden of neonatal tetanus over the last decade.
