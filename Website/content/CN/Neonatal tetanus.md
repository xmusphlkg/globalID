# Neonatal tetanus

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease that affects newborns, typically within the first 28 days of life. It is caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani, which enters the body through unclean delivery practices or contaminated umbilical cord care. The bacterium produces a toxin that affects the nervous system, leading to muscle stiffness and spasms. Without proper medical intervention, the mortality rate is extremely high. Preventative measures include maternal immunization, hygienic childbirth practices, and proper newborn care. Despite global efforts, neonatal tetanus remains a significant health issue in low-resource settings.

## Highlights

- Substantial decline in neonatal tetanus cases from 2014 (27 cases in May) to virtually eliminated by 2024 (2 cases in May), denoting strong progress in disease control efforts over a decade. <br/>
- Mortality associated with neonatal tetanus has significantly reduced, with multiple years observing zero deaths, indicating improvements in both preventive measures and medical care. <br/>
- Sustained low incidence and mortality rates in recent years (2023-2024) reflect successful maintenance of control measures, such as maternal vaccination and hygiene during childbirth. <br/>
- The current disease situation as of May 2024, with 2 cases and no deaths, suggests the maintenance of a near-elimination status of neonatal tetanus in Chinese mainland.

Analyzing Neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals a significant downward trend in both incidence and mortality. In 2014, monthly cases were relatively high, peaking at 58 in September, with occasional deaths nearly every month, indicating possible outbreaks or gaps in immunization efforts. From 2015 to 2017, both cases and deaths began to decline, suggesting improvements in healthcare and immunization coverage. 

From 2018 to 2024, the trend of decreasing cases continued. Some months in 2021 and 2022 even reported zero cases, signifying a significant milestone in disease control. The data from 2020 showed consistently low numbers, potentially linked to broader public health measures during the COVID-19 pandemic. Although there were occasional spikes, such as six cases in January 2023, the overall reduction in both cases and deaths indicates successful public health initiatives, likely including vaccination campaigns and improved neonatal care. 

This analysis underscores the effectiveness of sustained public health efforts in controlling and reducing neonatal tetanus in mainland China over the last decade.
