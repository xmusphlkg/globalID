# Neonatal tetanus

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe bacterial infection in newborns, caused by *Clostridium tetani*. This pathogen produces a toxin that leads to muscle stiffness and spasms. Infection typically occurs when non-sterile instruments are used to cut the umbilical cord or during improper handling of the cord stump. Symptoms usually appear within the first two weeks of life, starting with difficulty in feeding and progressing to severe muscle rigidity and convulsions. High mortality rates are common, especially in low-resource settings. Preventive measures include maternal immunization with the tetanus toxoid vaccine and ensuring hygienic birth practices.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Neonatal tetanus cases have significantly decreased from 58 in September 2014 to single digits or zero in recent years, indicating successful control measures. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate Reduced:** Deaths have also declined, with recent years showing zero deaths in multiple months, suggesting improved neonatal care and vaccination coverage. <br/>
- **Stable Low Incidence:** Since 2020, monthly cases have largely remained below 5, demonstrating a stable low incidence of the disease. <br/>
- **Current Situation:** As of June 2024, the incidence remains very low with only 2 cases reported and no deaths, reflecting sustained control and prevention efforts. <br/>
