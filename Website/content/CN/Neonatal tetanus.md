# Neonatal tetanus

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe and often fatal disease affecting newborns, characterized by muscle stiffness and spasms caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. This bacterium, prevalent in soil, enters the body through unhygienic birth practices, primarily affecting infants in regions with limited access to maternal vaccination and clean delivery environments. Symptoms typically appear within 3 to 14 days post-birth, including lockjaw and rigidity. Preventative measures include immunization of pregnant women with tetanus toxoid and promoting sterile conditions during childbirth. Despite being preventable, neonatal tetanus remains a significant public health concern in under-resourced areas.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus cases in China have significantly declined since 2015, showing an overall downward trend in incidence from 33 cases to virtually zero in recent months.<br/>
- The disease saw a notable drop in case numbers starting in 2019, with a transition from sporadic cases to isolated incidents as of September 2025.<br/>
- Deaths also decreased in tandem with case numbers, with zero fatalities recorded consistently in 2025, indicating improved healthcare or vaccination measures.<br/>
- Sustained reduction in cases suggests effective preventative strategies, although vigilance is required to maintain this status and prevent resurgence.<br/>

Analyzing the reported data for neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China illustrates a noteworthy decline over time. Initially, during 2015 and 2016, the case numbers remain fairly consistent, fluctuating between 10 to 33 cases per month, with occasional spikes in deaths. From the start of 2017 onwards, the numbers begin to decrease more noticeably, with cases mostly dipping below 20. This trend suggests possible improved healthcare interventions, better awareness, and increased vaccination coverage. Neonatal tetanus predominantly affects newborns, stemming from unhygienic childbirth practices; hence, improvements in maternal and neonatal care protocols could significantly influence these figures.

Further evaluation shows that the peak in case numbers during the earlier years gradually transitions into minimal figures from 2021 onward, consistently remaining in the single digits with various months recording zero cases. Notably, deaths have substantially tapered off, with none reported since mid-2023. Looking at the continuity of this dataset, these observations indicate an overall positive trajectory in controlling neonatal tetanus. Challenges, however, may still persist in certain regions, as sporadic cases suggest gaps in healthcare service delivery or failure in hygiene standards during childbirth. This comprehensive reduction in cases could align with broader initiatives like the expansion of maternal health programs and continuous efforts to ensure safe birthing practices, which are crucial for sustained success in eliminating neonatal tetanus.
