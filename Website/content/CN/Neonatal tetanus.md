# Neonatal tetanus

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe bacterial infection caused by *Clostridium tetani* in newborns, typically contracted through contaminated umbilical cord stumps or unsanitary birth practices. The bacteria produce toxins that affect the nervous system, leading to muscle stiffness, spasms, and difficulty breathing. It is most common in low-resource settings with inadequate neonatal care and hygiene practices. Symptoms usually appear within a few days of birth. Neonatal tetanus is preventable through proper maternal immunization and safe delivery practices. Without treatment, it can be fatal, but early intervention with antibiotics and supportive care improves survival rates.

## Highlights

1. **Declining Cases**: Neonatal tetanus cases have significantly decreased since 2014, with fewer than 5 cases per month in recent years, indicating improved vaccination and healthcare efforts. <br/>
2. **Low Mortality**: Deaths from neonatal tetanus have been rare, with most months reporting no deaths in the past few years, suggesting successful prevention measures. <br/>
3. **Seasonal Fluctuations**: Cases fluctuate seasonally, with minor spikes in certain months, though these are generally low in comparison to earlier years. <br/>
4. **Ongoing Vigilance**: Despite the decrease, occasional cases continue to be reported, emphasizing the need for sustained immunization and healthcare interventions. <br/>

The data on neonatal tetanus in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a clear decline in both cases and deaths over the years. Early years, like 2014 and 2015, saw higher case counts and multiple deaths, with some months reporting up to three fatalities, such as in October 2015. However, by 2017, both cases and deaths significantly decreased, with many months recording zero or only a few cases. The trend continued through 2020 and 2021, with only isolated cases reported and few deaths, suggesting improvements in prevention and care.

By 2022 and beyond, the situation had further stabilized, with monthly cases fluctuating between 0 and 6 and deaths being rare. This decline is likely due to the success of national immunization programs, enhanced maternal and neonatal healthcare, and better sanitation. By 2024, cases were minimal, and deaths were essentially nonexistent in several months, indicating that neonatal tetanus is increasingly rare in China. While the data shows progress, occasional cases highlight the need for continued vigilance in maintaining immunization coverage and addressing any gaps, especially in rural or underserved regions.
