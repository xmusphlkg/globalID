# Neonatal tetanus

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease affecting newborns, caused by the bacterium *Clostridium tetani*. The infection typically occurs when unclean instruments are used to cut the umbilical cord or when proper postnatal hygienic practices are not followed. Toxin production by *C. tetani* interferes with nerve function, leading to muscle stiffness, spasms, and difficulties in feeding. Neonatal tetanus is preventable through maternal immunization, clean delivery practices, and proper cord care. The disease is most common in low-resource settings with limited access to healthcare and vaccination services.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus cases have declined sharply in the Chinese mainland since 2015, falling from monthly double digits to consistently low single digits by 2021, and now approaching zero.<br/>
- Deaths similarly decreased; most recent years record either zero or one death per year, with only one fatality observed in 2025.<br/>
- The disease is no longer endemic, reflecting the effectiveness of national immunization and perinatal hygiene efforts.<br/>
- Sporadic isolated cases still occur, indicating vigilance for at-risk populations remains important, but overall neonatal tetanus poses a minimal public health threat by the end of 2025.<br/>

The multi-year trend of neonatal tetanus cases in the Chinese mainland demonstrates a marked decline in incident numbers and mortality from 2015 through 2025. In 2015 and 2016, monthly cases were consistently in double digits, peaking at 28 in December 2015 and maintaining a pattern of 10–23 per month throughout most of 2016. This period also saw deaths sporadically distributed across months, with peak lethality sometimes disconnected from peak case months. From 2017 onwards, monthly numbers fell slowly but steadily, reaching predominantly single digits after mid-2018. By 2019, much of the year saw minimal monthly cases, a trend that continued and deepened through subsequent years.

After 2020, the data reflects a dramatic decrease, with many months recording two or fewer cases and several months reporting none at all. Deaths became exceptional events, with some years recording only one or two deaths and several years without any fatalities. By 2023 and beyond, neonatal tetanus approached near-elimination levels, with months of zero cases interspersed with isolated reports, and almost no deaths observed. This pattern suggests rapidly improving disease surveillance, immunization coverage, and perinatal care practices, effectively minimizing both incidence and mortality in recent years. The most recent data for 2024 and 2025 registers only sporadic cases with virtually no deaths, underscoring the sustained impact of public health interventions and reflecting progress towards the disease's elimination.

Neonatal tetanus deaths in mainland China have markedly declined from late 2015 to 2025. In the early years (2015–2019), deaths occurred sporadically alongside fluctuating case numbers, with some months reporting multiple deaths—such as December 2016 and several in 2019—often during periods of higher incidence. From 2020, both cases and deaths decreased significantly, and most months—sometimes entire years—saw no reported fatalities despite occasional cases. Anomalies occasionally appeared, such as a death in March 2021 with no reported cases, pointing toward possible reporting errors or late diagnoses.

As the years progressed, deaths became rare and disconnected from case spikes. By 2023 and through early 2025, fatalities were almost eliminated, with only a single death (July 2025) against a backdrop of very low case counts. This persistent reduction signals widespread success in public health interventions: improved immunization coverage, enhanced maternal health, and better neonatal care have minimized both the number of cases and the risk of fatal outcomes. The current profile of neonatal tetanus in Chinese mainland is one of near-elimination, as sporadic cases are no longer consistently associated with deaths, and overall disease burden is now very low, reflecting substantial progress in prevention and management over the past decade.
