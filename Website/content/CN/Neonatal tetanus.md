# Neonatal tetanus

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe bacterial infection occurring in newborns, generally caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. This bacterium is typically introduced through unhygienic delivery practices and the use of non-sterile instruments to cut the umbilical cord. Once the bacteria enter the body, they produce a potent toxin that affects the nervous system, leading to muscle stiffness, spasms, and ultimately, respiratory failure. Neonatal tetanus is a significant cause of infant mortality in developing countries with limited access to healthcare and sanitation. It is preventable through maternal immunization with the tetanus toxoid vaccine and improved birthing conditions.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decrease in Cases**: Neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China have decreased from 42 in October 2014 to zero in October 2024, indicating successful public health interventions.  
<br/>

- **Sustained Low Mortality**: Deaths have remained very low or zero in recent years, with no deaths reported since August 2022.  
<br/>

- **Consistent Decline Since 2015**: A steady decline in both cases and deaths from 2015 onwards highlights effective vaccination coverage and improved childbirth practices.  
<br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of October 2024, neonatal tetanus is nearly eradicated in China, with recent data showing sporadic cases and no deaths throughout the year.  
<br/>

The data for neonatal tetanus in mainland China reflects a significant downward trend in reported cases from October 2014 to October 2024, suggesting successful public health interventions. In 2014, the monthly cases ranged between 32 and 42, but by 2024, they had dropped to generally below three cases per month. This decline over a decade indicates progress in preventive measures, such as maternal immunization and improved hygiene practices during childbirth. Moreover, the number of deaths related to the disease also decreased noticeably, implying better treatment and management of cases.

While the overall trend is encouraging, some variability remains evident. For instance, there were occasional spikes, such as in October 2015 and October 2019, with an increased number of deaths. These spikes may suggest temporary lapses in public health measures or variations in regional healthcare access and quality. By the subsequent years, especially 2021 onwards, the data show a significant stabilization with zero deaths reported for most months, indicating robust control over the disease. The observed decline and stabilization of neonatal tetanus cases underscore the ongoing need to sustain and potentially expand current health policies and education efforts to ensure continued progress and prevention of any future upsurge in cases.

The initial years, particularly from late 2014 to 2016, experienced higher numbers both in cases and fatalities compared to subsequent years. For instance, October 2014 reported 42 cases and one death, while October 2015 saw 26 cases and three deaths. These figures suggest possible fluctuations influenced by seasonal factors, healthcare accessibility, or public health interventions. Notably, the year 2015 showed a relatively high number of deaths with periodic spikes, indicating potential gaps in treatment or reporting during those months.

As the years progressed, there is a discernible decline in both cases and deaths, with a marked decrease noted after 2016. By 2019, the fatality numbers had generally settled to lower levels, often registering zero deaths for extended periods, reflecting potentially improved healthcare measures, vaccination coverage, and awareness campaigns. The data from 2020 onwards illustrates a stable pattern with occasional isolated cases and fewer months registering fatalities, underscoring progress in neonatal care and maternal health education. However, the persistence of even sporadic cases and deaths suggests that complete eradication requires ongoing vigilance, community engagement, and consistent health system support.
