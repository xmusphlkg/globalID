# Neonatal tetanus

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal infection affecting newborns, primarily occurring in developing countries where immunization and hygienic birth practices may be inadequate. It results from the contamination of the umbilical stump with Clostridium tetani spores, often due to non-sterile cutting instruments or the application of harmful substances. The bacteria release toxins causing muscle stiffness, spasms, and difficulty in feeding and breathing. Symptoms typically appear within days of birth and can progress rapidly. Prevention involves ensuring maternal vaccination with tetanus toxoid and promoting clean delivery practices. With increased vaccination coverage, neonatal tetanus rates have significantly declined worldwide.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline:** Neonatal tetanus cases in China have significantly decreased from 2015 through July 2025, indicating effective public health interventions.<br/>
- **Substantial Mortality Reduction:** There has been a notable reduction in deaths, with zero deaths reported in recent months until a single death in July 2025.<br/>
- **Recent Stability:** From 2023 onwards, the monthly case count remains consistently low, often zero to three cases, suggesting a maintained control over transmission.<br/>
- **Infrequent Outbreaks:** Occasional spikes, such as the three cases in March 2025, emphasize the need for continued vigilance in neonatal health systems.<br/>

The data on neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals notable trends in incidence and mortality over a decade. Initially, in the latter half of 2015 and early 2016, there were relatively high case numbers, with monthly cases fluctuating between 10 to over 30. Despite the consistent reporting of cases, the mortality rate was low, with deaths often in the single digits. This pattern might suggest improved healthcare access or more effective neonatal care practices that help manage symptoms and prevent fatalities, albeit with some variability in monthly case numbers.

As the timeline progresses, from 2017 onwards, there is a marked decline in the number of cases, which correlates with decreasing mortality as well. By 2020 and onwards, both reported cases and deaths fall dramatically, with months frequently showing zero fatalities and minimal case numbers, typically less than five. This observed trend may reflect better vaccination coverage or community health initiatives focusing on maternal and neonatal care. Especially noteworthy is the drop in cases during the years 2021 to 2025, indicating a successful reduction in the incidence of this preventable neonatal condition. However, there remains a small, often negligible presence of cases, suggesting that while significant progress has been made, neonatal tetanus has not been entirely eradicated, necessitating ongoing vigilance and healthcare strategies.
