# Neonatal tetanus

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe but preventable disease affecting newborns, often within the first 28 days of life, caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. This bacterium releases a toxin that attacks the nervous system, leading to muscle spasms and rigidity. The condition commonly occurs in rural areas where deliveries take place in unhygienic conditions or where umbilical cords are cut with contaminated instruments. Symptoms usually appear 3 to 14 days after birth, starting with difficulty in feeding and increasing irritability. Prevention involves maternal immunization with tetanus toxoid and promoting clean childbirth practices and proper newborn care.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus cases in Chinese mainland have markedly decreased from a peak of 33 cases in September 2015 to consistently low numbers, typically below 5 per month since 2020. <br/>
- The number of deaths has also declined over the years, with periods of zero deaths, indicating improved treatment or preventive measures. <br/>
- From 2021 onwards, cases largely stabilized between 0-2 monthly, showcasing significant control over the disease. <br/>
- By August 2025, neonatal tetanus incidents are nearly non-existent, suggesting the disease is under effective prevention and control measures. The sporadic cases occurring do not have corresponding deaths, reflecting advancements in healthcare. <br/>

The data on neonatal tetanus in mainland China indicates a declining trend in cases from August 2015 to August 2025. Initially, monthly cases ranged from 29 to 33 between August and December 2015, with deaths reported in each of these months. This reflects ongoing challenges at that time in preventing neonatal tetanus. Starting in 2016, a steady decrease in cases becomes evident, with monthly counts dropping to single digits by 2017. Deaths were not reported consistently every month, suggesting either improved healthcare access or more effective prevention strategies.

From 2018 onwards, neonatal tetanus cases rarely exceeded five per month, and mortality remained low. In 2020, monthly cases further declined, frequently recorded between one to three, accompanied by consistently low mortality figures. This trend continued into 2021 and beyond, with cases mostly between zero to two per month and deaths virtually disappearing. Such a decline indicates significant progress in neonatal tetanus prevention, likely due to enhanced prenatal care, wider access to tetanus immunization, and better hygienic practices during childbirth. Occasional deaths still occur, such as in July 2025, possibly reflecting isolated incidents rather than systemic failures. Overall, these trends highlight the effective public health interventions that have contributed to the dramatic reduction of neonatal tetanus in China over the observed period.
