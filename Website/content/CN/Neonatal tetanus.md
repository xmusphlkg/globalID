# Neonatal tetanus

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal bacterial infection affecting newborns, primarily occurring in low-resource settings. Caused by Clostridium tetani spores entering the body through unhygienic birth practices, it manifests within the first two weeks of life. The disease is characterized by severe muscle spasms, rigidity, and difficulties in feeding, leading to high mortality rates. Prevention relies on maternal immunization with tetanus toxoid during pregnancy and ensuring clean delivery practices. Global health initiatives continue to focus on vaccination and education to reduce neonatal tetanus incidence. Despite progress, complete eradication remains an ongoing challenge.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China have shown a significant decline from 58 cases in September 2014 to only 2 cases per month in 2024, reflecting enhanced prevention efforts. <br/>
- Deaths have been consistently low since 2016, with no fatalities reported since August 2022, highlighting improved access to medical care and vaccination coverage. <br/>
- The number of monthly cases dropped from double digits between 2014 and 2016 to nearly zero from 2021 onwards, indicating the near elimination of the disease. <br/>
- The consistent absence of cases and deaths since 2023 suggests that neonatal tetanus is well-controlled, with sporadic, isolated cases remaining. <br/>

The reported data on neonatal tetanus in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a gradual decline in both the number of cases and deaths over time. In the earlier years, particularly 2014 and 2015, cases remained relatively high, with monthly cases often exceeding 30, and deaths occurring almost every month. For example, 58 cases and 2 deaths were reported in September 2014, while May 2015 saw 32 cases but no deaths. However, by 2016, both cases and fatalities began to decrease significantly, with some months recording no deaths despite cases being reported, such as June and July 2016.

From 2017 onwards, the number of cases and deaths stabilized at lower levels, often with fewer than 10 cases per month. By 2020 and beyond, cases dropped even further, with most months reporting fewer than 5 cases and no deaths in many instances. The decline continues into 2024, with only a handful of cases (2 to 3 per month) and no reported deaths in recent months. This trend reflects improved healthcare measures, such as maternal vaccination and better sanitary practices during childbirth, contributing to the near elimination of neonatal tetanus in China.
