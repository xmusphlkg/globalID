# Neonatal tetanus

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease affecting newborns, caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. It occurs when the spores of the bacterium enter the body through the umbilical stump, often due to non-sterile delivery practices. The disease is characterized by muscle stiffness and spasms, leading to difficulty in feeding and breathing. Neonatal tetanus is preventable through maternal immunization with the tetanus toxoid vaccine and ensuring hygienic delivery and postnatal care. Despite global efforts, it remains a significant health issue in regions with limited access to healthcare and vaccination services.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China decreased from 20-33 cases per month in 2015 to fewer than 5 cases per month from 2020 onwards. <br/>

- **Reduction in Mortality**: Deaths have significantly decreased, with no deaths reported from March 2021 to January 2025, indicating improved healthcare interventions. <br/>

- **Stabilization in Recent Years**: Since 2020, cases have stabilized at very low levels, often with months reporting zero cases, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of January 2025, no new cases or deaths were reported, highlighting the success of public health efforts in eradicating neonatal tetanus. <br/>

The data on neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows a significant decline in both reported cases and deaths over the years. In 2015, the number of cases was relatively high, with monthly cases ranging from 17 to 33 and a total of 15 deaths throughout the year. This trend of higher incidence continued into early 2016, but by the end of 2016, there was a noticeable reduction in both cases and deaths. The decline continued steadily through 2017 and 2018, with cases dropping to single digits per month and deaths becoming increasingly rare.

By 2019, the number of cases had further decreased, with most months reporting fewer than 10 cases and very few deaths. This trend of low incidence continued into the early 2020s, with some months reporting zero cases. The data from 2021 onwards shows a consistent pattern of very low case numbers, often zero or just a few cases per month, and no deaths reported in many months. This suggests that public health interventions, such as improved maternal vaccination programs and better healthcare access, have been effective in controlling neonatal tetanus in the region. The data for 2025 indicates a continuation of this trend, with no cases reported in January and only sporadic cases in subsequent months, highlighting the sustained success of these interventions.
