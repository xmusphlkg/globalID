# Neonatal tetanus

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease affecting newborns, caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. It typically occurs when the umbilical stump is contaminated, often due to non-sterile delivery practices. The bacteria produce a toxin that leads to muscle stiffness and spasms, starting with the jaw and progressing to the rest of the body. Without treatment, the condition can lead to complications such as respiratory failure. Prevention is primarily through maternal immunization with the tetanus toxoid vaccine and ensuring hygienic birth practices. Neonatal tetanus remains a significant health issue in regions with limited healthcare access.

The data on neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a significant decline in both the number of cases and deaths over the years. Initially, in 2014 and 2015, the number of cases was relatively high, with monthly cases often exceeding 20 and deaths occurring almost every month. For instance, December 2014 reported 34 cases with 1 death, and throughout 2015, the monthly cases fluctuated between 15 and 33, with deaths ranging from 0 to 3 per month. This period indicates a higher burden of neonatal tetanus, possibly due to gaps in immunization coverage or healthcare access.

From 2016 onwards, there is a noticeable downward trend in both cases and deaths. By 2017, the monthly cases had reduced significantly, often staying below 10, and deaths became sporadic. This trend continues into the subsequent years, with 2020 and beyond showing a further reduction in cases, often reporting zero deaths. By 2024, the data reflects a consistent low incidence, with most months reporting 2 or fewer cases and no deaths. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, such as increased vaccination coverage, better maternal healthcare, and enhanced public health education. The data suggests a successful public health intervention in reducing the incidence and mortality of neonatal tetanus in mainland China over the decade.

The data on neonatal tetanus in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a significant decline in both cases and deaths over the years. For instance, in December 2014, there were 34 cases with 1 death, and in October 2015, there were 26 cases with 3 deaths. This period reflects a higher burden of neonatal tetanus, possibly due to gaps in immunization coverage or healthcare access.

However, from 2016 onwards, there is a noticeable reduction in both the number of cases and deaths. By 2020, the monthly cases had dropped to single digits, and deaths became rare, with some months reporting no deaths at all. This trend continues into 2024, where the number of cases remains low, and no deaths are reported in most months. This decline could be attributed to improved healthcare interventions, such as increased vaccination coverage, better maternal healthcare, and enhanced public health strategies targeting neonatal tetanus prevention. The data suggests a successful public health response in reducing the incidence and mortality of neonatal tetanus in the region.
