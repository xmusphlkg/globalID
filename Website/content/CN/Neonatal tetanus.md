# Neonatal tetanus

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease affecting newborns, typically within the first 28 days of life. It is caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani, which thrives in environments with low oxygen, such as soil and animal intestines. Infection usually occurs through unhygienic practices during childbirth, such as cutting the umbilical cord with contaminated instruments. Symptoms include muscle stiffness and spasms, feeding difficulties, and severe respiratory issues. The disease is preventable through maternal immunization with the tetanus vaccine and improved delivery practices. Without treatment, neonatal tetanus has a high mortality rate.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus cases have significantly declined from 2015 through March 2025, with cases decreasing from a monthly high of 33 to occasional months of zero cases by 2025. <br/>
- Monthly deaths from neonatal tetanus have remained consistently low, typically ranging between 0 and 3 per month, indicating improvements in disease management and treatment. <br/>
- The period from 2015 to early 2025 experienced fluctuations in cases, but a general downward trend is evident, reflecting effective vaccination and preventive strategies. <br/>
- The consistent low incidence of deaths since 2017 suggests enhanced clinical management and possibly increased awareness of neonatal care practices over time. <br/>

The trend persisted into 2017 with single-digit monthly cases, indicating improved control over the disease.

From 2018 onwards, neonatal tetanus cases remained predominantly low, usually fewer than 10 cases per month, and deaths were sporadic. This pattern continued into 2019 and beyond, with most months reporting very few or zero cases, highlighting effective vaccination and sanitation efforts. Starting in 2020, many months reported minimal or zero cases, with almost no deaths, reflecting significant progress in disease management.

By 2020, months frequently reported fewer than five cases, indicating stabilization at very low incidence levels. Occasional spikes in cases happened but remained minimal compared to earlier years, showcasing sustained prevention strategies.
