# Neonatal tetanus

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a life-threatening condition caused primarily by the bacterium Clostridium tetani, which thrives in unsanitary environments. It typically occurs when non-sterile instruments are used to cut the umbilical cord or when traditional substances are applied to the cord stump, introducing the bacteria. This disease predominantly affects newborns in low-resource settings with limited access to maternal immunization and clean delivery practices. Symptoms such as muscle stiffness and spasms surface within days after birth, leading to high mortality rates. Prevention hinges on maternal vaccination, clean delivery practices, and proper newborn umbilical stump care.

## Highlights

1. Neonatal tetanus cases have decreased significantly from 2015, which recorded higher monthly cases, to near-zero cases by 2025, indicating successful control efforts. <br/>
2. Deaths due to neonatal tetanus have also reduced, with sporadic occurrences from 2020 onwards, demonstrating effective medical interventions and immunization programs. <br/>
3. The consistent decline in cases since 2019 suggests improved maternal and neonatal healthcare practices and strengthened public health measures across Chinese mainland. <br/>
4. As of June 2025, neonatal tetanus remains rare, highlighting China's progress towards the disease's elimination, although continued vigilance and preventative strategies are essential for sustained success. <br/>

This reduction suggests successful public health measures, likely involving improved immunization coverage and maternal and neonatal healthcare.

Mortality rates also reflect a decrease, showing advancements in clinical practices and emergency medical treatments. Initially, multiple deaths occurred monthly, such as two deaths in January and July of 2016, indicating notable severity at those times. However, the trend shows almost no deaths in later years, aligning with the decline in cases. By 2023, several months report zero deaths, reflecting significant progress potentially driven by increased public health education, timely immunization of pregnant women, and enhanced hospital delivery practices. This positive trajectory highlights substantial improvements in reducing neonatal tetanus's impact in mainland China, demonstrating effective disease control and prevention strategies over the decade studied.
