# Neonatal tetanus

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease occurring in newborns, caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. It typically results from unsanitary delivery practices where non-sterile instruments cut the umbilical cord, allowing tetanus spores to enter. Common in regions with inadequate maternal healthcare, neonatal tetanus manifests through muscle stiffness and spasms, usually within days of birth. Preventing this disease involves ensuring maternal immunization with tetanus toxoid during pregnancy, promoting hygienic childbirth practices, and administering immediate postnatal care. Global health initiatives strive to eliminate neonatal tetanus through improved healthcare accessibility and community education.

## Highlights

- Neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China have significantly decreased from 58 cases in September 2014 to consistently less than 5 cases monthly since 2021, indicating successful control efforts. <br/>
- The number of deaths has also declined, with frequent months showing zero fatalities since 2019. This trend represents an improvement in case management and healthcare infrastructure. <br/>
- Recent data (as of August 2024) shows a stable low incidence, with 2 cases per month consistently since January 2024, signaling sustained control of the disease. <br/>
- Continued vigilance and immunization efforts are essential to maintain this low incidence and further prevent neonatal tetanus in the region. <br/>

The data on neonatal tetanus cases and deaths in mainland China from August 2014 to August 2024 shows a general decline in both reported cases and deaths over the ten-year period. In the earlier part of the timeline, from 2014 to 2016, the monthly cases ranged from as high as 58 in September 2014 to as low as 8 in November 2016. Deaths during this period were somewhat consistent but typically low, with occasional spikes, such as 3 deaths reported in October 2015. The trend in cases shows a slow but steady decrease after 2016, with cases often falling below 10 per month from 2017 onward.

By 2019, cases consistently remained in the single digits, with some months seeing no cases at all, and deaths became even rarer. From 2020 to 2024, there were extended periods with very few or no deaths reported, with only isolated occurrences, such as 1 death in August 2022. The last two years, especially, indicate that the incidence of neonatal tetanus has become relatively rare, with most months seeing no more than a couple of cases and no deaths. This pattern likely reflects the success of public health interventions such as vaccination campaigns and improvements in neonatal care and hygiene practices.

However, the death-to-case ratio remains relatively low overall, indicating that while tetanus cases occurred, fatal outcomes were less frequent.

From 2016 onward, a significant decrease in both cases and deaths can be observed, with some months reporting zero deaths despite a small number of cases. By 2017, the cases dropped to single digits per month, and deaths became rare. The pattern continues into 2020-2024, where monthly cases remain consistently low (often fewer than 5 cases per month), and deaths are almost non-existent. The sporadic occurrence of deaths in certain years, such as 2020 and 2022, indicates that while neonatal tetanus has not been fully eradicated, improvements in immunization and healthcare likely played a role in reducing mortality.
