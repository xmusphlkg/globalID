# Neonatal tetanus

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Neonatal tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease affecting newborns, caused by the bacterium Clostridium tetani. It occurs when the spores of the bacterium enter the body through the umbilical stump, often due to non-sterile delivery practices. The disease is characterized by muscle stiffness and spasms, leading to difficulty in feeding and breathing. Neonatal tetanus is preventable through maternal immunization with the tetanus toxoid vaccine and ensuring hygienic delivery and cord care practices. Despite global efforts, it remains a significant health issue in regions with limited access to healthcare and vaccination services.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases**: Neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China have decreased from 26 in February 2015 to 1 in February 2025, indicating effective public health measures. <br/>

- **Mortality Reduction**: Deaths have also decreased, with no recent fatalities, highlighting improved healthcare and vaccination efforts. <br/>

- **Stabilization in Recent Years**: Since 2020, cases have remained consistently low, often below 5 per month, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>

- **Ongoing Vigilance Required**: Despite low numbers, continued surveillance and vaccination efforts are essential to maintain control and prevent resurgence. <br/>

The data on neonatal tetanus cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows a significant decline in both the number of cases and deaths over the years. In 2015, the number of cases was relatively high, with monthly cases ranging from 17 to 33 and a total of 13 deaths reported throughout the year. However, by 2025, the number of cases had decreased dramatically, with most months reporting zero or one case and no deaths. This trend suggests a successful implementation of public health interventions aimed at reducing neonatal tetanus, such as improved maternal vaccination coverage, better hygiene practices during childbirth, and increased access to healthcare services.

The data also indicates a notable reduction in the mortality rate associated with neonatal tetanus. In the earlier years, particularly 2015 and 2016, there were several months with multiple deaths, whereas in the later years, deaths became rare, with only sporadic occurrences. This improvement in survival rates could be attributed to enhanced medical care and timely treatment of affected neonates. The overall trend reflects the effectiveness of sustained public health efforts and the importance of maintaining high vaccination coverage and healthcare access to prevent neonatal tetanus.
