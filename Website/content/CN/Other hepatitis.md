# Other hepatitis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation caused by factors apart from the common hepatitis A, B, and C viruses. This category includes hepatitis D and E viruses, as well as non-viral causes such as autoimmune hepatitis, alcoholic hepatitis, and drug-induced hepatitis. Transmission methods, clinical manifestations, and risks vary depending on the specific type. Other hepatitis types can lead to acute or chronic liver disease, with some progressing to cirrhosis or liver failure if untreated. Recognition and differentiation of the underlying cause are critical for effective management and prevention strategies.

## Highlights

- Case numbers for Other hepatitis in Chinese mainland showed a significant decline from over 2,200 monthly cases in late 2015 to a range of 500–700 cases per month in 2025, indicating effective long-term disease control.<br/>
- Mortality remains very low, with most months reporting zero deaths; only two deaths occurred during the entire year of 2025.<br/>
- The number of reported cases fell sharply between 2019 and 2021, likely reflecting improved prevention measures, changes in reporting, or altered health-seeking behavior.<br/>
- As of December 2025, Other hepatitis is now a low-incidence, low-mortality disease in the Chinese mainland, posing minimal public health burden.<br/>

Analysis of monthly reports for "Other hepatitis" in the Chinese mainland from late 2015 to 2025 shows a marked, sustained decrease in case numbers. Initial incidence commonly exceeded 2,000 cases per month through 2016, dropping below 2,000 by 2017, and settling mostly under 900 per month after 2021. By 2024–2025, monthly counts consistently remained between roughly 400 and 750 cases. There is no clear evidence of pronounced seasonality or recurring annual peaks in reported cases; instead, the reduction is steady and gradual, suggestive of broad improvements rather than transient changes.

Reported deaths are rare throughout the dataset, with most months recording zero deaths and only isolated incidents with one or two fatalities. Even during months of higher case numbers, death figures remained extremely low. This pattern suggests that both detection and management of "Other hepatitis" have been effective, or the forms included are generally mild. The parallel decline in both incidence and mortality hints at successful public health interventions, improved hygiene, medical care accessibility, or changes in exposure risks. Overall, the data demonstrate a positive trajectory in controlling "Other hepatitis" over the past decade, characterized by declining burden and minimal lethality in the affected population.

Despite fluctuating incidence, never more than two deaths occurred in any single month throughout this period. In most months—across all years—zero deaths were reported; fatalities appeared only sporadically, often with long intervals of no deaths. Early years (2016–2019) saw a handful of months with one death, and two deaths occurred only twice: June 2018 and December 2019. These events showed no clear correlation with seasonal or monthly spikes in cases.

After 2022, deaths became even less frequent as reported cases notably declined, with nearly all months registering zero fatalities and case-count reductions persisting. Years 2023–2025 report just three deaths total, all months otherwise without fatality. This pattern suggests that overall lethality of “Other hepatitis” has remained extremely low, even as incidence fell from above 2,000 cases per month in 2015–2016 to fewer than 1,000 since 2021. The persistently minimal death rate could indicate improvements in clinical management, shifts in disease reporting or classification, or changes in predominant hepatitis etiologies. The data consistently reflect “Other hepatitis” as a category with exceptionally low case-fatality compared to other notifiable infectious diseases.
