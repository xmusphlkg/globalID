# Other hepatitis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation caused by factors beyond the well-known hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E viruses. These include autoimmune hepatitis, where the immune system mistakenly attacks liver cells, and alcoholic hepatitis resulting from excessive alcohol consumption. Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) is linked to obesity and metabolic syndrome. Drug-induced hepatitis arises from adverse reactions to medications or toxins. Chronic conditions, such as Wilson's disease and hemochromatosis, can also induce liver inflammation. Identifying the underlying cause is crucial for proper management and treatment, as these forms can lead to serious liver damage or cirrhosis if left untreated.

## Highlights

- There has been a clear declining trend in the number of "Other hepatitis" cases in Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024, with cases dropping from over 2,700 in October 2014 to just 438 by October 2024. <br/>
- The decline in cases is particularly steep post-2019, correlating with increased public health measures during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. <br/>
- Deaths have remained low and sporadic throughout the observed period, highlighting successful management and treatment strategies in place. <br/>
- The consistent reduction in cases suggests effective disease control and prevention strategies by health authorities, though monitoring remains crucial to sustain these improvements. <br/>

Throughout these years, certain months show variability, such as peaks that appear occasionally, like March 2015 with 2,715 cases. Yet, the overall trajectory seems downward, especially significant post-2019. This could imply improved detection, prevention efforts, or other health interventions impacting the spread of the disease.

In terms of fatalities, the data reflects sporadic incidences of deaths associated with Other hepatitis, with a few instances scattered across the given timeline. Notably, deaths remain relatively low, with many months showing zero fatalities. Specific peaks in deaths, like in December 2019 where two deaths were recorded, coincide with lower numbers of reported cases thereafter, suggesting possible interventions or heightened awareness post these events. However, the mortality rate appears consistently minimal compared to case counts, which could suggest a relatively good management of the cases or severity of infections not reaching fatal levels frequently. The analysis of the reported deaths from other hepatitis in mainland China reveals a low mortality rate over the observed period, spanning from October 2014 to October 2024. Despite fluctuations in the number of cases, the death toll remained remarkably low. For many months, especially during the years 2014, 2016, 2017, and 2018, there were successive months with zero recorded deaths. However, sporadic deaths did occur, generally not exceeding one or two per month. The cumulative death toll over the entire ten years appears minimal when compared to the total number of reported cases, highlighting the relatively low fatality ratio of this category of hepatitis during the observed period.

From a temporal perspective, there were few instances of multiple deaths in a month, such as January 2015, December 2019, and June 2018, where two deaths were reported, indicating occasional spikes in mortality. However, these instances are outliers rather than trends, contributing to the broader context of episodic mortality. In recent years, particularly from 2020 onwards, the number of cases consistently decreased, coinciding with a further reduction in deaths, often reporting zero fatalities for consecutive months. The incidence of cases and deaths seems possibly influenced by broader public health measures and advances in medical care, which might have contributed to controlling and reducing the impact of hepatitis in the population over time.
