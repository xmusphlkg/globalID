# Other hepatitis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation caused by factors distinct from the well-known hepatitis viruses A, B, C, D, and E. This category includes autoimmune hepatitis, where the immune system attacks liver cells, and alcoholic hepatitis, linked to excessive alcohol consumption. Nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) arises from fat accumulation in the liver not due to alcohol, leading to inflammation and scarring. Additionally, hepatitis can result from drug-induced liver injury or infections by less common viruses like Epstein-Barr or cytomegalovirus. These diverse origins necessitate varied diagnostic and treatment approaches, emphasizing the complexity of liver health issues beyond viral hepatitis.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Other hepatitis in mainland China has significantly declined from a high of 2,489 cases in March 2016 to 546 cases in October 2025. <br/>
- Monthly reported cases consistently decreased, with a substantial drop observed from 2018 onwards, suggesting effective disease control measures or changes in reporting. <br/>
- Mortality rate remains extremely low with sporadic deaths, totaling only a few annually and none reported for multiple months or entire years within the timeframe. <br/>
- The fluctuation in case numbers towards the end of 2024 and into 2025 indicates possible cyclic patterns or seasonal influences requiring further analysis. <br/>

Between October 2015 and October 2025, the reported cases of "Other hepatitis" in mainland China show a general decline, with some fluctuation. Initially, in 2015, monthly cases were around 2400, but these numbers gradually decreased over the years. The period around 2020 marks a sharper decline, possibly due to the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, which could have impacted healthcare-seeking behaviors and reporting practices. By 2022, the cases reached their lowest in December with 347 cases, suggesting effective public health measures or changes in diagnostic criteria.

Mortality rates remained low throughout the period, with many months reporting zero deaths, indicating either effective clinical management or the relatively low severity of the hepatitis included in this category. Although sporadic increases in deaths are noted, they do not follow any discernible upward trend over time. Factors such as healthcare improvements, educational campaigns, or better access to treatment might have contributed to controlling the spread and reducing the disease burden. Temporary peaks, like those in 2016, might be due to changes in diagnostic or reporting practices or short-term outbreaks. Despite these fluctuations, the overall trend indicates a positive trajectory in managing these hepatitis cases, highlighting progress in public health strategies or treatment efficacy.
