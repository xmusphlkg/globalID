# Other hepatitis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Other hepatitis encompasses infectious and non-infectious causes of liver inflammation beyond the common hepatitis A, B, and C viruses. It includes hepatitis D, a rare form requiring hepatitis B for replication, and hepatitis E, typically transmitted via contaminated water in endemic areas. Non-viral causes involve autoimmune hepatitis, where the body's immune system attacks liver cells, and alcoholic hepatitis, stemming from excessive alcohol consumption. Drug-induced hepatitis results from adverse reactions to medications or toxic substances. Diagnosing and managing these conditions demand a comprehensive approach, emphasizing prevention, lifestyle modifications, and medical interventions tailored to the specific type of hepatitis.

## Highlights

- **Gradual decline in cases**: From a high of over 3,000 cases in 2014, there has been a steady decline, with cases dropping to around 500-600 by 2024, indicating improved control measures. <br/>
- **Minimal mortality**: Deaths have remained consistently low, with no more than 2 deaths per month and several months recording zero deaths, suggesting a low case-fatality rate. <br/>
- **Recent stabilization**: In 2023-2024, the number of cases has plateaued around 500-700 per month, reflecting a stabilized but ongoing transmission. <br/>
- **Potential seasonal pattern**: Peaks often occur in March, May, and July, suggesting a possible seasonal influence on transmission. <br/>

In the earlier years, from 2014 to 2016, there was a gradual decline in cases, from over 3,000 in mid-2014 to fewer than 2,000 by the end of 2016. This drop continues through 2017 and 2018, where the number of reported cases hovers around 1,500 before falling further in 2019. Deaths remained relatively low throughout these years, with occasional spikes, such as in 2014 and 2016, but overall stayed minimal compared to the number of cases.

From 2020 onward, a more significant reduction in case numbers is evident, particularly in early 2020, which could be influenced by changes in public health measures due to the COVID-19 pandemic. This trend of lower case counts persists through 2022 and 2023, with monthly cases consistently under 1,000. The data suggests that despite fluctuations in monthly cases, the mortality rate remains extremely low, with sporadic increases in deaths but no significant upward trend. By 2024, the reported cases are stable, though the numbers remain lower than in earlier years, indicating a possible long-term downward trend.
