# Other hepatitis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation caused by factors other than the well-known hepatitis viruses (A, B, C, D, and E). This category includes autoimmune hepatitis, where the body's immune system attacks liver cells, and drug-induced hepatitis, resulting from adverse reactions to medications or toxins. Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), a severe form of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, also falls under this umbrella. These conditions can lead to liver damage, fibrosis, or cirrhosis if untreated. Diagnosis typically involves blood tests, imaging, and sometimes liver biopsy, with treatment tailored to the underlying cause.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, hepatitis cases decreased significantly, from over 2,000 per month in 2015 to under 700 in early 2025, indicating effective public health measures. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: The mortality rate remains low, with sporadic deaths, suggesting effective treatment and management. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations**: Fluctuations in case numbers suggest potential seasonal factors influencing transmission. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: In recent years, case numbers have stabilized, indicating consistent control over disease spread. <br/>

The data on "Other hepatitis" cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several trends. Initially, from 2015 to 2017, there is a noticeable decline in the number of reported cases, with a peak in March 2015 at 2715 cases, gradually decreasing to 1560 cases by December 2017. This period also shows sporadic deaths, with a maximum of two deaths in June 2018. The decline in cases could be attributed to improved public health measures, increased awareness, or changes in reporting practices.

From 2018 onwards, the data shows a continued decrease in cases, reaching a low of 347 cases in December 2022. This trend suggests a sustained effort in controlling the spread of hepatitis, possibly through vaccination campaigns, better sanitation, or healthcare interventions. However, the number of deaths remains relatively low and sporadic throughout the years, indicating that while the disease's prevalence has decreased, its lethality has not significantly changed. The data from 2023 to early 2025 shows a slight increase in cases, peaking at 767 in August 2023, but still remains lower than the initial years, suggesting a potential stabilization or minor resurgence that may require further investigation.
