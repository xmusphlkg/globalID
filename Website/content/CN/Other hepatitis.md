# Other hepatitis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation caused by factors beyond the primary hepatitis viruses (A, B, C, D, and E). It includes autoimmune hepatitis, where the immune system attacks liver cells; alcoholic hepatitis, resulting from excessive alcohol consumption; and drug-induced hepatitis, triggered by medications or toxins. Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), related to metabolic syndrome and obesity, is another type. These forms can range from mild to severe and may lead to chronic liver damage or cirrhosis if untreated. Diagnosis involves blood tests, imaging, and sometimes liver biopsy to identify the cause and assess the extent of liver injury.

## Highlights

- **Sustained decline in cases**: Reported cases of Other hepatitis have decreased significantly from over 2,400 monthly cases in 2015 to just 586 in May 2025, marking a long-term downtrend. <br/>
- **Low mortality rates**: Deaths remain rare, averaging fewer than one per month in recent years, with no deaths recorded from March to May 2025. <br/>
- **Post-2020 stabilization**: After a sharp drop in 2020, likely COVID-19 related, case numbers have remained stable between 500–750 monthly cases since mid-2022. <br/>
- **Effective public health response**: The persistent reduction indicates improved surveillance, treatment access, and possibly successful prevention strategies. <br/>

The reported data for “Other hepatitis” in mainland China from mid-2015 through mid-2025 illustrates a clear downward trajectory in annual case counts, with notable fluctuations in monthly incidence. The initial period, particularly from 2015 to 2016, maintained consistently high case counts ranging between 2,000–2,500 per month. From 2017 onward, a gradual decline is observable, suggesting the effects of improved public health interventions, increased diagnostic accuracy, adjusted classification practices, or epidemiological transitions driven by changes in underlying etiologies of non-A/B/C hepatitis cases. The downward trend accelerated in 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic’s onset, likely driven by a combination of altered healthcare utilization, reduced viral transmission due to lockdown and hygiene measures, or shifts in disease surveillance priorities.

From 2021 to 2025, the number of cases stabilized at lower monthly figures, generally below 1,000, with several months under 600 reports. Despite these lower counts, small spikes occurred sporadically each year, possibly reflecting localized outbreaks or increased detection during certain seasons. Deaths remained rare, averaging fewer than 1 per month, suggesting relatively low virulence or effective case management. Notably, mortality clusters (e.g. two deaths in June 2018 and December 2019) appear isolated without sustained increases, indicating no persistent increase in severity during the observed decade. Overall, the data suggests successful containment and reduced disease burden of “Other hepatitis” over the observed period, though continued surveillance is essential.
