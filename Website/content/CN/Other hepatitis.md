# Other hepatitis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to less common types of hepatitis infections beyond hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E. These can include viral hepatitis caused by uncommon viruses, autoimmune hepatitis, alcoholic hepatitis, and drug-induced hepatitis. Each type involves liver inflammation, but the causes vary from viral agents to immune system dysfunction or exposure to harmful substances. Symptoms often resemble those of more common hepatitis types, including jaundice, fatigue, and abdominal pain. Diagnosis typically involves blood tests and imaging studies, while treatment depends on the specific cause, ranging from antiviral medications to lifestyle changes or immunosuppressants.

## Highlights

- The incidence of Other hepatitis in the Chinese mainland has shown a consistent decline from 2015 to 2025, with cases decreasing from over 2,400 to below 600. <br/>
- Deaths associated with Other hepatitis remained relatively low, often below two per month, indicating an improvement in disease management and outcomes. <br/>
- There is a noticeable drop in cases since 2018, which could be attributed to enhanced public health interventions and increased awareness. <br/>
- As of September 2025, the disease situation suggests stable control, with case numbers relatively steady, indicating successful containment efforts by health authorities. <br/>

Throughout the years, cases fluctuated seasonally, indicating potential environmental, behavioral, or policy influences on disease incidence deserving further examination.

Analyzing "Other hepatitis" deaths in mainland China from September 2015 to September 2025 reveals important trends. Initially, between 2015 and 2016, deaths were minimal, averaging less than one per month despite cases often exceeding 2,000 monthly. This indicates effective healthcare management or less severity of infections despite high cases. Sporadic deaths began in mid-2016, yet remained low even as monthly cases declined.

By 2018, both cases and deaths showed a downward trend, with cases consistently falling below 1,000 per month by late 2020 and 2021. This suggests improved healthcare strategies in diagnosing and treating hepatitis, or reduced transmission rates. Sporadic peaks in deaths persisted occasionally but were not proportionate to case increases, signifying an ongoing challenge despite general improvements in managing the disease.

The data reflects that while the overall burden decreased, isolated fatalities indicate continuing complexities associated with "Other hepatitis." These periods of mortality, although less frequent, highlight the necessity for continued public health efforts and healthcare interventions to address specific complications or challenges tied to the disease. Sustaining these efforts can mitigate risks further even as case numbers decline, ensuring better management and potentially reducing fatalities related to hepatitis in the future.
