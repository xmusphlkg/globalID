# Other hepatitis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation not caused by the well-known hepatitis viruses A, B, C, D, or E. This category includes hepatitis resulting from less common viruses, autoimmune disorders, alcohol, drugs, toxins, and metabolic diseases. The clinical presentation can range from asymptomatic to severe liver dysfunction. Diagnosis requires excluding the major hepatitis viruses and identifying the underlying cause through medical history, laboratory tests, and imaging. Management depends on the etiology, often involving antiviral medications, immunosuppressants, lifestyle changes, or addressing the specific offending agents to prevent liver damage progression.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decrease in Cases**: Since 2014, cases of other hepatitis on the Chinese mainland have shown a decreasing trend, from around 3,000 cases per month in 2014 to under 600 cases by mid-2024. <br/>
- **Stabilized Mortality Rates**: The number of deaths has remained low throughout the years, with occasional months showing zero deaths, indicating effective management of the disease. <br/>
- **Recent Fluctuations**: There are minor fluctuations in monthly case numbers from 2023 to mid-2024, averaging around 600 cases, suggesting a stabilization phase. <br/>
- **Public Health Implications**: Continued surveillance and preventive measures are essential to maintain control and further reduce case numbers in coming years. <br/>

The data on "Other hepatitis" in mainland China from July 2014 to July 2024 shows a notable declining trend in the number of reported cases, particularly after 2018. From mid-2014 to mid-2016, the monthly cases were consistently above 2,000, peaking in mid-2014 with over 3,000 cases reported. However, after 2017, there is a marked decrease in the number of cases, with monthly figures often below 2,000, eventually dropping to just a few hundred cases by 2024. This decline could suggest improvements in public health measures, increased vaccination rates, or other interventions that reduced the incidence of this disease.

In terms of mortality, the number of deaths remains relatively low throughout the observed period, with most months reporting zero or one death, and only a few instances of two deaths. This low mortality rate indicates that while the incidence of "Other hepatitis" may have been significant in earlier years, the disease was likely being managed effectively, with most cases not leading to fatal outcomes. The sustained reduction in both cases and deaths over the years suggests that "Other hepatitis" became less of a public health concern in mainland China over this period.
