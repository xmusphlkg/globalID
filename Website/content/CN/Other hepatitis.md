# Other hepatitis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to forms of liver inflammation not primarily caused by the well-known hepatitis A, B, C, D, or E viruses. It includes autoimmune hepatitis, drug-induced hepatitis, and alcoholic hepatitis among others. Autoimmune hepatitis is characterized by the body's immune system attacking liver cells. Drug-induced hepatitis results from reactions to medications, while alcoholic hepatitis is caused by excessive alcohol consumption damaging liver cells. Diagnosis typically involves liver function tests, imaging studies, and liver biopsies. Management depends on the specific type, with treatments ranging from medication adjustments and lifestyle changes to immunosuppressive therapies.

## Highlights

1. Other hepatitis cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant overall decline from 2015 to June 2025, dropping from over 2500 cases/month to under 600 cases/month in recent months. <br/>
2. Deaths associated with Other hepatitis have consistently remained low throughout the data period, with most months reporting zero deaths. <br/>
3. The years from 2022 to 2025 indicate stabilization in the number of cases, fluctuating under 700, suggesting improved control of the disease. <br/>
4. The data from 2025 shows signs of a minor resurgence when the cases rose above 700 in January 2025, warranting continued monitoring despite the decrease. <br/>

The data for hepatitis cases labelled as "Other" in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 display a discernible downward trend. Initially, the monthly reported cases ranged around 2,000 but gradually decreased, dropping below the 1,000-mark in 2019, and continued to remain lower through the subsequent years until 2025. Specifically, this decline from 2015 to 2025 can be attributed to public health interventions, increased awareness, or better preventive measures. Although there are some fluctuations within this timeframe, the overarching pattern suggests an improvement in controlling the spread of these hepatitis cases. 

Throughout the observed period, sporadic deaths associated with these cases are recorded, though in very low numbers, with one or two deaths approximately every few months before reaching 2025, where no deaths are reported in the available data. This suggests better case management or earlier detection and intervention efforts which may have reduced the case fatality rate. The decrease in both cases and mortality could indicate advancements in healthcare systems, better diagnostics, or public health policies effectively mitigating the factors contributing to the transmission and severity of hepatitis classified under "Other".
