# Other hepatitis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to types of liver inflammation not classified under the well-known hepatitis viruses A, B, C, D, or E. These can include hepatitis resulting from autoimmune disorders, alcohol consumption, drug-induced damage, or secondary infections like Epstein-Barr virus, cytomegalovirus, and others. Autoimmune hepatitis occurs when the body's immune system mistakenly attacks liver cells. Alcoholic hepatitis is related to excessive alcohol intake, while drug-induced hepatitis can result from the use of medications, supplements, or illicit drugs. Identifying the cause of other hepatitis is crucial for appropriate management and treatment to prevent further liver damage.

## Highlights

- Other hepatitis cases in mainland China have exhibited a consistent decline from 2015 to 2025, with monthly cases decreasing from over 2,000 in 2015 to under 600 by August 2025. <br/>
- The number of deaths has remained very low throughout the period, occasionally fluctuating but overall decreasing. In 2024 and 2025, deaths were mostly absent. <br/>
- Dramatic reductions in cases began around 2018, likely due to improved prevention and control measures. Monthly cases fell below 1,000 from the end of 2018 onwards. <br/>
- Recent years, particularly past 2023, show a stabilization in case numbers, suggesting effective ongoing surveillance and hepatological healthcare measures. <br/>

The dataset charts monthly records of other hepatitis cases and deaths in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025, revealing fluctuations in case numbers. Initially, during 2015-2016, case reports frequently exceed 2,000 monthly, with a gradual decline noted over the years, reaching below 500 by late 2024 and early 2025. Despite the higher early figures, deaths remain infrequent, suggesting mitigating factors. As case numbers decrease, deaths occur sporadically, with some months in certain years showing more than one death, although these remain rare throughout the dataset.

No direct correlation between case numbers and fatalities is evident, with low case counts not consistently equating to fewer deaths and high counts not predictably resulting in more fatalities. Monthly stretches without reported deaths suggest generally benign infections or effective management. Periodic deaths, despite fewer cases later on, highlight potential vulnerabilities in specific population segments or under certain conditions. This pattern indicates potential areas for investigation into the health system's response to severe hepatitis cases, the impact of other health factors, and socio-environmental conditions affecting mortality rates. Overall, the dataset suggests a general decline in cases without a corresponding linear decrease in deaths, pointing to complex factors influencing hepatitis mortality despite improved case management over the reporting period.
