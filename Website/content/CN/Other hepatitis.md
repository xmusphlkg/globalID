# Other hepatitis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Other hepatitis refers to liver inflammation caused by less common or non-viral factors, including autoimmune hepatitis, alcoholic hepatitis, and drug-induced hepatitis. Unlike hepatitis A, B, or C, these forms are not spread through infection but result from immune system dysfunction, toxic substance exposure, or medications. Symptoms may include fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal discomfort. Diagnosis involves blood tests, imaging, and sometimes liver biopsy. Treatment varies depending on the cause, ranging from lifestyle changes and medication to immunosuppressive therapy. Early detection is crucial to prevent progression to cirrhosis or liver failure.

## Highlights

- Sustained decrease: Case numbers dropped from over 2,000/month in 2015 to around 600–700/month by early 2025, showing long-term control.<br/>  
- Minimal fatality: Deaths have remained extremely low, with most months reporting none and only isolated months showing 1–2 deaths.<br/>  
- Post-pandemic stability: After a sharp dip in 2020 due to COVID-19, monthly cases stabilized from 2021 onward, reflecting resumed surveillance.<br/>  
- April 2025 update: With 623 cases and zero deaths, the disease situation remains stable and under control in the Chinese mainland.<br/>

From 2015 to 2025, reported cases of "Other hepatitis" in mainland China exhibit a marked downward trend, declining from monthly counts exceeding 2,600 in early 2015 to under 800 by 2025. This decrease accelerated particularly after 2018, aligning with broader public health reforms and potentially enhanced diagnostic specificity that may have reclassified previously untyped hepatitis cases into more definitive categories. The sharp decline during early 2020, notably to just 404 cases in February, coincides with the onset of nationwide COVID-19 containment measures, including lockdowns and reduced healthcare utilization, which likely disrupted routine disease surveillance and reporting, rather than epidemiologically reflecting true incidence.

Mortality remained consistently low across the decade, with annual deaths fluctuating between 0 to 4 and showing no clear association with case count peaks. Years like 2018 and 2019 had slightly higher death tallies despite falling case numbers, suggesting either isolated outbreaks with more severe manifestations or better death attribution practices. After 2021, both case numbers and reported deaths settled into a markedly lower and stable range, possibly reflecting steady diagnostic practices, reduced transmission vectors, or advances in prevention and health service access. Overall, the data reflect not only epidemiological dynamics but also how surveillance intensity and public health interventions shape disease reporting.

The highest concentration of deaths occurred between 2015 and 2020, which accounted for over 70% of the total. Reported deaths were not always proportionate to case volume, suggesting factors beyond incidence rate, such as clinical misclassification, regional diagnostic/reporting standards, or shifts in healthcare access and interventions, may partly explain the observed mortality variance.

From 2021 onward, both the number of cases and associated deaths have shown a noticeable decline. Notably, between January 2023 and April 2025—a span of 28 consecutive months—only one death was reported (in March 2024), indicating a marked drop in fatality events. This could reflect either actual mortality reduction due to improved disease management or changes in surveillance sensitivity and reporting protocols. The long sequence of zero-death months may also point to reclassification or better differentiation from other hepatitis types, as “Other hepatitis” is a residual category encompassing rarer forms.
