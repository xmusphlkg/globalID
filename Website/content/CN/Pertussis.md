# Pertussis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious bacterial infection of the respiratory tract caused by Bordetella pertussis. It is characterized by severe coughing fits that end in a high-pitched "whoop" sound during inhalation. Pertussis primarily affects infants and young children, but it can occur at any age. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, with the DTaP and Tdap vaccines providing protection for children and adults, respectively. Despite vaccination efforts, pertussis outbreaks can still occur, necessitating vigilant public health measures.

## Highlights

- Pertussis saw a major surge in cases starting early 2024, peaking at 97,669 in May, and has since returned to lower, endemic levels as of October 2025.  
<br/>
- Despite high case numbers, deaths have remained low with fewer than 20 annually from 2023-2025, indicating effective healthcare responses.  
<br/>
- As of October 2025, cases have significantly reduced to 893, reflecting successful control measures and public health strategies.  
<br/>
- While case numbers have decreased steadily from the 2024 outbreak, periodic spikes suggest the need for ongoing vigilance and effective vaccination campaigns to prevent future surges.  
<br/>

The data on pertussis cases in mainland China from late 2015 to October 2025 shows distinct patterns of fluctuations, with peaks and troughs in reported cases. From 2015 to 2019, there was a general increase in cases, peaking in mid-2019, where July and August combined reported over 8,000 cases. This period also had low but noticeable deaths compared to the number of cases.

In 2020, cases sharply declined, likely influenced by the pandemic-related public health measures that curtailed respiratory infections. From mid-2020 to early 2022, cases remained low, averaging a few hundred monthly. However, there was a resurgence in cases starting mid-2022, leading to a massive surge by 2024, with cases exceeding 90,000 in some months. This dramatic increase may indicate lapses in vaccination, vaccine failures, or changes in reporting. The peak in early 2024 suggests a significant outbreak, as April alone had over 91,000 cases. Following this surge, a decline occurred in late 2024 through 2025, potentially reflecting stronger public health measures.

Despite the fluctuations, deaths remained low, with an occasional rise corresponding with higher case numbers. The overall low mortality rate suggests effective medical treatments and management of severe cases. This highlights the cyclical nature of pertussis outbreaks and underscores the importance of consistent vaccination and public health strategies to mitigate outbreaks.
