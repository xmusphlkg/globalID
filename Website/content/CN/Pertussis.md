# Pertussis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It primarily affects infants and young children, but can occur in individuals of any age. Pertussis is characterized by severe coughing fits, which can lead to difficulty breathing, vomiting, or rib fractures. The "whooping" sound occurs as patients struggle to inhale after coughing. Spread through respiratory droplets, pertussis can be prevented by vaccination. The DTaP vaccine is recommended for children and the Tdap vaccine for older children, adolescents, and adults to reduce infection risk and protect vulnerable populations.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline:** Pertussis cases in the Chinese mainland peaked at 97,669 in May 2024 but have drastically decreased to 1,774 by August 2025, indicating effective containment measures. <br/>

- **Mortality Rate:** Despite the high number of cases, overall mortality remains low, with a few sporadic deaths each year. No deaths have been reported from March to August 2025. <br/>

- **Seasonal Pattern:** There's a noticeable seasonal fluctuation in cases, with peaks often occurring from Spring to Summer. <br/>

- **Improvement in 2025:** The first eight months of 2025 show a steady decline, suggesting an overall improvement in controlling the epidemic. <br/>

The analysis of pertussis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 indicates a fluctuating trend with distinct patterns over the years. Initially, from mid-2015 through 2016, cases were relatively lower and consistent, with numbers mostly ranging from 300 to 750 per month. The incidence started rising notably in 2017, peaking sharply in the summer months and continuing this upward trend into 2018. During this period, the monthly cases reached over 4,000 by August 2018, showing a seasonal pattern of increasing during the warmer months likely attributable to increased social interactions and potentially lower vaccination rates or waning immunity in certain age groups.

This upward trend continued, marked by the highest peaks in mid-2024, where monthly cases soared dramatically, surpassing 90,000 in April 2024, likely indicating a substantial outbreak or reporting anomaly. Comparing the case numbers from 2019 till 2024, a distinct surge is observable especially during April to July 2024, which was accompanied by an increased but still low mortality rate relative to the number of cases. The subsequent months showed a consistent decline into 2025, suggesting a potential control of the outbreak by public health interventions or improved vaccination coverage. The data illustrate how public health responses likely impacted the cases, and the importance of monitoring for the re-emergence of vaccine-preventable diseases like pertussis, emphasizing the critical role of sustained vaccination programs and timely outbreak response.
