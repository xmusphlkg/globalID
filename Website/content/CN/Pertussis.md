# Pertussis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, or whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium *Bordetella pertussis*. It spreads through respiratory droplets and primarily affects infants and young children, although adolescents and adults can also be infected. The disease typically begins with mild respiratory symptoms resembling a cold, followed by severe coughing fits that may end in a high-pitched “whoop” sound when inhaling. Complications can include pneumonia, seizures, and, in severe cases, death—particularly in infants. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure, with the DTaP and Tdap vaccines offering protection against pertussis.

## Highlights

- Pertussis cases surged dramatically in 2024, peaking in April with over 91,000 cases—the highest monthly figure recorded—before declining gradually into early 2025. <br/>
- From 2015 to 2023, the disease showed seasonal fluctuations with moderate increases between June and September, but annual case totals remained relatively stable. <br/>
- Beginning in late 2023, a sharp uptick was observed, likely indicating reduced immunity or gaps in vaccination coverage, although the death rate remained low across all periods. <br/>
- As of April 2025, the number of reported cases (4,506) continues to decline from the 2024 peak, suggesting improving control, but surveillance and immunization efforts must be sustained. <br/>

The data on pertussis in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals distinct fluctuations in case numbers, presenting an intriguing narrative about the disease's epidemiology in the region. Initially, there is a gradual increase in cases from 2015, culminating in a peak in 2019 when the highest monthly cases recorded in August were 4,388. This increase might be reflective of several factors, including potential lapses in vaccination coverages, changes in bacterial virulence, or perhaps enhanced detection and reporting mechanisms. Interestingly, from 2015 to 2019, mortality associated with pertussis remained extremely low, reflecting possible effective healthcare interventions minimizing fatalities despite increasing cases.

However, a dramatic surge is evident from late 2023 through mid-2024, with the data displaying unprecedented escalations, peaking at 91,272 cases in April 2024. This sharp rise is not isolated, as it aligns with slight increases in mortality, indicating a potential change in the disease's impact or a healthcare system under strain. Following this peak, there is a marked decline in cases by the end of 2024, stabilizing into 2025. This pattern suggests responses may have been initiated, possibly involving vaccine campaigns or public health interventions, effectively bringing pertussis under control. The volatility in numbers over this decade indicates variations in infection dynamics and might suggest a need for regularly updated public health strategies focused on maintaining vaccination coverage and disease surveillance.

Between 2015 and early 2025, the reported deaths due to pertussis in mainland China remained consistently low relative to the substantial variation and, in many years, rapid escalation in reported case numbers. From 2015 until 2018, despite moderate seasonal and annual fluctuations in cases, annual deaths remained under two per year, with the majority of months reporting zero fatalities. Isolated deaths occurred sporadically and did not appear to correlate strongly with seasonal peaks in incidence, suggesting that case severity remained largely unchanged or that public health interventions and access to care mitigated mortality efficiently, even during peak transmission months.

This trend began to shift in late 2019 and especially intensified across 2023 and 2024, where surges in both notifications and fatalities became pronounced. In 2024 alone, a dramatic spike in reported cases—peaking in April at over 91,000—was accompanied by corresponding increases in monthly deaths, hitting a high of eight in February. The increase in absolute deaths, while still comparatively low to total case numbers, signals either an increased burden on the healthcare system, potential strain in case management during outbreak surges, or shifts in pathogen virulence or host susceptibility. Nonetheless, even at its peak in early 2024, the case-fatality rate remained exceptionally low, reinforcing the efficacy of the Chinese public health system in preventing mortality across large-scale pertussis outbreaks.
