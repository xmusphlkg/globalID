# Pertussis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, also known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium *Bordetella pertussis*. It primarily affects infants and young children but can occur at any age. The infection is characterized by severe coughing fits followed by a distinctive "whoop" sound during inhalation. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Pertussis can lead to serious complications, particularly in unvaccinated populations. Immunization with pertussis-containing vaccines is the most effective preventive measure. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are important for managing outbreaks.

## Highlights

- Pertussis incidence in mainland China rose sharply from late 2023, peaking in spring–summer 2024 with monthly cases exceeding 90,000 in April and May.<br/>
- Following the peak, cases declined steeply from June 2024, stabilizing at much lower levels by the end of 2025 (about 1,300 reported in December 2025).<br/>
- The disease’s mortality remained low throughout, with brief increases paralleling high case months in early–mid 2024 but dropping to zero deaths from mid-2025 onward.<br/>
- The current situation (December 2025) is characterized by significantly reduced transmission, indicating successful containment after the 2024 surge.<br/>

From late 2015 through 2019, pertussis cases in Chinese mainland showed a gradual annual increase with seasonal peaks, rising from several hundred to over 4,000 monthly. Deaths remained sporadic and low across these years, often recorded as zero or one per month, suggesting both limited outbreak severity and generally effective clinical management. A sharp drop in cases occurred in 2020-2021, consistent with the COVID-19 pandemic’s impact on both disease transmission and reporting, followed by a steady resurgence through 2022 and 2023, when monthly figures once again reached several thousand.

A striking surge emerged from late 2023 through much of 2024: cases escalated dramatically, peaking at more than 97,000 in a single month (May 2024), a magnitude far surpassing prior years. Despite this explosion in reported cases, deaths increased only slightly; most months in this period still recorded single-digit fatalities. This disparity may reflect improved case detection, diagnostic criteria changes, expanded surveillance, or a transient drop in population immunity—especially in the context of post-pandemic effects on routine vaccination. After the unprecedented 2024 peak, incidence declined quickly by late 2024 and into 2025, returning to pre-surge levels. The minimal mortality alongside large case numbers underlines effective clinical response, demographic shifts in susceptibility, and/or a pattern of predominantly milder cases during the surge.

Pertussis deaths in mainland China are rare and scattered, typically occurring as isolated cases even amid substantial rises in case numbers. From 2015 through 2022, most months recorded no deaths, with only sporadic appearances—rarely more than one per month—despite progressively growing case notifications and pronounced seasonal case peaks. The low mortality, which remained largely unchanged during pandemic-induced declines and rebounds in notifications, suggests both the effectiveness of healthcare interventions and a probable undercurrent of cases occurring in age groups at lower risk of fatal outcomes.

A striking shift emerges in 2023 and particularly 2024, marked by unprecedented increases in reported pertussis cases alongside notable upswings in deaths. From August 2023 through February 2024, multiple months saw up to eight fatalities, a sharp contrast to earlier years. However, this uptick in deaths did not consistently mirror peak months for case notifications; periods with the highest incidence sometimes reported few or no deaths, and mortality quickly tapered after the initial outbreak crescendo. This weak temporal alignment between deaths and cases, even amid very high transmission, may reflect changes in disease severity, reporting practices, or affected population demographics. Overall, fatality rates remained low relative to case counts, with deaths accentuating principally during periods of exceptional case surges, underscoring the importance of close monitoring and targeted intervention during outbreaks.
