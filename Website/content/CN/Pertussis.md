# Pertussis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It is characterized by severe coughing spells that can last for weeks and may include the signature high-pitched "whoop" sound during inhalation. Pertussis primarily affects infants and young children, but it can also occur in adolescents and adults. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Vaccination with the DTaP (diphtheria, tetanus, and acellular pertussis) vaccine is the most effective way to prevent infection. Despite vaccination efforts, pertussis remains a significant public health concern due to waning immunity over time.

## Highlights

- There has been a significant surge in Pertussis cases in the Chinese mainland from January 2024, with cases peaking at 97,669 in May 2024 before declining. <br/>
- Deaths due to Pertussis have also increased, with a notable spike in early 2024 and more fluctuations observed throughout the rest of the year. <br/>
- Following the peak in mid-2024, the number of cases has been gradually decreasing but remains notably higher than in pre-2024 levels, with 4,186 cases recorded in March 2025. <br/>
- Public health interventions may be needed to address the recent surge in cases and prevent further outbreaks. <br/>

The analysis of pertussis cases in mainland China from March 2015 to March 2025 reveals a fluctuating pattern with notable surges in certain periods. Initially, from 2015 to 2018, the data shows a gradual increase in reported cases, reaching a peak in August 2018 with 4,134 cases followed by a decline in the subsequent months. From 2019 onwards, monthly cases varied significantly, with peaks in the summer months, as seen in July 2019 with 3,635 cases. However, starting in October 2020, there was a substantial decrease in reported cases that continued through 2021. With 2021 seeing a resurgence of cases peaking in December at 2,254.

From 2022 onwards, the data indicate a dramatic rise in pertussis cases, particularly from March 2024, where cases soared, peaking in April with an unprecedented 91,272 cases. This surge represents a striking deviation from earlier trends, possibly reflecting increased transmission rates or reporting changes. Between March 2024 and November 2024, the number of cases decreased progressively, stabilizing within the range of several hundreds to thousands per month by March 2025. Throughout the dataset, the number of deaths associated with pertussis remains relatively low, suggesting high survival rates or effective medical interventions despite the rise in cases. The peaks and troughs observed might be attributed to variations in vaccination coverage, seasonal factors, public health initiatives, or diagnostic improvements.

From 2015 to early 2023, death occurrences linked to pertussis in mainland China remained sporadic and low, with only isolated months recording individual deaths, despite gradual increases in case numbers through this period. The fatality profile showed that even during months with sharp rises in cases—such as August 2018 and mid-2019—the number of deaths rarely exceeded one per month. Notably, 2020 to 2021 reflected relatively low incidence and minimal mortality, likely influenced by non-pharmaceutical interventions during the COVID-19 pandemic. This suppression effect seems to have delayed transmission but not eliminated susceptibility.

A marked shift appears beginning in late 2023 and intensifying sharply throughout 2024, where a significant case surge is mirrored by an uptick in deaths. Between January and May 2024, cases escalated from 15,275 to over 97,000 monthly, while deaths climbed in step from 5 to a peak of 8 in February, with recurrent increases thereafter. While the case fatality rate remains relatively low, the growing absolute death count raises concerns regarding disease surveillance, population immunity gaps, and especially protection in vulnerable age groups. The emergence of monthly deaths greater than one in several consecutive months from late 2023 through 2024 marks a departure from the earlier pattern and suggests a shifting burden that warrants urgent epidemiologic investigation and public health response.
