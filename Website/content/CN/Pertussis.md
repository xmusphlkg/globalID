# Pertussis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Pertussis, also known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium *Bordetella pertussis*. It is characterized by severe coughing fits that can lead to difficulty breathing, particularly in infants and young children. The disease spreads through respiratory droplets and can be prevented by vaccination. Pertussis can be particularly dangerous in infants under 1 year old, leading to complications such as pneumonia, seizures, and even death. Adults and older children may experience milder symptoms but can still transmit the bacteria to vulnerable individuals.

## Highlights

- **Significant Increase in Cases in 2024**: Pertussis cases in China surged dramatically in the first half of 2024, peaking in April with over 91,000 cases and remaining high through August.  
<br/>  
- **Lower Mortality Rates**: Despite the increase in cases, fatalities have remained relatively low, with only 3 deaths reported between January and October 2024.

The data on pertussis in the Chinese mainland shows fluctuating trends, with notable peaks in cases during the mid-2010s and a sharp resurgence in 2024. After a relatively stable period in early 2020, likely due to COVID-19 control measures, pertussis cases spiked significantly in late 2023 and into 2024. The rise in cases was particularly pronounced from January to April 2024, with cases reaching over 90,000 in April alone. Despite these high case numbers, deaths remain low, with only a few reported sporadically throughout the years.

The sharp increase in 2024, following a peak of 6,410 cases in November 2023, suggests pertussis may have spread more widely, potentially due to reduced immunity in the population or disruptions in vaccination programs. The low fatality rate amidst this surge could indicate improvements in clinical management or that the disease is less likely to result in severe outcomes. However, the significant rise in cases highlights the ongoing need for enhanced surveillance, vaccination efforts, and public health responses, particularly following the pandemic, to control pertussis and prevent further outbreaks.
