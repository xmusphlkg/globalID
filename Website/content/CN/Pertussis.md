# Pertussis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It is characterized by severe coughing fits that can last for weeks, sometimes leading to a "whooping" sound during inhalation. Pertussis spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing, primarily affecting infants and young children but can occur in any age group. Vaccination is critical for prevention, typically administered as part of the DTaP (diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis) vaccine series in children. Complications can include pneumonia, seizures, and, in severe cases, death, particularly in unvaccinated children.

## Highlights

- Pertussis case numbers in mainland China saw a significant peak in 2024, with the highest cases recorded in April 2024 at 91,272 cases, indicating a major outbreak. <br/>
- From mid-2024, there was a notable decline in cases, with cases falling dramatically to 1,028 by September 2025, suggesting effective control measures. <br/>
- Deaths associated with Pertussis remained relatively low throughout the period, with slight increases during the 2024 peak. However, no deaths have been recorded in recent months of 2025. <br/>
- The current situation as of September 2025 reflects a successful response to the 2024 outbreak, with cases substantially reduced and under control. <br/>

The data on pertussis cases in mainland China between 2015 and 2025 shows dynamic trends with notable surges. Initially, monthly cases remain relatively low, below 1000 until 2017, suggesting sporadic outbreaks with intermittent peaks and occasionally reported deaths. However, from 2017 to 2019, an upward trend emerges, peaking in mid-2019 at over 4000 cases monthly, with occasional deaths indicating low overall mortality.

The year 2020 witnesses a sharp decrease in cases, likely influenced by the global COVID-19 pandemic, altering transmission and public health focus. In 2021 and 2022, a resurgence occurs, with cases climbing to over 5000 per month, possibly due to relaxed pandemic measures and shifting public health priorities. The dramatic spike in early 2024, reaching over 90,000 cases in April, suggests a major outbreak potentially linked to changes in immunity, vaccine coverage gaps, or the emergence of a more virulent strain. Following this peak, cases decline significantly, reflecting possible successful interventions or a natural post-outbreak decline.

By 2025, cases settle back to pre-2017 levels, stabilizing in the low hundreds monthly, signifying an effective long-term public health response. The data highlights the cyclical nature of pertussis outbreaks and underscores the need for sustained vigilance and adaptive immunization strategies to manage disease spread successfully.
