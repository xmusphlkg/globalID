# Pertussis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It is characterized by severe coughing fits, which can be followed by a high-pitched "whoop" sound during inhalation. Pertussis spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It poses a significant risk to infants and unvaccinated individuals. Early symptoms resemble a common cold, including runny nose and mild cough, but can progress to severe and prolonged coughing spells. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure, typically provided in combination with tetanus and diphtheria vaccines.

## Highlights

- Pertussis cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant upward trend since early 2024, peaking at over 97,000 cases in May 2024. <br/>
- Deaths due to Pertussis remain relatively low compared to the number of cases, with a noticeable increase in fatalities at the start of 2024 but decreasing by July 2024. <br/>
- The sharp rise in cases during 2024, particularly from March to May, suggests a potential outbreak or increased transmission, requiring urgent public health interventions. <br/>
- Despite fluctuating seasonal patterns in previous years, the current surge highlights the need for enhanced surveillance and vaccination efforts to control the spread. <br/>

The data on pertussis cases in mainland China from July 2014 to July 2024 reveals several key trends. Initially, the number of cases fluctuated modestly, with monthly cases generally remaining under 1,000 until mid-2017. However, a significant upward trend began in 2017, with cases exceeding 1,000 in several months and peaking at 4,134 cases in August 2018. This increase continued, with notable fluctuations, culminating in a sharp spike in the first half of 2024, where monthly cases soared to an unprecedented 97,669 in May and even higher to 91,272 in April. This escalation suggests a widespread outbreak or a surge in the disease's transmission during this period.

The death toll associated with pertussis remained low throughout most of the period, with the highest number of deaths recorded in recent monthsâ€”eight in February 2024. The increasing cases and deaths in 2024 indicate a severe public health concern, possibly reflecting either a decrease in vaccination coverage, an increase in susceptible populations, or a more virulent strain of the disease. The data from 2024, particularly, suggests that the situation may have reached a critical point, necessitating urgent public health interventions.
