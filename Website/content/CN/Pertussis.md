# Pertussis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Pertussis, or whooping cough, is a contagious respiratory disease caused by Bordetella pertussis bacteria. It mainly affects infants and young children but can infect people of any age. The disease is marked by severe coughing fits that can last for weeks, often accompanied by a "whooping" sound when inhaling. Pertussis spreads through respiratory droplets from coughs or sneezes of an infected person. Vaccination, included in DTaP for children and Tdap for older individuals, is the best prevention. Early diagnosis and antibiotics can help manage symptoms and reduce transmission. Effective control relies on high vaccination coverage.

## Highlights

- **Surge in Cases (2024):** A dramatic rise in pertussis cases is evident, peaking in April 2024 with 91,272 cases, then gradually decreasing in following months. <br/>
- **Mortality Trends:** Alongside the surge, deaths increased, notably 8 in February 2024, suggesting healthcare challenges. <br/>
- **Fluctuations (2014-2023):** Significant case increases began around 2017 with intermittent surges, peaking in 2022 before a sharp decline and the 2024 spike. <br/>
- **Urgent Intervention Needed:** The sharp rise in cases and deaths underscores the necessity for enhanced vaccination and robust public health strategies to manage the outbreak and minimize impact. <br/>

The data on Pertussis cases in mainland China from October 2014 to October 2024 reveals several distinct trends and fluctuations in disease incidence. Initially, from late 2014 to early 2018, there was a noticeable rise in the number of reported cases. This increase was marked by periodic peaks, notably during the summer months, which can be associated with certain environmental factors or population behaviors that promote disease transmission. The peak years within this period, specifically 2017 and 2018, demonstrated a significant escalation, culminating in cases exceeding 4,000 per month during late summer of 2018.

After 2018, a peculiar shift occurred with a temporary slowdown of reported cases beginning in 2020. This decline coincides with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, suggesting possible impacts from public health measures like lockdowns or increased public awareness and hygiene practices that inadvertently reduced the spread of other infectious diseases like Pertussis. However, post-pandemic years, particularly in 2022, saw a resurgence with a substantial increase in cases. This resurgence hit unprecedented numbers in 2024, with cases skyrocketing to tens of thousands per month, raising concerns over potential vaccine hesitancy, waning immunity in the population, or changes in the bacterial pathogen itself. Deaths remained relatively low considering the scale of the outbreak, suggesting effective clinical management of the disease or robust healthcare response despite the increased caseload.
