# Pertussis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, also known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium *Bordetella pertussis*. It spreads through respiratory droplets and primarily affects infants and young children, though individuals of any age can contract it. The disease is characterized by severe coughing fits that may end in a high-pitched "whoop" sound during inhalation. Complications can include pneumonia, seizures, and even death, particularly in unvaccinated infants. Pertussis can be prevented through vaccination, typically administered as part of the DTaP/Tdap immunization series, which is essential for controlling outbreaks and protecting public health.

## Highlights

- Pertussis cases surged dramatically in 2024, peaking in April with over 91,000 reported cases â€” the highest monthly count in a decade. <br/>
- The outbreak began in late 2023, with a sharp escalation sustained through mid-2024; deaths also increased, with 30 fatalities reported in 2024. <br/>
- As of May 2025, case numbers have declined significantly to 4,260, signaling a potential containment of the outbreak. <br/>
- Seasonal trends persist, with mid-year peaks, but recent high-incidence years suggest reduced herd immunity or gaps in immunization coverage. <br/>

The reported data for pertussis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 exhibits significant fluctuations, with a dramatic increase in the later years. Initially, the cases ranged from about 300 to 1,600 each month, with a few isolated deaths documented annually, indicating a relatively stable pattern between 2015 and 2019. However, starting in 2018, there was a noticeable increase in case numbers, reaching a peak in 2019, after which the figures dropped significantly in 2020, likely attributable to public health measures introduced in response to the COVID-19 pandemic impacting transmission dynamics.

From early 2021 until mid-2023, there was a clear resurgence in pertussis cases with numbers escalating substantially, culminating in peaks during 2024, where monthly cases soared into tens of thousands, a level far exceeding previous years. This sharp rise in cases alongside a non-proportionate number of deaths suggests potential changes in surveillance practices, vaccine coverage, or pathogen characteristics influencing transmission dynamics. Additionally, the rise in cases towards the end of 2023 and throughout 2024, followed by a subsequent decline in 2025, may reflect cycles of epidemic waves or enhanced intervention measures curtailing the spread. The fluctuations observed stress the critical need for ongoing surveillance and vaccination efforts to mitigate the impact of pertussis in the region, particularly given its global status as a re-emerging infectious disease despite widespread vaccine availability.
