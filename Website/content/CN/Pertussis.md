# Pertussis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It primarily affects infants and young children but can occur in people of all ages. The disease is characterized by severe coughing spells that can last for weeks, often making it difficult to breathe and leading to the distinctive "whoop" sound when the person gasps for air. Pertussis is spread through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing. Vaccination, especially with the DTaP vaccine for children and Tdap booster for older children and adults, is the most effective prevention method.

## Highlights

- The data reveals significant fluctuations in monthly pertussis cases between 2015 and 2025, with a peak in 2024 followed by a marked decline. <br/>
- Deaths associated with pertussis were rare, but there were observed occasional increases in fatal cases around periods of high incidence, especially during early 2024. <br/>
- The year 2024 witnessed unprecedented case numbers, particularly from March to August, possibly indicating an outbreak period, before stabilization at lower levels in 2025. <br/>
- The trend of cases in 2025 shows a continued decline from 2024, suggesting effective control measures or increased immunity within the population. <br/>

The pertussis case data from mainland China between June 2015 and June 2025 reveals notable trends. Initially, from 2015 through 2016, cases were moderate, typically ranging from the 300s to over 1,000 per month, with occasional fatalities. During this time, pertussis maintained a somewhat endemic presence, with no extreme peaks in incidence.

Starting in mid-2017, there was a significant escalation in cases through 2019, reaching an unprecedented 4,134 cases in August 2018. This shift suggests changes in pertussis epidemiology, potentially influenced by public health policies, vaccination coverage, or pathogen dynamics. Despite high case numbers, low death rates may indicate improved disease management or a less virulent strain impacting populations.

In contrast, 2020 saw a sharp decline in cases during the global COVID-19 pandemic, as social restrictions likely reduced transmission rates. However, late 2023 to mid-2024 saw a dramatic surge in cases, peaking at tens of thousands monthly—the highest in the dataset. Although deaths remained low relative to cases, this spike suggests issues such as waning immunity or vaccine effectiveness. The decrease by 2025 hints at stabilization possibly due to corrective measures, emphasizing the need for continued robust monitoring and adaptive health responses to effectively manage pertussis.

The epidemiological data on pertussis in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals interesting trends in reported deaths relative to reported cases. Between June 2015 and October 2016, the monthly number of cases ranged from 332 to 999, with sporadic deaths reported, specifically in November 2015 and several months throughout 2016. A noticeable increase in pertussis cases occurred from 2017 through 2024, peaking in April 2024 with 91,272 reported cases. During this period, the deaths were sporadic, generally low compared to the number of cases, suggesting a low case-fatality rate, likely due to effective medical interventions and vaccinations.

The data from 2023 onwards shows an upward trajectory in cases, reaching significantly high numbers, particularly in April 2024. Despite the high incidence of cases during peak months, the number of deaths remains consistently low, reflecting either improvements in healthcare delivery or changes in disease virulence. The notable fluctuations in monthly case numbers during the latter years could be attributed to cyclical trends, vaccination coverage, and public health responses. It highlights the critical importance of vaccination programs and healthcare access in mitigating the mortality impact of pertussis, even amid rising cases, as reflected more visibly from 2023 onwards.
