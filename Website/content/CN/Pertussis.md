# Pertussis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium *Bordetella pertussis*. It is characterized by severe coughing fits that often end in a "whooping" sound when the person breathes in. Pertussis spreads through respiratory droplets and is particularly dangerous for infants and young children, potentially leading to severe complications such as pneumonia, seizures, and even death. Vaccination is the primary method of prevention, with the DTaP vaccine recommended for children and the Tdap booster for adolescents and adults. Early diagnosis and antibiotic treatment are crucial.

## Highlights

- **Dramatic Surge**: Pertussis cases peaked in April 2024 with 91,272 cases, a major increase compared to prior years.<br/>

- **Declining Trend**: After the April peak, cases have dropped to 17,316 by September 2024, indicating improved control measures or natural decline.<br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite high case numbers, deaths remain few, suggesting effective management and treatment of the disease.<br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: Cases typically rise mid-year, suggesting seasonal transmission patterns influenced by environmental or social factors.<br/>

From September 2014 to September 2024, pertussis cases in mainland China exhibited notable fluctuations. Between late 2014 and 2016, cases remained relatively low, averaging a few hundred per month. A significant rise began in 2017, with monthly cases surpassing 1,000 and peaking at 1,605 in August. This trend continued into 2018, with cases reaching over 4,000 in August, indicating potential declines in herd immunity or vaccination compliance.

A temporary decline occurred in 2020, possibly due to the COVID-19 pandemicâ€™s impact on health systems or public health measures. Cases surged again in 2021 and 2022, with a peak of 5,355 in August 2022. However, 2023 saw an unprecedented escalation, culminating in a dramatic rise in 2024, reaching 27,078 cases in March.

Despite the rising number of cases, fatalities remained relatively low, though there was a slight increase in deaths correlating with the spikes in cases. This pattern suggests possible outbreaks, waning immunity, or changes in the healthcare system.
