# Pertussis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Pertussis is characterized by severe coughing fits followed by a "whooping" sound when the patient breathes in. It can affect people of all ages but is most dangerous for infants and young children. Complications can include pneumonia, seizures, and, in severe cases, death. Vaccination is the most effective preventive measure. Despite immunization efforts, outbreaks continue due to waning immunity and variable vaccine coverage.

## Highlights

1. There was a drastic increase in Pertussis cases from 2024, peaking in April with over 90,000 cases before declining gradually through early 2025. <br/>
2. The mortality rate remained low throughout the years; deaths remained consistently minimal even during peak case numbers, suggesting effective management. <br/>
3. Pertussis cases have sharply decreased, now reported as below 3,000 in July 2025, highlighting successful control measures after the peak outbreak period. <br/>
4. The upward trend that started in 2023 indicates periodic surges, necessitating vigilance and continued public health interventions to sustain low incidence rates. <br/>

Analyzing pertussis cases in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals distinct trends. Initially, cases were relatively low, averaging around 600 per month in 2015, with occasional fluctuations. By mid-2017, the data suggests seasonal peaks in the summer months, particularly in July and August, when case numbers exceed 1,000. A significant jump occurs in 2018, with August recording 4,134 cases, marking a dramatic increase from previous years. This rise continues through 2019, indicating potential changes in transmission dynamics or public health challenges.

In 2020, a notable decline in cases is observed, likely correlated with COVID-19 pandemic restrictions reducing transmission opportunities. However, 2022 and 2023 see a resurgence, culminating in a substantial spike in 2024, with cases peaking in April at over 91,000. This unprecedented level, combined with a slight rise in deaths, suggests possible shifts in disease severity or public health response issues. By 2025, a decline is notable, indicating potentially effective interventions or natural epidemiological changes.

Throughout this period, deaths remain low compared to the high case counts, although an uptick is seen from 2023 onwards. Overall, the patterns highlight factors such as vaccination, immunity, pathogen mutation, and public health strategies, underscoring the importance of continuous surveillance and adaptive healthcare responses to manage pertussis effectively.
