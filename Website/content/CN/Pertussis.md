# Pertussis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. It primarily affects infants and young children, but can also impact adults. The disease is characterized by severe coughing spells followed by a high-pitched "whoop" sound during inhalation. Pertussis can lead to complications such as pneumonia, seizures, and even death, particularly in infants less than six months old. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent pertussis, with the DTaP vaccine recommended for children and Tdap booster shots for adolescents and adults.

## Highlights

- Significant surge in Pertussis cases in early 2024, with the number reaching 97,669 in May, compared to 1,334 in May 2023. <br/>
- Noticeable increase in related deaths, peaking at 8 deaths in February 2024 and 7 in April 2024, indicating a growing severity. <br/>
- Historical data shows fluctuations with notable peaks in mid-2018 and a gradual decline until early 2022, followed by a resurgence from mid-2022 onwards. <br/>
- The recent exponential rise in early 2024 highlights an urgent need for public health interventions and increased surveillance to control the outbreak. <br/>

The reported data for Pertussis in Chinese mainland from 2014 through early 2024 indicates an escalating trend in incidence, particularly starting from 2018. Initially, cases exhibit a seasonal peak in the warmer months each year, with moderate fluctuations. However, from 2018 the trend shows a marked and continuous increase in reported cases. This suggests factors beyond cyclical seasonal variation, such as gaps in vaccine coverage or changes in pathogen transmission, may be contributing to the rise.

By late 2023 and early 2024, the number of cases surged exponentially, with January 2024 registering 15,275 cases. This substantial rise warrants scrutinized epidemiological investigation. Despite the increase in cases, the mortality rate remains low, suggesting effective clinical management of diagnosed cases but underlining the need for enhanced preventative measures. The data signals an imperative for public health interventions to curb the increasing trend of Pertussis infections.

Notable rises in cases are seen from mid-2017 onwards, leading to a sharp escalation starting in mid-2018. This trend reaches its peak with an explosive increase in cases during 2024, especially April, which accounted for 91,272 cases alone. 

When considering deaths, the data demonstrates sporadic fatalities across the timeline, with no deaths reported in many months despite increased cases. This indicates an elevated fatality rate during periods with exceptionally high case numbers.
