# Plague

## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*. It primarily affects rodents but can be transmitted to humans through flea bites, direct contact with infected animals, or inhalation of respiratory droplets. There are three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic plague. Bubonic plague, the most common, is characterized by swollen, painful lymph nodes. Historically, plague caused devastating pandemics such as the Black Death in the 14th century. Modern outbreaks are rare but still occur in some regions. Prompt antibiotic treatment is effective when administered early.

## Highlights

- Sporadic plague cases were observed sporadically between 2016 and 2024, with annual occurrences fluctuating between 0 and 4 cases. <br/>
- There has been a decrease in cases and deaths reported since August 2024, suggesting successful containment and management efforts. <br/>
- Fatalities remained relatively low, with occasional spikes, notably in 2020 and 2023, indicating that public health interventions may have mitigated severe outcomes. <br/>
- As of May 2025, no new cases or deaths have been reported, reflecting sustained control measures and favorable surveillance outcomes in the Chinese mainland.<br/>

Between May 2015 and May 2025, reported cases of plague in the Chinese mainland remained sporadic, with extended periods of zero incidence interspersed with occasional single-case months and a few concentrated clusters. Notable surges occurred in November 2019 (4 cases), August 2020 (2 cases), and August 2023 (4 cases), suggesting sporadic flare-ups rather than continuous transmission. Most years exhibited either zero or one case annually. The earliest post-zero case occurred in June 2016, with a single case and no associated death, signaling the sporadic re-emergence of the pathogen after over a year of inactivity.

Fatality distribution was inconsistent. Several isolated deaths were reported, such as in December 2017, September 2019, August 2020, September 2022, August 2023, and again in August 2024. Interestingly, 2019 had both the highest case count and multiple months with reported infections, including one death and a burst of four cases in November. Seasonal recurrence is implied by a moderate clustering in late summer to early autumn (August–September). Despite the absence of a sustained upward trend, the temporal clustering of both cases and deaths in August across multiple years highlights the necessity for active surveillance, especially during this period.

Notably, plague mortality was sporadic, with lethal events isolated in time and space. The earliest death appeared in December 2017, followed by a mortality event in September 2019. The year 2020 had three plague-related deaths, including two in August and one in January, despite only four cases registered that year—indicating a high case fatality ratio. Isolated deaths reemerged in 2022 (September), 2023 (August), and most recently in August 2024. Each of these cases was often singular or clustered with minimal case counts, maintaining a fluctuating but low incidence pattern with occasional fatal progression.

Throughout the decade, fatality events never exceeded two in a single month, except for August 2020. There is also observable summer seasonality in cases tied to fatalities, with August appearing three times across different years (2020, 2023, 2024) as the month of both incidence and death. Despite low prevalence overall, several of the fatal cases occurred during months with only a single or few reported infections, underscoring the pathogen's persistent but latent potential for lethality and the importance of swift detection and clinical response when outbreaks arise.
