# Plague

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. Historically notorious for pandemics like the Black Death in the 14th century, it primarily affects rodents but can be transmitted to humans through flea bites, direct contact, inhalation, or consumption of contaminated animals. There are three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic. Symptoms can include fever, chills, and swollen lymph nodes. Without prompt antibiotic treatment, plague can be fatal. It remains endemic in certain regions, highlighting the importance of monitoring and control measures to prevent outbreaks. Vaccination efforts and public health interventions help manage its spread.

## Highlights

- Plague cases in mainland China have shown sporadic occurrences over the past decade, with no consistent annual trend, indicating control measures are effective despite occasional spikes. <br/>
- The highest number of cases was reported in November 2019, with 4 cases, but there were no deaths associated with this spike, suggesting effective case management. <br/>
- There are periodic increases in cases with corresponding deaths, notably in August 2020 and August 2023, suggesting possible seasonal factors or localized outbreaks. <br/>
- As of September 2025, there have been no reported cases or deaths for several months, indicating a period of stability in the control of the disease. <br/>

The data on Plague cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals sporadic incidences with relatively few occurrences. Most months show zero cases and zero deaths, indicating that the Plague is not endemic in this region during this period. However, isolated incidents are recorded intermittently, typically ranging from one to four cases, with certain years exhibiting cases in September, December, and occasionally in months like July and August. Notably, there are years—such as 2016, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024—where one or two isolated incidents result in mortality, underscoring the seriousness of the disease despite the low frequency of cases.

Moreover, the pattern of cases suggests a non-seasonal but sporadic occurrence with an absence of consecutive monthly transmissions. The onset of cases often appears abrupt and unlinked from any previous month's trends, indicating discrete outbreaks perhaps linked to isolated environmental, human, or animal factors. The years 2019 and 2023 particularly stand out due to multiple reported cases in a month or in a series of months, suggesting potential episodic clusters or heightened vigilance in disease reporting during those periods. Despite the low numbers, the presence of fatalities underscores the importance of maintaining surveillance and readiness to manage potential sporadic outbreaks of Plague on the mainland, particularly in regions that experience animal or wildlife interactions conducive to its spread.
