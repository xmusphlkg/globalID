# Plague

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Plague is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*. Transmitted primarily through bites from infected fleas, it can also spread via direct contact with infected animals or inhalation of respiratory droplets. Plague is historically notorious for pandemics such as the Black Death, which devastated Europe in the 14th century. There are three main forms: bubonic, characterized by swollen lymph nodes; septicemic, involving bloodstream infection; and pneumonic, affecting the lungs and enabling person-to-person transmission. Although antibiotics have significantly reduced mortality rates, early diagnosis and treatment remain critical to managing outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Plague incidence remains low**: Since 2014, the number of reported plague cases has been sporadic, with several years showing no cases at all. <br/>
- **Small outbreaks observed**: Notable surges occurred in August 2020 (2 cases, 2 deaths), August 2023 (4 cases, 1 death), and August 2024 (2 cases, 1 death), indicating a potential seasonal pattern. <br/>
- **High fatality in some years**: Mortality rates were particularly high during outbreaks, such as in 2014, 2020, and 2024. <br/>
- **Recent stabilization**: By September 2024, no new cases or deaths were reported, showing a temporary control over the disease. <br/>

The data on plague cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows sporadic outbreaks, with notable peaks in specific years. The highest number of cases occurred in 2019, with 6 cases spread across September and November, though there were no deaths except for a single case in September. Other notable years include 2020 and 2023, where small clusters of cases appeared, particularly in August of both years. While the overall incidence of plague is low, the fatality rate can be significant when cases occur, as seen in 2020 and 2023, where about half of the cases resulted in deaths.

The most consistent period of plague inactivity was from 2015 to mid-2016, followed by another lull from 2017 to early 2019. These periods may suggest effective public health measures or limited exposure, though the isolated spikes in some years indicate that plague, while controlled, remains a public health concern in China, especially in remote or rural areas where transmission from wildlife may occur. Monitoring and rapid response seem crucial in preventing widespread outbreaks.
