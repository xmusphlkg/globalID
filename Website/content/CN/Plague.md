# Plague

## Introduction

Plague is a serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, transmitted primarily through the bites of infected fleas or contact with contaminated animals. Historically, it has caused devastating pandemics, notably the Black Death in the 14th century, which decimated populations in Europe and Asia. Plague manifests in three forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic, each with distinct symptoms. Modern medicine has made it treatable with antibiotics, but it remains a public health concern, particularly in regions with poor sanitation and significant rodent populations. Early diagnosis and rapid treatment are crucial for preventing severe outcomes.

## Highlights

- Sporadic cases of the plague in mainland China show a decline in frequency, with the highest monthly cases occurring in November 2019 and August 2023 at 4 each. <br/>
- Mortality rates varied, with certain months experiencing fatal cases (e.g., July and October 2014, December 2017) while others did not despite recorded infections. <br/>
- Since 2020, fewer than 5 cases have been reported annually, indicating improved control measures or reduced transmission. <br/>
- As of May 2024, no new cases or deaths have been reported since November 2023, suggesting a potential stabilization of the disease. <br/>

The data on plague cases in Chinese mainland between May 2014 and May 2024 shows occasional small outbreaks with overall low incidence rates. Generally, the number of reported cases is less than five annually, with peaks observed in warmer months (May to August). This seasonality corresponds with increased activity of fleas, the primary vectors for plague, and greater human-rodent interactions.

Mortality rates are notable, with several months showing a 100% mortality for reported cases, suggesting either delays in diagnosis and treatment or issues related to the virulence of the strains involved. Some months, such as June 2016 and August 2021, show zero deaths despite reported cases, possibly indicating successful treatment interventions or less severe cases. 

The data also shows a small cluster in August 2023 with four cases and one death followed by an isolated case without fatality in November, suggesting variability in public health responses or different circumstances of transmission. The sparse yet consistent occurrences of cases demonstrate that while plague is not widespread, it remains a significant public health concern due to its potential for rapid transmission and high case-fatality rates if untreated. The overall pattern indicates successful containment with effective monitoring and response strategies, crucial for managing such sporadic outbreaks.

Crucially, these were the only reported cases leading to deaths in that year. The data shows no reported cases or deaths for the entire year of 2015, suggesting either successful interventions or a natural lull in the outbreak.

A second discernible rise in plague cases and deaths occurs in 2019, with another three fatalities reported among six documented cases, resulting in a 50% case fatality rate. 2020 sees varied outcomes, including one non-fatal case in July and two fatal cases in August. Similarly, no cases or deaths were reported in most months from 2021 to 2022, interrupted by intermittent occurrences such as a completely non-fatal instance in August 2021 and one fatality in July 2022. August 2023 signals a significant spike with four new cases and one death, translating to a 25% case-fatality ratio, though subsequent months reverted to the no-case trend. The pattern indicates sporadic outbreaks, potentially linked to focused zoonotic transmission or environmental factors, alongside periods of effective control.
