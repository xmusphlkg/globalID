# Plague

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*, typically transmitted to humans through fleas that infest rats. It has caused major pandemics throughout history, including the Black Death in the 14th century. Plague presents in three forms: bubonic (swollen lymph nodes), septicemic (blood infection), and pneumonic (lungs). Symptoms include fever, chills, and swollen lymph nodes. Without treatment, it can be fatal. Modern antibiotics are effective in treating the disease, but prompt medical care is essential to prevent complications and spread.

## Highlights

- **Low and Sporadic Cases**: Plague cases in China have remained rare, with a significant reduction in frequency since 2017. Cases typically occur in isolated incidents, often in remote areas. <br/>
- **No Major Outbreaks**: There have been no large-scale outbreaks or widespread transmission of plague in the Chinese mainland since 2017. <br/>
- **Recent Cases**: The most recent cases were reported in August 2024, with two cases and one death, marking a sporadic occurrence in the past few years. <br/>
- **Mortality**: Despite occasional cases, mortality remains low, with only a few deaths recorded in the last decade. <br/>

The data for plague cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows generally low and sporadic incidences. Most months report zero cases, indicating a stable, low-risk environment. Notable exceptions include one case in June 2016, a death in December 2017, and a small surge in August 2020 with two deaths. The most recent significant occurrence was in August 2023, which saw four cases and one death. These instances suggest localized outbreaks rather than widespread transmission.

The overall pattern of isolated cases, often with minimal fatalities, points to the disease’s persistence in specific ecological areas, likely linked to wildlife reservoirs, such as rodents. Despite these occasional outbreaks, the data suggests effective surveillance and control measures, as the number of cases remains low over the years. The low mortality rate further reflects improvements in diagnosis and treatment. 

While rare, the presence of plague in certain months underscores the importance of continued monitoring, as even isolated incidents can pose public health risks. The absence of cases in many consecutive years indicates that, for the most part, China has managed to prevent large-scale outbreaks, but the occasional surge highlights the need for vigilance.
