## Introduction

The plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*. It primarily affects rodents and is transmitted to humans through the bites of infected fleas. There are three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic, with bubonic being the most common and characterized by swollen lymph nodes, fever, and chills. Historically, the plague caused devastating pandemics, including the Black Death in the 14th century. Though now rare, it remains a public health concern in some regions and can be treated effectively with antibiotics if diagnosed promptly.

## Highlights

- Sporadic cases reported, peaking in 2019, 2020, and 2023, indicating localized outbreaks. <br/>
- If observed closely, deaths often coincide with months of case reports, suggesting challenges in timely medical intervention. <br/>
- No sustained epidemic, but cases and deaths show occasional clustering, particularly in summer months. <br/>
- The recent spike in August 2023 highlights the need for continuous monitoring and readiness for potential outbreaks. <br/>

Between May 2014 and May 2024, Chinese mainland plague data illustrate episodic incidents, with occasional outbreaks and numerous periods without cases. A cluster of cases with a 100% fatality rate was observed in 2014 with three deaths, whereas 2016 through 2020 exhibited isolated events, with a notable increase in 2019, six reported cases, of which one was fatal. A peak in August 2020 resulted in two fatalities. Following a relative decline in 2021 and 2022 with sporadic non-fatal instances, August 2023 saw a sudden increase with four cases and one death. Overall, the data suggest that plague in China occurs infrequently but can have a high case-fatality rate during outbreaks. The fluctuating pattern of cases underscores the importance of sustained surveillance and research into the factors influencing outbreaks to inform public health strategies.
