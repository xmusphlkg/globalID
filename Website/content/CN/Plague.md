# Plague

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Plague is a zoonotic disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. It primarily affects rodents and can be transmitted to humans through flea bites, direct contact with infected animals, or person-to-person via respiratory droplets. Characterized by three major forms—bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic—the disease historically caused pandemics, most notably the Black Death in the 14th century, which decimated Europe’s population. Symptoms vary but often include fever, chills, and swollen lymph nodes. Despite its historical impact, modern antibiotics are effective in treating the plague, and early diagnosis significantly reduces mortality. Prevention includes controlling rodent populations and avoiding flea exposure.

## Highlights

- The overall case count for plague in the Chinese mainland has been relatively low, with occasional sporadic cases reported over the past decade, indicating it is not currently a significant health threat. <br/>
- The years 2019 and 2020 saw a slight uptick in reported cases and deaths, including a peak in August 2020 with two deaths. <br/>
- From mid-2023 to mid-2024, there was an increase in the number of cases and associated fatalities, most notably in August of both years. <br/>
- As of October 2025, no cases or deaths have been reported for the year, suggesting potential containment or underreporting in recent months. <br/>

From the data provided, plague cases in mainland China appear relatively infrequent, suggesting sporadic outbreaks rather than sustained transmission. The data from 2015 to 2025 show months and years with zero reported cases and deaths, interspersed with occasional cases and small clusters. Notably, most years have a single month of reported cases, though 2019 stands out with November reporting four cases, deviating from the general pattern of singular incidences. The death counts, though sporadic, suggest that while cases arise, there is generally successful containment and treatment when they do occur, with few resulting in fatalities.

Periods like June 2016, December 2019, and August 2023 indicate potential outbreak clusters with cases being reported over consecutive months. The cases are sporadic and difficult to predict, although there seems to be a minor trend of increased reporting in mid to late summer months, for example, in August 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024, suggesting a seasonal component possibly related to environmental factors or host activity. Despite these occurrences, the data lacks a distinct upward trend or significant annual increase, highlighting the relatively effective ongoing surveillance and control measures in place within the region.
