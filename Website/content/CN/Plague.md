## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. It primarily affects rodents and is transmitted to humans through the bites of infected fleas. Historically, plague has caused several pandemics, including the Black Death in the 14th century, which decimated Europe's population. There are three main forms of plague: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic, each affecting different parts of the body. Symptoms can include fever, chills, weakness, and swollen lymph nodes. Modern antibiotics are effective in treating plague, but without prompt treatment, it can be fatal. Public health measures and surveillance are crucial in preventing outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Low Incidence:** The data shows sporadic cases of plague with a total of 19 cases reported from 2016 to February 2025, indicating a low incidence rate. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate:** Out of the 19 cases, 7 resulted in death, suggesting a high mortality rate of approximately 37%. <br/>
- **Temporal Clusters:** Notable clusters of cases occurred in November 2019, August 2020, and August 2023, indicating potential seasonal or environmental factors influencing outbreaks. <br/>
- **Recent Stability:** Since August 2024, no new cases have been reported, suggesting effective control measures or natural decline in transmission. <br/>

The data on plague cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 shows a sporadic occurrence of cases with a few notable peaks. The first recorded case appears in June 2016, with no deaths reported. A significant event occurs in December 2017, where one case results in a death, indicating a possible severe form of the disease or delayed medical intervention. The year 2019 sees a small cluster of cases, with a peak in November when four cases are reported, though no deaths occur, suggesting effective medical response or less virulent strains.

In 2020, there is a notable increase in both cases and deaths, particularly in August, where two deaths are reported from two cases, indicating a potential outbreak or a more virulent strain. The data for 2021 and 2022 shows isolated cases with no deaths, except for September 2022, which records one death. The year 2023 sees another peak in August with four cases and one death, followed by a single case in November. The pattern suggests that while the plague is not endemic, it occasionally resurfaces, possibly due to environmental factors or zoonotic transmission, necessitating ongoing surveillance and rapid response capabilities to prevent outbreaks.
