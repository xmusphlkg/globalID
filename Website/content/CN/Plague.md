## Introduction

The plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*. Historically, it has caused several pandemics, including the Black Death in the 14th century, which decimated Europe's population. The disease primarily affects rodents and is transmitted to humans through flea bites or contact with infected animals. There are three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic, each with distinct symptoms. Bubonic plague, the most common form, is characterized by swollen lymph nodes, fever, and chills. Modern antibiotics can effectively treat the plague, but without treatment, it can be fatal.

## Highlights

- **Low Incidence:** The data shows sporadic cases of plague with a total of 19 cases from 2014 to December 2024, indicating a low incidence rate. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate:** Out of the 19 cases, there were 7 deaths, resulting in a mortality rate of approximately 36.8%. This highlights the severity of the disease when it occurs. <br/>
- **Temporal Clusters:** Notable clusters of cases occurred in November 2019, August 2020, and August 2023, suggesting potential seasonal or environmental factors influencing outbreaks. <br/>
- **Recent Trends:** The most recent cases in August 2024 resulted in 2 cases and 1 death, indicating ongoing but limited transmission. Continued surveillance and preventive measures are essential. <br/>

The data on plague cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 shows a sporadic occurrence of the disease, with a total of 19 cases and 7 deaths over the ten-year period. The data indicates that the plague is not endemic, as there are long stretches of time with no reported cases. Notably, there are isolated spikes in cases, such as in November 2019 with four cases and no deaths, and August 2023 with four cases and one death. These spikes suggest possible localized outbreaks or increased surveillance and reporting during those periods.

The mortality rate among reported cases varies, with some months showing a high case-fatality ratio, such as August 2020, where two out of two cases resulted in death. This variability could be due to differences in the virulence of the plague strains, the timeliness and effectiveness of medical interventions, or the health status of the affected individuals. The data also highlights the importance of continued surveillance and rapid response capabilities to manage and contain potential outbreaks, given the historical significance and potential severity of plague infections.
