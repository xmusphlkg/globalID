# Plague

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*. It primarily affects rodents and is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected fleas or by handling contaminated animal tissues. Plague manifests in three clinical forms: bubonic (characterized by swollen lymph nodes), septicemic (infection spreads in the bloodstream), and pneumonic (affecting the lungs and transmissible via respiratory droplets). Historically, plague has resulted in several pandemics, including the Black Death in the 14th century. Today, cases still occur worldwide, but modern antibiotics significantly reduce mortality rates.

## Highlights

- Plague incidence in mainland China has remained extremely low over the past decade, with sporadic cases reported and most months showing zero cases and deaths.<br/>
- Occasional outbreaks occurred in late summer and early autumn, notably in 2019, 2020, 2022, 2023, and 2024, rarely exceeding one to four cases per outbreak.<br/>
- Case fatality rate fluctuated but remained relatively high; deaths occurred in several years, suggesting timely surveillance and response are required.<br/>
- As of December 2025, no new cases or deaths have been reported since August 2024, indicating successful containment and a stable epidemiological situation.<br/>

Between December 2015 and December 2025, plague case reporting in the Chinese mainland shows a distinctive pattern of sporadic outbreaks with long intervals of zero case incidence. Most months have no reported cases, while only a few months each year present isolated cases, often with a gap of several months or years between events. The temporal clustering of cases suggests a persistent but marginal presence within certain ecological niches, with outbreaks occurring independently rather than as part of sustained transmission. For instance, 2019 and 2020 each saw months with brief clusters (four cases in November 2019, and a peak of two deaths in August 2020) but otherwise demonstrated protracted periods of quiescence. Mortality events—though infrequent—occur inconsistently, reflecting either isolated severe presentations or potential delays in detection and intervention.

Looking at the distribution of cases and deaths, there is no clear seasonality, though a few clusters tend to aggregate in summer and late summer months (e.g., July–August), possibly reflecting increased animal or vector activity. The absence of prolonged chains of transmission indicates effective containment, surveillance, and rapid public health response. Occasional spikes (such as the four cases in August 2023 or November 2019) may be indicative of localized zoonotic spillover or environmental perturbations affecting reservoir populations. The overall epidemiologic pattern is one of intermittent, low-level risk, periodically manifesting with isolated human infections and very limited associated mortality.

From late 2015 to mid-2024, reported plague deaths in the Chinese mainland were rare but recurrent, totaling eight across the period. Fatalities tended to concentrate in specific months—December 2017, September 2019, January 2020, August 2020, September 2022, August 2023, and August 2024. The clustering in August and September suggests a seasonal effect, possibly related to increased zoonotic transmission risks during these months, which is consistent with known plague ecology in endemic regions.

Cases and deaths did not always align neatly by month, with instances of deaths occurring in months with no reported cases, such as January 2020, likely reflecting reporting lags or retrospective corrections. Case fatality was consistently high in months with single cases, and in August 2020 multiple deaths corresponded with increased cases, indicating either a localized outbreak or delayed detection and treatment. Most years exhibited zero deaths, and where cases did occur, mortality rates varied—from 100% fatality in some months to none in others with several cases. This persistent if sporadic mortality reveals continued lethality, underscoring the need for vigilance in surveillance and rapid clinical response, particularly in vulnerable rural or frontier populations where the disease is endemic and awareness or access to care may be limited.
