# Plague

## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. Historically known as the "Black Death," it has caused several pandemics, with the most devastating occurring in the 14th century, wiping out a significant portion of Europe's population. Plague is primarily a disease of rodents, transmitted to humans through flea bites or direct contact with infected animals or their tissues. There are three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic, each with distinct symptoms. While modern antibiotics are effective in treating plague, prompt diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent severe complications or death.

## Highlights

- The incidence of plague in the Chinese mainland remained sporadic, with cases appearing intermittently over the years with notable peaks such as August 2020 and August 2023. <br/>
- Mortality, although present, contributed to a total of only seven deaths over the timeframe, indicating that case severity might be low or managed effectively. <br/>
- Recent data for 2025 shows no reported cases or deaths, suggesting either successful control measures or a natural downtrend in plague incidence. <br/>
- Emphasis on surveillance and preventive strategies seems crucial to sustaining low case numbers and minimizing the public health impact of the disease. <br/>

The reported cases of plague in mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 demonstrate a sporadic occurrence with isolated incidents rather than a widespread outbreak. Over the ten-year span, there were intermittent and isolated reports of the plague, with a total of 19 cases reported in various months, with notable occurrences in November 2019 (4 cases) and August 2023 (4 cases). However, deaths were relatively rare, with only 7 recorded deaths throughout the entire timeframe. These include single deaths in December 2017, September 2019, January 2020, August 2020 (two deaths), September 2022, and August 2024. 

The distribution of cases and deaths suggests an infrequent but persistent potential for plague in the region, with no discernible trend of increasing frequency or severity over the years, indicating effective public health measures in place. Moreover, the episodic nature and low mortality rate point to either efficient treatment protocols or timely isolation and containment efforts. The exact causes behind the notable peaks in cases during certain months (such as August 2023) would require further investigation into environmental or human behavioral changes, potential lapses in public health vigilance, or zoonotic factors that temporarily increased transmission risk. Such insights would be critical for preventing future outbreaks and maintaining public health safety.
