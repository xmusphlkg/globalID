## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. It primarily affects humans and other mammals. Transmitted through flea bites, contact with contaminated fluids or tissues, and respiratory droplets, it manifests in three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic. Bubonic plague is characterized by swollen lymph nodes, known as buboes. Historically, plague has led to pandemics with substantial mortality, including the Black Death in the 14th century. Today, cases are rare and often treatable with antibiotics, but it remains endemic in some regions, posing a threat as a potential bioterrorism agent.

## Highlights

1. Plague cases in mainland China are sporadic, with occasional clusters primarily from 2019 to 2024. Notable peaks happened in November 2019 with 4 cases. <br/>

2. Deaths are rare but occurred in 2017, 2019, 2020, 2022, 2023, and 2024, indicating variability in mortality rates. <br/>

3. Cases mostly emerge between July and December, suggesting seasonal patterns affecting transmission. 2025 shows no new cases through July, indicating potential control or containment. <br/>

4. Trends indicate low transmission rates; years like 2021 and the first half of 2025 have no activity, pointing to sporadic outbreaks rather than a continuous epidemic. <br/>

The reported data on plague cases in the Chinese mainland reveals a pattern of low and sporadic incidence from July 2015 to July 2025. Noteworthy is the low overall count of cases, with occasional spikes. The years with reported cases include 2016, 2017, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024, however, with a long-term trend of zero cases in many months. Importantly, the disease largely shows no consistent seasonal trend, with cases appearing irregularly across the years, indicating isolated incidents rather than seasonal outbreaks.

The mortality rate remains minimal, despite the few occasions where the reported cases resulted in deaths. For instance, in August 2020 and September 2022, slight increases in case counts corresponded with recorded fatalities. Similarly, 2019 and 2023 witnessed months where deaths were documented. The sporadic nature of outbreaks, along with the limited number of cases, suggests effective containment and control measures are likely in place. Despite the challenges posed by the sudden appearance of cases at different times, the quick response appears to have mitigated the potential for broader transmission and high fatalities in the region. This indicates a robust public health infrastructure capable of managing potential plague incidences across the mainland.
