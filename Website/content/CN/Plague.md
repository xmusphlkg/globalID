# Plague

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Plague is a zoonotic disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. It primarily affects rodents and is transmitted to humans through the bites of infected fleas. There are three main forms of plague: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic. Bubonic plague, characterized by swollen lymph nodes, is the most common. Septicemic plague occurs when the infection spreads to the bloodstream, and pneumonic plague affects the lungs and can be transmitted from person to person via respiratory droplets. The disease has caused several devastating pandemics throughout history, with the most infamous being the Black Death in the 14th century.

## Highlights

- Sporadic outbreaks of plague have been observed in China between 2014 and 2024, with irregular case counts and some fatal outcomes. <br/>
- Significant outbreaks occurred in July 2014 (1 case, 1 death), October 2014 (2 cases, 2 deaths), and August 2023 (4 cases, 1 death). <br/>
- The most recent case was reported in November 2023, indicating ongoing risk of plague transmission despite sporadic patterns. <br/>
- Overall, cases and deaths are relatively low, with occasional years seeing no cases at all, reflecting effective control measures but underscoring the need for vigilance. <br/>

Analyzing plague data from mainland China between 2014 and 2024 reveals sporadic outbreaks with occasional fatalities. In 2014, singular cases in July and October resulted in deaths, indicating potential for lethal outbreaks. Plague cases significantly decreased after 2014, with a solitary incident reported in mid-2016 where the patient survived, suggesting effective control or less favorable transmission dynamics.

During the following years, scattered cases occasionally surfaced without substantial mortality. In 2019, a small cluster in September and November indicated a brief rise in transmission with varied outcomes. The trend continued with cases emerging and retreating across different years. August 2020 saw a mortality spike with both reported cases resulting in death, differing from the general pattern of low lethality. Meanwhile, isolated fatalities in later years like September 2022 underscore ongoing risks.

By 2023, four cases arose in August, including a death, suggesting brief reemergence and the continuous need for public health vigilance. Overall, the data indicate a low incidence and mortality rate throughout the years. This pattern could show successful control strategies, yet the intermittent outbreak recurrence highlights the necessity of consistent surveillance. The plague persists in animal reservoirs, thus requiring ongoing monitoring and readiness to mitigate potential periodic threats.
