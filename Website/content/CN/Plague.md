## Introduction

Plague is a severe infectious disease caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, primarily transmitted through fleas that infest rodents. It has three main forms: bubonic, septicemic, and pneumonic. Bubonic plague, the most common form, is characterized by swollen lymph nodes, fever, and chills. Septicemic plague occurs when the bacteria enter the bloodstream, while pneumonic plague affects the lungs and can be transmitted between humans through respiratory droplets. Historically, plague pandemics, such as the Black Death in the 14th century, have caused significant mortality and social disruption, highlighting the importance of public health measures in controlling outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Low Incidence:** The overall incidence of plague in the Chinese mainland remains low, with sporadic cases reported intermittently over the years. <br/>

- **Mortality Trends:** While there have been occasional deaths, the mortality rate is not consistently high, indicating effective management and treatment of cases. <br/>

- **Recent Activity:** Notable spikes in cases occurred in November 2019, August 2020, and August 2023, suggesting potential seasonal or environmental factors influencing outbreaks. <br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of January 2025, there are no new reported cases or deaths, indicating a period of stability and control over the disease. <br/>

The data on plague cases in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 shows a sporadic occurrence of cases with a few notable peaks. The majority of months report zero cases and deaths, indicating that the plague is not endemic in the region and outbreaks are rare. However, there are isolated instances of cases, such as in June 2016, December 2017, September 2019, and several months in 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024. These occurrences suggest that while the plague is largely under control, there are occasional outbreaks that may be linked to specific environmental or social factors, such as contact with wildlife or travel-related cases.

The data also highlights a few months with higher case numbers, such as November 2019 with four cases and no deaths, and August 2023 with four cases and one death. These spikes could indicate localized outbreaks that were quickly contained, as evidenced by the low number of deaths relative to cases. The presence of deaths in some months, such as December 2017, September 2019, August 2020, and August 2024, suggests that while the healthcare response is generally effective, there are still challenges in managing severe cases. Overall, the data reflects a successful public health strategy in preventing widespread outbreaks, but it also underscores the need for continued vigilance and rapid response capabilities to address sporadic cases.
