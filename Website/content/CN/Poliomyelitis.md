# Poliomyelitis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Poliomyelitis, commonly known as polio, is a highly infectious viral disease primarily affecting children under five years of age. It is caused by the poliovirus, which spreads through person-to-person contact, contaminated water, and food. The virus multiplies in the intestine, from where it can invade the nervous system, potentially causing paralysis. While many infections are asymptomatic or cause mild flu-like symptoms, severe cases can lead to irreversible paralysis. There is no cure for polio, but it can be prevented through vaccination. Effective immunization programs have significantly reduced polio cases worldwide, leading to eradication efforts in many regions.

## Highlights

- No reported cases or deaths of Poliomyelitis in Chinese mainland from August 2014 to August 2024 indicate sustained elimination. <br/>
- Consistent zero incidence suggests effective implementation of vaccination programs and public health measures. <br/>
- Continuous surveillance is necessary to prevent re-importation and maintain polio-free status. <br/>
- The data emphasizes China's ongoing commitment to global polio eradication efforts and highlights successful public health strategies. <br/>

The data provided for Poliomyelitis cases and deaths in mainland China from August 2014 to August 2024 shows a consistent pattern of zero reported cases and deaths over the entire decade. This lack of recorded incidents suggests that poliomyelitis transmission has been effectively controlled or eliminated in the region during this period. It reflects the success of public health interventions, including widespread immunization campaigns, which have contributed to the sustained absence of the disease.

The data aligns with global efforts towards poliomyelitis eradication, especially as China has been free from indigenous wild poliovirus cases since 1994. The ongoing monitoring and reporting of zero cases indicate a robust surveillance system that continues to ensure the disease does not re-emerge. However, such vigilance is essential to prevent potential outbreaks, particularly from imported cases or vaccine-derived strains, which can still pose a threat in areas where vaccination coverage may decline.
