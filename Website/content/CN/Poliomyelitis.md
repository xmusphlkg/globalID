# Poliomyelitis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Poliomyelitis, commonly known as polio, is a highly infectious viral disease caused by the poliovirus. It primarily affects children under five years of age, leading to symptoms ranging from mild, flu-like illness to severe paralysis and, in some cases, death. The virus spreads through person-to-person contact, often via contaminated food and water. While no cure exists, polio can be prevented through immunization with the oral polio vaccine (OPV) or inactivated polio vaccine (IPV). Major global vaccination efforts led by organizations like the WHO have drastically reduced polio incidence, bringing the disease close to eradication.

The data reveals that from November 2014 to November 2024, the Chinese mainland has consistently reported zero cases and zero deaths due to poliomyelitis. This trend suggests that the nation has successfully maintained a state of polio-free status for over a decade, a significant achievement in light of the global challenges posed by the disease. The absence of any new cases or deaths throughout this period may indicate the effectiveness of ongoing vaccination programs and public health surveillance in the country. Moreover, China's rigorous efforts in immunization campaigns, combined with strict monitoring systems, likely played a central role in preventing outbreaks.

It is important to recognize that while no cases have been reported, this data does not rule out the possibility of occasional isolated outbreaks or environmental detection of poliovirus, especially given the challenges in achieving absolute eradication globally. However, the consistent zero case report over such a long duration aligns with global polio eradication goals and highlights China's success in maintaining high levels of immunity coverage. Continued vigilance, including monitoring for vaccine-derived poliovirus and ensuring that immunization rates remain high, will be essential for sustaining this polio-free status in the future.
