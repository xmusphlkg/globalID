# Poliomyelitis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Poliomyelitis, commonly known as polio, is a highly infectious viral disease primarily affecting children under five years old. It is caused by the poliovirus, which spreads via fecal-oral transmission or, less frequently, through contaminated food or water. The virus infects the nervous system, leading to a range of symptoms from mild flu-like conditions to severe, permanent paralysis. The introduction of effective vaccines in the 20th century significantly reduced the global incidence of polio. However, it remains endemic in a few regions, necessitating continued immunization efforts to achieve global eradication.

## Highlights

- From June 2015 to June 2025, Chinese mainland reported no cases or deaths from poliomyelitis, indicating effective control of the disease.  
<br/>

- Continuous zero incidence over a decade underscores the success of extensive vaccination programs and public health efforts in maintaining polio-free status.  
<br/>

- The sustained absence of cases highlights effective surveillance systems that have likely prevented poliovirus resurgence despite global challenges.  
<br/>

- As of June 2025, a key epidemiological trend is the maintenance of polio eradication, reflecting strong healthcare infrastructure and community engagement.  
<br/>

The data for poliomyelitis in the Chinese mainland from the middle of 2015 through June 2025 indicate consistent zero reported cases and deaths. This persistent absence of reported cases over a decade strongly suggests effective control measures have been established or sustained. One possible interpretation of this trend could be the successful continuation of the immunization efforts, particularly the national vaccination campaigns that provide widespread coverage for children. The Chinese government has historically put great emphasis on the elimination of vaccine-preventable diseases, and this data might reflect that commitment. The absence of cases aligns with global eradication efforts that have been successfully eliminating polio across many regions.

Another perspective could be the active surveillance systems in place working efficiently to detect and manage any potential outbreaks that might not surface in official records. Surveillance is a critical component that complements vaccination efforts, ensuring that even the slightest hint of polio virus circulation is promptly identified and dealt with. The data seem to support the notion that the Chinese mainland has achieved robust control over polio, standing as a testament to healthcare policies and practices targeted towards infectious disease control. Moreover, the sustainable zero cases reflect global cooperation and adherence to international health initiatives aimed at eradicating polio, thereby reducing the risk of importation from other areas where the virus might still be present.
