## Introduction

Poliomyelitis, commonly known as polio, is an infectious disease caused by the poliovirus. It primarily affects children under the age of five, leading to symptoms ranging from mild flu-like manifestations to severe neurological complications. In a minority of cases, the virus invades the nervous system, causing paralysis, muscle weakness, and potentially permanent disability. Polio spreads through contact with infected fecal matter or, less frequently, through oral secretions. The global effort to eradicate polio has significantly reduced its incidence, largely due to widespread vaccination campaigns, although it persists in a few regions with limited vaccine access.

## Highlights

1. **Poliomyelitis maintains a zero-case status:** Since July 2015, the Chinese mainland has consistently reported no cases of poliomyelitis, indicating effective public health measures. <br/>

2. **Successful eradication efforts:** Over the past decade, China has successfully eradicated poliomyelitis within its borders, reflecting its commitment to vaccination and disease surveillance protocols. <br/>

3. **Continued monitoring required:** Despite the absence of cases, the potential risk from international cases necessitates ongoing vigilance and robust immunization programs to prevent reintroduction. <br/>

4. **Public health leadership:** China exemplifies global health leadership in maintaining disease elimination, offering insights and strategies for other regions aiming for similar eradication success. <br/>
