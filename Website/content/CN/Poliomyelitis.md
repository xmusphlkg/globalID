# Poliomyelitis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Poliomyelitis, commonly known as polio, is a highly infectious viral disease caused by the poliovirus. It primarily affects children under five years of age, leading to paralysis and, in severe cases, death. The virus spreads through contaminated water and food or through direct contact with an infected person. While most infections are asymptomatic, a small percentage can result in severe neurological complications. Vaccination has been instrumental in reducing polio incidence worldwide, with the Global Polio Eradication Initiative aiming to eliminate the disease. Despite significant progress, polio remains endemic in some regions, necessitating ongoing public health efforts.

## Highlights

- **Zero Cases and Deaths:** From January 2015 to January 2025, there have been no reported cases or deaths due to poliomyelitis in the Chinese mainland. This indicates a successful control and prevention strategy. <br/>

- **Sustained Eradication:** The data reflects a sustained eradication of poliomyelitis over a decade, suggesting effective vaccination campaigns and public health measures. <br/>

- **Public Health Success:** The absence of cases highlights the effectiveness of China's public health infrastructure in maintaining polio-free status, aligning with global eradication efforts. <br/>

- **Continued Vigilance Required:** Despite the success, ongoing surveillance and vaccination efforts are crucial to prevent re-emergence, especially with global travel and potential importation risks. <br/>

This trend indicates a successful public health strategy in controlling and potentially eradicating the disease within the region. The sustained zero cases over a decade suggests effective vaccination campaigns, public awareness initiatives, and robust surveillance systems that have likely contributed to this outcome.

The Chinese mainland's experience could serve as a model for other regions still grappling with poliovirus transmission. Continued vigilance and investment in immunization programs will be essential to maintain this status and prevent any resurgence of the disease in the future.
