# Poliomyelitis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Poliomyelitis, commonly known as polio, is a highly infectious viral disease primarily affecting children under the age of five. It is caused by the poliovirus, which spreads through contact with feces or contaminated food and water. The virus can invade the nervous system, sometimes leading to paralysis or even death. Notably, many infections are asymptomatic, yet about 1% can result in permanent paralysis. Global efforts spearheaded by organizations like the WHO have reduced polio cases significantly through widespread vaccination campaigns. However, the disease remains a concern in areas with inadequate healthcare infrastructure and vaccination coverage.

## Highlights

- The data shows zero reported cases and deaths from poliomyelitis in the Chinese mainland from September 2015 to September 2025. <br/>
- This consistent lack of cases indicates successful containment and prevention efforts, such as effective vaccination campaigns. <br/>
- The sustained absence of poliomyelitis highlights China's status as a polio-free region, supporting global eradication goals. <br/>
- Continued surveillance is essential to ensure early detection of any potential cases and to maintain this disease-free status. <br/>

This consistently reflects the effectiveness of public health measures and vaccination efforts that have been tirelessly implemented. The lack of polio cases indicates high vaccination coverage, crucial for maintaining immunity within the population, and suggests strong healthcare infrastructure and surveillance systems. These efforts align with global initiatives aimed at polio eradication, emphasizing the importance of coordinated international health strategies.

The ongoing absence of cases highlights significant progress in disease control, likely due to extensive immunization campaigns and public awareness regarding the importance of vaccines. China's success might be attributed to widespread administration of oral and inactivated polio vaccines, which would effectively create herd immunity and prevent virus transmission. Furthermore, the zero incidence can serve as an example for other regions still combating polio, showcasing the strategies and actions needed to manage vaccine-preventable diseases.

The sustained eradication indicates a robust approach to health security, where continuous monitoring prevents the re-emergence of poliovirus. This achievement underlines the impact of strategic investments in public health infrastructure and the critical role of international collaboration to sustain such disease elimination over the long term.
