# Rabies

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral infection that targets the central nervous system of mammals, including humans, and is caused by the rabies virus. Transmission occurs through the saliva of infected animals via bites or scratches. Once clinical symptoms, such as fever, headache, anxiety, and paralysis, appear, the disease is nearly always fatal. However, rabies is preventable with prompt post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP), involving thorough wound cleaning and a series of vaccinations. Control measures include vaccinating domesticated animals and public awareness programs. Without timely intervention, rabies progresses to severe neurological symptoms, leading to coma and, ultimately, death.

## Highlights

- A significant decline in rabies cases and deaths was observed from 2015 to 2023, with cases dropping from a peak of 86 in August 2015 to 12 in August 2023. <br/>
- Starting in late 2023, both cases and deaths have shown a gradual uptrend, culminating in 32 cases and 27 deaths by August 2025. <br/>
- The re-emergence of higher case numbers since late 2023 suggests potential gaps in vaccination or increased contact with rabid animals. <br/>
- Despite increased recent counts, overall trends from 2015 indicate improved disease control efforts, though vigilance is necessary to address the recent rise. <br/>

The rabies data for mainland China from August 2015 to August 2025 reveals an initial decrease in cases and deaths, followed by stabilization and a slight rise in recent years. During 2015-2016, cases and deaths were high, peaking at 86 cases in August 2015 and 91 deaths in December 2015. From early 2016 onwards, a clear decline is observed, with cases eventually falling below 15 per month from 2022 to 2023, suggesting effective rabies control through increased animal vaccination, better access to post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP), and enhanced public education.

However, from 2024, there is a noticeable increase, with cases reaching 32 and deaths 27 by August 2025. This resurgence may result from challenges in maintaining vaccination programs, changing domestic animal populations, or variations in surveillance and reporting. It might also reflect differences in regional rabies control implementation or shifts in animal-human interactions due to societal or environmental changes. 

These recent trends highlight a need for sustained public health strategies to reinforce the progress made in rabies control. Maintaining vaccination coverage, ensuring accessible PEP, and continuous public awareness campaigns remain crucial. This approach can help manage potential increases and sustain long-term reduction in rabies cases and fatalities. Consistent vigilance and resource allocation are essential for effective disease prevention and control.
