# Rabies

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease caused by the rabies virus, primarily transmitted through the bite of an infected animal. It affects the central nervous system, leading to inflammation of the brain and, if untreated, is almost always fatal. Symptoms typically begin with fever and discomfort, progressing to anxiety, confusion, and agitation. As the disease advances, paralysis, hallucinations, and hydrophobia may occur. Rabies is preventable through vaccination of pets and post-exposure prophylaxis for humans after potential exposure. Globally, it remains a significant public health concern, particularly in regions with high populations of stray dogs.

## Highlights

- **Overall Decline:** From 2015 to 2025, rabies cases and deaths in the Chinese mainland have generally decreased, reflecting improved control measures. <br/>

- **Monthly Fluctuations:** Despite the overall decline, monthly data shows fluctuations, indicating possible seasonal or regional variations in rabies transmission. <br/>

- **2024 Increase:** The year 2024 experienced a slight rise in cases and deaths, suggesting a need to investigate potential causes like changes in vaccination rates or exposure. <br/>

- **Current Status (January 2025):** As of January 2025, cases and deaths remain low compared to peak years, highlighting the effectiveness of ongoing rabies prevention and control efforts. <br/>

The data on rabies cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, in 2015, there was a relatively high number of cases and deaths, with monthly cases often exceeding 50 and deaths frequently surpassing the number of cases, indicating a high fatality rate. This trend continued into early 2016, but by mid-2016, both cases and deaths began to decline. This decrease continued steadily through 2017 and 2018, with a notable reduction in both metrics, suggesting improvements in rabies control measures or public health interventions.

From 2019 onwards, the data shows a further decline in both cases and deaths, reaching some of the lowest numbers in 2020 and 2021. This period might reflect the impact of enhanced vaccination programs, public awareness campaigns, or other preventive measures. However, starting in 2022, there is a slight uptick in cases and deaths, which continues into 2024. This resurgence could be attributed to various factors, such as changes in public health policy, vaccine availability, or environmental factors affecting rabies transmission. The data for 2025 shows a slight increase in both cases and deaths compared to the previous years, indicating a need for renewed focus on rabies prevention and control strategies.
