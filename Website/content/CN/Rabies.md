# Rabies

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a deadly viral zoonotic disease that affects the central nervous system of mammals, including humans. Caused by the rabies virus of the Lyssavirus genus, it is primarily transmitted through the bite or scratch of an infected animal, most commonly dogs. Once clinical symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal. Initial signs include fever and tingling at the exposure site, progressing to neurological symptoms such as agitation, hydrophobia, and paralysis. Rabies is preventable through prompt post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) and vaccination. It remains a public health concern, especially in regions with poor animal vaccination coverage.

## Highlights

- Rabies cases in mainland China have shown a sharp long-term decline from monthly peaks of over 80 cases in 2015 to low double digits since 2021, reflecting progress in disease control and prevention. <br/>
- From 2022 to April 2025, reported monthly cases remained generally under 20, with occasional minor rebounds, indicating low but persistent transmission. <br/>
- Deaths closely track cases, reinforcing the disease’s near-100% fatality rate once symptoms appear, and highlighting the continued importance of timely post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP). <br/>
- As of April 2025, incidence remains low but not eliminated; sustained surveillance, animal vaccination, and public education remain key to further reducing risk. <br/>

Rabies cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 show a clear long-term declining trend, particularly from 2015 to 2020. In 2015, monthly cases ranged from 43 to 86, and deaths were consistently high. This pattern continued through 2016, though with some fluctuations. From 2017 onward, both metrics began to decline more steadily, with deaths falling below 50 per month and cases often under 40, indicating improved prevention and healthcare measures.

Between 2018 and 2022, reported cases mostly remained below 50 and often under 20 from 2020 onward. This period may reflect intensified rabies control efforts, better post-exposure treatment access, and behavioral changes possibly influenced by concurrent public health events like the COVID-19 pandemic. From 2023 through early 2025, monthly cases and deaths generally remained low, though there was a slight uptick during mid-to-late 2024. However, levels stayed much lower than those seen in 2015–2016.

Overall, the data indicate substantial progress in reducing rabies burden over the decade. Periodic fluctuations—particularly small resurgences in 2024—highlight the need for sustained vigilance, especially in rural areas where exposure risk is traditionally higher. Despite isolated increases, the consistent downward trend suggests effective long-term control strategies have been implemented.

The highest annual fatalities appear in this year with 44 deaths in April to 91 in December, suggesting the urgency of rabies as a public health concern that prompted preventive interventions like vaccination campaigns and awareness programs.

Over the subsequent years, monthly fatalities saw a gradual decline, especially notable since 2020, aligning with the global increase in rabies control programs and improved access to post-exposure prophylaxis. By 2023, reported deaths had notably decreased, maintaining a pattern of fewer fatalities even as overall cases fluctuated slightly. While a few peaks remain sporadically across years, the data indicate effective public health measures and improved healthcare infrastructure are gradually suppressing the disease's fatality rate. Nonetheless, maintaining vigilance through continuous public health strategies is vital as occasional spikes, such as notable increased deaths in December months, suggest potential seasonal variations or reporting lags requiring further investigation.
