# Rabies

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral zoonotic disease caused by the rabies virus, typically transmitted to humans through the bite or scratch of an infected animal. The virus affects the central nervous system, leading to progressive and fatal encephalitis if untreated. Common reservoirs include dogs, bats, raccoons, and foxes. Early symptoms include fever, headache, and malaise, progressing to neurological signs such as agitation, confusion, hydrophobia, and paralysis. Rabies is preventable through post-exposure prophylaxis, including wound care and vaccination, but is almost always fatal once clinical symptoms appear.

## Highlights

- Rabies incidence in Chinese mainland has substantially decreased since 2015, with reported monthly cases and deaths dropping by over two-thirds between 2015 and 2025.<br/>
- Since 2022, annual case numbers have stabilized at a low level; however, a modest increase was observed from mid-2024 through 2025, suggesting a possible slowing of progress.<br/>
- Mortality remains high relative to incidence, underscoring persistent gaps in timely post-exposure prophylaxis and public awareness.<br/>
- As of December 2025, rabies is not eliminated and continues to pose a public health threat, requiring sustained control and prevention efforts, especially in rural regions.<br/>

Rabies data from the Chinese mainland between late 2015 and 2025 indicate a pronounced decline in both reported cases and deaths from 2015 through early 2023. Initial case peaks of over 80 per month in 2015 fell to consistently less than 15 per month by 2021, with deaths showing a similar pattern and a persistently high case-fatality ratio, consistent with rabies’ near-universal lethality once symptoms appear. The sharp drop from 2018 onward suggests the effectiveness of sustained public health measures—such as widespread vaccination, better post-exposure prophylaxis, and improved awareness.

However, the period from mid-2023 through 2025 shows cases and deaths rebounding, with monthly totals often returning to the 20–30 range and several months nearly matching peak values observed a decade prior. This reversal may be attributed to setbacks in control strategies, possible changes in local dog populations or human exposure patterns, or improved detection and reporting practices. Throughout, minor seasonal fluctuations are observed but are less prominent than the overarching annual trends. The data emphasize both significant progress in rabies reduction and emerging vulnerabilities, with the persistently high mortality underscoring critical gaps in rapid treatment and prophylaxis accessibility. Renewed efforts are required to sustain reductions and prevent a full-scale recurrence of rabies transmission.

Rabies mortality data from the Chinese mainland show a persistently high case-fatality rate from 2015 to 2025, with monthly deaths frequently matching or exceeding reported cases—demonstrating the disease’s almost invariable fatal outcome once symptoms develop. The overall trend indicates a notable decline in both cases and deaths from 2015 through late 2022, with some months dropping to single-digit figures by 2022 and 2023. This downward movement suggests ongoing public health interventions, better awareness, and improved access to post-exposure prophylaxis over time.

Despite these gains, the alignment of deaths with cases remains striking, reflecting that symptomatic rabies continues to be overwhelmingly fatal, with lapses in timely intervention or healthcare access likely contributing. An uptick in both numbers from late 2024 through 2025 signals possible setbacks or reporting anomalies, but the core issue persists: the fatality probability remains extremely high. Seasonality appears in certain years, with winter months occasionally showing increased deaths, which may relate to animal bite patterns or delays in seeking medical care. Overall, the data highlight the effectiveness of intervention but also expose the ongoing challenges in achieving zero rabies deaths, underscoring the need for sustained prevention, public education, and rapid response to exposures to drive the case numbers—and deaths—down further.
