# Rabies

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a deadly viral disease that affects the central nervous system, typically leading to fatal encephalitis in mammals, including humans. It is primarily transmitted through the saliva of infected animals, with bites from rabid dogs being the most common mode of transmission to humans. Symptoms, often appearing after a latent period, include fever, anxiety, hydrophobia, paralysis, and confusion, progressing to coma and death if untreated. While preventable through vaccination, rabies remains a significant public health issue, particularly in regions with inadequate animal control and vaccination programs. Immediate medical attention post-exposure can be life-saving.

## Highlights

- Between 2015 and mid-2025, rabies cases in the Chinese mainland have generally decreased, peaking in mid-2015 and showing a notable reduction thereafter. <br/>
- Recent data from 2025 indicates a resurgence in cases and deaths, with a steady increase from February to June, suggesting an emerging uptick in transmission. <br/>
- The fluctuation in monthly cases and deaths from 2015 to 2023 stabilizes after a marked decline in early 2020, indicating potential success in control measures before the recent rise. <br/>
- Continued vigilance and enhanced prevention strategies are necessary to address the recent increase in rabies cases and mortality in 2025. <br/>

The rabies data from the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 highlights a significant decline in both cases and deaths over this period. In 2015, the monthly cases and deaths were high, peaking in December with 81 cases and 91 deaths. This reflects the challenges faced in rabies control during that time, likely due to under-vaccination and difficulties in managing the disease in both domestic and wild animals. 

Moving into 2016, there was a noticeable drop in cases and deaths, stabilizing to lower numbers by 2018. This decline suggests improved efforts in preventive measures such as vaccination campaigns and public awareness initiatives. The trend of decreasing cases continued into 2019 and further into 2020, likely aided by the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have inadvertently reduced rabies transmission due to lockdowns and increased public health measures.

From 2021 to 2022, cases and deaths dropped to occasionally single-digit numbers, indicating effective rabies management strategies. However, from mid-2023, a slight increase in cases is observed, although they remain significantly lower than the early years of the dataset. By 2025, while there is a slight rise, the numbers do not revert to the high pre-2016 levels, showcasing the lasting impact of improved rabies control measures over the decade.

Analyzing the rabies mortality data from the Chinese mainland between June 2015 and June 2025 uncovers notable trends. Initially, from mid-2015 to early 2016, there are high mortality rates, peaking at 91 deaths in December 2015, with case numbers falling only gradually into 2016. This high death toll suggests potential inefficiencies in rabies management or healthcare response during this period. Gradual decreases in cases and mortality through mid-2016 may indicate improved healthcare protocols, greater public awareness, and increased access to post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

From mid-2017 onward, both cases and deaths see a notable decline, reaching their lowest in February 2022, with 4 deaths reported. This trend suggests enhanced rabies control measures, like dog vaccination programs and broader public health efforts. However, minor fluctuations do occur post-2020 with occasional spikes such as in late 2022. These irregularities could stem from regional disparities in healthcare service delivery or temporary disruptions in rabies vaccine supplies. Interestingly, from 2024, there is a slight increase in both deaths and cases, highlighting ongoing challenges that require robust responses to once again effectively contain fatalities.

Overall, the data reveal improvements in reducing rabies mortality through various interventions, though sporadic increases in mortality indicate periods where reinforcing rabies control measures and public health policies is essential.
