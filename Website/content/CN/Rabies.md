# Rabies

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral zoonotic disease that affects mammals, including humans, primarily through the bite or scratch of an infected animal, commonly dogs. It is caused by the Rabies virus, belonging to the Lyssavirus genus. Once symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal, making prevention through vaccination crucial. The virus targets the central nervous system, leading to brain inflammation and eventual death. Initial symptoms resemble the flu, progressing to neurological manifestations like agitation, hallucinations, and hydrophobia. Rabies poses a significant public health challenge, especially in regions with inadequate veterinary and healthcare services, emphasizing the importance of vaccination and animal control.

## Highlights

- Rabies cases in China have declined significantly from monthly averages above 50 in 2015–2016 to fewer than 20 cases per month by 2023–2025, indicating long-term control success. <br/>
- 2024–2025 saw slight increases in monthly cases, especially from mid-2024, suggesting a need for continued surveillance and local-level response. <br/>
- Despite low case counts in recent years, mortality rates remain high, with case-fatality ratios often near or at 100%, underscoring gaps in timely access to post-exposure prophylaxis. <br/>
- Seasonal peaks are less distinct now compared to earlier years, reflecting improved prevention, though vigilance is essential during warmer months when exposure risk may rise. <br/>

The data from May 2015 to May 2025 indicates a clear downward trend in rabies cases and deaths in mainland China over the ten-year period. In 2015, monthly case numbers often exceeded 60, with peaks such as 86 in August 2015, and deaths closely followed these peaks. However, starting in 2016, cases began a gradual decline, further accelerating after 2018. By 2020, many months recorded cases below 20 and deaths in a similar or even lower range, with some fluctuation that might reflect isolated outbreaks or delays in case reporting.

From 2021 onward, the pattern remains one of consistently low incidence, with most months under 20 cases and an apparent decoupling between cases and deaths at times—suggesting improved post-exposure prophylaxis and better access to healthcare. The resurgence of slightly higher monthly numbers in 2024–2025 (though still modest compared to 2015) may hint at localized changes in surveillance, animal control policies, or underreporting corrections. Still, the consistent containment over time suggests sustained public health efforts and broader rabies control in animal populations.

From mid-2015 to mid-2025, reported rabies deaths in mainland China have shown a marked decline, dropping from monthly death tolls frequently exceeding 50 between 2015 and 2017 to predominantly lower double-digit figures from 2020 onwards. This trend suggests sustained improvements in rabies prevention, control strategies (e.g., dog vaccination and post-exposure prophylaxis), and public awareness interventions. The period from late 2015 to 2018 saw high fatality rates with several months recording more deaths than reported cases—potentially reflecting delayed diagnosis, underreporting of infections, or a backlog of previous exposures that culminated in death. A reproducible seasonal pattern is not strongly evident, but some clustering of higher deaths in the second half of each year is observed, which could relate to agricultural cycles and rural exposure risks.

From 2019 onwards, both incidence and deaths continued to fall, with monthly deaths dropping below 20 in most cases post-2021. However, sporadic spikes still occur, including in December 2020, 2021, and 2022, which may hint at reporting artifacts or lags during year-end. Notably, even in recent years with low case counts, the case-fatality rate remains very high, indicating that almost all confirmed cases still lead to death—emphasizing persistent gaps in timely access to effective post-exposure treatment or failure to implement it. The public health implication remains the critical need for sustained prevention, especially dog vaccination and prompt post-exposure prophylaxis.
