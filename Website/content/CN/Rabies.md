# Rabies

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease that affects mammals, including humans, typically transmitted through the saliva of infected animals via bites or scratches. The rabies virus belongs to the Lyssavirus genus and targets the central nervous system, leading to fatal encephalitis if untreated. Early symptoms resemble the flu, such as fever and headache, but progress to neurological manifestations like confusion, paralysis, and hydrophobia. Rabies is preventable through prompt post-exposure prophylaxis, including wound cleansing and vaccination. The disease remains a significant public health issue, especially in regions where domestic dog transmission is prevalent.

## Highlights

1. Rabies cases and deaths in Chinese mainland have shown a fluctuating trend over recent years, with a significant decline observed from 2015 to 2023, followed by a slight increase from mid-2024 onwards. <br/>

2. The reduction in case numbers post-2019 suggests a successful implementation of rabies control programs and increased vaccination efforts, contributing to lower incidence rates. <br/>

3. The increase in cases and deaths throughout 2025 indicates potential challenges in maintaining control measures, highlighting the need for continuous vigilance and adaptation of strategies. <br/>

4. Despite improvements, ongoing education, vaccination, and monitoring initiatives are crucial to sustain progress and address emerging concerns related to rabies transmission. <br/>

The rabies data from 2015 to 2025 in mainland China reveals notable fluctuations in cases and deaths, with peaks in late 2015. A decline in both metrics begins in early 2016, indicating effective management and response protocols. The pattern shows that higher cases often precede increases in deaths, demonstrating the direct impact on fatality rates once cases rise.

From 2018 to 2020, there was a marked reduction in rabies cases and fatalities, hinting at successful public health interventions and improved animal control measures. Although there were slight oscillations from 2021 onwards, the overall trend suggested a resurgence, yet not exceeding previous highs. Towards 2025, the data hints at potential challenges maintaining the low numbers achieved, emphasizing the importance of continued health strategies to effectively control rabies.

Monthly fluctuations suggest possible seasonal factors influencing rabies transmission, which warrants further investigation into external influences like climate or wildlife interactions. The data indicates that once rabies cases occur, the outcomes are often critical, highlighting the necessity of prevention as the best defense against this deadly disease. While mid-decade successes in reducing rabies are evident, the fluctuations caution against complacency, underscoring the need for continuous adaptation and vigilance in health strategies to sustain these achievements over the long term.
