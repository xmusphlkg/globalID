# Rabies

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease that affects the central nervous system of mammals, including humans. It is primarily transmitted through the bite of an infected animal, most commonly dogs, bats, raccoons, and skunks. The virus travels from the bite site to the brain, causing inflammation and severe neurological symptoms such as fever, headache, agitation, and confusion. Once clinical symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal. However, it is preventable through prompt administration of post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP), which includes wound cleaning, rabies immunoglobulin, and a series of rabies vaccinations.

## Highlights

1. **Steady Decline in Cases and Deaths**: From mid-2014 to 2024, there has been a noticeable decline in both cases and deaths, indicating progress in controlling the disease.<br/>
2. **Seasonal Fluctuations**: The data shows periodic increases, particularly during the summer months, suggesting a seasonal pattern in rabies transmission.<br/>
3. **Recent Low Case Numbers**: In 2023 and early 2024, the number of cases has consistently been low, with most months reporting fewer than 15 cases.<br/>
4. **Improved Case Fatality Ratio**: The decline in the number of deaths alongside cases suggests improvements in rabies management and treatment practices.<br/>

The data on rabies cases and deaths in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals a significant downward trend in both cases and fatalities. In the early period of 2014-2016, the monthly cases and deaths were relatively high, with some months reporting over 100 cases and deaths, such as September 2014 (105 cases and 97 deaths). However, this trend showed a marked decline over the years, particularly from 2017 onwards. By the end of 2022, the monthly cases and deaths had reduced substantially, with figures often below 20, indicating an overall improvement in rabies control and prevention measures.

Notably, the period from 2020 to 2024 witnessed a further reduction in cases and deaths, with the numbers often dropping into single digits. This decline could suggest enhanced public health interventions, including better vaccination coverage, increased public awareness, and improved animal control measures. Despite these improvements, occasional spikes, such as the 29 deaths reported in December 2020, indicate that rabies remains a public health concern, necessitating sustained efforts in surveillance, vaccination, and public education to maintain and further the gains achieved in reducing the incidence and mortality of rabies.
