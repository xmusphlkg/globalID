# Rabies

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease caused by the rabies virus, typically transmitted through the bite or scratch of an infected animal. It primarily affects mammals, including humans, leading to inflammation of the brain. Symptoms include fever, headache, anxiety, confusion, and eventually paralysis, leading to death if untreated. Rabies is preventable through vaccination, both for animals and humans exposed to the virus. Once clinical symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal. It is most commonly found in wild animals such as bats, raccoons, and foxes, but domestic animals like dogs can also be carriers.

## Highlights

- **Declining cases and deaths**: Rabies cases and deaths have significantly decreased over time. In November 2014, there were 85 cases and 79 deaths, while November 2024 saw 18 cases and 18 deaths. <br/>
- **Seasonal trends**: Rabies cases are more frequent during warmer months, especially from June to August. <br/>
- **Ongoing risk in rural areas**: Rabies remains a concern, particularly in rural regions with frequent dog bites. <br/>
- **Effective control measures**: The decline in cases suggests successful vaccination campaigns for pets and improvements in post-exposure prophylaxis for humans. <br/>

The rabies data from China between 2014 and 2024 shows a gradual decline in both cases and deaths over time, with the peak of reported cases occurring in 2015 and early 2016. During these years, monthly cases often exceeded 70, and deaths reached as high as 95 in January 2015. However, from 2018 onwards, both case numbers and mortality steadily decreased, likely due to improved public health measures such as vaccination campaigns, increased awareness, and better access to post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP). 

Despite the overall decline, some fluctuations persist, with notable increases in 2018 and 2024, particularly in the warmer months. For instance, in August 2024, there were 18 reported cases and 19 deaths, reflecting the seasonal risk, especially in rural areas where human-dog interactions are more frequent. Although the number of rabies cases has diminished, the fatal nature of the disease, with death rates often around 50%, highlights the continuing risk if timely treatment is not administered. Ongoing vigilance and targeted control efforts remain crucial to reducing rabies-related mortality further.
