# Rabies

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral zoonotic disease primarily transmitted through the bites of infected animals, most commonly dogs. It affects the central nervous system, leading to acute encephalitis and, if untreated, is almost invariably fatal once symptoms appear. Initial signs include fever and tingling at the bite site, progressing to neurological manifestations such as agitation, hydrophobia, and paralysis. Though preventable through timely post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) and vaccination of animals, rabies remains a significant public health concern in many regions, particularly in Asia and Africa, where canine rabies is widespread.

## Highlights

- Rabies cases and deaths have significantly declined from 2015 to 2024, with 2015 marking a peak and gradual decrease visible in subsequent years. <br/>
- Recent years show a stable low incidence, with 13 cases and 10 deaths reported in May 2024, compared to higher numbers a decade earlier.<br/>
- There was a notable decrease in reported cases and deaths from 2019 onwards, aligning with strengthened public health measures.<br/>
- Seasonal trends indicate a higher number of cases in warmer months, but the overall level remains controlled.<br/>

An analysis of the rabies cases in Mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals significant fluctuations in the number of reported rabies cases and deaths over the years. Initially, from mid-2014 through the end of 2015, the reported cases generally ranged from high 60s to mid-100s, with a corresponding high number of deaths, displaying a grim picture of rabies-associated mortality. There was a noticeable sharp decline in both cases and deaths through 2016 and 2017, suggesting an intervention or improvement in rabies control mechanisms during that period. 

The trend persisted into 2018, with further decreases in both cases and fatalities, setting a relatively lower baseline compared to previous years. By 2020, the reported cases fell dramatically, with a minor, stable rise in later months. This downward trend continued into 2021 and 2022, interspersed with occasional spikes. Interestingly, 2023 marked a slight uptick but remained below the high levels observed in the earlier years. The first half of 2024 shows a commendable dip, possibly an indication of ongoing successful control measures or public health interventions. The detailed monthly data underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and robust strategies to sustain and improve rabies control efforts in the region.
