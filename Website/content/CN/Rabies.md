# Rabies

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease that causes acute inflammation of the brain in humans and other mammals. The virus is primarily transmitted through the bite of an infected animal, most often dogs. Once symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal, but it is preventable through prompt post-exposure prophylaxis. Symptoms may include fever, headache, excess salivation, muscle spasms, paralysis, and mental confusion. Vaccination of domestic animals and wildlife control are crucial strategies for prevention. Rabies occurs in over 150 countries and territories, with more than 95% of human deaths occurring in Asia and Africa.

## Highlights

- Rabies cases in mainland China have seen a general decline from 2014 to 2024, from over 90 cases in 2014 to fewer than 20 in 2024, signaling improved control efforts. <br/>
- Deaths have also decreased but remain significant, with occasional spikes such as in August 2024, where 19 deaths occurred despite relatively few cases. <br/>
- Seasonal variations are observed, with higher cases and deaths typically occurring in the summer and early autumn months each year. <br/>
- Despite progress, recent data from 2024 shows a slight resurgence in both cases and deaths, indicating the need for ongoing public health vigilance. <br/>

The rabies cases and deaths in mainland China from August 2014 to August 2024 exhibit a significant downward trend over time. In 2014 and 2015, monthly cases generally ranged between 50 and 105, while deaths closely followed, peaking at 95 deaths in January 2015. However, after 2016, both cases and deaths started declining steadily. By 2020 and beyond, the monthly cases mostly stayed under 30, and the number of deaths similarly decreased, reflecting the success of potential interventions like vaccination campaigns and public health initiatives targeting rabies control. For instance, the period from 2022 to 2024 shows sustained low numbers, with a few months reporting fewer than 10 cases or deaths.

Interestingly, certain periods display an unusual spike in deaths compared to cases. For instance, in January 2015, deaths outnumbered reported cases (95 deaths versus 78 cases), suggesting possible delays in reporting, under-reporting of cases, or other anomalies in data collection. Additionally, the latest data from July and August 2024 shows a small rise in both cases and deaths (18 cases, 19 deaths in August 2024), which may require further investigation to determine whether this is a temporary fluctuation or signals a need for renewed intervention efforts.
