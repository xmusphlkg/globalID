# Rabies

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease that affects the central nervous system, leading to brain inflammation. It is primarily transmitted through the bite of an infected animal, commonly dogs. Once symptoms appear, rabies is nearly always fatal, making early intervention crucial. The disease presents initially with flu-like symptoms, progressing to neurological manifestations such as anxiety, confusion, and hallucinations. Vaccinations for both humans and animals are essential preventive measures. Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is effective if administered promptly following exposure. Rabies remains a significant public health concern, particularly in regions with limited access to healthcare and vaccination.

## Highlights

- Rabies cases in mainland China have shown a steady decline from 105 cases in September 2014 to 17 in September 2024, reflecting improved control efforts.<br/>
- Deaths also followed a decreasing trend, dropping from 97 in September 2014 to 12 in September 2024.<br/>
- Notable drops occurred in the years 2019–2021, with cases consistently below 20 and deaths decreasing significantly, suggesting effective public health interventions during this period.<br/>
- Despite the overall decline, a slight rise in both cases and deaths was observed from mid-2023 to September 2024, indicating potential areas for renewed focus on rabies prevention.<br/>

The reported data for rabies in mainland China from September 2014 to September 2024 shows a general decline in both cases and deaths over the years. In 2014, the monthly rabies cases ranged between 85 and 105, with a high death toll reflecting nearly 80-95% of cases resulting in death. This high mortality rate is consistent with rabies being a fatal disease once symptoms appear. However, starting in 2015, there is a gradual downward trend in both cases and deaths, with notable dips in early 2016, where cases dropped to as low as 29 (April 2016), and deaths followed a similar downward trajectory.

The decline continues into 2020-2024, with some fluctuation, especially during 2020-2022, where cases briefly increase during certain months (e.g., December 2020, January 2021). However, the overall trend remains downward, with a significant reduction by 2022–2024, where monthly cases and deaths rarely exceed 20. The decrease in cases could be attributed to better rabies control measures, vaccination efforts, and public health interventions. However, the fatality rate remains high, indicating that while there may be fewer cases, the disease's deadliness persists, and timely interventions in post-exposure treatment are critical.
