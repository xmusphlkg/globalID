# Rabies

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease caused by the Rabies virus, a member of the Lyssavirus genus. It primarily affects mammals, including humans, and is transmitted through the saliva of infected animals, typically via bites. Rabies attacks the central nervous system, leading to inflammation of the brain, and is almost always fatal once symptoms appear. Early symptoms include fever, headache, and general weakness, progressing to neurological symptoms such as confusion, paralysis, and hydrophobia. Rabies is preventable through timely administration of post-exposure prophylaxis and vaccination of domestic animals. Effective control requires coordinated efforts in animal vaccination and public awareness.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline Over Time:** From 2015 to early 2022, cases and deaths showed a substantial decrease, indicating improved control measures.<br/>
- **Fluctuations in 2020-2023:** Some months in 2020-2023 displayed spikes in cases and deaths, reflecting potential challenges in maintaining consistent control efforts, possibly due to the global COVID-19 pandemic.<br/>
- **Slight Increase in Late 2024 to 2025:** A rising trend is seen, particularly from July 2024 onwards, with cases and deaths steadily increasing, reaching a peak in August 2025.<br/>
- **Current Concern:** As of October 2025, the resurgence in cases and deaths indicates a need for renewed focus on rabies prevention and control measures.<br/>

The data on rabies in the Chinese mainland presents a fluctuating trend of reported cases and deaths from 2015 to 2025. In the initial years, 2015 and 2016, there is a noticeable high in both cases and deaths, with numbers often exceeding 50 per month. The incidence peaks in late 2016, where the figures occasionally veer into the 70s. A noteworthy aspect during these early years is the general correlation between the reported cases and subsequent deaths, often with deaths approaching or occasionally surpassing the number of cases, which may highlight reporting lags or complexities in case monitoring and verification.

Beginning in 2017, a gradual decline in the number of reported cases and deaths becomes evident, continuing through to 2020. During this period, a reduction in numbers could potentially be attributed to improved rabies prevention strategies such as increased vaccination efforts and public awareness campaigns. Post-2020, the data suggest a stabilization and a relatively consistent plateau at lower levels compared to earlier years, with typical monthly cases hovering under 20, a trend persisting into 2023. However, 2024 and onwards see slight increases in cases once more, though not reaching previous highs. This upturn might suggest a complex interplay of factors such as possible relaxation of preventive measures, shifts in surveillance efforts, or changes in factors influencing rabies transmission dynamics.

The rabies data from 2015 to 2025 in mainland China highlights several important trends. In 2015 and 2016, rabies cases and deaths were notably high, with some instances where deaths exceeded cases, particularly late in 2015. This could suggest issues in reporting or classification, possibly indicating the need for improved post-exposure treatment protocols. By 2017 to 2019, there was a decline in cases and deaths, likely due to effective public health measures such as vaccination campaigns and better access to post-exposure prophylaxis.

From 2020 to 2022, the trend towards declining rabies-related mortality continued, although there were sporadic surges. These might reflect localized outbreaks or lapses in vaccination coverage, especially in rural areas with less access to healthcare resources. In 2023 and 2024, the data indicate stabilization with periodic fluctuations in cases and deaths. This suggests isolated issues that might require renewed attention to rabies control efforts, particularly in areas with fluctuating healthcare access and awareness levels.

Towards mid-2024 and into 2025, there is an observed uptick, pointing to a potential need for refreshed control measures. Continuing public health interventions, including consistent vaccination efforts and public education, remain crucial to maintaining control over rabies and reducing mortality rates effectively. The data underscores the importance of sustained public health strategies and immediate medical response in managing rabies cases.
