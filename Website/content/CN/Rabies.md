# Rabies

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Rabies is a viral disease caused by the Lyssavirus, primarily transmitted through the saliva of infected animals via bites or scratches. It affects the central nervous system, leading to inflammation of the brain and is nearly always fatal once symptoms appear. Initial symptoms include fever, headache, and general weakness, progressing to neurological manifestations such as agitation, hallucinations, and hydrophobia. Rabies is preventable through vaccination, both pre-exposure for at-risk individuals and post-exposure prophylaxis for those potentially exposed. It remains prevalent in regions with large populations of stray dogs, highlighting the importance of animal vaccination and public health education.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline**: Rabies cases in Chinese mainland have trended consistently downward from a high of 86 cases in August 2015 to much lower figures through 2024. <br/>
- **Gradual Increase in 2025**: Starting in February 2025, there is a noticeable uptick in cases, reaching 27 by July 2025. <br/>
- **Mortality Rate Reduction**: Deaths have also decreased significantly, with fewer deaths observed from 2023 to 2024, although a slight rise is seen in 2025. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variability**: Rabies exhibits monthly and seasonal fluctuations, with notable spikes during late summer and early autumn months. <br/>

An analysis of rabies cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to mid-2025 displays a fluctuating but generally declining trend. In mid-2015, there was a spike with cases peaking at 81 in December and deaths at 91. However, a decline followed in 2016-2017, signifying possible improvements in rabies management. For instance, March 2016 reported 32 cases and 33 deaths, a marked decrease from earlier months.

The downward trend continued from 2018 onward, with overall lower incidences. By 2019, reported cases consistently trended downward, and from 2020 there was a notable drop; February and March 2020 reported just 12 and 15 cases respectively. Into 2021 and 2022, cases remained lower, suggesting that control measures were effective. However, a slight increase appeared from late 2023 into 2024, although the numbers were still lower compared to earlier years. For example, cases in July 2024 rose to 16, and by the end of 2025, cases were at 27 in July.

Overall, despite early peaks, the long-term efforts in rabies control appear to have significantly reduced incidence in the region. This data underscores the importance of sustained rabies prevention strategies to manage fluctuations and keep cases at manageable levels.

Rabies deaths in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveal fluctuating patterns with a general decline over this period. Initially, in 2015, monthly deaths often exceeded case numbers, highlighting challenges in treatment or reporting accuracy. Towards year's end, mortality peaked as cases surged, likely reflecting poor access to prophylaxis or insufficient awareness regarding rabies prevention. As years progressed, especially from 2016 to 2018, both cases and deaths saw a notable decline, indicating improved public health measures, vaccination rates, and disease management protocols.

From 2019 onwards, deaths stabilized while cases fluctuated slightly, suggesting positive outcomes from veterinary control, awareness campaigns, or effective post-exposure treatments. Continuous improvements likely contributed to reducing mortality despite some increases in cases. However, late 2023 to 2025 data show a resurgence in cases, aligning with an increase in deaths, particularly notable from 2024. Such trends may be due to lapses in public health strategies or changes in surveillance intensity. Despite resurgence, annual deaths remained more controlled compared to early years, indicating sustained management efforts.

Overall, this analysis illustrates the journey from managing high rabies mortality rates to stabilizing and slightly reducing them. Continuous vigilance, community engagement, and adapting strategies to new challenges are essential to further reduce rabies deaths and improve health outcomes on the mainland.
