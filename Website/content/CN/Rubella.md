# Rubella

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection characterized by mild, flu-like symptoms and a distinctive red rash. Caused by the rubella virus, it primarily spreads through respiratory droplets in the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes. While usually mild in children and adults, rubella poses significant risks to pregnant women, potentially leading to congenital rubella syndrome in the developing fetus, which can result in miscarriage, stillbirth, or severe birth defects. Vaccination is the primary prevention method, significantly reducing incidence rates worldwide.

## Highlights

- Rubella incidence in mainland China has dramatically declined since the 2019 peak (9,095 cases in May), reaching just 63 cases in March 2025. <br/>
- Since 2020, monthly case numbers have remained consistently low with no months exceeding 200 cases, indicating sustained control. <br/>
- No rubella-related deaths have been reported since March 2022, suggesting improved case management and/or lower disease severity. <br/>
- Seasonal trends (peaks in spring) observed before 2020 have diminished, and reported counts have stabilized, reflecting possible impacts of vaccination and surveillance. <br/>

Analyzing the reported Rubella cases in mainland China from 2015 to March 2025 reveals distinct trends. Initially, in 2015, there was a noticeable peak during the year's early months, with April recording 2113 cases, followed by a significant decline in subsequent months. This pattern, with higher cases in the spring and summer and a decline later, persists throughout the analyzed period, aligning with typical Rubella seasonality.

A remarkable spike occurred in 2019, with cases reaching a high of 9095 in May. This surge contrasts sharply with previous and later years, suggesting an outbreak or anomaly warranting further investigation. While deaths were rare, one occurred in June 2019 and another in March 2022. Post-2019, a marked reduction in cases is observed, with numbers remaining low from 2020 onwards. This decline could be due to enhanced immunization, public health measures linked to the COVID-19 pandemic, or improved surveillance and reporting systems.

Overall, the data underscores a significant control over Rubella incidences in recent years, likely attributable to sustained vaccination efforts. The substantial reduction in cases from 2020 to 2025 indicates effective management and control of the disease, ensuring lower incidence rates and mitigating potential outbreaks. This analysis highlights the importance of continued vigilance and immunization to maintain these gains.

From 2015 to early 2025, reported rubella deaths in mainland China remain exceedingly rare, with only three fatalities recorded over the span of 10 years, despite over 60,000 reported cases. The first death occurred in March 2015 during a peak of 1,232 cases, the second in June 2019 aligned with a surge to 6,029 cases, and the third in March 2022 with just 98 recorded cases. Notably, these deaths did not consistently correspond to the highest incidence periods, such as in May 2019 with 9,095 cases but zero deaths. This indicates a low case-fatality ratio and suggests either that most infections remained mild or were effectively managed clinically.

The absence of consistent mortality despite variable case counts, especially post-2019, could reflect improved vaccination coverage, early detection practices, and heightened surveillance systems following the 2019 rubella resurgence. Moreover, the steady decline in case numbers after 2020, with consistently low monthly figures and no reported deaths, supports the impact of sustained public health interventions. The rarity of rubella-related mortality over an extended surveillance period highlights both the success of immunization strategies and the relatively low virulence of circulating rubella strains in the population during this timeframe.
