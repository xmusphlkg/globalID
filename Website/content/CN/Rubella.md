# Rubella

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection caused by the rubella virus. It typically presents with mild symptoms such as a low-grade fever, sore throat, and a distinctive red rash that starts on the face and spreads to the rest of the body. The virus primarily affects children and young adults. While generally mild, rubella is particularly dangerous for pregnant women as it can lead to serious congenital defects, including deafness, heart defects, and intellectual disabilities in the unborn child. Vaccination is the most effective method of prevention.

## Highlights

- Significant decrease in rubella cases: From a peak of 9,095 cases in May 2019 to only 82 cases in May 2024. <br/>
- Low mortality rate: Only four deaths recorded over the last 10 years, indicating effective clinical management. <br/>
- Major outbreak in 2019:  Significant spike during 2019, emphasizing the need for robust surveillance and vaccination efforts. <br/>
- Sustained low incidence since 2020: Cases have remained consistently low since 2020, reflecting effective public health interventions. <br/>

The rubella case data for the Chinese mainland shows notable variability over the observed period. Initial years from mid-2014 to early 2016 displayed a higher case count, with peaks typically in the spring months. For instance, May 2014 registered 2,491 cases, and March-April 2015 saw a peak of 1,232 to 2,113 cases. This pattern suggests a seasonality to rubella outbreaks, aligning with a higher transmission rate in spring, which is common in temperate regions. Deaths remained exceedingly rare, with only three fatalities recorded throughout the entire dataset, indicating a low case-fatality ratio in these instances.

A remarkable surge in cases was observed in 2019, peaking at a substantial 9,095 cases in May, reflecting a pronounced outbreak or potentially an accumulative case identification improvement due to enhanced surveillance or reporting accuracy. This spike was followed by a sharp decline during the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020, with monthly cases dropping dramatically to single and low double digits, such as 90 in May 2020. This substantial reduction in rubella cases can be attributed potentially to increased public health measures, social distancing, and reduced international and domestic travel which concurrently affected the transmission dynamics. Over the subsequent years, the case counts remained relatively low, with minor fluctuations, never reaching pre-pandemic levels. Consistent low numbers from 2021 onwards suggest maintained strict public health interventions or improved herd immunity through vaccination coverage.
