# Rubella

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection caused by the Rubella virus. It primarily affects children and young adults, spreading through respiratory droplets. Symptoms are typically mild, including rash, fever, and swollen lymph nodes. However, Rubella poses a significant risk during pregnancy, potentially causing congenital rubella syndrome (CRS), which can lead to severe birth defects or miscarriage. Vaccination, particularly the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine, is highly effective in preventing infection. Thanks to widespread immunization, Rubella has been largely controlled in many regions, though outbreaks can still occur where vaccination coverage is low.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Rubella cases have dropped from a peak of 9,095 in May 2019 to just 39 in September 2024. This indicates successful control efforts. <br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Higher cases are observed in spring, with peaks typically from March to June, suggesting seasonal transmission patterns. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** Despite case fluctuations, the death rate remains extremely low with only two deaths reported, reflecting effective healthcare or the mild nature of the disease. <br/>

- **Recent Stabilization:** Since 2020, cases have stabilized at lower levels, rarely exceeding 200 per month, indicating successful and sustained control measures. <br/>

Analyzing the rubella data from the Chinese mainland reveals distinct patterns and trends from 2014 to 2024. A significant surge in cases is observed in early 2015, with a peak in April at 2,113 cases, following a generally low incidence in late 2014. This increase might correlate with seasonal factors or lapses in vaccination coverage. The surge is followed by a notable decline, reaching a low point by the end of 2015 and continuing a downward trend into 2016, indicating effective public health interventions or increased vaccination efforts.

The data also highlights an alarming spike in rubella cases in 2019, peaking dramatically in May at 9,095 cases. This resurgence could be attributed to factors such as waning immunity, gaps in vaccination coverage, or increased virus circulation. After 2019, cases sharply decline, particularly evident in 2020 and beyond, with occasional minor fluctuations. The sharp decrease post-2019 might be influenced by heightened public health measures and possibly the global focus on pandemic responses. By 2024, the number of cases appears substantially reduced, maintaining figures well below 100 in most months, suggesting sustained control measures and vaccination efforts have effectively managed the disease transmission.
