# Rubella

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection caused by the rubella virus. It is generally mild in children and adults, characterized by symptoms such as a low-grade fever, sore throat, and a rash that typically starts on the face and spreads. Rubella is of particular concern for pregnant women as it can lead to congenital rubella syndrome (CRS) in the fetus, resulting in birth defects. The disease is preventable through vaccination, which is commonly administered as part of the measles-mumps-rubella (MMR) vaccine. Rubella is transmitted via respiratory droplets from infected individuals.

## Highlights

- Rubella cases in Mainland China have significantly decreased since the peak in 2019, with a dramatic reduction from 9,095 cases in May 2019 to only 65 cases in July 2025. <br/>
- No deaths have been reported since June 2019, indicating effective disease management and a likely improvement in vaccine coverage and healthcare accessibility. <br/>
- Seasonal fluctuations still exist, with higher incidence seen in early spring months, but the overall number of cases remains steady, with 41-75 cases reported monthly in the first half of 2025. <br/>
- The sustained low incidence reflects successful public health interventions and immunization efforts over recent years, enhancing population immunity against Rubella. <br/>

From mid-2015 to mid-2025, rubella cases in mainland China displayed notable fluctuations. Initially, monthly cases ranged below 1,000. However, in 2019, there was a significant spike, with cases peaking at over 9,000 in May. This surge could be due to gaps in immunization or changes in reporting practices. Despite this sharp increase in 2019, only one death was reported, highlighting the effective management of severe outcomes.

After 2019, cases declined markedly, reaching double-digit monthly figures by 2025. This reduction could be attributed to enhanced vaccination efforts, increased public awareness, and improved control measures. The sharp decline post-2019 suggests an effective response by health authorities to manage outbreaks and protect vulnerable groups, particularly pregnant women who face the risk of congenital rubella syndrome (CRS).

The persistence of low cases reminds us of the importance of maintaining high vaccination coverage to prevent resurgence. While rubella's impact has been effectively minimized, continuous immunization and monitoring are essential to sustaining these gains. Overall, the data reflects a successful reduction in rubella transmission over the years, owing largely to vigilant public health strategies. However, the recurrent low-level cases indicate that vigilance in disease monitoring remains crucial to preventing potential future outbreaks.
