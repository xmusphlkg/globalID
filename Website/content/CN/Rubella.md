# Rubella

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection characterized by a distinctive red rash. Caused by the Rubella virus, it primarily affects children and young adults. Symptoms typically include a low-grade fever, sore throat, swollen lymph nodes, and a rash that starts on the face and spreads to the rest of the body. Transmission occurs via respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. While generally mild in children, rubella poses serious risks during pregnancy, potentially leading to congenital rubella syndrome. Vaccination, part of the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine, is crucial for prevention.

## Highlights

- There was a significant outbreak in 2019, with cases peaking at 9,095 in May, but the annual counts have sharply declined since then. <br/>
- A dramatic decrease in Rubella cases has been observed post-2019, remaining under 300 cases per month and continually decreasing over subsequent years. <br/>
- The data indicates an effective control of the disease, with the number of cases declining to 45 in September 2025. <br/>
- Deaths attributed to Rubella have been notably rare, with only two recorded deaths between 2015 and 2025, reflecting the disease's relatively low mortality rate on the Chinese mainland.<br/>

The rubella case data for the Chinese mainland from September 2015 to September 2025 shows notable trends and variations. Starting in 2015, case numbers rise noticeably in spring and early summer, peaking in April and May each year. The standout year is 2019, with cases escalating dramatically to 9095 in May, marking a possible outbreak or increased transmission. This year also recorded the period's only death in June, signifying a possible health system alarm.

After 2019, rubella cases decline significantly, which could be attributed to heightened public health interventions, vaccination efforts, or changes in social behavior due to the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to practices like mask-wearing and social distancing. These actions might have helped reduce rubella transmission opportunities, given the timing coincides with the pandemic's global impact starting in 2020. By 2020, cases substantially dropped, and this downward trend continued through 2025, with monthly figures mostly staying below 100 cases, indicating effective control measures or possibly achieving herd immunity.

The data suggests a responsive public health infrastructure possibly enhancing vaccination coverage or adopting effective containment strategies. Overall, rubella incidence appears to have been successfully managed beyond 2019, reflecting improved proficiency or changes in public health strategies that have contributed to consistently low rubella transmission rates from 2020 onwards.
