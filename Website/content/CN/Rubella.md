# Rubella

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection caused by the rubella virus. It primarily affects children and young adults, characterized by a distinctive red rash, mild fever, and swollen lymph nodes. While generally mild in children, rubella can have serious consequences if contracted by pregnant women, leading to congenital rubella syndrome (CRS) in the fetus, which can cause severe birth defects or miscarriage. The disease spreads through respiratory droplets, making vaccination crucial for prevention. Immunization programs have significantly reduced its prevalence, particularly in regions with widespread vaccine coverage.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** After peaking in 2019, with 9,095 cases in May, rubella cases have plummeted, reaching only 51 in August 2025, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** The mortality remains minimal, with just two deaths reported since 2019, highlighting the disease's low fatality risk and effective healthcare response. <br/>
- **Stabilized Seasonal Trends:** Seasonal fluctuations have stabilized, with recent months showing consistently low case numbers. <br/>
- **Sustained Control Achieved:** Continuous low case numbers since 2023 reflect successful vaccination and monitoring efforts, ensuring controlled rubella transmission in China. <br/>

A marked increase is seen early in 2019, with cases peaking in May at 9,095, after a steady gradual rise from the beginning of that year. This surge suggests possible lapses in herd immunity or disturbances in vaccination programs, allowing for greater virus transmission. Notably, there is only one recorded death during this period, emphasizing the typically low fatality rate of Rubella, though suggesting potential issues with healthcare access amidst high case numbers.

Post-2019, there is a considerable decline in cases, corresponding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020. The dramatic drop in numbers, with monthly cases stabilizing below 200, likely reflects the impact of stringent public health measures like social distancing and reduced mobility, combined with increased public focus on infection prevention. This period may indicate successful containment or increased natural immunity within the population. However, the continuous appearance of some cases underscores the necessity for ongoing vaccination efforts to fully eliminate the disease. The fluctuations in the dataset throughout the years might point to possible issues like waning immunity or inconsistencies in vaccination coverage, highlighting the importance of continuous surveillance and responsive public health strategies.
