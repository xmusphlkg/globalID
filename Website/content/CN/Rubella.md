# Rubella

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection caused by the rubella virus. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and commonly affects children and young adults. Typical symptoms include mild fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes, but the infection may also be asymptomatic. Rubella is generally mild in children; however, infection during pregnancy can result in congenital rubella syndrome, leading to serious birth defects. Vaccination is the cornerstone of rubella prevention and control, significantly reducing disease incidence worldwide through immunization programs.

## Highlights

- Rubella incidence in Chinese mainland has declined sharply since a peak in 2019, when monthly cases exceeded 9,000; post-2020, cases stabilized well below 200 per month.<br/>
- The last five years (2021–2025) show sustained low transmission, with cases dropping to fewer than 70 per month throughout 2025 and no deaths reported since March 2022.<br/>
- The dramatic reduction is likely attributable to high vaccine coverage and effective public health intervention, minimizing outbreaks and severe outcomes.<br/>
- As of December 2025, rubella remains at historically low levels, indicating excellent ongoing control within the region.<br/>

Rubella case reports in the Chinese mainland from late 2015 to early 2025 display marked fluctuations, with a pronounced epidemic in 2019. During this year, monthly cases rapidly increased from 797 in January to a peak of 9,095 in May, followed by a steep decline in the latter half of the year. In contrast, previous years (2016–2018) saw moderate seasonality with springtime elevations but no comparable outbreak. Starting in 2020, incidence sharply dropped, rarely exceeding 200 monthly cases and often falling below 100, with further decreases noted from 2023 onwards, where case numbers consistently hovered in the double digits. These patterns suggest a combination of post-epidemic response measures, improved vaccination coverage, and the indirect effects of COVID-19 containment policies reducing transmission of multiple infectious diseases.

Mortality associated with rubella remained extremely low across the decade, with only two deaths recorded (June 2019 and March 2022) despite thousands of cases, highlighting its generally mild course in most individuals. The transition to very low case counts after 2019, sustained through 2024 and early 2025, may reflect durable collective immunity and strengthened surveillance. Altogether, rubella incidence has been markedly suppressed since the large 2019 epidemic, indicating significant progress in rubella control and resilience of health systems to prevent widespread transmission.
