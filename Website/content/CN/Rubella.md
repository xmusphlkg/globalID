# Rubella

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection characterized by a distinctive red rash. It's caused by the rubella virus, primarily transmitted through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms often include mild fever, rash, and swollen lymph nodes. While generally mild in children and adults, rubella poses significant risks during pregnancy, potentially leading to congenital rubella syndrome, which can cause serious birth defects. Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent rubella and its complications.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Rubella cases dropped from 9095 in May 2019 to 73 in June 2024, showing effective control measures.<br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** Only three deaths reported since 2014, indicating a low mortality rate for Rubella.<br/>
- **Stable Low Case Numbers Post-2020:** Since 2020, monthly cases have stayed below 300, indicating sustained control.<br/>
- **Seasonal Trends:** Data show seasonal peaks in spring and declines in summer and winter.<br/>

The data for Rubella cases in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 demonstrates significant fluctuations in monthly reported cases. The period from 2014 to mid-2015 shows a noticeable decline from 1,662 cases in June 2014 to a low of 202 cases by October 2015, suggesting effective public health measures or seasonal variations. However, a spike occurs in March 2015 with 1,232 cases, peaking at 2,113 cases in April 2015. This variability continues, with notable peaks in April and May of each year, particularly in 2016 and 2019, the latter experiencing a dramatic surge reaching 9,095 cases in May 2019. This peak contrasts sharply with the consistently lower numbers in subsequent years.

From mid-2019 onwards, the trend shifts towards a gradual decrease in cases, with a sharp decline observed during the COVID-19 pandemic period in 2020 and 2021, where monthly cases dropped to double digits. This decrease aligns with heightened public health interventions and social distancing measures. Post-pandemic years from 2022 to mid-2024 show stabilized lower case counts, rarely exceeding 200 per month, indicative of sustained control measures or possibly the residual effects of the pandemic-induced behavioral changes. Throughout the decade, deaths from Rubella remained exceptionally rare, with only three fatalities recorded in March 2015, June 2019, and March 2022, highlighting the relatively low mortality risk associated with Rubella in this region.
