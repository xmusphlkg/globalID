# Rubella

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection caused by the rubella virus. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a mild rash, low-grade fever, and swollen lymph nodes. While rubella is generally mild in children and adults, it can cause severe birth defects if a pregnant woman contracts the virus, especially in the first trimester. Vaccination with the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine is the most effective preventive measure against rubella.

## Highlights

1. **Declining Rubella Cases**: Rubella cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a marked decline from 2019, with cases dropping significantly from thousands to under 100 per month since 2022. <br/>

2. **Low Mortality**: There have been no reported deaths from rubella since 2015, indicating successful control of severe cases. <br/>

3. **Seasonal Variability**: Although cases have decreased overall, there are still seasonal fluctuations, with slight increases in cases observed in certain months, such as in May and November. <br/>

4. **Sustained Control Efforts**: The consistent low case numbers in recent years suggest continued effective vaccination and public health measures. <br/>

The reported data on rubella in mainland China shows a fluctuating pattern, with a significant surge in cases from 2014 to 2019. Monthly cases peaked in 2019, reaching over 9,000 in May and 7,471 in April. After this peak, cases dropped sharply starting in 2020, likely due to a combination of factors such as improved vaccination coverage, public health interventions, and possible disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. By 2020, monthly cases consistently stayed below 300, signaling a substantial reduction in transmission.

From 2020 to 2024, rubella cases continued to decline, with most months in 2023 and 2024 reporting fewer than 100 cases. The absence of reported deaths since 2015 further suggests improvements in disease management and control. This reduction in cases aligns with enhanced vaccination efforts and public health initiatives targeting rubella. However, occasional spikes, like those in early 2024, highlight that rubella remains a concern, especially in specific populations. Overall, the trend indicates significant progress, but continued vigilance is needed to sustain this control and prevent future outbreaks.
