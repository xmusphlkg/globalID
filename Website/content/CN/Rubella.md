# Rubella

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Rubella, or German measles, is a contagious viral infection signified by a red rash and mild symptoms like low-grade fever and swollen lymph nodes. It mainly affects children and young adults. While usually mild in children, rubella poses substantial risks to pregnant women, as it can cause congenital rubella syndrome in the fetus, leading to severe birth defects. The virus spreads through respiratory droplets. The MMR vaccine (measles, mumps, rubella) is highly effective in preventing rubella. Its widespread use has significantly decreased the incidence of the disease globally, marking it as a public health success.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend:** Rubella cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant decline from the high numbers observed in 2019, with cases peaking at 9,095 in May 2019 to much lower levels by 2025. <br/>
- **Low Mortality:** Despite the fluctuations in case numbers, mortality rates have remained exceptionally low, with only two deaths reported in the entire data set. <br/>
- **Stabilization in Recent Years:** From 2020 onwards, the number of reported cases has remained relatively stable, particularly in 2025, with monthly cases ranging between 41 and 75. <br/>
- **Recent Low Levels:** As of October 2025, Rubella cases remain low, suggesting effective ongoing public health interventions and surveillance efforts. <br/>

The reported data for Rubella cases in the Chinese mainland from October 2015 to October 2025 indicates fluctuating trends in infection rates. In late 2015 and early 2016, the case numbers started low but began increasing during the early months of 2016, peaking between March and May 2016 with a noticeable surge. A gradual decline followed, and the numbers reduced through the remainder of 2016. The pattern of seasonal peaks in the spring and decline in the latter half of the year characterizes the Rubella cases during these years, asserting a prevalent seasonal factor influencing transmission.

The year 2019 marked a significant surge in Rubella cases, characterized by an extraordinary increase beginning in March, reaching a peak in May, and then sharply declining thereafter. This anomaly may suggest either an outbreak event or an increase in reporting accuracy or surveillance. In contrast, from 2020 onwards, a marked reduction in reported cases is observed, aligning with heightened public health interventions possibly influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic's effects on general public health measures and surveillance. This decline continued steadily through to 2025, indicating a sustained control over Rubella infections with numbers significantly lower than those in previous years, showcasing effective public health interventions and/or possible widespread immunization, thereby limiting outbreaks and transmission substantially.
