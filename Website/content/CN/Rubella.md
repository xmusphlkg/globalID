# Rubella

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Rubella, also known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection characterized by a distinctive red rash. It is caused by the rubella virus and primarily affects children and young adults. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets from an infected person. Symptoms include mild fever, sore throat, and swollen lymph nodes, often accompanied by the rash. While generally mild in children, rubella can have serious consequences if contracted by pregnant women, potentially leading to congenital rubella syndrome in the fetus. Vaccination is the most effective prevention method, typically administered as part of the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** Rubella cases in the Chinese mainland have significantly decreased from a peak in 2019, with over 9,000 cases in May 2019, to around 60-70 cases per month in 2024. <br/>

- **Low Mortality:** The disease has maintained a low mortality rate, with only two deaths reported from 2014 to 2024, indicating effective management. <br/>

- **Seasonal Trends:** There is a seasonal pattern, with higher cases in spring, though the overall trend is downward. <br/>

- **Current Stability:** As of December 2024, the situation is stable with low case numbers, suggesting effective public health interventions and vaccination efforts. <br/>

Initially, there was a notable increase in cases from late 2014 through mid-2015, peaking in April 2015 with 2,113 cases. This was followed by a gradual decline, reaching a low in late 2016. However, a resurgence occurred in 2019, with a dramatic spike in cases, peaking in May 2019 at 9,095 cases. This spike was followed by a sharp decline in 2020, likely influenced by public health measures related to the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have inadvertently reduced the transmission of other infectious diseases, including Rubella.

From 2020 onwards, the number of cases remained relatively low and stable, with minor fluctuations. The data indicates a consistent decrease in cases from 2021 to 2024, with the lowest numbers recorded in 2024. Throughout the entire period, the number of deaths remained extremely low, with only three deaths reported over the ten-year span. This suggests that while Rubella cases have varied significantly, the disease has maintained a low mortality rate, likely due to effective medical interventions and possibly high vaccination coverage. The overall trend reflects the impact of public health strategies and possibly changes in population immunity levels over time.
