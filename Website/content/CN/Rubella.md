# Rubella

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Rubella, commonly known as German measles, is a contagious viral infection primarily impacting children and young adults. Caused by the rubella virus, it is characterized by a distinctive red rash, low-grade fever, and swollen lymph nodes. Transmitted through respiratory droplets, rubella is typically mild but poses significant risks during pregnancy. Infection in pregnant women can lead to congenital rubella syndrome (CRS), resulting in severe birth defects or miscarriage. Vaccination effectively prevents rubella, making the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine pivotal in global eradication efforts, significantly reducing incidence rates and preventing CRS.

## Highlights

- Rubella cases in China have experienced a significant decline since the peak in 2019, which saw over 9,000 cases in May. By August 2024, the number had reduced to 70 cases.<br/>
- Deaths from Rubella are rare, with only a few instances recorded between 2015 and 2022. The most recent death was in March 2022.<br/>
- Rubella cases have remained low and stable since 2020, consistently staying under 200 cases per month.<br/>
- As of August 2024, there are no reported deaths, and the disease burden remains significantly reduced compared to previous years.<br/>

The data on Rubella cases in the Chinese mainland between August 2014 and August 2024 shows distinct patterns of fluctuation over time. From 2014 to early 2019, the number of reported cases generally increased, peaking dramatically in May 2019 with 9,095 cases. This rise was followed by a significant decline in the second half of 2019 and into 2020. The highest monthly death toll occurred in March 2015 and June 2019, with one death each, indicating a relatively low mortality rate associated with the disease.

From 2020 onward, the trend reveals a marked decrease in Rubella cases, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have influenced both transmission and reporting. Rubella cases reached their lowest point during the pandemic years, particularly in 2021 and early 2022, with monthly cases consistently below 200. By 2023 and into 2024, the cases remained at a lower, stabilized level, fluctuating between 33 and 110 per month without any reported deaths, suggesting effective control or reduced transmission in recent years.
