# SARS-CoV

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus (SARS-CoV) is a zoonotic virus belonging to the genus Betacoronavirus. First identified in Guangdong, China, in 2002, it caused a global outbreak in 2002–2003, resulting in over 8,000 cases and nearly 800 deaths. SARS-CoV primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and close person-to-person contact. Its clinical presentation typically includes fever, cough, and difficulty breathing, with progression to pneumonia or acute respiratory distress syndrome in severe cases. The virus’s natural reservoirs are believed to be bats, with transmission to humans possibly facilitated by intermediate hosts.

## Highlights

- There have been no reported cases or deaths from SARS-CoV in the Chinese mainland from December 2015 through December 2025, indicating sustained disease absence. <br/>
- The data strongly suggests that SARS-CoV transmission has remained interrupted and under control for the past decade. <br/>
- No seasonal or incident spikes are observable, highlighting the effectiveness of ongoing surveillance and containment strategies. <br/>
- As of December 2025, SARS-CoV does not pose a public health threat in the region, but continued vigilance and laboratory surveillance remain essential to detect potential re-emergence promptly. <br/>

An examination of the reported SARS-CoV data spanning from December 2015 through December 2025 in the Chinese mainland reveals absolute absence of cases and deaths, with each monthly entry consistently recording zero incidences. This consistent reporting across a decade implies no evidence of SARS-CoV outbreaks or transmission within this region during the time period assessed. The unbroken pattern of zeroes throughout the months and years examined, including during periods of heightened public health surveillance due to other emerging respiratory pathogens, suggests robust monitoring and no detection of SARS-CoV resurgence.

Analysis of this dataset over such a prolonged period provides a strong indication of sustained control after the original 2002–2003 epidemic. The absence of both cases and deaths is noteworthy, given that ongoing zoonotic risks and periodic novel coronavirus outbreaks typically require vigilant surveillance. Here, there are no seasonal, annual, or sporadic spikes indicating clusters or imported cases. The uniformity in reporting, coupled with the length of surveillance, supports the conclusion of maintained interruption of SARS-CoV human transmission in the Chinese mainland environment throughout the period analyzed.
