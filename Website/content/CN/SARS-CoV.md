# SARS-CoV

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus (SARS-CoV) is a virus that emerged in 2002 in Guangdong, China. It caused a global outbreak in 2003, infecting over 8,000 people in 26 countries with a mortality rate of around 10%. SARS-CoV spreads primarily through respiratory droplets and close contact. Symptoms include fever, cough, and difficulty breathing, often leading to pneumonia. The outbreak was controlled through quarantine and travel restrictions, and no new cases have been reported since 2004. SARS-CoV is part of the coronavirus family, which also includes other viruses like MERS-CoV and SARS-CoV-2.

## Highlights

- **No new SARS-CoV cases since 2004**: The data from 2014 to November 2024 shows zero reported cases of SARS-CoV in mainland China, indicating successful containment of the disease. <br/>
- **Sustained absence of fatalities**: Alongside the lack of new cases, no deaths have been recorded from SARS-CoV since 2004, demonstrating ongoing public health success in managing the disease. <br/>
- **Continuous vigilance**: Despite no recent cases, monitoring and preparedness remain critical to prevent the potential re-emergence of the virus, especially given global interconnectedness. <br/>
- **Stable epidemiological situation**: As of November 2024, the epidemiological situation for SARS-CoV in China remains stable with no new developments, confirming effective control measures. <br/>

The data reveals a continuous period from 2014 to 2024 in the Chinese mainland where there were no reported cases or deaths due to SARS-CoV. This unbroken trend of zero cases suggests that SARS-CoV, which caused the 2002-2003 outbreak, has been effectively contained within China in the years following the epidemic. Surveillance and control measures may have been robust in the aftermath of the outbreak, significantly reducing the potential for new transmissions. Despite the occasional risk of re-emergence, as with many zoonotic viruses, the absence of reported cases for over a decade reflects both successful public health interventions and the relatively limited ongoing zoonotic spillover during this period.

It is also notable that the consistent absence of cases over such a long stretch of time could suggest a lack of new outbreaks or a decline in the virus's ability to spread effectively. Additionally, the global and domestic focus on other emerging infectious diseases, such as the COVID-19 pandemic starting in 2019, might have influenced resource allocation, but SARS-CoV appears to have remained a concern primarily in the past. While this data might also reflect the limitations of case reporting or surveillance, the consistent trend offers an optimistic view of Chinaâ€™s ability to manage and prevent outbreaks of SARS-CoV following the initial crisis.
