# SARS-CoV

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus (SARS-CoV) is a zoonotic virus that emerged in Guangdong, China, in late 2002. It causes SARS, a contagious and sometimes fatal respiratory illness. SARS-CoV is a member of the Betacoronavirus genus and is thought to have originated in bats, with civet cats as intermediary hosts. The virus spreads via respiratory droplets and close person-to-person contact. The outbreak led to over 8,000 cases and nearly 800 deaths across 29 countries before being contained in 2003. No human cases have been reported since 2004, but it remains a virus of concern.

## Highlights

- No reported SARS-CoV cases or deaths have occurred in mainland China from April 2015 through April 2025, indicating sustained disease elimination over a full decade. <br/>
- The data supports continued surveillance success, with zero monthly incidence or mortality consistently maintained. <br/>
- There is no evidence of local transmission, re-emergence, or imported cases during this ten-year observation period. <br/>
- As of April 2025, SARS-CoV remains absent from the population, reinforcing the effectiveness of current public health monitoring and response infrastructure. <br/>

From April 2015 through April 2025, the data for SARS-CoV in the Chinese mainland consistently reports zero new cases and zero deaths each month. This sustained absence of reported cases over a ten-year span indicates no re-emergence or sporadic transmission of SARS-CoV among the population during this period. The data continuity also suggests a robust surveillance system capable of detecting cases, had any occurred. It's likely that post-2003 epidemic control strategies—such as improved detection, reporting systems, and biosecurity in animal-human interfaces—have remained effective.

This long-term disease quiescence contrasts sharply with the acute outbreak of SARS in 2002–2003 and underscores the success of containment policies since. The lack of resurgence could also reflect the elimination of specific zoonotic spillover sources or heightened public health preparedness against coronaviruses. Continued absence of transmission implies strong intersectoral coordination and ongoing vigilance in monitoring potential reintroduction threats, either from wildlife reservoirs or laboratory-related risks.

The continuous absence of SARS-CoV deaths in mainland China from April 2015 to April 2025 reflects a sustained epidemiological silence following the initial outbreak in 2002–2003. Across this 10-year timespan, official surveillance reported zero confirmed SARS-CoV cases and consequently zero deaths, suggesting complete interruption of human transmission. The data indicates that no resurgence, reintroduction via zoonotic spillover, or lab-related incident occurred to disrupt the post-eradication status quo during this monitored period.

The depth of this zero-death profile also implies effective long-term containment at multiple levels: public health infrastructure, international monitoring, and biosecurity. Given the natural reservoir of SARS-like coronaviruses in animals, the complete absence of human infections correlating with death underscores success in preventing interspecies crossover, particularly at the animal–human interface. With no recorded fatalities, the prevention landscape appears robust, although ongoing vigilance remains essential due to the persistent ecological risk of related emerging pathogens.
