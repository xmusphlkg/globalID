# SARS-CoV

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus (SARS-CoV) is a novel coronavirus identified in 2003, responsible for the SARS outbreak that began in Asia in 2002. It is an enveloped, positive-sense, single-stranded RNA virus, belonging to the Betacoronavirus genus. SARS-CoV primarily spreads through respiratory droplets, leading to symptoms like fever, cough, and severe respiratory illness. The virus binds to the host cell's angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptor, facilitating entry. With a high transmission rate and significant morbidity, SARS-CoV prompted global health responses, highlighting the need for rapid diagnostic, treatment, and containment measures to control outbreaks effectively.

## Highlights

- No recorded cases or deaths of SARS-CoV in the Chinese mainland from June 2015 to June 2025 indicate strong containment or absence of the virus. <br/>
- Robust surveillance and public health measures may have contributed to the sustained absence of SARS-CoV cases across a decade. <br/>
- The consistent lack of incident cases suggests successful prevention strategies or the non-circulation of the virus in the population. <br/>
- The data reflects effective monitoring and possibly high public health readiness against any potential re-emergence of SARS-CoV. <br/>

The provided data on SARS-CoV cases in mainland China from 2015 to mid-2025 indicates a complete absence of reported cases and deaths over this ten-year period. This consistent zero-reporting suggests that SARS-CoV has not been a public health issue in China during these years, contrasting starkly with the 2002-2003 outbreak when SARS first emerged, causing significant morbidity and mortality in the region. The absence of cases may reflect successful containment and eradication measures following the initial SARS outbreak, alongside improvements in global surveillance, rapid response capacities, and stringent public health policies.

This data trend underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and advanced preparation to safeguard against potential re-emergences. Such surveillance is critical in identifying and responding promptly to any new threats posed by coronaviruses, enhancing the resilience of health systems. The data also highlights the impact of maintaining stringent biosecurity and hygiene protocols, which may have played a role in preventing outbreaks. Moreover, it suggests that cooperation at the international level coupled with robust local health infrastructure has been effective in controlling zoonotic transmissions, which are typically how such viruses re-enter human populations.
