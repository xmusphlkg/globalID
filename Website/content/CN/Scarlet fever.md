# Scarlet fever

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by the Group A Streptococcus bacteria, the same bacteria responsible for strep throat. It primarily affects children and is characterized by a distinctive red rash, hence the name "scarlet." Other symptoms include a high fever, a sore throat, flushed cheeks, and a "strawberry" tongue appearance. The condition is spread through respiratory droplets from an infected person's coughs or sneezes. Once a common and serious childhood illness, scarlet fever is now easily treatable with antibiotics, usually penicillin or amoxicillin, and complications are rare when managed promptly.

## Highlights

- **Recent Case Surge**: Scarlet fever cases in China increased from 1,209 in August 2023 to 2,028 in August 2024, showing a sharp rise. <br/>

- **Seasonality**: Peaks in cases are consistently observed during late spring and early summer, suggesting a seasonal trend related to school settings. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite significant fluctuations in cases, the disease remains low in fatality, with the last reported death in 2020. <br/>

- **Post-Pandemic Resurgence**: Case numbers dropped significantly during the pandemic but have since risen, returning to pre-pandemic levels. <br/>

Scarlet fever cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 exhibit significant seasonal and year-to-year variation, with notable peaks during spring and early summer (April to June) and declines in the winter months. From 2014 through 2019, there is a consistent annual rise in cases, with peak values in May or June, such as 9,992 cases in June 2015 and 13,053 cases in December 2019. This period also displays a consistent pattern of zero reported deaths except for a single death in April 2015. The incidence drops sharply from 2020, coinciding with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, which may reflect either the direct impact of control measures or changes in reporting practices. By 2020, monthly case counts plummeted, reaching a low of 444 cases in March of that year.

After 2020, the case numbers slowly recover but remain lower than pre-pandemic levels until 2023, when a more substantial resurgence is observed, particularly in the latter half of the year. In 2024, the cases rise steeply again, reaching 12,397 in June, comparable to the high levels seen in earlier years. Interestingly, throughout this entire 10-year period, only three deaths are reported, suggesting that despite the fluctuations in case numbers, scarlet fever remained largely non-fatal. This data may suggest a resilient healthcare response or improved disease management, particularly in the context of heightened public health measures during the pandemic.

The analysis of Scarlet fever data in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals a notable trend in both cases and deaths. Throughout the decade-long period, the number of Scarlet fever cases fluctuated significantly, with peaks typically occurring in the spring and early summer months (April to June), as seen in 2015, 2017, and 2019. These peaks occasionally exceeded 10,000 cases per month, such as in May 2015, June 2017, and May 2024. Despite these high case numbers, the reported death count remained exceptionally low, with only a total of four deaths recorded between 2014 and 2024. These occurred in April 2015, January 2020, and September 2020, reflecting a fatality rate far below 0.01%.

This low mortality rate is indicative of Scarlet fever's transition from a historically dangerous disease to one with minimal fatal consequences in this context, likely due to improved healthcare, antibiotics, and public health measures. The spike in deaths in 2020 may warrant further investigation, as it coincides with the global COVID-19 pandemic, which could have impacted healthcare accessibility or reporting systems. However, the overall fatality numbers remain negligible compared to the high case counts, suggesting that Scarlet fever, while still prevalent in China, poses a minimal risk in terms of mortality.
