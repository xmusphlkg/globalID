# Scarlet fever

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is a contagious bacterial illness primarily affecting children, caused by the Group A Streptococcus bacteria, the same organism responsible for strep throat. It is characterized by a distinct red rash, sore throat, fever, and a "strawberry" tongue. The rash usually begins on the chest and spreads to other parts of the body, feeling like sandpaper. Scarlet fever was once a serious illness but is now less dangerous, thanks to effective antibiotics. Prompt treatment is crucial to prevent complications such as rheumatic fever. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets, direct contact, or sharing contaminated items.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend:** Scarlet fever cases in the Chinese mainland have decreased from peak levels between 2016-2019, significantly dropping after 2020. Improved public health measures could contribute to this decline. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** Scarlet fever has consistently low mortality, with deaths reported only in 2020, indicating effective clinical treatments despite thousands of cases annually. <br/>
- **Seasonal Patterns:** Cases typically peak in spring and early summer, indicating a cyclical pattern possibly influenced by climate or school schedules. <br/>
- **Current Situation (2025):** As of August 2025, cases remain low compared to previous peaks, emphasizing the importance of maintaining public health efforts to prevent resurgence. <br/>

Scarlet fever cases in the Chinese mainland display fluctuations, likely influenced by seasonality and public health measures. From 2015 to 2019, cases often spiked in late spring and early summer months, common when children gather in schools and social activities increase. For example, 2017 saw peaks of over 11,000 cases in May and June, indicating robust transmission. From early 2020, a sharp decline in cases coincides with the COVID-19 pandemic's impact, which altered disease dynamics due to social distancing and heightened hygiene protocols. Cases dropped dramatically to well under 1,000 per month during much of 2020.

As restrictions eased post-2021, scarlet fever cases began to resurge, reflecting increased social interactions and a return to pre-COVID patterns. Despite these fluctuations, deaths remain minimal throughout, with just two fatalities reported, underscoring scarlet fever's low mortality rate with proper treatment. The data indicate a consistent seasonal wave pattern, vital for informing public health strategies and managing disease control efficiently. The pandemic's disruption highlights the influence of broader health measures on specific diseases, offering insights into balancing interventions for both scarlet fever and other respiratory diseases in the future. Understanding these trends can help optimize responses to seasonal peaks and improve public health resilience.
