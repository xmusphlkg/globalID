# Scarlet fever

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is a contagious disease caused by toxin-producing strains of *Group A Streptococcus* bacteria, the same organism responsible for strep throat. It primarily affects children aged 5 to 15 years and spreads through respiratory droplets or direct contact. Symptoms begin with a sore throat, fever, and headache, followed by a characteristic red rash that feels like sandpaper and often starts on the chest before spreading. Additional signs include a "strawberry" tongue and flushed cheeks. Early antibiotic treatment is essential to prevent complications such as rheumatic fever or kidney inflammation. Scarlet fever cases are more common in winter and spring.

## Highlights

- Scarlet fever cases in China show clear seasonal trends, peaking in late spring to early summer (May–June), with consistent annual recurrence. <br/>
- After a sharp drop in 2020–2022 likely due to COVID-19 measures, reported cases rebounded notably in 2023 and surged in 2024–2025. <br/>
- Despite rising case numbers, no deaths have been reported since late 2020, indicating stable disease management and effective treatment. <br/>
- As of May 2025, 7,844 cases were recorded, lower than May 2024 but still above pre-pandemic May averages, suggesting ongoing heightened transmission activity. <br/>

Monthly case records from 2015 to 2025 demonstrate a recurring seasonal pattern in scarlet fever incidence within mainland China, with cases surging typically from April through June and again in November and December. Prior to 2020, case numbers followed clear annual cycles with consistent peaks—most notably in May and June—with some years like 2018 and 2019 reaching above 12,000 monthly cases. The disease showed little to no mortality throughout the period, with only two deaths reported in 2020, suggesting either effective treatment protocols or underreporting of severe outcomes. 

The COVID-19 pandemic had a marked impact, as evidenced by the sharp case drop starting February 2020, when movement restrictions and non-pharmaceutical interventions led to historically low transmission rates. Post-2020, case counts remained significantly reduced until a gradual resurgence began in late 2022, culminating in a steep increase through 2024 and into 2025. The surge in 2024 surpasses even pre-pandemic peak levels, implying recovery of transmission routes and possibly lower population immunity due to reduced circulation in preceding years. The consistent zero-death count post-2021 may reflect further public health improvement or changes in health system responsiveness.
