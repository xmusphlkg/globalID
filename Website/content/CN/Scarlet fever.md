# Scarlet fever

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Streptococcus pyogenes, also known as group A Streptococcus. It primarily affects children and is characterized by a red rash, sore throat, fever, and a "strawberry" tongue. The rash typically begins on the chest and spreads to other parts of the body. Scarlet fever is transmitted through respiratory droplets from an infected person. While it was once a serious childhood illness, it is now easily treatable with antibiotics. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent complications such as rheumatic fever.

## Highlights

- **Fluctuating Case Numbers:** Scarlet fever cases in mainland China have varied, with peaks in spring and early summer. The highest was in June 2024, with 12,397 cases. <br/>

- **Recent Trends:** As of February 2025, cases have decreased to 2,551, showing a potential seasonal decline. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate is very low, with only two deaths reported, indicating effective disease management. <br/>

- **COVID-19 Impact:** A notable drop in cases occurred in 2020, likely due to COVID-19 public health measures, which may have also reduced scarlet fever transmission. <br/>

The data on Scarlet fever cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows distinct seasonal patterns, with peaks typically occurring in the spring and early summer months (April to June). This suggests a seasonal influence on transmission, possibly linked to school terms and increased indoor activities during colder months.

From 2015 to 2019, there is a general upward trend in cases, with significant peaks in May and June. The year 2019 records particularly high numbers, with December reaching over 13,000 cases. However, in 2020, there is a dramatic decline in reported cases, likely due to public health measures and social restrictions from the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have reduced Scarlet fever transmission. This decline continues into 2021, with a gradual increase in cases as restrictions ease.

By 2022, cases begin to rise again, though not reaching pre-pandemic levels until 2024, where a significant increase is observed, particularly in June with over 12,000 cases. This resurgence could be attributed to the relaxation of public health measures and increased social interactions. Throughout the period, the number of deaths remains extremely low, indicating effective clinical management and treatment. The data highlights the importance of continuous monitoring and public health interventions to manage Scarlet fever outbreaks effectively.

This decline could be attributed to the public health measures implemented to control COVID-19, such as lockdowns and increased hygiene practices, which may have inadvertently reduced the transmission of Scarlet fever. Overall, the low mortality rate and the seasonal pattern of cases highlight the importance of continued surveillance and targeted public health strategies to manage Scarlet fever effectively.
