# Scarlet fever

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Streptococcus pyogenes, also known as group A Streptococcus. It primarily affects children and is characterized by a red rash, sore throat, fever, and a "strawberry" tongue. The rash typically begins on the chest and spreads to other parts of the body. Scarlet fever is transmitted through respiratory droplets from an infected person. While it was once a serious childhood illness, it is now easily treatable with antibiotics. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent complications such as rheumatic fever or kidney disease.

## Highlights

- **Rising Trend in Cases**: Scarlet fever cases in the Chinese mainland have increased significantly from 2020 to 2025, peaking in June 2024 with 12,397 cases. <br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: Higher case numbers are typically observed from April to June each year, indicating a potential link to environmental or social factors. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite high case numbers, the mortality rate remains low, with only two deaths reported from 2015 to 2025. <br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of January 2025, cases remain elevated at 6,353, highlighting ongoing transmission and the need for continued public health measures. <br/>

The highest number of cases recorded in a single month was 9,992 in June 2015, followed by a consistent decline in cases until 2016, where numbers stabilized at lower levels. The trend appears to show a cyclical pattern, with spikes in certain months, particularly in the spring and summer, which may correlate with environmental factors or increased social interactions during these periods.

From 2020 onwards, there is a marked decrease in the number of cases, with monthly totals often falling below 3,000. This decline could suggest improved public health measures, increased awareness, or changes in reporting practices. Notably, the data indicates that despite the fluctuations in cases, fatalities remain extremely low, with only two reported deaths throughout the entire dataset. This suggests that while Scarlet fever remains a public health concern, the mortality rate is effectively managed, highlighting the importance of ongoing surveillance and intervention strategies to maintain this trend.
