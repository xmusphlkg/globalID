# Scarlet fever

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is a contagious bacterial illness predominantly affecting children and is caused by Group A Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes). It often follows a throat infection or skin infection like impetigo. Characterized by a distinctive red rash, it also presents symptoms such as a high fever, sore throat, and a "strawberry" tongue. Historically called scarlatina, it was once more severe, but with modern medicine, particularly antibiotics, it is now easily treatable. However, early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent complications such as rheumatic fever and kidney problems. Scarlet fever spreads through respiratory droplets and direct contact.

## Highlights

- Scarlet fever cases in the Chinese mainland reached a high in 2019, with over 10,000 cases in June. A significant decline occurred in 2020, likely due to COVID-19 measures, followed by a gradual resurgence. <br/>
- Seasonal peaks consistently occur around May to July, though the number of cases fluctuates each year. <br/>
- Mortality rates have remained very low, with only a few deaths reported, suggesting effective medical interventions or milder infections. <br/>
- As of June 2025, cases have risen to 8,718, showing a resurgence from pandemic lows, highlighting the importance of continued public health vigilance and preventive measures. <br/>

The scarlet fever data from 2015 to 2025 in mainland China shows seasonal trends and fluctuations in case numbers. Generally, cases surge in late spring and early summer, with May and June consistently having high counts. This seasonal peak might be due to favorable conditions for Group A Streptococcus transmission and school attendance patterns where children, mostly affected, have increased contact. For example, May 2017, 2018, and 2019 exhibit case numbers surpassing 9,000, reinforcing the pattern of heightened transmission during these months. Conversely, lower numbers in late winter, notably February, highlight the disease's seasonality, possibly due to school holidays, colder weather, and reduced social interactions.

The data also shows a significant drop in 2020, coinciding with the impact of COVID-19. Public health measures such as enhanced hygiene, school closures, and reduced interactions may have inadvertently curbed scarlet fever spread. Post-2020, a gradual return to seasonal trends occurs, though with some variation in case numbers, possibly reflecting changes in public health practices. Notably, there were very few deaths throughout the years despite varying case numbers, suggesting mild disease progression and effective health responses. This underscores the importance of continuous monitoring and readiness to manage possible complications.

The available data for Scarlet fever in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to mid-2025 highlight the disease burden in terms of cases. Notably, the data also reflect a rare incidence of mortality, with only two deaths reported—one in January 2020 and another in September 2020. Throughout the remaining months across the eight-year span, no fatalities were recorded, suggesting effective management and treatment of the disease or potentially less virulent strains being prevalent in the region. This low mortality rate of Scarlet fever in China contrasts with the relatively high number of cases over the years, indicating that while the disease remains a common communicable disease, it is generally not life-threatening in this context.

The sharp peak of cases, especially in specific months such as May and June for several years, aligns with the known seasonal fluctuations in Scarlet fever cases, which generally increase in colder months possibly due to closer indoor congregations and school settings. However, the consistent absence of deaths through most of the examined years, despite varying case numbers, emphasizes the robustness of public health interventions, such as timely diagnosis and access to antibiotics, in preventing complications and severe outcomes of the disease. Active surveillance and successful disease containment strategies may also contribute to these outcomes, suggesting well-established healthcare protocols effectively curb the lethality of Scarlet fever in the area.
