# Scarlet fever

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Streptococcus pyogenes*, also known as group A Streptococcus. It primarily affects children aged 5 to 15 years and presents with symptoms such as a red rash, high fever, sore throat, and a "strawberry" tongue. The rash typically starts on the chest and spreads to other parts of the body. Scarlet fever is highly contagious and can be transmitted through respiratory droplets or direct contact with an infected person's skin. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are crucial to prevent potential complications.

## Highlights

- Significant rise in cases in May, with 10,271 recorded in May 2024, indicating a potential seasonal peak. <br/>
- Continuous upward trend observed since late 2023, suggesting increasing transmission rates or improved reporting mechanisms. <br/>
- Comparison to previous years shows fluctuating monthly cases, with notable reductions during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020-2022. <br/>
- Death rates remain consistently low, with no fatalities reported in 2024, suggesting effective clinical management of the disease. <br/>

The data for Scarlet fever in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 displays notable seasonal variations and trends. Peak incidence generally occurs in late spring and early summer, especially in May and June. For instance, May 2014 saw 7,113 cases, increasing to 9,130 in May 2015, and reaching 11,388 in May 2017. Comparatively, January and February show lower numbers, reflecting possible seasonality in transmission. 

Significantly, there was a marked decline during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, with cases dropping to as low as 580 in February 2020, likely due to public health interventions like lockdowns and social distancing. Post-2020, cases trended upwards again, with 10,271 reported by May 2024, indicating a return to pre-pandemic levels. 

Despite these fluctuations, mortality due to Scarlet fever remains consistently low, with only three deaths reported over the entire period. This suggests effective treatment and early diagnosis have kept fatality rates minimal. Overall, while Scarlet fever remains a public health concern with periodic surges, particularly in late spring and early summer, the extremely low death rate underscores the effectiveness of existing clinical and public health responses. Continuous monitoring remains crucial to manage and mitigate future outbreaks.
