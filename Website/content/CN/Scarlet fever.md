# Scarlet fever

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by group A Streptococcus bacteria, often following a throat infection like strep throat. It typically affects children between 5 and 15 years old. Symptoms include a red, sandpaper-like rash, sore throat, fever, and a "strawberry" tongue. The rash usually starts on the neck, chest, and armpits, spreading to other parts of the body. Scarlet fever is contagious and spreads through respiratory droplets. With appropriate antibiotic treatment, the condition is usually manageable, but untreated cases can lead to complications such as rheumatic fever or kidney issues.

## Highlights

- **Scarlet Fever Incidence Trends**: Scarlet fever cases peaked in the mid-2010s, with over 10,000 monthly cases in 2017-2018, but have since decreased significantly. In November 2024, 5,111 cases were reported. <br/>

- **Seasonality**: The disease exhibits a clear seasonal pattern, with higher cases from April to July, peaking in summer. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Deaths remain rare, with only two fatalities (2020), indicating a low fatality rate. <br/>

- **Recent Decline**: After a sharp decline in 2020, case numbers have remained lower, with a slight increase in 2024. <br/>

The Scarlet fever data from mainland China shows fluctuating seasonal patterns from 2014 to 2019, with peak cases typically occurring in the winter months, particularly in December. For example, in December 2019, cases spiked to 13,053, one of the highest in the dataset. Despite these outbreaks, the mortality rate remained very low, with only two deaths reported—one in April 2015 and another in September 2020. This suggests that while the disease saw periodic surges, it did not lead to significant fatalities.

A notable decline in cases occurred in 2020, likely linked to COVID-19 control measures such as lockdowns, social distancing, and mask-wearing. For instance, February and March 2020 saw drastic drops in reported cases, which may also reflect reduced healthcare access and reporting. As pandemic restrictions eased, cases began to rise again, especially from mid-2021, with numbers returning to higher levels by mid-2024. The resurgence suggests a return to normal transmission and reporting patterns. Overall, the data indicates that while Scarlet fever outbreaks can be seasonal and widespread, they are typically mild, with few deaths, even during significant surges in cases.
