# Scarlet fever

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is a contagious bacterial infection caused by group A Streptococcus, the same bacteria responsible for strep throat. It predominantly affects children aged 5 to 15. Characterized by a red, sandpaper-like rash, fever, and a "strawberry" tongue, scarlet fever often follows or accompanies a sore throat. The bacteria produce toxins leading to these symptoms. Diagnosis is typically confirmed with a throat swab culture. Scarlet fever was once severe, but with antibiotics like penicillin, it is now treatable and complications are rare. However, prompt treatment is essential to prevent potential complications, such as rheumatic fever.

## Highlights

- **Decline in case numbers due to COVID-19**: Scarlet fever cases dropped sharply in 2020 and remained low through 2022, likely due to COVID-19 mitigation efforts like lockdowns and school closures. <br/>
- **Resurgence in 2023-2024**: Case numbers rebounded in 2023, continuing into 2024, with a peak of over 12,000 cases in June 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal patterns**: Annual peaks consistently occurred between May and July, with lower transmission in the winter months. <br/>
- **Low mortality rate**: Deaths remain extremely rare, with only 3 deaths reported over the 10-year period. <br/>

An analysis of the scarlet fever cases in mainland China between September 2014 and September 2024 reveals notable fluctuations in the incidence of the disease. The data indicates a seasonal pattern, with higher case counts typically observed during the spring and early summer months (April to June) each year. This trend can be seen across multiple years, such as in 2015, 2016, 2017, 2018, and 2019, where May and June consistently recorded the highest monthly cases. This cyclic increase in cases may be linked to environmental factors, school attendance cycles, or other epidemiological drivers.

Scarlet fever cases surged significantly in certain years, notably in 2019 and 2024, reaching over 13,000 and 12,000 cases, respectively, during peak months, suggesting periodic outbreaks or changes in transmission dynamics. Despite these variations, mortality has remained exceptionally low, with only two reported deaths over the entire period (April 2015 and September 2020). The sharp decline in cases during early 2020, with record lows in February through May, corresponds with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, potentially reflecting reduced transmission due to public health interventions like lockdowns and social distancing.

The analysis of scarlet fever deaths in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals an extremely low mortality rate despite the large number of reported cases over the years. Across the dataset, only three deaths were reported, one in April 2015, one in January 2020, and another in September 2020. This indicates that, while the disease continues to cause significant morbidity, with monthly case counts often exceeding several thousand, the fatality rate is nearly negligible. These figures reflect the fact that scarlet fever, although contagious, has become a highly manageable illness, likely due to timely medical intervention and the availability of antibiotics, as well as public health measures.

Notably, the peak in cases does not correspond to an increase in deaths. For example, in months such as May 2017 and June 2024, when cases exceeded 11,000 and 12,000 respectively, there were no reported deaths. This suggests that even during periods of high transmission, the healthcare system effectively controlled severe outcomes. The three reported deaths may reflect individual cases with complications or delays in treatment, but overall, the data highlights the low mortality risk associated with scarlet fever in this region.
