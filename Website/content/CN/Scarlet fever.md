# Scarlet fever

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is a contagious bacterial infection primarily affecting children, caused by Group A Streptococcus bacteria, the same responsible for strep throat. Characterized by a distinctive red rash covering most of the body, scarlet fever often presents with a sore throat, fever, and a "strawberry" tongue appearance. The rash typically feels like sandpaper and commonly starts on the neck and face before spreading. Although once considered a serious illness, timely treatment with antibiotics, particularly penicillin or amoxicillin, makes it manageable today. However, early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent potential complications, such as rheumatic fever or kidney disease.

## Highlights

1. Scarlet fever cases rose significantly in early 2025, reaching 8,718 in June, before decreasing to 4,268 in July, mirroring the seasonal trend of lower incidence in warmer months. <br/>
2. Mortality is extremely low, with only two reported deaths over five years, indicating effective treatment and healthcare measures to manage the disease. <br/>
3. Seasonal fluctuation is evident, with higher cases during late spring and early summer months annually. Addressing these seasonal peaks could be beneficial. <br/>
4. Data shows cyclical outbreaks, peaking every few months, likely influenced by environmental factors and school activities. Efforts should focus on preventing periodic resurgence. <br/>

The scarlet fever data from 2015 to 2025 in mainland China reveals fluctuating seasonal trends, with cases typically peaking during winter and spring. In 2015, case numbers started modestly before increasing significantly, reaching a peak in December. From 2015 to 2019, cases generally escalate each winter, with a notable peak in December 2019 at over 13,000 cases. The pattern suggests that colder months facilitate higher transmission rates. However, in early 2020, there is a significant decline in cases, likely due to public health measures implemented during the COVID-19 pandemic, which could have reduced scarlet fever transmission through improved hygiene and social restrictions.

Post-2020 data shows reinstated seasonal patterns, though with lower peak case numbers. Deaths remain exceptionally low throughout, with only isolated instances in 2020. By 2023 and onwards into 2025, the data indicates a resurgence in cases, although they do not reach the pre-pandemic levels, suggesting lasting impacts of previous interventions or possible new factors influencing transmission and reporting. This data highlights consistent seasonal trends overshadowed briefly by pandemic-related measures, underscoring the effectiveness of public health policies in reducing disease incidence and demonstrating how factors such as environmental conditions and public behavior can influence transmission dynamics.
