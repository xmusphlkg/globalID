# Scarlet fever

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by group A Streptococcus bacteria, the same bacteria responsible for strep throat. It primarily affects children between 5 and 15 years old. The illness is characterized by a sore throat, high fever, and a distinctive red rash that feels like sandpaper. Other symptoms may include a “strawberry” tongue and flushed cheeks. Transmission occurs through respiratory droplets or contact with infected surfaces. Though once a serious childhood illness, scarlet fever is now treatable with antibiotics. Early diagnosis and treatment reduce complications such as rheumatic fever or kidney disease.

## Highlights

- Scarlet fever cases showed a marked decrease during 2020, likely due to the COVID-19 pandemic measures, with cases dropping to as low as 442 in April 2020. <br/>
- Since 2020, there has been a general upward trend in cases, with numbers steadily increasing after pandemic restrictions eased, reaching 6,465 cases in April 2025. <br/>
- The data indicates a seasonal pattern, with peaks typically occurring in the months around late spring and early summer (e.g., May and June). <br/>
- Mortality from Scarlet fever remains low, with only two deaths reported throughout the provided data period, indicating effective treatment and management. <br/>

Throughout 2015–2019, scarlet fever in mainland China demonstrated a strong seasonal pattern, with two distinct annual peaks: one in late spring to early summer (May–June) and another in late autumn to early winter (November–December). Each year saw rising case counts from March, peaking around May or June, followed by a second rise starting in September and peaking again before year-end. Case counts steadily increased year over year, culminating in over 13,000 cases in December 2019. Mortality remained virtually absent, with no reported deaths during this entire five-year span.

In 2020, cases sharply declined due to the impact of COVID-19 pandemic containment measures, with monthly counts dropping below 1000 for most of the year. Although cases began to rebound slowly in 2021–2022, they never returned to pre-pandemic levels. In 2023 and 2024, a notable resurgence is evident, with cases rising progressively and 2024 June surpassing previous highs at nearly 12,400 cases. Nonetheless, the case fatality remains extremely low, recording only 3 deaths across the entire dataset (two in 2020). This trend reflects scarlet fever's generally mild clinical course in recent years and the effectiveness of interventions despite periodic surges.
