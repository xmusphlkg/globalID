# Scarlet fever

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by group A Streptococcus bacteria, which also cause strep throat. It primarily affects children aged 5 to 15 years. The illness is characterized by a sore throat, high fever, and a distinctive red rash that feels like sandpaper, often starting on the chest and spreading. Other symptoms may include a strawberry-like appearance of the tongue, flushed face, and peeling skin. Scarlet fever is transmitted through respiratory droplets. Early diagnosis and antibiotic treatment are important to prevent complications and limit transmission.

## Highlights

- Scarlet fever incidence in mainland China demonstrates strong seasonality, with peaks in late spring and early summer and lower counts in winter and early autumn.<br/>
- After a sharp decrease in cases during 2020–2022 (likely due to COVID-19 interventions), a rebound began in 2023, reaching pre-pandemic levels by 2024.<br/>
- Case numbers in 2025 show a moderate decline from 2024, but remain above early 2020–2022 levels, indicating ongoing transmission.<br/>
- Notably, the disease remains non-lethal; no deaths have been reported since 2020, underscoring effective case management and a mild clinical course.<br/>

Between late 2015 and 2019, scarlet fever cases in mainland China followed a pronounced seasonal pattern, with peaks from May to July and December-January, and troughs in February and August. Annual case numbers rose steadily, reaching monthly counts above 10,000 by 2018 and 2019, particularly in late spring and early summer months; deaths remained negligible, indicating effective case management or low severity. However, in 2020, there was an abrupt and dramatic drop in reported cases across all months, synchronous with the onset of COVID-19 containment measures, which likely disrupted transmission routes and health services: for example, June 2019 had 10,052 cases, while June 2020 reported just 677 cases. Deaths were rare but registered in 2020 for the first time with one each in January and September.

Following the sharp drop, cases gradually increased through 2021 and 2022 but did not reach pre-pandemic levels, suggesting a protracted effect of social interventions or sustained changes in population interactions. By 2023 and especially in 2024, case numbers rebounded, approaching high levels observed in 2018-2019, with the typical bimodal seasonal distribution reestablished and zero deaths recorded throughout, implying that the disease's pattern reverted in response to lifted COVID-19 restrictions. The data highlight a cyclical, seasonally-dependent disease burden susceptible to large but reversible perturbations from public health interventions, with notable resilience and low fatality overall.

Scarlet fever in the Chinese mainland demonstrates a striking discordance between high morbidity and exceptionally low mortality across a full decade. Annual cases consistently climb into the tens of thousands, peaking with substantial seasonal surges in late spring and early winter, yet deaths are virtually absent, with only two fatalities reported — one each in January and September 2020. This translates to a mean annual case fatality rate approximating zero (<0.001%), reinforcing the impression that scarlet fever is overwhelmingly non-lethal in this population and context.

The stability of these data, shown clearly during both pre-pandemic and post-pandemic periods, suggests a robust public health response and likely effective clinical management. The transient uptick in deaths observed in 2020 corresponds temporally with broader disruptions to healthcare delivery during the COVID-19 pandemic, but no enduring change in fatality is evident thereafter. These figures reflect improved antibiotic access, early recognition, and possibly a predominance of less virulent Group A Streptococcus strains. In sum, while scarlet fever remains constantly present and epidemically active, its burden in China is almost entirely limited to morbidity, with mortality kept at nearly negligible levels.
