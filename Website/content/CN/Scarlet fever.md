# Scarlet fever

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Scarlet fever is a contagious infectious disease caused by the bacterium Streptococcus pyogenes, or group A Streptococcus, which also causes strep throat. Primarily affecting children aged 5 to 15, it is characterized by a sudden onset of fever, sore throat, and a bright red rash that feels like sandpaper. The rash typically begins on the chest and spreads to other parts of the body. Other symptoms include a red, swollen tongue known as "strawberry tongue." While antibiotics effectively treat scarlet fever, early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent complications such as rheumatic fever or kidney disease.

## Highlights

- Scarlet fever in the Chinese mainland saw significant fluctuations, with a notable increase from 2015 to 2019 and a marked decline during 2020. <br/>
- The peak incidence was in 2019, with October and December showing elevated case numbers (13,053 cases in December). <br/>
- Post-2019, the cases have been generally lower compared to pre-2020 levels, likely influenced by public health interventions and awareness. <br/>
- As of October 2025, the disease remains monitored with 2,073 cases and no deaths, indicating effective control measures are still in place, despite a minor resurgence compared to 2023. <br/>

The scarlet fever data from mainland China between October 2015 and October 2025 shows distinct seasonal trends with varying case numbers. Typically, there is a rise in cases during late-year months, with spikes often occurring in November and December. For instance, high case counts were recorded in December 2019, reaching 13,053. A secondary increase is regularly observed from May to July, hinting at potential environmental or social factors influencing disease spread.

A notable deviation occurs in 2020, where cases significantly plummeted, likely due to stringent COVID-19 public health measures. From March to August 2020, monthly cases dropped from thousands to the lower hundreds, starkly contrasting with previous years' data. This year's decrease reflects the impact of pandemic restrictions on the spread of other infectious diseases. Following 2020, case numbers gradually increase, especially by mid-2023, although they remain lower than pre-pandemic levels, suggesting lasting effects of behavioral changes or interventions induced by the pandemic. 

Throughout this period, fatalities were remarkably low, with only 2020 recording two deaths, indicating a slight increase in disease severity during the pandemic phase. This historical data underscores the importance of continued public health efforts and timely interventions during peak periods to manage scarlet fever effectively, leveraging insights for proactive resource planning.

The lack of mortality despite relatively high case counts suggests that Scarlet fever in the Chinese mainland during these years remained largely non-lethal, perhaps due to effective public health interventions, antibiotics usage, or natural immunity in the population.
