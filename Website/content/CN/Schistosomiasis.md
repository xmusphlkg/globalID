# Schistosomiasis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. This disease primarily affects people in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in areas with limited access to clean water and adequate sanitation. Schistosomiasis is transmitted through contact with freshwater containing larval forms of the parasite, released by infected freshwater snails. Once inside the human body, the larvae mature into adult worms and reside in blood vessels, leading to various health issues such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, and liver and spleen damage. It is a major public health concern, impacting millions globally.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a significant decline from 2015 to 2025, with cases dropping from over 10,000 to single digits by 2025. <br/>
- The reduction in cases has been consistent since 2018, indicating successful disease control measures over the years. <br/>
- Mortality from schistosomiasis remains extremely low, with only a single documented death recorded in February 2020, showcasing effective healthcare interventions. <br/>
- While eradication has not yet been achieved, sustained low case counts suggest that schistosomiasis in the region is now under strong control. <br/>

The data on schistosomiasis cases in Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals a significant downward trend. In 2015, the number of cases was high, peaking in November with 10,481 cases, which aligns with the transmission season when human contact with contaminated water is increased. The subsequent years, particularly from 2016 onwards, show a rapid decline in case numbers. By 2017, the monthly cases had significantly dropped, with only 39 cases reported in January. This suggests successful control measures or environmental changes affecting transmission.

From 2018 onwards, the cases decline even further, often dropping to single-digit numbers monthly. This dramatic decrease may be due to intensified interventions like improved sanitation, access to clean water, snail control, and better diagnosis and treatment protocols. Notably, the data records no deaths from the disease, except one reported in February 2020, indicating effective disease management once cases occur. By 2025, the numbers remain exceedingly low, demonstrating sustained success in public health efforts.

Overall, the continued reduction reflects significant progress in schistosomiasis control, highlighting successful public health strategies and enhanced living conditions that have reduced exposure risks. The data represents an encouraging trend toward potentially eliminating the disease as a public health concern in the region.
