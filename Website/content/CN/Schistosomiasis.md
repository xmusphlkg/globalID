# Schistosomiasis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, especially in sub-Saharan Africa. Infection occurs through contact with freshwater contaminated by larvae released from infected freshwater snails. Once inside the human body, the parasites mature and lay eggs, leading to symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, bloody stool, or hematuria. Chronic infection can result in liver, kidney, bladder, or intestinal damage. Schistosomiasis is both a public health concern and a socioeconomic burden due to its impact on individual health and community productivity.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases have significantly declined since a peak in 2015, from over 10,000 cases in November 2015 to only 3 cases by August 2024. <br/>
- There has been a marked reduction in disease incidence since 2017, with fewer than 100 cases per year from 2018 onwards. <br/>
- Mortality from Schistosomiasis remains extremely low, with only one reported death in February 2020 throughout the entire period. <br/>
- The most recent data from August 2024 shows minimal transmission, reflecting ongoing control efforts and the near-elimination of Schistosomiasis in mainland China. <br/>

The reported data on schistosomiasis cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 demonstrates significant fluctuations in case numbers, with distinct patterns of seasonal and year-to-year variability. A notable peak occurred in 2015, particularly from May to November, where cases surged dramatically, reaching the highest point in November with 10,481 cases. This spike contrasts with the preceding and following years, suggesting an outbreak or other contributing environmental or social factors. By 2016, the case numbers had significantly decreased but still experienced some degree of seasonality, especially between September and November.

From 2017 onward, the overall number of cases continued to decline steadily, with occasional smaller peaks, such as those observed in September and October of 2017 and again in December 2018. By 2020, the number of cases had reached single digits in most months, except for sporadic small increases, such as in February 2020, when a death was also reported. After 2020, the annual case counts remained consistently low, suggesting effective control measures or environmental changes contributing to the reduction in transmission, with the last significant uptick occurring in December 2022.

From 2015 to 2017, there were surges in the number of cases, peaking at over 10,000 in November 2015, but none of these spikes corresponded to an increase in deaths. The persistent absence of deaths, alongside the downward trend in reported cases, reflects both improved disease management and possible advancements in treatment or public health measures, significantly reducing the risk of severe complications or mortality associated with schistosomiasis in China.
