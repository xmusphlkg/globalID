# Schistosomiasis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in Africa, Asia, and South America. The disease is transmitted through contact with freshwater contaminated by the larvae released from infected snails. Once inside the human body, the parasites mature and reproduce, causing symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, and blood in the urine or stool. Chronic infection can lead to severe organ damage. Control measures include snail control, improved sanitation, and mass drug administration with praziquantel.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Schistosomiasis cases in the Chinese mainland have dramatically decreased from thousands in 2015 to single digits by 2025, indicating successful control measures. <br/>

- **Consistent Zero Deaths:** Throughout the years, the number of deaths has remained negligible, with only one death reported in February 2020, highlighting effective treatment and management strategies. <br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Initial data shows higher cases during warmer months, but this trend diminishes over time, suggesting improved environmental and public health interventions. <br/>

- **Sustained Low Incidence:** By 2025, the disease incidence is consistently low, with only sporadic cases, reflecting sustained public health efforts and possibly nearing elimination. <br/>

The data on Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows a significant decline in reported cases over the years. In 2015, the number of cases was notably high, peaking in November with 10,481 cases. This high incidence could be attributed to various factors, including increased transmission during the warmer months when the snail hosts are more active, or possibly improved detection and reporting mechanisms. However, from 2016 onwards, there is a marked decrease in the number of cases, with a significant drop observed by 2017. This trend suggests effective public health interventions, such as improved sanitation, snail control measures, and mass drug administration, which are known strategies for controlling Schistosomiasis.

By 2020, the number of cases had reduced dramatically, with only a few cases reported each month, and a single death recorded in February 2020. This decline continued through 2025, with some months reporting zero cases. The sustained low incidence of Schistosomiasis in recent years indicates successful long-term control efforts and possibly a move towards elimination in certain regions. The data reflects the impact of continuous public health efforts and possibly changes in environmental factors that affect the lifecycle of the parasite and its snail hosts. However, the persistence of even low numbers of cases highlights the need for ongoing surveillance and control measures to prevent resurgence.
