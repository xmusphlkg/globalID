# Schistosomiasis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, commonly known as Bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects the urinary and intestinal tracts. Humans become infected through contact with contaminated freshwater inhabited by snails that serve as intermediate hosts. The parasites burrow into the skin, migrating through the body to mature in the blood vessels. Symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, blood in the urine, and, in chronic cases, can lead to liver, kidney, or bladder damage. Schistosomiasis remains prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, and its control is focused on improved sanitation, snail control, and mass drug administration.

## Highlights

- Significant reduction in cases: Schistosomiasis incidences decreased dramatically from thousands in 2015 to single digits by 2025, indicating successful disease control measures. <br/>
- Seasonality impact diminished: Initial patterns showed seasonal spikes, especially in autumn; however, recent data shows consistent low numbers, suggesting effective intervention. <br/>
- Mortality remains low: Despite a high number of cases in earlier years, deaths have been rare, with only one recorded death in 2020. <br/>
- Continued vigilance needed: Although current cases are minimal, sustained monitoring and preventive strategies are essential to prevent reemergence. <br/>

The data presented shows a noticeable decline in Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China over the span of a decade. Initially, the reported cases are high, with significant peaks observed in 2015 and 2016, particularly in months such as October and November. This pattern reflects traditional epidemiological observations where Schistosomiasis transmission rates are higher in post-monsoon periods due to increased water activities in endemic areas. The subsequent years demonstrate a dramatic decrease in case numbers, with consistent reductions year over year. By 2019, monthly case figures resemble sporadic incidents rather than endemic transmission, a testament to likely successful disease control interventions.

Notably, the persistence of some cases and the lone fatality recorded in February 2020 suggests challenges in completely eliminating the disease. This single death underlines the importance of continuous surveillance and health interventions to manage and prevent the spread of Schistosomiasis. From 2021 onwards, cases remain extremely low, indicating effective control mechanismsâ€”potentially a combination of improved sanitation, access to health care, widespread use of anti-schistosomal drugs, and perhaps ecological changes affecting snail habitats. The data from recent years, showing mostly single-digit monthly cases, portray a public health success in drastically reducing Schistosomiasis incidence and potentially moving towards its eradication.
