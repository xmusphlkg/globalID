# Schistosomiasis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode flatworms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and South America. Humans acquire the infection through contact with freshwater contaminated with larval forms released by freshwater snails, the disease's intermediate host. The parasites penetrate the skin and develop into adult worms living in the blood vessels. Symptoms range from mild, such as a rash and itchy skin, to severe, including liver damage, kidney failure, infertility, and bladder cancer, depending on the species.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** There has been a substantial reduction in schistosomiasis cases from thousands in 2015 to single-digit figures by 2024. <br/>
- **No Recent Deaths:** Since 2020, there has been only one reported death (February 2020), indicating improved management and treatment. <br/>
- **Stabilization of Incidence:** From 2020 onwards, the incidence rate has remained consistently low, with most months reporting fewer than 10 cases. <br/>
- **Effective Control Measures:** The continuous decline and low case numbers reflect successful long-term control measures in the region. <br/>

Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China exhibit significant variability over the analyzed period from 2014 to mid-2024. In 2015, there was a notable surge in cases, peaking at 10,481 in November, which suggests an intensified transmission period possibly linked to environmental or socio-economic factors conducive to the disease's spread. This spike contrasts sharply with much lower case numbers reported in subsequent years, particularly after 2017, where the incidence of the disease shows a consistent decline. The sharp increase in 2015 and early 2016 may indicate a particular outbreak or changes in surveillance and reporting systems, while the steady decline in cases in the later years suggests effective control measures or a natural reduction in transmission intensity.

Post-2016, the disease appears to have largely diminished in prevalence, with annual peaks much lower than in previous years, culminating in very low case numbers from 2018 onwards. Deaths remained rare, with only one recorded death in February 2020. This overall trend likely reflects the success of public health interventions and control strategies, such as snail control, mass drug administration, and improvements in water and sanitation, which have led to a significant reduction in the burden of Schistosomiasis in China. However, the small number of cases persisting into 2024 suggests that while the disease is largely controlled, it has not been entirely eradicated.
