# Schistosomiasis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus *Schistosoma*. It primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in areas with poor sanitation. Infection occurs when skin comes into contact with freshwater infested with larval forms released by freshwater snails, the diseaseâ€™s intermediate host. Once inside the body, the parasites mature and reproduce in the blood vessels, leading to inflammation and organ damage. Schistosomiasis can cause chronic illness, impairing growth and cognitive development in children. Control strategies include mass drug administration, improved sanitation, snail control, and public health education.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China have dramatically declined from thousands per month in 2015 to single digits by early 2025, indicating significant disease control success. <br/>
- The data reveals the highest incidence of cases during late summer and autumn months up to 2016, but case numbers have stabilized at a minimal level throughout the year since 2020. <br/>
- Deaths from Schistosomiasis are virtually non-existent, with only one recorded death in February 2020, showcasing effective treatment and management. <br/>
- The trend indicates China has made substantial progress toward controlling Schistosomiasis, achieving near-elimination levels by March 2025. <br/>

The data for Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China from 2015 to the early part of 2025 shows a significant decline in the number of reported cases over the ten-year period. In 2015, there were high incidences, particularly noticeable from May through November, with monthly cases often exceeding 2000 and peaking in October and November with cases well above 9000 and 10,000, respectively. However, after 2015, there is a clear reduction in reported cases. For instance, 2016 saw a substantial decrease with most months reporting fewer than 1000 cases. By 2017, the numbers dropped even further, maintaining generally below 200 cases monthly except for occasional spikes in September and October.

From 2018 onwards, the declining trend continued prominently, with most months recording fewer than 100 cases. Noteworthy is a significant drop from dozens to single digits in later years, reaching the lowest overall figures around early 2020 and beyond. The year 2020 is particularly notable as it shows an abrupt fall with most months recording fewer than ten cases, and in February, the first reported death appears in the data. Subsequent years maintained minimal case counts, highlighting a persistent reduction, likely due to effective public health interventions and control measures. By 2024 and early 2025, monthly reported cases rarely exceeded five, with several months showing zero cases, indicating sustained control and perhaps nearing elimination of Schistosomiasis in the region.
