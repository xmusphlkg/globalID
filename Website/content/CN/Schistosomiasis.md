# Schistosomiasis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects populations in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in Africa, Asia, and South America. The disease is transmitted through contact with freshwater contaminated by the larvae released from infected snails. Symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, and blood in the urine or stool. Chronic infection can lead to liver damage, kidney failure, infertility, or bladder cancer. Control measures focus on reducing snail populations, improving sanitation, and administering praziquantel, an effective antiparasitic medication.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Schistosomiasis cases in the Chinese mainland have dramatically decreased from thousands in 2015 to nearly zero by January 2025, indicating successful control measures. <br/>
- **Consistent Zero Deaths:** Throughout the years, the number of deaths has remained consistently low, with only one death reported in February 2020, suggesting effective treatment and management. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Initial data shows seasonal peaks, particularly in the latter months of 2015, but these fluctuations have diminished over time, reflecting improved disease control strategies. <br/>
- **Sustained Low Incidence:** Since 2020, the incidence of schistosomiasis has remained low, with most months reporting single-digit cases, highlighting the effectiveness of ongoing public health interventions. <br/>

The data on Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals a significant decline in reported cases over the years. In 2015, the number of cases was notably high, peaking in October and November with 9,338 and 10,481 cases, respectively. This period marks the highest incidence within the dataset, suggesting a possible outbreak or heightened transmission during these months. However, from 2016 onwards, there is a marked decrease in the number of cases, with a substantial drop observed by 2017. By 2018, the monthly cases had reduced to double digits, and this trend of low incidence continued in subsequent years.

The data indicates a successful control and reduction of Schistosomiasis in the region, likely due to effective public health interventions, improved sanitation, and possibly changes in environmental management that reduced the habitat for the snail hosts. By 2020, the number of cases had decreased to single digits per month, with only one death reported in February 2020. This trend of low incidence persists through to 2025, with several months reporting zero cases. The consistent low numbers in recent years suggest that Schistosomiasis is no longer a major public health concern in mainland China, reflecting the success of sustained control measures and possibly indicating a move towards elimination.

This consistent decline in cases and the absence of deaths in recent years could be attributed to successful public health interventions, improved sanitation, and effective disease control measures.
