# Schistosomiasis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis is a parasitic disease caused by Schistosoma worms, commonly affecting populations in tropical and subtropical regions. The infection occurs when skin comes into contact with contaminated freshwater, where larvae from infected snails penetrate the skin. The adult worms reside in blood vessels, causing damage to organs such as the liver, intestines, and bladder. Symptoms can include abdominal pain, diarrhea, blood in urine, and liver damage. Chronic infection may lead to severe complications, including fibrosis and cancer. Treatment typically involves praziquantel, which targets the adult worms. Prevention focuses on improving sanitation and controlling snail populations.

## Highlights

1. **Significant Decline in Cases**: Schistosomiasis cases in China have drastically reduced over the past decade, with monthly cases often in the single digits since 2020. The last report for November 2024 shows zero cases. <br/>

2. **No Deaths Reported**: No fatalities from Schistosomiasis have been recorded since 2014, indicating effective control measures. <br/>

3. **Epidemic Peaks in 2015**: The disease reached its peak in 2015, with monthly cases surpassing 10,000, but has since seen a sharp decline. <br/>

4. **Continued Surveillance**: Despite the decline, small outbreaks continue to be monitored, as evidenced by sporadic cases in the last few years. <br/>

The reported data for schistosomiasis in China over the past decade shows a significant decline in both cases and deaths, with no fatalities reported since 2014. The peak in cases occurred in 2015, with notable spikes between May and November, which may reflect seasonal factors such as increased contact with contaminated water during warmer months. The largest surge, in September and October 2015, coincides with the monsoon season in many regions of China, which is known to exacerbate the spread of waterborne diseases like schistosomiasis. These patterns suggest that environmental and seasonal conditions may have played a critical role in the transmission dynamics of the disease.

After 2015, there was a steady reduction in the number of reported cases, with especially low numbers observed from 2019 onwards. The decline could be attributed to improved public health interventions, including mass treatment programs, enhanced sanitation, and better control of snail vectors. Despite this improvement, sporadic cases were reported, especially in the summer and early autumn months, suggesting that some regions may still experience localized outbreaks. The ongoing occurrence of cases, even at lower levels, highlights the persistence of the disease in certain endemic areas, despite the overall downward trend. The absence of deaths in the more recent years points to effective medical treatment and the broader success of control efforts.

Despite fluctuations in the number of cases, the absence of mortality in the dataset suggests that either effective medical interventions, timely diagnoses, or reporting practices are preventing fatalities. From 2015 to 2023, the number of reported cases varied widely, peaking at 10,481 in November 2015, yet no deaths were attributed to the disease during these years. The only exception comes in February 2020, when a single death was recorded, though it was an isolated incident within an otherwise death-free period.

Notably, the number of cases showed a marked decline in the latter part of the data set, particularly from 2018 onward, where case numbers dropped significantly. This trend could indicate improvements in public health initiatives, control measures, or a shift in the environmental and socio-economic factors that influence the spread of Schistosomiasis. The consistent absence of reported deaths despite periodic surges in cases points to the strength of China's healthcare system in managing and mitigating the effects of the disease. However, the fact that deaths are not reported, even with significant fluctuations in case numbers, raises questions about potential underreporting or misclassification, though the lack of any fatalities in the data could also indicate successful eradication efforts.
