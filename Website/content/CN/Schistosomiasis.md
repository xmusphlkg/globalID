# Schistosomiasis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by blood flukes of the genus *Schistosoma*. It is transmitted through contact with freshwater contaminated by larval forms released from infected snails. The parasites penetrate human skin, migrate through the body, and mature in blood vessels. Major species affecting humans include *S. haematobium*, *S. mansoni*, and *S. japonicum*. The disease can cause chronic illness affecting the urinary tract or intestines, with symptoms like abdominal pain, diarrhea, and hematuria. Schistosomiasis primarily affects sub-Saharan Africa, South America, the Middle East, and parts of Asia. Control relies on preventive chemotherapy and sanitation.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in China have drastically declined from a monthly peak of over 10,000 in 2015 to just 1 case in April 2025, indicating sustained epidemic control.<br/>
- Mortality remains exceptionally low, with only one documented death across the entire reporting period (February 2020), reflecting strong case management.<br/>
- Between 2023 and 2025, monthly incidence remained in single digits, highlighting a transition from disease reduction to near elimination status.<br/>
- Seasonal fluctuations present in early years have disappeared, signaling successful interruption of transmission and improved public health interventions.<br/>

Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China have shown a marked and sustained decline from 2015 through 2025. In 2015, monthly reported cases ranged from over 1,000 to nearly 10,500, peaking in the autumn months—consistent with transmission patterns affected by seasonal agriculture and freshwater exposure. By 2016, cases dropped significantly, reflecting an initial sharp reduction that was sustained and even improved upon in subsequent years. From 2017 onward, annual totals continued to diminish, with the most dramatic decreases seen between 2018 and 2020. Notably, from 2021 through 2025, monthly case counts were consistently in the single digits, with many months reporting zero cases. The lone recorded death during this decade occurred in February 2020.

This downward trend suggests highly effective public health interventions, possibly including mass drug administration, snail control, health education, and improved sanitation. The complete absence of seasonal spikes after 2019 indicates interruption of local transmission or more robust control strategies. The consistency in zero death counts year-over-year (except 2020) further reflects both successful early case detection and effective clinical management. Recent data support the notion that Schistosomiasis is nearing elimination as a public health concern in China.

This extraordinary low mortality despite high case counts in early years implies effective clinical management and a likely predominance of non-severe infections. From 2018 onward, case numbers dropped steeply from thousands to merely dozens annually, marking a significant trend in disease control and surveillance, possibly due to intensified public health interventions, snail vector control, mass drug administration, or infrastructure improvements.

The presence of just one death over a decade indicates a shift of schistosomiasis from a life-threatening condition to a controllable public health issue within this region. Furthermore, the absence of seasonal mortality patterns, despite seasonally variable incidence—a phenomenon visible in higher numbers during the summer and early autumn months in initial years—reinforces the conclusion that case detection and treatment have been both timely and effective. The singular death, occurring in early 2020, could represent an outlier in clinical progression, compounded comorbidities, or delayed diagnosis, rather than an endemic trend.
