# Schistosomiasis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a tropical disease caused by parasitic worms of the genus Schistosoma. It affects over 240 million people worldwide, primarily in sub-Saharan Africa, South America, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. The infection is acquired through contact with contaminated freshwater harboring snails that release larvae. Symptoms range from rashes and fever to chronic issues such as liver and kidney damage. Control strategies include mass drug administration of praziquantel, snail control, improved sanitation, and health education. Schistosomiasis poses significant public health challenges and impedes socioeconomic development in endemic regions.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China have dramatically decreased since the peak in 2015, when over 10,000 cases were reported in November. By September 2024, no cases were recorded.<br/>
- The disease trend shows a steady decline after 2016, with monthly cases dropping below 10 by 2020 and remaining consistently low in subsequent years.<br/>
- No deaths were reported in most years except for one fatality in February 2020.<br/>
- Recent data for 2023-2024 indicates sporadic cases, with a total of fewer than 10 cases reported monthly, highlighting strong control efforts in place.<br/>

The data on schistosomiasis cases in the Chinese mainland from September 2014 to September 2024 shows significant fluctuations in reported cases, with a notable spike in 2015 and a general decline in later years. In 2015, the number of cases increased sharply, peaking in November with 10,481 cases. This surge could be attributed to specific environmental or social factors, such as favorable transmission conditions or increased exposure due to occupational activities. Conversely, the absence of reported deaths throughout the entire period suggests that intervention measures, such as effective treatment protocols and health interventions, were successful in managing the mortality associated with the infection.

Moving into subsequent years, a marked decrease in cases is observed. By 2017, monthly cases had reduced significantly, and this downward trend continued through to 2024, often with cases staying in single digits. This declining incidence may reflect the successful implementation of targeted control strategies, including public health initiatives, enhanced sanitation, and educational campaigns. Moreover, the presence of a single death in February 2020 stands out, representing an outlier within the dataset. Constant low levels of case numbers in later years indicate effective disease management and potential achievement of control or near-elimination status. These trends underscore the importance of sustained public health efforts and vigilant monitoring to prevent resurgence.

This could suggest widespread access to effective treatment or successful preventive measures that control severe disease progression. The notably low case counts in the later years could also be an outcome of sustained public health efforts or possibly underreporting, but with no corresponding increase in mortality, these data patterns largely reflect well-managed schistosomiasis scenarios over this decade.
