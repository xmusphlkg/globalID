# Schistosomiasis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects individuals in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in areas with poor sanitation. The disease is transmitted through contact with freshwater contaminated by the larvae released from infected snails. Once inside the human body, the parasites mature and reproduce, leading to symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, and blood in the urine or stool. Chronic infection can cause severe organ damage. Control efforts focus on improving sanitation, snail control, and mass drug administration with praziquantel.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Schistosomiasis cases in the Chinese mainland have dramatically decreased from a peak of 10,481 cases in November 2015 to consistently low single-digit monthly cases by 2024. <br/>
- **Zero Deaths Since 2020:** There have been no reported deaths from Schistosomiasis since February 2020, indicating effective disease management and treatment. <br/>
- **Sporadic Cases in Recent Years:** From 2021 onwards, the number of cases has remained minimal, with several months reporting zero cases, suggesting successful control measures. <br/>
- **Sustained Control Efforts:** The data reflects sustained public health efforts and possibly improved sanitation and healthcare access, contributing to the near elimination of the disease by December 2024. <br/>

The data on Schistosomiasis in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows a significant decline in cases. In 2015, there was a peak, with October and November reporting the highest numbers at 9,338 and 10,481 cases, respectively. This surge might be due to environmental factors or increased detection efforts. However, from 2016 onwards, there is a marked decrease, indicating effective control measures and public health interventions.

By 2018, the number of cases had dropped significantly, with most months reporting fewer than 30 cases. This trend continues, with 2020 and beyond showing consistently low numbers, often in single digits. Notably, there is a single death reported in February 2020, an anomaly in the dataset, as no other deaths are recorded throughout the period.

The sustained low incidence of cases in recent years suggests that Schistosomiasis is being effectively managed in mainland China. This success is likely due to improved sanitation, health education, and targeted treatment programs. The data reflects a successful public health strategy in controlling and potentially moving towards the elimination of Schistosomiasis as a public health concern in the region. Overall, the trend indicates a positive trajectory in the fight against Schistosomiasis, with significant progress made over the decade.
