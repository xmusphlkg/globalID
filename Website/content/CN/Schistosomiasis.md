# Schistosomiasis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects people in tropical and subtropical regions, especially in communities lacking access to clean water and adequate sanitation. The disease is transmitted when individuals come into contact with contaminated freshwater containing snails that release infectious larvae. These larvae penetrate human skin, mature into adult worms, and produce eggs, causing damage to organs such as the liver, intestines, bladder, and, in some cases, the central nervous system. Symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, anemia, and in severe cases, organ damage.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China have declined sharply from over 10,000 monthly cases in late 2015 to single digits each month by 2024–2025. <br/>
- Zero fatalities have been recorded every year except for one death in February 2020, indicating effective disease management and treatment. <br/>
- Seasonal peaks observed in autumn months (especially September–November) during early years have nearly disappeared by 2021 onwards. <br/>
- As of May 2025, only one new case was reported, reflecting sustained control efforts and the near-elimination of local transmission. <br/>

The data from 2015 to 2025 reflect a dramatic and sustained decline in reported Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China. In 2015 alone, cases exceeded 10,000 in several months—peaking in November at 10,481—with all months showing substantial case numbers. However, by 2016, cases were significantly reduced, particularly noticeable from January to April, which reported monthly figures below 300. The downward trend progressed over the years, with 2017 showing marked seasonality—spiking in September and October—before collapsing to double-digit numbers throughout 2018. By the end of 2019, annual cases had dropped to fewer than 200, with only minor peaks and most months reporting single-digit cases.

From 2020 onwards, transmission appears to be near-eliminated, with consistently low counts. The only reported death in the entire dataset occurs in February 2020. Between 2021 and 2025, monthly totals generally remain below 10 and often hover around 0–3, suggesting that Schistosomiasis has been brought close to elimination levels. This intense reduction shows a sustained and likely systematic public health response, with no resurgence evident over the last eight years. Seasonality seen in earlier years (such as peaks during the fall months) has disappeared entirely, further indicating successful control efforts.
