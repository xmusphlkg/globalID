# Schistosomiasis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis is a parasitic disease caused by blood flukes (trematodes) of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa. The infection occurs when people come into contact with freshwater contaminated with larval forms of the parasite, which penetrate the skin. Schistosomiasis can cause a range of symptoms, from mild itching and rash to more severe issues like liver damage, kidney failure, and bladder cancer. It is a major public health concern, affecting over 200 million people worldwide.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in the Chinese mainland peaked in 2015, with a maximum of 10,481 cases reported in November.<br/>
- A significant decline in cases occurred from 2016 onwards, with numbers falling below 100 cases per month from 2017 onwards.<br/>
- Deaths were rare, with only one recorded in February 2020 throughout the entire period.<br/>
- By June 2024, the disease prevalence had decreased drastically, with cases often being in the single digits monthly, reflecting effective control measures and public health interventions.<br/>

The reported data for Schistosomiasis in mainland China shows notable fluctuations in the number of cases over the years. In 2014, the number of cases started moderately with the highest at 463 cases per month, then declined towards the end of the year. A significant surge in cases was observed in 2015, peaking in October with 9,338 cases and November with 10,481 cases, indicating a potential outbreak or heightened reporting and detection efforts. The numbers began to decrease sharply in December 2015 and continued on a downward trend through 2016, though there were occasional spikes, such as in October 2016 with 2,135 cases.

From 2017 onwards, the number of cases reduced substantially, reflecting possible improvements in disease control measures. By 2019, the monthly reported cases generally remained below 50, except for occasional months like September 2017 with 1,166 cases. The trend of low case numbers continued into 2020 and beyond, with the majority of months reporting single-digit cases. This sustained low incidence suggests that the measures to control Schistosomiasis have been largely effective, though the single reported death in February 2020 highlights that vigilance and sustained efforts are necessary to maintain control and prevent resurgence.
