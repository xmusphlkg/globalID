# Schistosomiasis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode flatworms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, notably in Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and South America. The disease spreads through contact with freshwater contaminated by the larval forms released by freshwater snails. Common symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, bloody stool, and blood in the urine. Chronic infection can lead to significant organ damage, particularly affecting the liver, intestines, lungs, and bladder. Effective control relies on mass drug administration with praziquantel and improved sanitation and snail control.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Cases:** Schistosomiasis cases have drastically declined from over 9,000 monthly cases in 2015 to single-digit figures by 2025, indicating successful control efforts. <br/>
- **Zero Deaths:** The data consistently shows zero deaths from Schistosomiasis, highlighting effective treatment and management strategies. <br/>
- **Stable Low Numbers:** Since 2024, case numbers remain minimal, showing successful ongoing surveillance and prevention measures. <br/>
- **Recent Improvements:** The year 2025 maintains low case numbers, suggesting continued success in reducing the disease's presence in the Chinese mainland. <br/>

Initial figures from late 2015 and early 2016 indicate a significant occurrence with thousands of cases reported monthly, particularly peaking at 10,481 cases in November 2015. The cases steadily decreased in subsequent years, marking a significant public health achievement. Notably, there was a pronounced decline in reported cases by 2017, with numbers dropping to mere double digits in most months from 2018 onward. By 2025, the data suggests a stable control of the disease, with reported cases consistently staying in single digits or even zero, indicating sustained efforts in disease management, public health education, and perhaps effective treatment and prevention strategies.

Throughout this period, mortality rates remained notably low across all years, with only a single fatality recorded in February 2020. This could reflect improvements in medical care, early diagnosis, and effective treatment methods, contributing to successful disease management. The overarching trend in the data underscores a significant public health success, showcasing how sustained interventions can lead to the near-eradication of a once prevalent disease. The drastic reduction and stabilization at near-zero levels from 2021 onwards highlight the effectiveness of China's healthcare policies and programs aimed at controlling schistosomiasis.

This indicates a near-complete reduction in the mortality threat of the disease within this period, likely due to improved control measures, timely diagnosis, and effective treatment strategies. Schistosomiasis, historically a significant public health concern, seems to have been managed effectively to prevent fatalities.

Despite this success in terms of mortality, the data demonstrates varied incidence rates over the years, with notable periodic peaks, particularly from late 2015 to 2018, where case numbers ranged from hundreds to thousands annually. However, a significant drop in cases is observed post-2018, maintaining mostly single-digit monthly counts through to 2025. The persistent yet markedly low incidence of new cases suggests sustained control measures, although the few recurring new cases indicate a potential for endemicity in certain areas. Continuous monitoring and targeted interventions in affected regions remain essential to prevent any resurgence and maintain the reduced mortality occurrences.
