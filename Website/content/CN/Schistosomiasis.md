# Schistosomiasis

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It is transmitted when people come into contact with freshwater contaminated by larval forms released from infected snails. The disease is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly sub-Saharan Africa. Acute and chronic manifestations include fever, abdominal pain, blood in urine or stool, and organ damage. Diagnosis relies on detecting eggs in stool or urine samples. Treatment primarily involves praziquantel. Schistosomiasis affects millions globally and poses significant public health challenges in endemic areas.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in Chinese mainland have declined dramatically since 2015, with annual cases dropping from thousands to single digits by 2025.<br/>
- No deaths have been reported since 2020, highlighting significant improvements in disease control and management.<br/>
- Seasonal spikes seen prior to 2018 have disappeared, with sporadic, very low case numbers distributed evenly throughout recent years.<br/>
- As of December 2025, schistosomiasis is close to elimination with only three cases reported in the final month, reflecting sustained epidemiological gains and effective public health interventions.<br/>

Schistosomiasis in the Chinese mainland demonstrated a pronounced and rapid decline in reported cases from late 2015 through mid-2025. The data trace a dramatic reduction in transmission: from the thousands of cases recorded monthly in 2015â€“2017, most notably with seasonal spikes in the autumn (e.g., October 2017 with 3,144 cases and September 2017 with 1,166 cases), to single-digit monthly notifications from 2020 onward. This pattern suggests substantial epidemiological control, possibly reflecting the compounded impact of public health interventions such as snail habitat management, health education, chemotherapy campaigns, and improved village surveillance. The absence of deaths until a solitary fatality in February 2020 further attests to the effectiveness of integrated control measures and access to prompt diagnostics and treatment.

Peak periods within each year prior to 2020 consistently clustered in the post-monsoon months, mirroring the lifecycle dynamics of Schistosoma japonicum and its intermediate snail host. After December 2018, reporting was dramatically reduced and year-round transmission patterns were broken, as evidenced by sporadic and low-level monthly case counts with no notable seasonal amplification. The transition to near-elimination status by the 2020s, supported by persistent zero- or single-case incidence records extending into 2025, reflects ongoing vigilance and the sustainability of control strategies. This uninterrupted epidemiological suppression, with only rare sporadic case notifications, suggests that remaining cases may be imported, relic infections, or anomalies rather than indicators of active local transmission.

Between December 2015 and December 2025, reported deaths due to schistosomiasis in Chinese mainland are virtually nonexistent, with the single exception of one death in February 2020. Across more than a decade and thousands of diagnosed cases, the data reveal a striking decline in both incidence and fatality. In earlier periods, particularly from 2015 to 2018, the case count fluctuates in the hundreds to thousands per month, yet there are no deaths reported, indicating that even when schistosomiasis was more prevalent, mortality was likely mitigated by effective diagnosis, treatment, or possibly reporting practices. From 2019 onward, both incidence and mortality drop sharply, with most months showing single-digit cases and only the anomalous death in early 2020 disrupting an otherwise consistent zero-death profile.

This near-zero mortality over a sustained period underscores successful control measures in public health, improvements in water safety, and access to praziquantel or other therapies for affected regions. The solitary death in 2020 stands out against a decade of no reported fatalities and may reflect either exceptional clinical circumstances, rare complications, or possible changes in surveillance or reporting for that single month. Continuously low death rates, even as cases persisted at a lower baseline, suggest that schistosomiasis is no longer a major cause of mortality in China, and that ongoing vigilance in clinical management and public health initiatives have effectively prevented fatal outcomes in recent years.
