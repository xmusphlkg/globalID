# Schistosomiasis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharziasis, is a chronic disease caused by parasitic worms of the genus Schistosoma, prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions. The parasites are transmitted through contact with contaminated freshwater where intermediate host snails release infectious larvae. When these larvae penetrate human skin, they develop into adult worms residing in blood vessels, causing damage to organs such as the liver, bladder, or intestines. Symptoms can include abdominal pain, diarrhea, and hematuria, with long-term exposure leading to severe health complications. Schistosomiasis is a major public health concern, affecting millions globally, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in mainland China have significantly declined since 2015, when cases peaked, showing successful disease control efforts over the years. <br/>
- Cases dropped from thousands in 2015 to single digits in recent years, with zero deaths since 2021, indicating effective management and treatment of the disease. <br/>
- Seasonal trends show higher case numbers in late summer and early autumn, pointing toward environmental factors influencing transmission during these months. <br/>
- Importantly, the trend continues with very few cases in 2025, suggesting persistent control and monitoring, aiming for eventual schistosomiasis elimination. <br/>

In 2015, cases were notably high, peaking at 10,481 in November. This height reflects a period where substantial efforts, including snail eradication campaigns and increased community awareness, were underway to curb transmission. By 2016, there was a noticeable drop, with the lowest cases reported in August, suggesting these interventions were having a positive impact.

The downward trend continued significantly into 2017 and onwards, with brief upticks in certain months possibly due to the disease's seasonal nature or lingering transmission areas. From 2019 onwards, monthly reports indicate single-digit cases, showcasing the results of effective disease monitoring and management alongside improved sanitation and preventive measures. The solitary death reported in February 2020 could be an anomaly or highlight localized transmission challenges.

From 2020 to 2025, Schistosomiasis cases remain controlled, oscillating within single digits, underscoring the success of sustained control strategies. Overall, the data illustrates effective long-term disease management by health authorities in China, demonstrating the power of consistent public health efforts and their significant impact on reducing infectious disease prevalence.
