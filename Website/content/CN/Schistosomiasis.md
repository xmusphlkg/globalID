# Schistosomiasis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It primarily affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and South America. The parasites' lifecycle involves freshwater snails as intermediate hosts, and humans become infected through skin contact with contaminated water. Symptoms can range from mild, like itching and rash, to severe, including abdominal pain, diarrhea, liver damage, and even infertility. Schistosomiasis predominantly affects agricultural and fishing communities, and control strategies focus on public health education, snail control, and the administration of praziquantel, an effective antiparasitic medication.

## Highlights

- Schistosomiasis cases in China have significantly declined from peaks in 2015 and 2016, when monthly cases reached over 10,000, to consistently low numbers in recent years, often fewer than 10 per month. <br/>
  
- The decrease in cases reflects effective control and prevention efforts, with most recent months of 2024 reporting single-digit incidence and no fatalities, indicating a well-managed disease status. <br/>

- A notable milestone occurred in 2021 with a month reporting zero cases, highlighting potential progress towards elimination. <br/>

- The sustained low incidence rate over recent years suggests continuity in public health interventions and reinforces the trend of minimal disease impact. <br/>

The data for Schistosomiasis in mainland China from October 2014 to October 2024 indicate a significant reduction in the number of cases over time, highlighting the effectiveness of public health interventions. Initially, the data show peaks in cases, such as in May 2015 with 4,664 cases, and a striking increase in October 2015 with 9,338 cases. These numbers suggest that there were particular challenges or outbreaks during this period requiring targeted public health responses. However, the subsequent years witnessed a steady decrease in the number of reported cases, with noticeable declines beginning in late 2016. 

By 2017, the annual case count was markedly lower, and this trend continued into 2018 and beyond. From 2019 onwards, the monthly reported cases were predominantly in single digits, demonstrating a strong control over the transmission of Schistosomiasis. It is noteworthy that throughout this entire period, the number of deaths attributed to Schistosomiasis was minimal, with only one death recorded in February 2020. These trends reflect successful public health strategies, potentially including improved sanitation, access to clean water, health education campaigns, and effective medical interventions. Overall, these efforts have contributed to controlling and nearly eliminating the disease, as evidenced by the scarce number of cases in recent years.
