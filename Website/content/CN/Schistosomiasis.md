# Schistosomiasis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by trematode worms of the genus Schistosoma. It predominantly affects tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and South America. The disease occurs when people come into contact with freshwater contaminated by the larval forms of the parasite, commonly released by specific freshwater snails. Once inside the human body, the parasites mature and reproduce, causing a range of chronic health problems such as anemia, malnutrition, and organ damage. Schistosomiasis can impact socio-economic development and is considered a significant public health concern.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline:** Schistosomiasis cases in Mainland China have significantly declined from thousands in 2015 to nearly zero, with monthly cases remaining in the single digits since 2023. <br/>
- **No Recent Deaths:** No deaths have been reported since a single fatality in February 2020, indicating effective control and treatment measures. <br/>
- **Sustained Control Achievements:** The trend since 2021 showcases sustained control efforts, maintaining minimal transmission throughout the country. <br/>
- **Ongoing Monitoring:** Continued low case counts emphasize the importance of ongoing surveillance and preventive measures to sustain control and prevent potential resurgence. <br/>

The data for Schistosomiasis in mainland China shows notable trends over the past decade. In 2015, there was a sharp increase in reported cases, peaking at 10,481 in November. This surge likely resulted from environmental factors such as flooding or changes in farming practices that heightened exposure to infected waters. Administrative changes or enhanced surveillance could have also increased reporting. Despite the spike, no deaths were reported, suggesting effective medical management or variations in reporting practices.

From 2016 onwards, the data indicates a consistent decline in cases, reflecting successful public health interventions such as improved sanitation, disease awareness, and access to treatment. By mid-2021, case numbers dropped to single digits monthly, demonstrating the impact of sustained control measures, possibly through environmental modifications or targeted health campaigns. The solitary death reported in February 2020 may highlight anomalies in reporting or clinical challenges that need attention.

Subsequent years have shown persistently low case numbers, with no significant resurgence. This suggests that previous efforts have had lasting impacts on managing the disease. The continued low incidence underscores the importance of maintaining these control measures and ensuring that they are adaptable to changing environmental and societal conditions, to prevent any future outbreaks. Further research will be essential to sustain these gains and address any emerging risks effectively.
