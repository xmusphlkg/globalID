# Schistosomiasis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Schistosomiasis, also known as bilharzia, is a parasitic disease caused by blood-dwelling flukes of the genus Schistosoma. It is prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in areas with poor sanitation. Infection occurs when larval forms, released by freshwater snails, penetrate human skin during contact with contaminated water. The parasites mature and reproduce within the blood vessels, causing chronic inflammation and organ damage. Common symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, and bloody urine. Long-term complications may involve liver damage, kidney failure, and bladder cancer. Effective control measures include access to clean water, improved sanitation, snail control, and mass drug administration.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in Prevalence:** Cases dropped from thousands in 2015 to single digits and low double digits from 2019 onwards. <br/>
- **Zero Mortality:** There have been no deaths attributable to Schistosomiasis since February 2020. <br/>
- **Steady Control:** From 2020 to May 2024, monthly cases remained consistently low, indicating effective control measures. <br/>
- **Recent Stability:** From January to May 2024, the monthly reported cases ranged from 1 to 5, showing maintained disease control.<br/>

The data on Schistosomiasis in the Chinese mainland from May 2014 to May 2024 highlights significant trends and patterns. Initially, there was a notable increase in cases, peaking dramatically in 2015 with October and November recording exceptionally high numbers (9338 and 10481 cases respectively). This surge may be due to various factors like intensified transmission periods, improved detection methods, or heightened reporting accuracy. However, from December 2015 onwards, a sharp decline followed, dropping to 243 cases by January 2016 and continuing downward with occasional fluctuations.

From 2017 onward, there has been a consistent decrease in reported cases, signifying considerable progress in disease control. Monthly cases largely remained below 100 between 2018 and 2019, with isolated spikes in certain months. By 2020, cases dropped to single digits, maintaining relatively low levels through early 2024. The lone death reported in February 2020 underscores the need for sustained vigilance despite overall progress.

This sustained reduction in reported cases likely reflects successful public health interventions and disease control programs. Nonetheless, maintaining these achievements requires continuous surveillance and preventive measures to ensure the disease remains under control. Overall, the data illustrates significant strides in managing Schistosomiasis in the Chinese mainland.
