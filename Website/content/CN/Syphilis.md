# Syphilis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It is primarily spread through direct contact with syphilitic sores, which can occur on the external genitals, vagina, anus, rectum, or in the mouth. The disease progresses through multiple stages, including primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary phases, each with distinct symptoms. Early stages are often characterized by painless sores and rashes, while late-stage syphilis can result in severe health complications like neurological and cardiovascular issues. Syphilis is diagnosed through blood tests and effectively treated with antibiotics, typically penicillin, in its early stages.

## Highlights

- **Rising Trend:** Syphilis cases in Chinese mainland have shown an incremental trend from 2015 to 2023, peaking at 61,068 cases in August 2023, followed by a slight decrease in 2024 and 2025. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variability:** There is noticeable seasonal variation with cases often peaking between March and August and declining towards the end of each year. <br/>

- **Mortality Stability:** Despite the increasing number of cases, the death toll remains relatively low and stable, rarely exceeding ten deaths per month. <br/>

- **Current Situation 2025:** As of October 2025, monthly reported cases have decreased to 50,354, indicating a slight downward trend from previous highs. <br/>

The reported data on syphilis in Mainland China from October 2015 to October 2025 presents a dynamic picture of the disease's prevalence and impact. Initially, there was a rise in the number of syphilis cases, with some fluctuations across the months. For instance, starting at around 38,163 cases in October 2015, there was an upward trajectory in reported cases, peaking significantly during certain months such as March 2023 with 64,161 cases. Seasonal variations could be observed, potentially indicating spikes associated with changes in healthcare-seeking behavior or other socio-environmental factors.

An analysis of the data between 2019 and early 2020 reflects the influence of external global factors, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, where cases drastically dropped in February 2020 to 21,448, possibly due to reduced healthcare access during lockdowns and competing healthcare priorities. However, post-2020, the numbers illustrate a recovery and continued increase in case counts, indicating a resurgence or improved reporting. The death rates remain relatively low compared to the number of cases throughout the years, suggesting high treatment success or under-reporting of severe cases. Peaks in case numbers, such as in 2023-2025, align with improved diagnostic capabilities or increased public health focus on syphilis. Throughout the period reviewed, it is evident that while syphilis remains a significant public health concern, the trend and reporting efforts provide essential insights into public health dynamics and subsequent interventions.
