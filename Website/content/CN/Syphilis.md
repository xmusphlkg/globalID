# Syphilis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It progresses through distinct stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The primary stage is marked by a painless sore at the infection site, while the secondary stage involves skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. If untreated, the infection can become latent, potentially leading to severe complications in the tertiary stage, affecting organs like the heart and brain. Syphilis is primarily spread through sexual contact, but it can also be transmitted from mother to child during pregnancy. Early detection and antibiotic treatment are crucial for effective management.

## Highlights



- **Increasing Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, syphilis cases have risen significantly, peaking at 64,161 in March 2024, highlighting a growing health issue. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite rising cases, deaths remain low, with occasional spikes like 25 in January 2020, indicating effective treatment. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Cases often peak in the first half of the year, suggesting seasonal transmission factors. <br/>

- **Recent Trends**: As of February 2025, cases have slightly decreased from the previous year, suggesting stabilization or effective interventions. <br/>

Initially, from 2015 to 2019, there is a noticeable increase in the number of reported cases, with a peak in July 2019 at 55,367 cases. This upward trend could be attributed to various factors, including improved diagnostic capabilities, increased public awareness, or a genuine rise in transmission rates. The number of deaths, however, remains relatively low and stable throughout this period, suggesting that while the disease's prevalence may be increasing, its lethality is not significantly rising, possibly due to effective treatment options.

From 2020 onwards, the data shows fluctuations in case numbers, with a significant drop in February 2020, likely due to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on healthcare systems and reporting. However, the cases quickly rebound, reaching new highs in subsequent years, with a peak in March 2024 at 64,161 cases. The death toll remains low, with occasional spikes, such as in January 2020 and May 2023, which could be due to reporting anomalies or specific outbreaks. Overall, the data suggests a persistent challenge in controlling syphilis transmission, despite low mortality rates, highlighting the need for sustained public health efforts and targeted interventions.
