## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It is primarily transmitted through sexual contact and can also be passed from mother to child during pregnancy, leading to congenital syphilis. The disease progresses in stages: primary, characterized by painless sores; secondary, with skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions; latent, with no symptoms; and tertiary, causing severe health issues like neurological and cardiovascular damage. Syphilis is treatable with antibiotics, particularly penicillin, but can cause serious complications if untreated. Early detection and treatment are crucial to prevent long-term health consequences.

## Highlights

- Syphilis cases in Chinese mainland showed an upward trend from 2015 to 2025, peaking at 64161 cases in March 2024; however, recent data from 2025 suggests stabilization or slight decline with September cases at 53818. <br/>
- Mortality remained low despite cases fluctuations, with notable spikes, the highest number of deaths (25) in January 2020; recent months have consistently low mortality. <br/>
- Seasonal trends are observed, with rises in March-April possibly influenced by environmental or behavioral factors. <br/>
- Despite increases in cases, improved healthcare access and interventions might be keeping mortality rates low, reflecting enhanced management of the disease. <br/>

The syphilis data from Chinese mainland between 2015 and 2025 reveals notable trends and seasonal patterns in reported cases and associated deaths. Throughout this period, syphilis cases show fluctuations, with a typical pattern of lower instances in January and February, followed by increases from March onward. This cycle could stem from behavioral changes, public health interventions, or service accessibility. For example, February consistently shows fewer cases each year, succeeded by peaks in later months like March. Despite the rising case numbers, syphilis-associated deaths remain relatively low and stable, ranging from 1 to 19 annually, without a clear correlation to the variations in case numbers.

In recent years, starting in 2019, there is a significant rise in cases, reaching a peak in August 2023, with 61,068 cases, the highest in the dataset. This may be attributed to heightened transmission, changes in diagnostics and reporting, or other social factors. Cases remain high yet stabilized going into 2025, suggesting an equilibrium in transmission trends. Mortality remains fairly constrained over the years, though some spikes, like in 2020 and 2023, warrant further investigation to assess whether these were influenced by specific outbreaks, healthcare system dynamics, or shifts in disease virulence. Overall, despite increased cases, deaths have remained limited, suggesting effective management and intervention strategies.

The dataset covering syphilis cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 offers insight into the evolving situation of the disease. Over this period, we observe an increasing trend in syphilis cases, with numbers rising from around 30,000-40,000 per month in earlier years to consistently over 50,000 by 2025. This increase suggests factors contributing to heightened transmission, such as changes in population behavior or healthcare access.

Despite rising cases, syphilis-related deaths remained relatively low, typically under 10 per month. Occasional spikes, like 25 deaths in January 2020 and 10 deaths in May 2023, point to severe outbreaks or lapses in disease management, highlighting the need for focused prevention strategies. The consistently low mortality rate suggests effective interventions that prevent progression to fatal stages, reflecting improvements in treatment outcomes over time.

This data indicates the necessity of understanding factors driving case numbers, enhancing public health initiatives, and ensuring effective treatment outreach, especially in high-incidence regions. With syphilis being manageable when treated early, there is a need for better diagnostics, public health education, and removal of barriers to healthcare access. Continued efforts should focus on sexual health education and addressing socioeconomic factors that influence disease transmission.
