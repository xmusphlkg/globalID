# Syphilis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium *Treponema pallidum*. It progresses through four stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. Primary syphilis typically presents as a painless sore at the site of infection. If untreated, it can advance to secondary syphilis, causing skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. The latent stage shows no symptoms, but the infection persists. Tertiary syphilis can lead to severe health complications affecting the heart, brain, and other organs. Syphilis is curable with antibiotics, typically penicillin, especially when diagnosed early. Early detection and treatment are critical for prevention.

## Highlights

- Syphilis cases in mainland China have shown a general upward trend since 2015, peaking above 60,000 in several months of 2023–2024, with 54,898 cases reported in May 2025. <br/>
- Seasonal fluctuations persist, with higher case counts typically observed in mid-year months (June–August), reflecting possible behavioral or surveillance patterns. <br/>
- Reported deaths remain relatively low and stable, mostly in single digits monthly, suggesting improved early detection and treatment access. <br/>
- Despite improvements in case management, the sustained rise in incidence points to ongoing transmission, underlining the need for enhanced prevention and community-level interventions. <br/>

From mid-2015 through early 2025, reported syphilis cases in mainland China reveal clear seasonality and long-term growth trends. Monthly cases consistently dropped in the early part of each year—particularly February, coinciding with the Lunar New Year and reduced healthcare seeking—followed by peaks during mid to late summer. This cyclical rhythm remained intact through the decade. Overall case numbers increased year-over-year, with monthly reports surpassing 60,000 by mid-2024 from sub-40,000 levels in 2015, suggesting intensifying transmission, improved detection, or both. Notably, the most substantial growth occurred post-2018, accelerating into 2023–2024, when multiple consecutive months reported above 60,000 cases.

Mortality stayed low despite rising incidence, fluctuating between 1–10 deaths monthly, with brief spikes—25 deaths recorded in January 2020 aligns with early COVID-19 disruptions. Improvements in diagnosis and treatment accessibility may explain the decoupling between case surges and fatality rates. However, early 2020 to mid-2022 presented erratic trends in both metrics, likely influenced by pandemic-related system strain. In recent years, despite peaking cases, deaths remained mostly stable or declined—suggesting either milder clinical manifestations, enhanced intervention effectiveness, or reporting limitations for mortality compared to incidence.

These peaks may reflect healthcare disruptions, diagnostic delays, or demographic vulnerabilities during those specific periods.
