# Syphilis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It progresses through distinct stages: primary, characterized by a painless sore or chancre at the infection site; secondary, involving skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions; latent, where symptoms may disappear; and tertiary, potentially causing severe health issues like neurological and cardiac complications after many years if untreated. Transmission occurs via direct contact with syphilitic sores, primarily during sexual activity. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics, like penicillin, are crucial for preventing severe outcomes. Regular screening and safe sexual practices are essential for control and prevention.

## Highlights

- Syphilis incidence in Chinese mainland has shown an overall increasing trend from 2015 to 2025, with a notable surge since 2023, reaching peaks above 60,000 monthly cases. <br/>
- Between 2015 and early 2020, case numbers fluctuated with a slight upward trend, followed by a dip in early 2020 likely due to COVID-19 pandemic control measures. <br/>
- Since 2021, there has been a marked increase in syphilis cases, with persistent high numbers throughout 2023 and 2024, continuing into 2025. <br/>
- Mortality remains low, indicating effective clinical management; however, constant high incidence suggests ongoing transmission and potential gaps in public health interventions. <br/>

The syphilis case data from Mainland China between mid-2015 to mid-2025 reveals fluctuating patterns. Initially, cases dip during early months annually, possibly due to the Lunar New Year influencing reporting or clinic operations. Following this, there's a rise in cases peaking around mid-year, particularly June to August, before numbers decrease again toward year's end. However, starting in 2017, cases increase steadily, indicating an upward trend that peaks significantly by mid-2023 and continues into 2024.

This notable rise in cases might be due to enhanced diagnostic measures, increased screening efforts, or changing social dynamics like urbanization and increased population mobility. Despite these fluctuations in cases, the deaths remain low and stable, suggesting effective treatment or early detection is mitigating fatality risks. From 2019 onwards, cases notably rise, especially during mid-2023, reflecting possible shifts in behaviors or healthcare improvements.

While the infection rate appears to increase, the stable death count suggests effective medical interventions are in place. This disparity underscores the healthcare system's role in managing syphilis effectively despite rising infection rates. Overall, the data indicates the need for ongoing public health strategies, focusing on prevention and early intervention, to manage syphilis's growing incidence while keeping mortality low.
