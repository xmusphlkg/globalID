# Syphilis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It progresses through stages: primary (a painless sore at the infection site), secondary (skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions), latent, and tertiary (severe health issues affecting organs like the heart and brain). Mainly transmitted through sexual contact, it can also pass from mother to fetus during pregnancy. Without timely treatment, typically with penicillin, syphilis can lead to serious complications. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to effectively manage and prevent long-term health problems.

## Highlights

- **Rising Cases:** Syphilis cases have generally increased over the years, reaching a peak of 64,161 in March 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Variations:** There's a noticeable seasonal variation with higher cases typically reported from May to August each year. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rates:** Despite increasing cases, mortality rates remain low, with single-digit deaths per month. Notable exceptions include January 2020. <br/>
- **Recent Surge:** The most recent data, as of May 2024, indicates a high number of cases (60,962), suggesting ongoing challenges with controlling the spread. <br/>

Analyzing syphilis data in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals notable trends and fluctuations. From 2014 to 2019, there is a general upward trend in monthly reported cases, peaking at 55,367 in July 2019. Death rates remained relatively stable, averaging 6.5 monthly, rarely surpassing 10. There is a noticeable seasonal dip in February cases, likely linked to reporting reductions during the Chinese New Year holidays.

The COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 significantly impacted syphilis reporting, with a marked drop in cases to 21,448 in February 2020 and a spike in deaths that month to 25. A slow recovery is noted post-pandemic, with fluctuating numbers and a slight downturn in 2022. However, 2023 saw a sharp rise in cases, peaking at 61,068 in August, possibly indicating increased transmission or improved diagnostics/reporting. Despite rising cases, mortality rates did not notably increase, suggesting effective clinical management or enhanced healthcare interventions.

These trends underscore the critical need for ongoing public health efforts and meticulous monitoring to manage syphilis spread effectively. The data highlight underlying patterns and emphasize the importance of maintaining robust diagnostic and reporting infrastructure to address and mitigate syphilis transmission in the population.
