# Syphilis

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It progresses through distinct stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The primary stage is marked by a painless sore at the infection site, while the secondary stage can involve skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. If untreated, the infection can progress to the latent stage, potentially leading to severe complications in the tertiary stage, affecting organs like the heart and brain. Syphilis is treatable with antibiotics, particularly penicillin, but early detection is crucial to prevent long-term health issues.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Cases**: Syphilis cases have risen significantly from 2014 to 2024, peaking at 64,161 in March 2024, highlighting a growing public health issue. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite high case numbers, deaths remain low, with occasional spikes like 25 in January 2020, suggesting effective treatment or reporting variations. <br/>

- **Seasonal Patterns**: Higher case numbers are observed in spring and summer, possibly linked to increased social interactions. <br/>

- **Recent Decline**: A decrease in cases is noted in late 2024, with December recording 47,978 cases, indicating potential intervention success or natural disease cycle changes. <br/>

The data on syphilis cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, there is a noticeable increase in the number of reported cases over the years. From 2014 to 2019, the monthly cases generally fluctuate between 30,000 to 50,000, with a few months exceeding this range. However, starting in 2020, there is a marked increase, with several months reporting over 50,000 cases, peaking in March 2024 with 64,161 cases. This upward trend could be attributed to various factors, including increased transmission rates, improved reporting mechanisms, or changes in public health policies and awareness.

The number of deaths associated with syphilis remains relatively low compared to the number of cases, with most months reporting fewer than 10 deaths. This suggests that while syphilis is prevalent, it may not be as lethal, possibly due to effective treatment options available. However, there are occasional spikes in mortality, such as in January 2020 with 25 deaths, which may indicate periods of healthcare system strain or outbreaks in more vulnerable populations. Overall, the data underscores the importance of continuous public health efforts to manage and reduce the spread of syphilis, alongside ensuring access to effective treatment to keep mortality rates low.
