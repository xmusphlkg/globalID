# Syphilis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It progresses through distinct stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The primary stage typically presents with a painless sore at the site of infection, while the secondary stage involves skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. If untreated, the infection can enter a latent phase, eventually leading to severe complications in the tertiary stage, affecting organs such as the heart and brain. Syphilis is primarily spread through sexual contact, but it can also be transmitted from mother to child during pregnancy. Effective antibiotic treatment is available, typically involving penicillin.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trend in Cases:** Syphilis cases in the Chinese mainland have shown a rising trend from 2023 to 2025, with cases in 2025 remaining high compared to previous years. This suggests a growing epidemic concern. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Monthly data highlights seasonal peaks, with significant increases typically observed in spring months, such as March and April. <br/>
- **Mortality Stability:** Despite the volatile case numbers, the number of deaths attributed to syphilis remains relatively low, indicating improved disease management and treatment availability. <br/>
- **Public Health Priority:** The persistent increase in cases, combined with stable mortality rates, underscores the need for continuous public health efforts, emphasizing prevention, testing, and early treatment. <br/>

Initially, monthly cases ranged from 30,000 to 40,000, displaying seasonal variations. For example, in 2015 and 2016, lower cases were reported in the early months, reflecting potential reporting disruptions or reduced transmission during winter. This pattern continued until around 2020, indicating a seasonal impact on syphilis reporting or spread.

Starting in 2017, cases increased, peaking in summer months, with July 2019 reaching 55,367 cases. A significant drop occurred in February 2020, coinciding with COVID-19's onset, suggesting either genuine decreases or disruptions in healthcare services. By August 2023, the reported cases reached a high of 61,068, showing a gradual yet sustained increase, hinting at either persistent or escalating transmission or enhanced case detection strategies.

Throughout this period, deaths remained low, typically in single digits, with few exceptions, such as a spike in January 2020. Despite an increase in cases, consistently low mortality rates might suggest improved treatment and management or variations in case severity. Monthly deaths from syphilis remain low compared to infection rates, with the highest death count of 25 recorded in January 2020. This anomaly might indicate a more virulent strain, complications in untreated cases, or issues within the healthcare response during that time. Even as reported cases reach tens of thousands each month, the death toll mostly stays in the single digits. 

From 2015 to 2025, an upward trend in cases is observed, especially from 2023 where cases exceed 60,000 in certain months. Despite rising infection rates, the monthly death toll does not equally escalate; for instance, November and December 2023 each report only one death. Such patterns suggest improvements in healthcare infrastructure, disease management, and intervention techniques that reduce mortality. Several months over the years also show zero deaths despite high case counts, indicating effective healthcare responses preventing progression to fatal outcomes. By 2025, cases remain high, often exceeding 50,000, but fatalities predominantly remain low, reinforcing successful therapeutic management and increased disease awareness. This suggests that while syphilis remains prevalent, efforts in treatment and prevention may effectively mitigate its deadliness despite continued high transmission rates.
