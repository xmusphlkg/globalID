# Syphilis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium *Treponema pallidum*. It progresses through four stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. Primary syphilis typically presents as a painless sore at the site of infection, followed by skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions in the secondary stage. If untreated, the disease can enter a latent phase and eventually progress to tertiary syphilis, causing severe organ damage. Syphilis is primarily transmitted through sexual contact but can also be passed from mother to child during pregnancy. Early detection and treatment with antibiotics, especially penicillin, are highly effective.

## Highlights

- A significant upward trend in syphilis cases is observed from 2023 to early 2025, with monthly cases regularly surpassing 50,000 since March 2023, and peaking at 64,161 in March 2024. <br/>
- Deaths remain relatively low compared to cases, with monthly deaths mostly in the single digits over the years, suggesting that the fatality rate remains low. <br/>
- Seasonal variability is notable, with lower cases reported in the earlier months each year, suggesting potential seasonality in syphilis transmission or detection. <br/>
- The upward trajectory in cases should prompt public health officials to strengthen prevention and control measures to mitigate further spread. <br/>

The dataset on syphilis cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several patterns and trends. Initially, from 2015 to early 2016, monthly cases range from 28,685 to 43,205 cases, with a noticeable decrease in January and February, potentially due to lower reporting during the Chinese New Year.

From 2017 to 2020, reported syphilis cases gradually increase, with a peak in August, reflecting a seasonal pattern, possibly due to behavioral factors influencing transmission. By 2019 and early 2020, cases stabilize at a higher average, reaching over 50,000 monthly during summer months. This upward trend continues into the early 2020s, significantly peaking with over 60,000 cases in March 2024.

Deaths associated with syphilis remain relatively low, with predominantly single-digit monthly figures. While there is no clear trend in mortality, the consistency of low death rates despite increasing cases suggests effective clinical management or potential underreporting. An isolated spike in deaths in January 2020 aligns with higher case numbers that month, but overall, the mortality impact does not match the rising incidence. This persistence of low death rates, despite the rising number of cases, indicates effective medical care accessibility but may also highlight gaps in public health measures managing the disease's spread.

One standout period was January 2020, which saw a significant spike to 25 deaths—substantially higher than monthly averages. This may reflect delayed diagnoses or treatment disruptions linked to the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Smaller surges, such as 19 deaths in August 2017, also suggest isolated periods of elevated mortality possibly due to shifts in health service accessibility, regional outbreaks among high-risk populations, or data reporting anomalies. However, these spikes did not establish an increasing trend over the long term.

Year-to-year, the highest total annual deaths occurred in 2020, coinciding with a major public health crisis, while recent years—especially 2023 and beyond—have shown a return to lower fatality levels despite rising case numbers. This divergence suggests improved clinical management and earlier detection of cases. Moreover, the persistently low fatality rate—even when monthly cases have exceeded 60,000—implies that while syphilis remains a significant public health concern in terms of transmission, its lethality under current healthcare conditions is well-managed.
