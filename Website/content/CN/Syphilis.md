# Syphilis

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium *Treponema pallidum*. It typically progresses in four stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The primary stage is marked by painless sores or ulcers, while the secondary stage involves skin rashes and mucosal lesions. If left untreated, the infection can become latent, with no symptoms, but may later progress to tertiary syphilis, affecting organs such as the heart and nervous system. Early diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics, usually penicillin, are crucial to prevent long-term complications.

## Highlights

### Key Epidemiological Trends and Current Situation of Syphilis in Chinese Mainland (November 2024)

- **Rising Incidence:** Syphilis cases have shown an upward trend, with a marked increase from 34,705 cases in November 2014 to 52,434 cases in November 2024. <br/>
  
- **Relatively Stable Mortality:** Deaths have remained relatively low and stable, with most months reporting 3-7 deaths, and no significant spike in recent years. <br/>
  
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** There is evidence of seasonal variation, with peaks typically observed in March, May, and July. <br/>
  
- **Recent Decrease in Cases:** A slight decrease in new cases is noted in the last months of 2024 compared to earlier in the year.

The syphilis case data for the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 indicates a notable fluctuation in the number of reported cases, with a generally upward trajectory through the years. Starting in 2014, cases hovered around 34,000 to 36,000 per month, before increasing significantly in 2015 and continuing this upward trend through 2020. In 2020, a marked peak occurred, with cases rising sharply during the early months, especially in January, coinciding with a notable increase in deaths. Despite these peaks, the data suggests that by mid-2021, syphilis cases appeared to stabilize somewhat, with fewer large fluctuations.

There is a slight seasonal variation in reported cases, with higher incidences typically observed during the summer and fall months, particularly in July through October, aligning with increased sexual activity during these periods. Death rates, while low overall, show a similar trend of occasional spikes, such as in early 2020 and again in 2024. The overall case fatality rate remains low, ranging between 0.01% and 0.03%, indicating that while syphilis is a significant public health issue, fatalities are relatively rare in comparison to case numbers. The rising number of cases in recent years suggests the need for ongoing surveillance and targeted intervention strategies, particularly given the potential for underreporting and the chronic nature of the disease if left untreated.

The death rates associated with syphilis in China appear to be relatively low despite the high number of reported cases, showing a notable trend of minimal mortality even during periods of peak incidence. For instance, in 2020, the month of January saw an unusually high number of deaths (25), though this is an outlier compared to most other months where fatalities ranged from one to 10 individuals. Over the years, there have been sporadic increases in the death count, such as during some months in 2017 and 2020, but these increases are not sustained. By 2023 and into 2024, the number of deaths seems to stabilize, with monthly fatalities often remaining between one and seven.

One of the more interesting aspects of this dataset is the consistency in the low mortality rate relative to the large numbers of syphilis cases reported. This could indicate improvements in treatment protocols, access to healthcare, or potentially that syphilis-related deaths are more likely to be associated with complications such as cardiovascular or neurological involvement, which occur less frequently. In addition, a noticeable decrease in the number of deaths in 2022, where some months reported zero deaths, could reflect increased public health efforts or changes in reporting practices, which might have further contributed to minimizing mortality.
