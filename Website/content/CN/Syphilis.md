# Syphilis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium *Treponema pallidum*. It progresses through distinct stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The disease initially presents with painless sores, followed by skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. If untreated, it can lead to severe complications affecting the heart, brain, and other organs. Syphilis is transmitted primarily through sexual contact but can also be passed from mother to child during pregnancy, causing congenital syphilis. Diagnosis is made through blood tests, and treatment is typically with intramuscular penicillin. Early detection and treatment are essential to prevent long-term consequences.

## Highlights

- Syphilis cases in mainland China have shown a general upward trend since 2015, with monthly cases rising from ~38,000 in April 2015 to over 57,000 in April 2025. <br/>
- Seasonal fluctuations are consistent, with March–August typically showing higher case counts, suggesting potential behavioral or reporting influences. <br/>
- Although case counts are increasing, death rates remain relatively low and stable, averaging fewer than 10 deaths per month throughout the period. <br/>
- The sustained rise in infections, especially in recent years (2023–2025), indicates an ongoing public health concern, necessitating stronger prevention, screening, and treatment interventions. <br/>

From 2015 to early 2025, the monthly reported syphilis cases in mainland China demonstrate a clear trend of gradual annual increase with seasonal fluctuations. Cases typically dip in February, likely due to public holidays and reduced reporting or medical activity during the Chinese New Year, then sharply rebound in March and rise steadily through mid-year. A marked acceleration in monthly averages becomes apparent from 2017 onwards, likely indicative of broader testing, surveillance expansion, or genuine transmission upsurges. Notably, cases from 2023 onward are consistently high, with several months surpassing 60,000 cases. The pandemic-related suppression of 2020 early-year numbers—particularly a low in February—appears transient, as counts recovered in subsequent months.

Syphilis-related mortality remains relatively low across the period, averaging below 10 deaths monthly and showing no proportional increase parallel to rising case counts. Isolated spikes—such as 25 deaths in January 2020 and 19 in August 2017—are rare and do not persist, suggesting they may be influenced by localized outbreaks, delayed reporting, or classifications. The disconnect between increasing cases and stable mortality may reflect improved diagnosis, earlier treatment, and public health response capabilities. However, the continued rise in incidence rates—especially post-2022—signals ongoing transmission drivers that warrant sustained intervention and deeper epidemiologic investigation.

The deaths associated with syphilis in mainland China between April 2015 and April 2025 show a generally low but variable monthly mortality burden. The fatality numbers largely remain in the single digits, with occasional monthly outliers—January 2020 stands out sharply with 25 reported deaths, more than triple any preceding monthly figure, suggesting possible misclassification, reporting backlog, or health system stressors (potentially linked with COVID-19 emergence during that time). Another relatively high cluster occurred in August 2017 (19 deaths), which briefly departs from the pattern and warrants scrutiny into potential localized outbreaks or reporting inconsistencies.

From 2015 to early 2019, deaths averaged around 5–8 per month, showing moderate variability but no sustained upward trend. However, despite a general increase in reported cases over the years—especially post-2017—the corresponding deaths have not increased proportionately. In fact, several months in 2023–2024 report only 1–3 deaths, despite some of the highest case volumes in the dataset. This decline in case-fatality ratio may reflect improved diagnosis, earlier treatment access, or shifts in reporting practices. Overall mortality remains low considering the high and increasing case counts over the decade, signaling persistent but controlled lethality.
