# Syphilis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. It progresses through stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The primary stage often presents with a painless sore, while the secondary stage includes skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. If untreated, it progresses to latent syphilis, which can become tertiary, potentially affecting the heart, brain, and other organs. Syphilis is treatable with antibiotics, typically penicillin. Early detection and treatment are crucial to prevent serious complications. Prenatal screening is vital since congenital syphilis can occur if transmitted from an infected mother to her baby.

## Highlights

1. **Increasing Trend**: Syphilis cases in mainland China have gradually risen from 2015 to 2025, peaking notably in 2023 and 2024, with the highest being 58,247 cases in July 2023. <br/>

2. **Stable Mortality Rates**: Despite rising cases, monthly deaths mostly remained below ten, indicating stable mortality rates over the years. <br/>

3. **Seasonal Fluctuations**: Cases tend to peak in summer months (June-August), likely due to increased testing and health campaigns during these periods. <br/>

4. **Current Situation**: By June 2025, cases have slightly stabilized at 52,760, suggesting a potential plateau in the trend after recent peaks. <br/>

The syphilis data from mainland China between June 2015 and June 2025 reveals notable fluctuations. Initially, monthly cases remained around 40,000, with seasonal variations. Early 2016 saw a drop in cases, particularly in February, possibly due to reporting inefficiencies or seasonal factors. Following this, a rise in cases occurred, peaking in July 2019 at over 55,000. This summer peak is common in many diseases and could relate to increased mobility and transmission opportunities.

The early 2020s exhibited a decline, with February showing a substantial drop, likely influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic affecting healthcare access and diagnostics. However, from mid-2020 onwards, cases trended upwards, with notable annual dips each February, coinciding with the Chinese New Year when healthcare services traditionally slow down. By 2023 to 2025, numbers rose sharply, surpassing 60,000 by April 2024.

Several factors might be driving this increase, including enhanced detection and reporting, and possibly greater population movement. Importantly, despite high case numbers, deaths remain low, suggesting improved treatments or possibly less virulent strains. This data highlights the necessity for ongoing surveillance and targeted intervention strategies to manage and potentially curb this upward trend. Monitoring these patterns will be key in understanding causative factors and enhancing healthcare responses.

For example, in 2019, case numbers ranged from 42,795 in January to 54,217 in August, yet deaths were as low as 3 in some months, highlighting the potential impact of improved healthcare access and management strategies.

Seasonal and yearly variations in death rates also emerge, with notable spikes like the 25 deaths in January 2020 despite having fewer cases (39,671) than peak months in other years. These spikes might point to seasonal health factors or limitations in healthcare responses during those specific times. Despite the rising trend in reported cases—peaking in the mid-2020s—the death rate does not show a significant increase, suggesting advancements in medical treatments or changes in public health behaviors.

Overall, this dataset suggests advances in healthcare are maintaining a low mortality rate for syphilis despite increasing cases. Careful attention to the periods with death spikes could offer insights into potential healthcare or socio-economic areas for improvement during such times.
