# Syphilis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium *Treponema pallidum*. It progresses through distinct stages: primary, secondary, latent, and tertiary. The primary stage is marked by painless sores at the infection site. Secondary syphilis includes skin rashes and mucous membrane lesions. Without treatment, the disease progresses to the latent stage, which can last for years with no symptoms. Tertiary syphilis can cause severe health issues affecting the heart, brain, and other organs. Early detection and antibiotic treatment, typically with penicillin, are crucial for effective management.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Trend:** Syphilis cases in mainland China have shown a significant upward trend from 37,974 in June 2014 to 54,760 in June 2024, indicating an increasing prevalence.  
- **Fluctuating Death Rates:** Deaths have varied annually, with notable peaks, such as 25 deaths in January 2020, but overall remain low relative to cases.  
- **Seasonal Variations:** There is an observable fluctuation in cases across months, often peaking around mid-year, suggesting potential seasonal influences on transmission.  
- **Recent Surge:** The most recent data for 2024 indicates a substantial increase in cases, with March 2024 reporting the highest monthly figure of 64,161 cases.

Between 2014 and 2024, the reported syphilis cases in mainland China exhibit a general upward trend with significant fluctuations. Notably, the annual peak months for syphilis cases tend to vary, but the overall number of cases increases from around 37,974 cases in June 2014 to a peak of 64,161 cases in March 2024. This upward trend suggests a growing prevalence of syphilis over the years, indicating possible factors such as increased transmission rates, better detection and reporting mechanisms, or demographic changes. The monthly data reveals periodic spikes and troughs, with certain months like February consistently showing lower cases, likely due to shorter reporting periods and reduced healthcare services during the Chinese New Year.

Despite the increasing trend in syphilis cases, the mortality rate remains relatively low and stable, with monthly deaths rarely exceeding 10. The data highlights a few exceptional months, such as January 2020, where 25 deaths were reported, which may correlate with reduced healthcare access or other confounding factors like the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. However, overall, the data indicates that while syphilis remains a growing public health concern in terms of incidence, the fatality rate is controlled, likely due to effective treatment options. This analysis underscores the need for sustained public health efforts to manage and mitigate the spread of syphilis while maintaining low mortality through accessible treatment.
