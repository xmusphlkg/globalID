# Tuberculosis

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, primarily affecting the lungs but capable of involving other organs. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. TB can present as latent or active infection; the former is asymptomatic and non-contagious, while the latter causes symptoms like persistent cough, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB remains a leading global health issue, especially in low- and middle-income countries. Diagnosis involves skin tests, blood tests, imaging, and sputum analysis. Treatment typically requires a multi-drug regimen over six months or longer.

## Highlights

- **Long-Term Decline:** Monthly TB cases fell from over 100,000 in 2015 to about 59,000 in April 2025, showing effective control efforts. <br/>  
- **Rising Mortality:** TB deaths have increased since 2022, with monthly deaths over 300 at times, despite declining case counts. <br/>  
- **Seasonal Pattern:** Case counts consistently peak during spring months like March and April, then drop in winter, reflecting seasonal dynamics. <br/>  
- **Current Status (April 2025):** 59,246 new TB cases and 252 deaths were reported, indicating ongoing transmission and a need for sustained public health measures. <br/>

From 2015 to mid-2019, tuberculosis in the Chinese mainland showed a clear seasonal trend, peaking in spring and dropping in winter, with monthly cases generally between 70,000 and 110,000. Deaths during this period remained steady, usually below 220. A sharp decline in reported cases began in early 2020, aligning with the onset of COVID-19, hitting a low of 44,933 in February 2020. Although partial recovery followed, case numbers stabilized at a lower range of 60,000â€“80,000 from 2021 onward, likely due to reduced healthcare access or changes in reporting and public health priorities.

Starting in 2022, tuberculosis mortality rose considerably, often exceeding 300 deaths monthly despite reduced case counts. This suggests possible delays in diagnosis, treatment interruptions, or a rise in severe forms of TB. For instance, December 2023 recorded only 52,826 cases but the highest monthly death toll in the dataset at 416. Although mortality began to stabilize in early 2025, it remained high relative to earlier years. The decoupling of cases and deaths from 2022 onward signals a shift in disease dynamics and healthcare system pressure. It reflects an urgent need to reassess TB control measures, improve diagnosis and treatment accessibility, and mitigate pandemic-related impacts that may have worsened patient outcomes or disrupted long-standing TB surveillance systems.

From 2015 to early 2025, tuberculosis deaths in mainland China show several notable trends. Between 2015 and 2021, TB-related deaths fluctuated moderately, generally ranging between 100 and 200 per month. There were occasional spikes, such as in March 2017 and December 2019. These fluctuations largely corresponded with the case volumes, with deaths often peaking in winter and early spring. In 2020, during the initial COVID-19 outbreak, there was a significant drop in TB deaths and cases, particularly in February and March, suggesting potential underreporting or disruptions in healthcare access.

A shift is observed starting January 2022, marked by a significant increase in TB deaths despite declining case numbers. From 2022 to 2023, monthly deaths repeatedly exceeded 300, with a peak of 416 in December 2023, even as incident cases continued to decline. This trend indicates a possible deterioration in treatment efficacy, challenges in case detection, or systemic impacts from the COVID-19 pandemic. In 2024 and early 2025, TB mortality remains consistently high, above 200 deaths per month, while reported cases drop below 60,000. This suggests a worsening case-fatality rate that necessitates urgent epidemiological inquiry and public health response to identify potential gaps in TB management and control strategies.
