# Tuberculosis

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. It primarily affects the lungs but can spread to other organs. TB is transmitted through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or speaks. Common symptoms include persistent cough, chest pain, fever, night sweats, weight loss, and fatigue. TB is classified into latent and active forms; latent TB shows no symptoms but can progress to active disease. It remains a global health concern, particularly in low-income countries, despite being preventable and treatable through vaccination, antibiotics, and public health measures. Early diagnosis and adherence to treatment are crucial.

## Highlights

- Significant decline in the number of TB cases since 2015, with cases dropping from over 110,000 to less than 60,000 by March 2025. <br/>
- A marked increase in the number of deaths per month since 2022, indicating a rising mortality rate despite the lower number of cases. <br/>
- Fluctuations in TB cases align with seasonal trends, with peaks typically occurring in March and slight rises observed in June and July. <br/>
- Public health interventions appear to have reduced case counts substantially, but further efforts are needed to address the persistent and rising mortality rates. <br/>

From 2015 to 2025, tuberculosis (TB) in mainland China shows strong seasonal trends, with consistent case spikes each March. For example, March counts reached 113,242 in 2015 and stayed above 100,000 until 2018, before gradually declining in later years. This recurring peak may relate to environmental or social factors influencing diagnosis or disease transmission cycles.

Long-term data indicates a downward trend in overall TB cases. Early years frequently exceeded 100,000 monthly cases, while by 2024–2025, monthly figures drop to around 50,000–60,000, possibly reflecting stronger control measures, better access to care, early diagnosis, or vaccination impact. The sharp drop in early 2020 aligns with initial COVID-19 restrictions, which may have disrupted both transmission and diagnosis.

In contrast, TB mortality started rising sharply from 2022, with monthly deaths exceeding 300 for extended periods, peaking at 416 in December 2023. This occurs despite falling case numbers, suggesting worsening case severity, possible underreporting of mild cases, or rise in drug-resistant strains. The divergence of case and death trends indicates a need for closer inspection of care quality, delayed diagnoses, or changing patient demographics. While intervention efforts appear to reduce incidence, the surging fatality rates signal emerging challenges in treatment outcomes that require targeted attention to preserve progress against TB.

The reported data for tuberculosis (TB) in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to early 2025 indicates fluctuations in both case numbers and mortality. Key observations reveal a relative decline in reported cases of tuberculosis over the years, with a notable drop starting in 2019 and becoming more conspicuous through 2020 and 2021—a period coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic. Notably, during this same period, despite cases dropping, the deaths did not experience a proportionate decline. The consistent mortality figures suggest that even with fewer detected cases in those years, tuberculosis remained a significant health challenge, indicating potential underreporting or diminished healthcare access during the pandemic.

From 2022, the mortality rate significantly spikes, while the number of cases initially reduces then gradually rises again. The sharp increase in deaths in 2022 and early 2023 could be attributed to changes in healthcare access, delayed care during the COVID-19 pandemic, or perhaps the emergence of more lethal strains of the disease. In contrast, a noticeable decline in both cases and deaths is observed in 2024 into early 2025, hinting at possible improvements in TB control measures or healthcare system adjustments post-pandemic. The data reveals how TB mortality does not necessarily correlate directly with the number of cases and underscores the importance of robust health systems to manage such infectious diseases effectively, especially during global crises.
