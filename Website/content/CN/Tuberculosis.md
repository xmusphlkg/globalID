# Tuberculosis

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, primarily affecting the lungs but can also impact other parts of the body. It spreads through airborne particles when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include persistent cough, chest pain, weight loss, fever, and night sweats. TB can be latent, where the bacteria remain inactive without causing symptoms, or active, where it leads to illness. Treatment typically involves a lengthy course of antibiotics. Despite being preventable and curable, TB remains a significant global health challenge, particularly in low- and middle-income countries.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend in Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, tuberculosis cases in mainland China have significantly decreased, with a peak in early years and a reduction by January 2025. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Death Rates**: Despite fewer cases, death rates fluctuated, with a notable increase from 2022, peaking in December 2023. <br/>

- **COVID-19 Impact**: The sharp decline in cases during 2020 and 2021 may be linked to the COVID-19 pandemic, affecting reporting and healthcare access. <br/>

- **Recent Stabilization**: By January 2025, both cases and deaths show signs of stabilization, suggesting improvements in disease management and healthcare interventions. <br/>

The tuberculosis (TB) data from mainland China between 2015 and early 2025 shows distinct trends. From 2015 to 2019, TB cases exhibit a seasonal pattern, peaking in March and April, then declining towards year-end. Monthly cases generally exceed 80,000, with deaths ranging from 100 to 230. This period reflects a stable TB burden with minor annual fluctuations.

A notable shift occurs in 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic. Both TB cases and deaths decline significantly, likely due to public health measures like mask-wearing and social distancing, which may have reduced TB transmission. Additionally, disruptions in healthcare services and altered health-seeking behavior during the pandemic might have led to underreporting.

From 2021 onwards, while cases continue to decline, deaths show a gradual increase, suggesting challenges in TB management or changes in the virulence of strains. By 2025, both cases and deaths stabilize at lower levels compared to pre-pandemic years, indicating a potential long-term impact of the pandemic on TB epidemiology in China. This data highlights the complex interplay between public health interventions and infectious disease dynamics, underscoring the need for continued vigilance and adaptation in TB control strategies.

As the years progressed, a notable increase in deaths was observed, particularly in 2022, where monthly deaths frequently exceeded 300, indicating a concerning trend. This spike could be attributed to various factors, including potential healthcare system strains, increased drug resistance, or socio-economic challenges affecting treatment accessibility.

In the subsequent years, while the number of cases fluctuated, the death rates exhibited a troubling pattern, with several months in 2023 and 2024 recording high mortality figures, such as 416 deaths in December 2023. This suggests that despite ongoing public health efforts, TB remains a significant health challenge in the region. The data indicates a need for enhanced TB control measures, including improved diagnostic capabilities, treatment adherence strategies, and public health education to mitigate the impact of this disease on the population. The persistent high death rates, particularly in the context of fluctuating case numbers, underscore the complexity of managing TB in the Chinese mainland.
