# Tuberculosis

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. Primarily affecting the lungs, it can also impact other body parts. TB spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a persistent cough, chest pain, weight loss, and fever. TB can be latent, meaning individuals carry the bacteria but show no symptoms and aren't contagious. Active TB requires prolonged antibiotic treatment. Despite being preventable and curable, TB remains a major global health issue, particularly in developing countries. Early detection and treatment are crucial to control its spread.

## Highlights

- Tuberculosis cases have generally declined from 2014 to 2024, with a peak around 2015 and a notable reduction since 2020. <br/>
- Deaths have increased significantly from 2022 onwards, despite the decreasing number of cases, indicating a potential rise in disease severity or reporting changes. <br/>
- There is a marked decrease in cases and deaths from 2020 to 2021, likely due to COVID-19 impacts on healthcare and reporting systems. <br/>
- As of June 2024, both cases and deaths are lower compared to earlier years, but deaths remain relatively high, emphasizing the need for sustained TB control measures. <br/>

The reported data for Tuberculosis (TB) cases in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 exhibits significant fluctuations, reflecting both seasonal variations and broader epidemiological trends. The number of TB cases peaked notably in the spring months, such as March and April, for most years. For instance, March 2015 saw 113,242 cases, while March 2016 reported 114,627 cases. This pattern suggests a possible seasonal influence on TB incidence, potentially linked to climatic factors or population behaviors during these months. Additionally, a gradual decline in the total number of cases is observable over the years, particularly from 2019 onward, likely due to improved public health measures and TB control programs.

The mortality data accompanying the case reports also shows variable trends, with a notable increase in deaths towards the end of the reporting period. For example, the number of deaths was relatively stable around 100-200 per month until late 2021, after which there was a marked rise, reaching as high as 416 deaths in December 2023. This increase could be attributed to various factors, including possible drug resistance, changes in healthcare accessibility, or demographic shifts. The concurrent rise in deaths with the decline in cases suggests that while the number of new infections may be decreasing, the severity or treatment challenges of existing cases might be worsening.

The data on tuberculosis (TB) deaths in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 reveals several significant trends and patterns. Overall, there is an observable increase in TB deaths over the years. Notably, the number of deaths shows a substantial rise from 2019 onwards, peaking in late 2023 and early 2024. This period includes the highest reported death tolls, such as December 2023 with 416 deaths and January 2024 with 383 deaths. This marked increase in mortality could be associated with various factors, including potential changes in the healthcare system, variations in TB strain virulence, or public health challenges exacerbated by concurrent health crises like COVID-19.

Seasonal fluctuations are also evident in the data. Generally, higher TB death rates are observed during the spring and winter months, while lower death rates appear during the summer. For example, March and April frequently show elevated death counts compared to preceding and following months. Conversely, deaths tend to drop during the summer, as seen in several years including 2015 and 2020. These patterns could be linked to seasonal variations in human behavior and immunity, influencing TB transmission and exacerbation rates. Additionally, February consistently records fewer deaths, likely due to the shorter month and potentially lower reporting or actual reduction in cases.
