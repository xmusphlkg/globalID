# Tuberculosis

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection primarily caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It mainly affects the lungs but can spread to other organs and systems. TB is transmitted via airborne particles when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include persistent cough, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB can be latent or active, with latent TB showing no symptoms but carrying the potential for future activation. Active TB requires prompt medical treatment with antibiotics over several months. Despite global efforts to curb its spread, TB remains a major public health concern, particularly in developing countries.

## Highlights

- **Recent Case Decline**: A notable decrease in tuberculosis cases has been observed in Chinese mainland over the past decade, from 95,746 in August 2015 to 54,614 in August 2025, indicating ongoing control efforts. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations**: Case numbers show a seasonal pattern, generally higher in March and significantly lower in February, indicating factors like weather and healthcare access might be influential. <br/>

- **Deaths Trends**: The number of tuberculosis-related deaths fluctuates, showing a general decline post-2023, suggesting improved treatment success rates and healthcare interventions. <br/>

- **Increase in Death Ratio**: Although decreased, the deaths-to-cases ratio has slightly increased, indicating potential concerns in late-stage detection and comprehensive treatment strategies. <br/>

Analysis of tuberculosis data from mainland China, spanning August 2015 to August 2025, reveals notable fluctuations in reported cases and deaths. Initially, in 2015 and 2016, there is a gradual upward trend, peaking in March 2016. Cases and deaths increased again from March 2017, with deaths hitting a peak in August 2017. These trends could relate to heightened transmission during these months or seasonal reporting patterns. Each year displays a characteristic dip in cases during winter months, possibly due to less mobility and social interaction.

As 2019 approached, cases declined, a shift that corresponds with the emergence of COVID-19 in 2020. This decline may be attributed to public health interventions or shifts in healthcare system focus. The low point in cases in February 2020 highlights this potential impact. In contrast, from 2022 onwards, case numbers remained lower than in earlier years, despite peaks in deaths, suggesting improved detection but challenges in managing severe cases. The latter years, particularly 2024 onwards, show a slight decrease in both cases and deaths. This could be attributed to post-pandemic improvements in healthcare systems, streamlined management, and prevention strategies, alongside regular seasonal variations influencing transmission rates. Overall, the data reflects the dynamic interactions between public health interventions, seasonal patterns, and emerging global health challenges.

Initially, between 2015 and 2019, death numbers vary with some peaks, notably in March 2017 with 212 deaths and a subsequent spike to 230 deaths in December 2019, even as cases decline towards the year's end. This period suggests intermittent increases in deaths possibly due to seasonal factors, the efficacy of public health responses, or changes in detection rates.

In 2020, there is a noticeable decline in TB cases and deaths, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic, reaching the lowest deaths in February 2021. This drop is likely due to the pandemic-driven health policy shifts which might have impacted TB detection and reporting efforts. Nonetheless, from 2022 onwards, there is a significant rise in deaths, peaking mid-2022 with figures exceeding 300 monthly. This may reflect improved case detection after the pandemic, adjustments in diagnostic criteria, or the effects of evolving treatment strategies. The period extending into 2025 shows a general trend of lower deaths, though spikes in certain months, such as December 2023 with 416 deaths, indicate ongoing challenges and varying impacts of interventions. These observations highlight the dynamic burden of TB, requiring attentive monitoring and adaptive healthcare strategies.
