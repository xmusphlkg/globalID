# Tuberculosis

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Primarily affecting the lungs, it can also spread to other organs. TB spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a persistent cough, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB can be latent or active; latent TB is non-contagious and asymptomatic, while active TB is symptomatic and can spread to others. Early detection and treatment with antibiotics are crucial to control the disease and prevent complications. TB remains a significant public health concern, particularly in low-resource settings.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Trend in Cases:** From 2014 to 2024, Tuberculosis cases have generally decreased, with notable fluctuations especially post-2020.<br/>

- **COVID-19 Impact:** The initial COVID-19 outbreak in early 2020 caused a sharp drop in reported cases, likely due to reduced healthcare access and reporting.<br/>

- **Rising Mortality Rates:** Recent years, particularly 2022-2024, show an increase in deaths despite lower case numbers, suggesting either more severe disease or delays in diagnosis and treatment.<br/>

- **Seasonal Variations:** Consistent seasonal patterns exist, with notable peaks in early spring (March) and declines in winter months.<br/>

The dataset on Tuberculosis (TB) cases and deaths in mainland China from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals several significant trends. Between 2014 and 2016, TB cases and deaths fluctuated, with cases ranging from 75,541 to 114,627 and deaths peaking at 173 in some months. Seasonal variations are evident, with typically lower cases in February and December, possibly due to holidays like Chinese New Year.

From 2017 onwards, there was an uptick in reported cases, often exceeding 90,000 monthly, and increased variability in deaths, peaking at 218 in August 2017. This could indicate either a rise in TB incidence or improved detection. The advent of COVID-19 in 2020 significantly impacted TB reporting, with cases dropping to 44,933 in February 2020 due to healthcare system challenges.

Post-2020, TB case numbers gradually recovered but generally remained below pre-pandemic levels, highlighting potential long-term impacts on the healthcare system. By 2022, both cases and deaths showed significant monthly fluctuations, with deaths rising to over 300 per month, potentially due to more virulent TB strains or healthcare access issues. By 2024, while TB cases seemed to stabilize, sporadic surges in deaths persisted. This indicates an urgent need for enhanced TB control measures and reinforced healthcare strategies to mitigate mortality despite stabilization in TB incidences.
