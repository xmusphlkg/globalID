# Tuberculosis

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious infection caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis, primarily affecting the lungs, though it can impact other body parts. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. TB is one of the leading causes of death from infectious diseases globally, with millions of new cases annually. Symptoms include persistent cough, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. Diagnosis often involves skin tests, blood tests, chest X-rays, and sputum analysis. Treatment typically requires a prolonged course of antibiotics. Vaccination with the Bacillus Calmette-Gu√©rin (BCG) vaccine offers partial protection against TB.

## Highlights

- **Decrease in TB Cases**: From 2020 onwards, there is a notable decline in TB cases from over 75,000 to around 53,180 by June 2025, indicating successful intervention measures.<br/>
- **Fluctuating Death Rates**: Death rates remain relatively high, with deaths peaking at 416 in December 2023, then fluctuating around 200-300 monthly by mid-2025.<br/>
- **Seasonal Variation**: There is a recurring trend of higher TB cases typically observed in March and April, possibly linked to seasonal factors.<br/>
- **Impact of COVID-19**: A significant drop in cases and deaths was seen in early 2020, potentially due to COVID-19 responses affecting healthcare access and disease monitoring.<br/>

The dataset on Tuberculosis (TB) cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 shows significant trends and shifts. Initially, from mid-2015 through 2019, TB cases follow a relatively stable pattern with seasonal variations, peaking typically in March each year. This pattern aligns with higher transmission rates in colder months when more people remain indoors. There is a noticeable decline in TB cases from 2020, potentially due to improved healthcare measures or broader policy changes. 

TB-related fatalities, however, increased from early 2020 through 2024, peaking well above previous years, contrasting with the decline in case numbers. This rise in mortality could be linked to changes in the affected population's health status or interruptions in healthcare services during the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite the declining case numbers towards the latter part of this period, mortality remains high, indicating possible shifts in case severity or treatment access issues.

In summary, while TB cases have decreased, the rising mortality highlights challenges that require attention, including maintaining effective TB control programs. The data underscores the need for resilient healthcare strategies to manage TB effectively, especially during disruptions like pandemics, and emphasizes the importance of continuous monitoring and policy adjustments to address both incidence and fatality.
