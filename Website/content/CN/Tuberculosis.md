# Tuberculosis

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, primarily affecting the lungs but potentially impacting other body parts. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a persistent cough, chest pain, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB can be latent, where the bacteria remain inactive without symptoms, or active, where symptoms manifest and the disease is contagious. Diagnosis involves skin tests, blood tests, and chest X-rays. Treatment requires a long-term course of antibiotics. TB remains a significant global health challenge, particularly in developing countries.

## Highlights



- **Decreasing Trend in Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, tuberculosis cases in China have significantly decreased, indicating effective public health measures and improved management. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Death Rates**: Despite fewer cases, death rates fluctuated, with a notable rise in 2022 and 2023, suggesting challenges in treatment or reporting. <br/>

- **COVID-19 Impact**: The sharp drop in cases during 2020 and 2021 likely reflects the impact of COVID-19 measures, such as lockdowns, which reduced TB transmission. <br/>

- **Recent Stabilization**: By February 2025, both cases and deaths have stabilized, indicating a new equilibrium in TB control efforts. <br/>

The data on tuberculosis (TB) cases and deaths in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several trends and fluctuations. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, there is a noticeable seasonal pattern with peaks typically occurring in March and April, followed by a gradual decline towards the end of each year. This pattern is consistent with the known seasonal variation in TB incidence, often attributed to factors such as climate and human behavior. During this period, the number of cases generally remained above 80,000 per month, with deaths fluctuating between 100 and 230 monthly.

A significant decline in both cases and deaths is observed starting in 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic. This reduction could be attributed to public health measures such as lockdowns, which may have inadvertently reduced TB transmission. However, the data also shows a concerning increase in the number of deaths relative to cases from 2022 onwards, suggesting potential challenges in TB management or reporting during the pandemic. By 2024, the number of cases continues to decline, but the death rate remains relatively high, indicating a possible shift in the disease's severity or healthcare system challenges in managing TB effectively. This trend underscores the importance of maintaining robust TB control programs even amidst other public health crises.

The data on tuberculosis (TB) deaths in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends and fluctuations. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, the number of deaths per month generally ranged between 120 and 230, with some seasonal variations. Notably, there was a peak in deaths in December 2019, which coincides with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, potentially affecting TB diagnosis and treatment services. This period also shows a gradual decline in reported cases, which might reflect improved TB control measures or changes in reporting practices.

From 2020 onwards, the data indicates a significant increase in TB deaths, particularly from 2022 to 2023, where monthly deaths frequently exceeded 300. This rise could be attributed to several factors, including disruptions in healthcare services due to the pandemic, leading to delayed diagnosis and treatment. The data for 2024 and early 2025 shows a slight decrease in deaths, suggesting a possible stabilization or improvement in TB management. However, the persistent high number of deaths underscores the need for continued public health efforts to address TB, including strengthening healthcare systems, ensuring timely diagnosis and treatment, and addressing any pandemic-related healthcare disruptions.
