# Tuberculosis

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease primarily affecting the lungs, caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. It is transmitted through airborne particles when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a persistent cough, chest pain, and coughing up blood, along with systemic signs like fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB can be latent or active; latent TB does not show symptoms but can progress to active disease. Diagnosis typically involves a skin test, blood test, chest X-ray, and sputum analysis. Treatment includes a prolonged course of multiple antibiotics.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in Cases:** The monthly number of reported TB cases in the Chinese mainland has generally declined from July 2014 to July 2024, with the highest count in early 2015 decreasing over the years.<br/>
- **Observed Fluctuations in Deaths:** TB deaths have exhibited fluctuations, increasing sharply from December 2022 through early 2023, before showing some decline by mid-2024.<br/>
- **Recent Stabilization:** Throughout 2024, both cases and deaths have shown signs of stabilization at lower levels compared to previous peaks, indicating possible improved control measures.<br/>
- **Potential Challenges:** Despite the decline, the recent uptick in cases between May and July 2024 suggests vigilance is essential to prevent resurgence.<br/>

The tuberculosis (TB) data from mainland China between July 2014 and July 2024 shows notable fluctuations in cases and deaths. Initially, from 2014 to 2019, TB cases exhibited a gradual decline with seasonal variations. However, in 2020, there was a significant drop in reported cases, likely due to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, which affected healthcare services and reduced social interactions, possibly leading to underreporting during lockdowns. After 2020, cases began to rise again but did not reach the pre-pandemic levels by 2024.

TB mortality data reveals a slightly different pattern, with relatively stable deaths from 2014 until the end of 2019, despite fluctuations. During the pandemic, even though cases sharply declined, mortality did not decrease at the same rate, suggesting possible delays in treatment or increased disease severity. Post-2021, there was a noticeable rise in TB deaths, peaking in 2022 and staying elevated through 2024. This trend may indicate the long-term impacts of pandemic-related disruptions on TB control, such as deferred diagnosis and treatment, as well as potential changes in TB strain virulence or complications due to co-infections.

Overall, these patterns highlight the importance of continuous TB control efforts, even during global health emergencies, to mitigate adverse effects on both case incidence and mortality.

From 2014 to 2024, the data on tuberculosis (TB) deaths in mainland China reveals several patterns and trends. Between 2014 and 2019, the monthly deaths from TB were relatively stable, ranging mostly between 120 to 230 deaths per month, with some seasonal fluctuations possibly reflecting underlying epidemiological or healthcare access variations. Notably, there were slight increases in deaths during March and August across several years, indicating potential seasonal peaks in mortality, which could be related to delays in treatment initiation or increased transmission in the preceding months.

However, starting in 2022, there is a marked rise in TB-related deaths, peaking significantly with several months reporting over 300 deaths, reaching a high of 416 in December 2023. This upward trend in mortality despite a relatively stable or declining number of cases in some months suggests a potential increase in the severity or complexity of TB cases, challenges in healthcare delivery, or the emergence of drug-resistant TB strains. The sharp increase observed from 2022 onwards necessitates further investigation into factors such as changes in TB management practices, healthcare disruptions possibly due to the COVID-19 pandemic, or shifts in population health dynamics.
