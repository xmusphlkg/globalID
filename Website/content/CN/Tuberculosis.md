# Tuberculosis

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Primarily affecting the lungs, it can also impact other organs. TB spreads through airborne droplets when an infected individual coughs or sneezes. Though many exposed people remain asymptomatic due to latent infection, active TB can cause severe symptoms such as persistent cough, fever, weight loss, and night sweats. Diagnosis often involves a skin test, blood test, chest X-ray, and sputum analysis. Treatment requires prolonged use of antibiotics, typically lasting six to nine months. TB remains a significant global health issue, particularly in low and middle-income countries.

## Highlights

- **Tuberculosis cases have significantly declined**: From August 2014 to August 2024, cases dropped from 97,039 to 57,944, a notable decrease in reported infections.<br/>
- **Death rates have fluctuated**: The number of deaths peaked at 416 in December 2023, with recent months (2024) showing a range between 224 and 383.<br/>
- **Impact of COVID-19**: 2020 and 2021 saw dramatic reductions in TB cases, likely due to COVID-related public health measures, with cases in 2020 dropping as low as 44,933 in February.<br/>
- **Recent trends indicate slight recovery**: Although cases have decreased since 2014, recent months in 2024 suggest slight upticks in deaths, signaling the need for ongoing vigilance.<br/>

Tuberculosis (TB) cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 show fluctuations in both cases and deaths. A pattern of higher cases is evident in certain months, particularly in March, such as March 2015 (113,242 cases) and March 2016 (114,627 cases). This may be influenced by seasonal factors affecting transmission. Notably, TB cases dropped significantly in 2020, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic, potentially due to reduced healthcare access and shifts in public health focus.

Deaths remained relatively stable from 2014 to 2021, generally ranging from 100 to 200 per month. However, from 2022 onward, there was a notable rise in deaths, with July 2022 reporting 367 deaths, and several months in 2023 and 2024 exceeding 300 deaths. This increase in mortality, despite fewer cases in some months, raises concerns about healthcare capacity or the possibility of more severe TB strains. The data suggests a need for closer monitoring of recent years to understand the drivers behind rising mortality.

The tuberculosis-related deaths in mainland China from August 2014 to August 2024 show significant variability. Early in the period, from 2014 to 2016, monthly deaths ranged between 99 and 176, with case numbers fluctuating but remaining relatively stable. From 2017 onwards, there was a gradual increase in deaths, peaking in 2022, when monthly deaths frequently exceeded 300. Despite the rising death toll, the number of cases didn't always correspond proportionally.

Particularly from 2020 onwards, the number of cases showed a slight decrease, but deaths remained higher than in previous years. By late 2023 and into 2024, the data shows some of the highest death tolls, including 416 deaths in December 2023 and 383 in January 2024. These spikes may point to emerging challenges, such as treatment inefficacy, changes in TB virulence, or other health system stressors.

Overall, the increasing mortality rate, especially after 2022, suggests a need for deeper investigation into the causes of the rising deaths, especially given the declining or stable case counts in recent years.
