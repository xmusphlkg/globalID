# Tuberculosis

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis that primarily affects the lungs, though it can also impact other parts of the body. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. TB symptoms include persistent cough, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. Historically a deadly disease, TB can now be treated with antibiotics through a course lasting several months. Despite global efforts to control and reduce its spread, TB remains a significant public health challenge, particularly in low and middle-income countries with high burden rates.

## Highlights

- Tuberculosis cases show a general decreasing trend in Chinese mainland, dropping from over 90,000 monthly cases in 2015 to around 55,000 by September 2025. <br/>
- Death toll reveals concerning fluctuations, with heightened counts peaking in mid-2023; however, deaths have seen a downward trend again by 2025, standing at 262 in September. <br/>
- Intervention strategies might have impacted the case reduction, but mortality remains a significant issue, indicating the need for enhanced treatment access or quality. <br/>
- Seasonal variations hint at potential environmental or behavioral factors influencing case numbers, such as peaks in March or April followed by a decline towards the year's end. <br/>

The tuberculosis (TB) data from mainland China over the years reveal notable patterns and fluctuations in both case incidence and mortality rates. Initially, from 2015 to 2019, TB cases exhibited a seasonal trend, peaking each March and steadily declining through the year. For instance, cases rose significantly to 114,627 in March 2016, only to drop continuously thereafter. During this period, the overall pattern shows a gradual reduction, with cases falling from 95,081 in September 2015 to 71,631 in December 2019. However, deaths have risen noticeably during these years, peaking in December 2020 at 230 from earlier averages below 200.

From 2020 onwards, the data reflect a disruption likely influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic, evident in the sharp drop in reported cases during the early months of the pandemic with a notable dip to 44,933 cases in February 2020. As mobility restrictions and healthcare resources were redirected, TB surveillance and diagnosis may have been affected. While cases slightly recovered over the following months, the death toll increased sharply, peaking sporadically above 300 per month from January 2022 onward, signifying the potential impact of delayed diagnosis and treatment. By 2024 and 2025, cases stabilize somewhat at lower levels compared to previous years, but deaths remain high, indicating ongoing challenges in mortality management. Overall, these data underscore the importance of continuous and adaptive TB response strategies amidst evolving public health circumstances.

The tuberculosis data from the Chinese mainland showcases notable trends from 2015 to 2025. Between 2015 and 2019, cases range from approximately 70,000 to 110,000 monthly, with deaths typically less than 250 per month. This period displays fluctuating patterns, with case peaks occurring typically in March and April, often aligning with increases in deaths. This suggests potential influences such as seasonal changes or healthcare resource constraints during high transmission periods.

In 2020, there's a distinct shift: while monthly cases decrease, deaths start to rise notably from 2021 onwards, frequently exceeding previous norms. This is particularly evident from 2022, where we observe a significant and sudden increase in the death toll, with monthly deaths doubling, reflecting healthcare challenges potentially linked to the COVID-19 pandemic's impact. Despite consistent case numbers, the rise in mortality suggests potential disruptions in tuberculosis treatment and healthcare delivery, highlighting vulnerabilities in health system capacities and treatment effectiveness.

Overall, the data underscores the complex interplay between TB cases, deaths, and external factors affecting healthcare delivery, especially during the pandemic. The observed trends highlight the crucial need for strengthening health systems and ensuring resilient treatment strategies to manage TB effectively, especially in times of broader public health challenges that can exacerbate mortality rates even when case numbers fluctuate.
