# Tuberculosis

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which primarily affects the lungs but can also impact other parts of the body. It spreads through the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a persistent cough, chest pain, fatigue, weight loss, fever, and night sweats. TB is a major global health concern due to its ability to remain dormant and re-emerge, as well as the rising challenge of drug-resistant strains. Early detection and adherence to a prescribed course of antibiotics are essential for effective treatment and prevention of transmission.

## Highlights

- **Declining Cases:** There has been a noticeable decrease in tuberculosis cases from October 2018, with a continued downward trend into October 2024, where cases dropped from 84,680 to 52,943. <br/>
- **Fluctuating Death Rates:** Deaths have experienced fluctuations but show an upward trend from around late 2021, reaching a peak of 416 in December 2023 before slightly decreasing. <br/>
- **COVID-19 Impact:** The data from early 2020 indicates a significant drop in cases during COVID-19 restrictions, but cases rebounded in 2021 as restrictions eased. <br/>
- **Recent Patterns:** As of October 2024, both cases and deaths have shown a recent decline, potentially indicating effective public health measures and improved tuberculosis management. <br/>

Between 2014 and 2019, the number of reported TB cases generally decreased, with fluctuations around 70,000 to over 100,000 cases monthly, while deaths showed a slight upward trend, indicating potential challenges in managing more severe cases during those years. In 2020, there was a sharp decline in TB cases likely linked to COVID-19 public health measures, which may have reduced TB transmission due to increased hygiene and movement restrictions. However, from 2021 onwards, TB cases started to increase again, suggesting a possible normalization towards pre-pandemic levels or improved detection efforts.

By 2022, TB deaths became substantially higher, outpacing previous years, which could point to delays in healthcare delivery or heightened severity of TB cases post-pandemic. From 2023 to 2024, even as TB cases continued a modest decline, deaths remained relatively high, signaling ongoing difficulties in managing fatal TB outcomes or potential gaps in healthcare resources. The analysis of Tuberculosis (TB) deaths in the Chinese mainland reveals concerning trends across the years. From late 2014 to early 2017, TB deaths gradually increased with notable peaks, such as the March 2017 peak at 212. This period reflects a relatively stable number of cases but increasing deaths, suggesting possible challenges in treatment effectiveness or reporting accuracy. Moving towards 2017 through to 2020, fatalities rose even as the number of cases slightly decreased. The dip in reported cases during 2020, potentially influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic, coupled with sustained high deaths, may indicate disruptions in healthcare services or latent effects on TB health responses.

From 2021 onwards, TB deaths markedly increased, with figures surpassing 300 in early 2022 and peaking at 416 in December 2023. Despite declining reported cases, the persistent high mortality might suggest underreporting, insufficient diagnostics, or systemic health issues. By October 2024, the case numbers decreased, but deaths did not show a proportionate drop, highlighting a critical need for enhanced public health strategies, improved diagnostics, treatment access, and potentially vaccine deployment. The sustained increase in mortality rates underscores the urgency of bolstering TB control programs and addressing healthcare access disparities to curb the high fatality rates observed in recent years.
