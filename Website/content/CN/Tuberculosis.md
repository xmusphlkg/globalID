# Tuberculosis

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It primarily affects the lungs but can also impact other parts of the body. TB spreads through the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes, making close contact a significant risk factor. Symptoms include a persistent cough, chest pain, and coughing up blood, accompanied by fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB is a major global health issue, causing significant morbidity and mortality. Diagnosis typically involves a skin test, blood tests, and chest X-rays, while treatment requires a long course of antibiotics.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decline in TB Cases:** Since mid-2021, there has been a notable decrease in cases, with July 2025 recording 57,585 cases, a significant drop from previous years. <br/>

- **Fluctuating Mortality:** Though TB cases declined, related deaths rose from 2021 through 2023 before decreasing in 2025, indicating possible improvements in treatment. <br/>

- **Long-term Improvement:** TB incidence peaked from 2016 to 2019; recent figures are nearly half, suggesting a long-term positive trend. <br/>

- **Impact of COVID-19 Measures:** The sharp decrease in cases in early 2020 may reflect the effect of public health measures adopted during the COVID-19 pandemic. <br/>

The analysis of tuberculosis (TB) cases on the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reflects significant fluctuations. Initially, cases saw a declining trend, from approximately 102,986 in July 2015 to 71,631 in December 2019. This decrease was influenced by public health interventions and seasonal variations, with notable drops during February each year due to cold weather impacting disease spread. Starting in 2020, there was a noticeable decline in reported cases, likely affected by the COVID-19 pandemic. The pandemic shifted healthcare priorities and restrictions on movement may have reduced TB transmission, while possibly impacting case reporting accuracy.

Meanwhile, the TB-related death toll reveals crucial insights. Despite declining case numbers, deaths climbed significantly, peaking in recent years, particularly during 2022 and 2023. This rise suggests challenges in disease management or delays in medical intervention. The December 2023 spike to 416 deaths is particularly concerning, potentially indicating emerging drug-resistant TB strains or gaps in healthcare continuity. This calls for strategic improvements in case management, enhanced treatment accessibility, and strengthened surveillance systems. Addressing these issues is essential to curb fatalities and manage disease prevalence in the evolving health landscape affected by recent global events. Continued focus on comprehensive healthcare strategies will be crucial in managing TB effectively amidst these dynamics.

The analysis of tuberculosis (TB) deaths in Mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025 shows significant trends. Initially, from July 2015 to December 2017, TB deaths fluctuated between 127 and 218 per month with a rising trend and occasional peaks, notably in March 2017, which saw 212 deaths. Moving into 2018 and 2019, death counts remained relatively high, peaking at 230 in December 2019, despite declining case numbers in late 2019. This may reflect seasonal influences or variations in disease severity and reporting practices.

In 2020, amidst the COVID-19 pandemic, TB cases notably dropped, possibly due to increased public health measures. However, TB deaths surged dramatically in 2022, exceeding 300 per month and persisting through 2023. This rise in deaths, despite fewer cases, suggests increased severity, healthcare system strain, or more accurate mortality reporting during this period. By mid-2024 through 2025, TB mortality began to decline, returning closer to pre-pandemic levels, potentially indicating improved TB management and healthcare interventions or natural epidemic transitions.

Overall, these patterns highlight the complexity of TB dynamics and underscore the importance of continuous surveillance and flexible public health strategies in managing infectious diseases like tuberculosis, especially amidst other global health challenges.
