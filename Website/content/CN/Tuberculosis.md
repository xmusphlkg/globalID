# Tuberculosis

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease primarily affecting the lungs, caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. Symptoms include a persistent cough, fever, night sweats, and weight loss. TB can be latent or active, with latent TB being asymptomatic and non-contagious. Diagnosis involves skin tests, blood tests, and chest X-rays. Treatment typically requires a long-term antibiotic regimen lasting six to nine months. Prevention includes vaccination with the Bacillus Calmette-Gu√©rin (BCG) vaccine and public health measures to reduce transmission.

## Highlights

- **Case Decline Over the Years:** Tuberculosis cases have gradually decreased from ~95,893 cases in September 2014 to 55,609 in September 2024, indicating sustained progress in disease control. <br/>  
- **Mortality Increase:** Despite declining cases, deaths rose from 151 in September 2014 to 247 in September 2024, suggesting possible challenges in treatment or vulnerable populations. <br/>  
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Peaks in cases are commonly observed in spring months (e.g., March) across years, highlighting seasonal dynamics. <br/>  
- **Impact of COVID-19:** 2020 shows a dramatic drop in cases during the pandemic but rebounds in subsequent years, underscoring the impact of healthcare disruptions. <br/>

Analyzing tuberculosis (TB) cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 reveals notable trends. Between 2014 and 2016, case numbers fluctuate, peaking in spring months like March and April. For example, March 2015 and March 2016 show higher cases at 113,242 and 114,627, respectively. This pattern might indicate seasonal factors affecting TB transmission or detection. Over the years, there is a gradual decline in cases, possibly due to improved public health interventions, diagnostics, or treatment availability.

Mortality data shows a concerning increase in TB-related deaths in recent years, particularly in late 2022 and 2023, where deaths exceed 300 per month despite declining case numbers. This suggests challenges such as resistant TB strains or healthcare system strains affecting vulnerable groups. In 2020, both cases and deaths drop significantly, likely due to COVID-19 pandemic measures disrupting TB transmission or potentially leading to underreporting.

From 2022 to 2024, while TB cases decrease, there is a rise in mortality, peaking in December 2023. This trend suggests possible issues like treatment failures or healthcare access delays. While the overall case trend is declining, the increase in mortality highlights the need for continued monitoring and adaptation of public health strategies to address emerging TB control challenges.
