# Tuberculosis

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, primarily affecting the lungs but capable of involving other organs. It spreads through airborne droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. TB can be latent or active; latent TB shows no symptoms, while active TB causes coughing, weight loss, fever, and night sweats. Risk factors include weakened immunity, malnutrition, and crowded living conditions. Although treatable with antibiotics, inadequate treatment can lead to drug-resistant TB. TB remains a major global health issue, notably in low- and middle-income countries, despite widespread public health efforts and vaccination programs.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in TB Cases:** Monthly tuberculosis cases dropped from over 100,000 in 2015 to around 55,000 by May 2025, indicating improving control. <br/>
- **Fluctuating Mortality:** Deaths rose sharply between 2022–2024, peaking at over 400 in late 2023, but fell to 228 in May 2025—still above pre-2021 levels. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends Continue:** TB cases remain higher in spring and summer, consistent with long-term seasonal transmission patterns. <br/>
- **Pandemic Disruption Evident:** COVID-19 caused sharp drops in reported cases in early 2020 and contributed to spikes in TB mortality during the following years. <br/>

Observing the data from 2015-2025, there is a noticeable fluctuation in reported TB cases, with a higher number of cases between 2015 and 2018, followed by a declining trend until 2020. Interestingly, after 2020, the number of cases and deaths demonstrate another wave of increment, reaching peaks in certain months, such as March 2023, before steadily declining again. Notably, the month of February consistently registered lower cases compared to preceding months, possibly reflecting seasonal variations or reporting practices.

The yearly cycles suggest an underlying pattern or influence related to socio-economic changes, public health interventions, or operational factors within health systems. The dramatic reduction in cases and subsequent spike in deaths reported in December 2023 indicate potential anomalies or external factors impacting TB monitoring and management during this period. Various factors including practices of diagnosis, reporting accuracy, the effectiveness of control measures, and the socio-economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic could explain these variations. The consistent presence of TB and concurrent mortality underscores the necessity for ongoing vigilance and adaptation of health strategies to combat TB in the future.

China's tuberculosis (TB) death trends from 2015 through mid-2025 show several significant phases. During 2015–2019, monthly deaths stayed within a relatively narrow band (mostly between 120 and 220), with modest seasonal fluctuations and a loose correlation to case volume. However, starting in 2020, the impact of COVID-19—particularly restricted mobility and health service disruptions—might have contributed to a slight drop in detected TB cases but not a proportional reduction in deaths. This divergence begins to widen noticeably in 2022, where the death toll climbs sharply despite relatively flat or declining case figures. For example, 2022 saw monthly TB deaths consistently exceed 300 even as reported cases dwindled under 70,000 per month, suggesting a worsening case fatality rate or under-detection of mild to moderate infections.

The 2022–2024 window reflects the most concerning increase in mortality, with multiple months seeing TB deaths crossing 340, peaking at 416 in December 2023. This mortality spike, out of proportion with the decreasing TB caseload, may suggest systemic diagnostic delays, increased prevalence of drug-resistant strains, or indirect effects of COVID-era public health disruption. Although the death count begins to taper slightly in 2025, the persistent elevation compared to pre-2020 levels highlights a structural shift warranting deeper investigation, such as shifts in demographic vulnerability, treatment initiation delays, or healthcare access inequality during and after pandemic years.
