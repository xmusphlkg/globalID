# Tuberculosis

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection primarily affecting the lungs, caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Spread through airborne particles, it occurs when an infected person coughs or sneezes. TB can be latent or active; in latent TB, bacteria remain dormant without causing symptoms, while active TB leads to severe cough, weight loss, fever, and night sweats. Despite being preventable and treatable, TB remains a significant global health issue, particularly in low- and middle-income countries. Treatment involves a prolonged course of antibiotics, and the BCG vaccine offers some protection, especially in children.

## Highlights

- Tuberculosis cases in the Chinese mainland have declined from 114,627 in March 2016 to 50,265 in October 2025, indicating successful health interventions and campaigns. <br/>
- Mortality rates increased between 2019 and 2023, peaking at 416 deaths in December 2023. They have since reduced to 240 deaths as of October 2025, reflecting improved treatment and management strategies.<br/>
- Seasonal variations are evident, with case peaks typically in March and April each year, underscoring the need for targeted prevention efforts during these months.<br/>
- Recent data suggests stabilization of both cases and deaths, indicating a promising trend toward sustained control and better healthcare responses.<br/>

Reviewing the Tuberculosis (TB) data from mainland China, the reported cases between 2015 and early 2021 fluctuated around 70,000 to 100,000 monthly. Seasonal peaks often occurred in spring (March and April), suggesting potential patterns in disease transmission. Deaths remained mostly stable, typically below 200 monthly, though some months saw higher figures. A notable decrease in TB cases and deaths in early 2020 coincides with COVID-19's onset, likely affecting TB tracking as public health resources shifted, and restrictions possibly influenced spread dynamics.

Starting late 2021, an alarming rise in TB deaths emerged, with counts frequently exceeding 300 and reaching 400 by December 2023. Such increases point towards heightened disease lethality, possibly due to drug-resistant strains or healthcare delivery challenges. Meanwhile, the significant drop in cases by late 2022 hints at improved containment or reporting inconsistencies. By mid-2025, both cases and deaths show signs of stabilization, although higher mortality rates persist, indicating ongoing struggles in reducing TB lethality despite better disease spread control. This overview suggests that while containment efforts may be effective, addressing underlying mortality factors remains crucial. The trends highlight the importance of sustained focus on TB management amidst evolving public health landscapes, particularly against the backdrop of parallel health crises.

The epidemiological data on tuberculosis (TB) in mainland China shows distinct patterns in cases and deaths over the years. From 2015 to early 2019, TB deaths were relatively stable despite fluctuations in case numbers, suggesting potentially effective management efforts or reporting variances. However, beginning in 2019, TB deaths appeared to rise more sharply compared to the number of reported cases, peaking notably in early 2022 when deaths reached 304 for January, even as cases fluctuated.

Mid-2022 onwards reveals an intriguing pattern: high mortality rates persist despite declining case numbers. For instance, in April 2022, cases dropped to 61,185, but deaths soared to 341. This decoupling hints at factors like variations in disease severity, healthcare response, or reporting changes, potentially exacerbated by concurrent global health challenges. The period from late 2022 into 2023 shows deaths remaining significantly high, indicating long-term impacts of increased TB-related mortality from prior years.

By late 2023, while TB deaths remained elevated relative to 2015 levels, the stabilization in case numbers suggests potential improvements in managing and reporting the disease post-crisis. Understanding these shifts is crucial for implementing targeted interventions and crafting responses to evolving TB epidemiology in China amid and following global health disturbances.
