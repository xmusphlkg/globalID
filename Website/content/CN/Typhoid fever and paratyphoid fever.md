# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: December 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute, systemic infections caused by Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi and serotypes Paratyphi A, B, or C, respectively. Both diseases are primarily transmitted through ingestion of contaminated food or water. Symptoms typically include prolonged fever, abdominal pain, headache, and malaise. Severe cases may lead to complications such as intestinal hemorrhage or perforation. While typhoid fever tends to be more severe, the clinical presentations of both are similar. These diseases are major public health concerns in regions lacking adequate sanitation and clean water.

## Highlights

- Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in the Chinese mainland have declined significantly from 2015 to December 2025, falling from monthly peaks above 1,000 to below 700 cases in recent years.<br/>
- There is a consistent seasonal pattern, with higher case numbers in summer months (June–September), though overall seasonal peaks are less pronounced post-2020.<br/>
- Mortality remains extremely low, with most months recording zero deaths and only occasional single fatalities, indicating ongoing improvements in case management.<br/>
- As of December 2025, the situation is stable, with cases remaining at relatively low levels and effective control measures evident in the long-term downward trend.<br/>

Reported cases of typhoid and paratyphoid fever in mainland China from late 2015 to the end of 2025 display a distinct seasonal pattern, with incidence peaking consistently during the summer months, especially between June and September, and reaching the lowest points in winter. Peak monthly case counts often exceeded 1,000 throughout 2016-2019, notably in July and August, while in the years following 2020, a noticeable and sustained decrease in case numbers occurred. By 2023–2025, summer peaks rarely surpassed 700 cases, reflecting a substantial reduction in overall transmission. This long-term decline may relate to strengthened disease control measures, public health strategies, and especially the broader impact of non-pharmaceutical interventions and mobility restrictions during the COVID-19 pandemic, which also appeared as abrupt case drops from early 2020 onward.

Despite modest annual fluctuations, deaths remained strikingly rare (mostly zero or one per month), resulting in an exceptionally low case-fatality rate and pointing to improved clinical management and timely access to care. Notably, while monthly deaths sometimes increased slightly in specific years (e.g., mid-2017 and 2020), such events were exceptional and did not reflect a sustained trend. The evolving epidemiological profile — decreasing incidence, persistent seasonality, and scarce fatalities — suggests persistent but increasingly controlled endemicity, with transmission highly sensitive to environmental conditions and population behaviors.

Reported deaths from typhoid and paratyphoid fever in mainland China from late 2015 to the end of 2025 have been consistently low, with the case fatality rate near zero across nearly every month. Out of more than 100 reported months, deaths only sporadically occur, often as isolated cases, and the maximum observed in a single month was just three deaths (June 2017). Most months, regardless of fluctuations in case numbers—often higher during summer—do not show a corresponding rise in fatalities, indicating effective management and access to appropriate treatment.

No sustained clusters or upward trends in mortality are apparent, and there are long consecutive stretches with zero deaths, including recent years where deaths have become even rarer. The periodic deaths that do appear are scattered without seasonality or any cyclical pattern, further supporting the reliability of clinical care. The data do not signal outbreaks of heightened virulence or healthcare system stress; rather, the few deaths are likely attributable to isolated factors—a delay in care, comorbidities, or atypical severity. In summary, typhoid and paratyphoid fever remain present in the population, but fatal outcomes are extremely uncommon in the context of ongoing improvements in public health infrastructure and medical capabilities.
