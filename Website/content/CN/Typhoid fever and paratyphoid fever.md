# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: June 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by Salmonella Typhi and Salmonella Paratyphi bacteria, respectively. These diseases are primarily transmitted through ingestion of food or water contaminated with the feces of an infected person. Common symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, and constipation or diarrhea. Without timely treatment, these fevers can lead to severe complications and even death. They are prevalent in areas with inadequate sanitation and limited access to clean water. Vaccination and improved sanitation are key preventive measures, while antibiotics are crucial for treatment.

## Highlights

1. **Decline in Cases:** From 2015 to 2025, cases have consistently fallen, from over 1000 per month in 2015 to under 600 by June 2025, indicating improved control. <br/>  
2. **Low Mortality Rates:** Throughout the period, deaths have remained minimal, with the highest being three in June 2017, showing effective case management. <br/>  
3. **Seasonal Trends:** Cases tend to peak during the summer months (July to September), consistent with patterns associated with warmer climates and potential water contamination. <br/>  
4. **Recent Stability:** As of mid-2025, case numbers seem stable, suggesting effective interventions, although ongoing monitoring is essential to sustain this progress. <br/>

The data from mid-2015 to mid-2025 on Typhoid and Paratyphoid fever cases in the Chinese mainland highlights key trends and patterns. Initially, in June 2015, cases were relatively high at 1431, but there's a notable decline over the years, with June 2023 recording just 627 cases. Mortality rates are consistently low, with only sporadic deaths, implying effective treatment, improved sanitation, or better disease management. This general decline could be linked to enhanced public health measures and medical advancements.

Seasonal variations are pronounced, with the summer months (June to August) consistently showing higher case numbers each year; for instance, August 2018 saw 1466 cases. This aligns with Typhoid’s nature as a waterborne illness, where warmer months increase consumption of potentially contaminated water or food. Conversely, colder months regularly demonstrate fewer cases due to reduced exposure to contaminated sources.

Isolated deaths appear throughout the dataset but don’t correspond directly to the highest case months, indicating that fatalities might arise from specific incidents or vulnerabilities in certain populations, requiring targeted interventions. The overall pattern reveals the progress and ongoing challenges in managing Typhoid and Paratyphoid fevers in a large and complex population, emphasizing the importance of sustained public health efforts and infrastructure improvements.
