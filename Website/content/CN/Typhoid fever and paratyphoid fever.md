# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: December 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by the bacteria Salmonella Typhi and Salmonella Paratyphi, respectively. These diseases are transmitted through contaminated food and water, primarily in regions with inadequate sanitation. Symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, abdominal pain, and diarrhea or constipation. Without treatment, these fevers can lead to severe complications and even death. Vaccination and improved sanitation are key preventive measures. Antibiotics are effective treatments, but resistance is a growing concern. Both diseases are more prevalent in developing countries, affecting millions annually.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend in Cases:** Reported cases of typhoid and paratyphoid fever have decreased significantly from 918 in December 2014 to 308 in December 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks:** Higher case numbers are observed during summer months (June to August) each year, indicating seasonal variations. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate is low, with only sporadic deaths, reflecting effective disease management and treatment. <br/>
- **Stabilization in Recent Years:** From 2022 to 2024, case numbers have stabilized at lower levels, suggesting improved public health measures and increased awareness. <br/>

The data on typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in mainland China from December 2014 to December 2024 reveals several trends. Initially, there is a noticeable seasonal pattern, with cases generally peaking during the summer months (June to August) and declining during the winter months (December to February). This pattern is consistent with the transmission dynamics of typhoid fever, which is often linked to waterborne transmission and can be exacerbated by warmer temperatures and increased rainfall, leading to higher exposure to contaminated water sources.

Over the years, there is a gradual decline in the number of reported cases, particularly from 2020 onwards. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, better sanitation, and increased awareness and vaccination efforts. The data also shows a relatively low mortality rate, with only sporadic deaths reported, indicating effective clinical management and treatment of the disease. The reduction in cases and deaths over time suggests that interventions to control typhoid and paratyphoid fever in China have been effective, although continued vigilance and public health efforts are necessary to sustain these gains and further reduce the disease burden.
