# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by the Salmonella enterica serotypes Typhi and Paratyphi, respectively. Transmitted through contaminated food and water, these diseases are prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and hygiene, particularly in South Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, abdominal pain, and, in some cases, a rash. Without treatment, both can lead to severe complications or death. Vaccines exist for typhoid fever, offering partial protection, but there’s currently no vaccine for paratyphoid fever. Antibiotic treatment is the primary approach to managing these infections.

## Highlights

- Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in mainland China have significantly declined from monthly peaks of over 1,400 cases in 2015–2018 to under 300 cases by April 2025. <br/>
- Seasonal trends persist with higher case counts during summer months (June–August), though the amplitude has flattened in recent years. <br/>
- Annual deaths have remained low, with most months reporting zero fatalities; 2025 has only one death recorded as of April. <br/>
- The sustained downward trend since 2020 suggests improved disease surveillance, sanitation, and vaccination coverage. <br/>

The reported data on typhoid and paratyphoid fever in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals a consistent seasonal pattern and a decreasing long-term trend in case numbers. Peak incidences typically occurred mid-year, especially between June and September, likely reflecting environmental and behavioral factors favorable to bacterial transmission during warmer months. From a high of over 1400 cases monthly in summer months like June 2015 and August 2018, the data show a gradual decline in both seasonal peak intensity and baseline case levels. By 2023–2025, peaks were significantly lower, rarely surpassing 600 cases, which points to improved public health measures, surveillance, or reduced transmission.

Mortality remained low throughout the decade, with annual deaths never exceeding a few cases, and several years showing months with no recorded deaths. The highest monthly deaths were recorded in June 2017 (3 deaths) and in sporadic months across 2020–2021, reflecting rare but present fatal outcomes. Importantly, from 2022 onward, deaths appear nearly absent, coinciding with diminished case numbers. This mortality stability despite fluctuations in cases suggests sustained healthcare accessibility and effective case management even at periods of relative burden.

Out of over 100,000 reported cases during this period, only 19 deaths were recorded. Fatal outcomes emerged sporadically, typically isolated to a single month with just one recorded death, though there were brief spikes, notably in June 2017 with three deaths and in November 2020 with two deaths. No consistent seasonal mortality trends are evident, although warmer months (such as June–August) contributed to a slightly higher number of deaths, potentially due to increased disease transmission during these months. However, the monthly variance remains low overall.

Data from the past few years shows a sustained reduction in both reported cases and deaths. Specifically, no deaths were reported during the entire 2023 calendar year until October, and only one death was reported in early 2025. Notably, between February 2022 and January 2025—a span of 35 months—only three deaths were recorded. This ongoing decline may reflect improvements in diagnostics, timely treatment, public health infrastructure, and possibly vaccination strategies. The persistently low fatality indicates effective disease control and access to medical care, contributing to favorable outcomes in the vast majority of cases.
