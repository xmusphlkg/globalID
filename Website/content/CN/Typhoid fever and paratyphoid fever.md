# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by the bacteria Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi and Salmonella Paratyphi, respectively. These infections are transmitted via contaminated food and water, leading to systemic illness. Common symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, and diarrhea or constipation. In severe cases, complications such as intestinal perforation can occur. These illnesses are prevalent in areas with inadequate sanitation and limited access to clean water. Diagnosis is typically confirmed through blood cultures, while treatment involves antibiotics. Vaccination and improved sanitation are key preventive measures in endemic regions.

## Highlights

- **Steady decline in incidence**: Reported typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases have decreased significantly from over 1,000 monthly cases in 2015–2019 to fewer than 500 cases per month by 2025, reflecting improved control measures. <br/>
- **Low mortality rate**: Despite occasional deaths, overall fatalities have remained very low throughout the decade, indicating effective detection and treatment practices. <br/>
- **Seasonal trend reduction**: Earlier peak months (June–September) showed higher seasonal incidence, but recent years display a diminished seasonal pattern with less fluctuation across months. <br/>
- **Current situation in May 2025**: A total of 459 cases and no deaths were recorded, aligning with a stable trend of low incidence and mortality in recent years. <br/>

From 2015 to 2025, the incidence of typhoid and paratyphoid fever in mainland China exhibited clear seasonal and temporal patterns, with a consistent trend of case surges in the summer months—particularly from May through September—and declines in the winter. Peaks were most prominent in June through August, with the highest monthly case count occurring in August 2018 (1466 cases). This seasonality aligns with environmental and behavioral conditions that favor transmission, including elevated temperatures and increased food and water exposure risks. Over the years, a gradual decline in annual case totals is evident, particularly after 2019. For example, while 2017–2018 consistently reported monthly figures above 1000 during peak seasons, post-2021 rarely exceeded 700 in those same months.

Fatalities throughout the period remained low and sporadic, totaling under 20, with occasional single deaths spread across different months and years. No month recorded more than three deaths, and many months, particularly in recent years, recorded none. Since 2022, not only have deaths become even more infrequent, but the case fatality ratio has remained extremely low, reflecting improved clinical management and possibly enhanced early detection or community health interventions. The post-2020 decline may also relate to broader public health measures, including post-COVID sanitation campaigns, which may have additionally suppressed transmission of food- and waterborne infections like typhoid and paratyphoid fever.

For instance, in June 2017, there was the highest monthly fatality count (three deaths) despite similar or even higher case numbers occurring in other mid-year months without corresponding mortality, such as August 2018 (1,466 cases, 0 deaths). This irregularity implies external influences on mortality—possibly variations in healthcare access, delays in diagnosis, or demographic susceptibility—rather than sheer infection burden.

Temporal clustering of deaths offers further insight, notably in certain high-fatal months such as November–December 2020, which saw a spike to four deaths within two months (despite moderate case numbers), deviating from surrounding months without fatalities. A few outlier years, particularly 2017 and 2020, contributed disproportionately to the total death toll. In contrast, recent years, especially from 2022 to 2025, exhibited a sharp decline in mortality with only two confirmed deaths over a span of nearly four years, suggesting improvements in clinical management, surveillance, or disease control measures. The absence of a consistent seasonal pattern in deaths, unlike case distribution, reinforces the notion that mortality may be more reflective of healthcare responsiveness than transmission dynamics alone.
