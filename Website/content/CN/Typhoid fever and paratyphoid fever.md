# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi and serotype Paratyphi, respectively. These bacterial infections are transmitted through ingestion of contaminated food or water. Common in areas with poor sanitation, these fevers present with symptoms such as prolonged fever, fatigue, abdominal pain, headache, and, sometimes, a rash. Without treatment, these diseases can lead to severe complications or death. Diagnosis typically involves blood, stool, or bone marrow cultures. Vaccination, improved sanitation, and prompt antibiotic treatment are critical for prevention and control.

## Highlights

- Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in mainland China have steadily declined from 2014 to 2024, with a noticeable reduction in monthly case numbers from 1,787 in September 2014 to 612 in September 2024. <br/>
- Deaths remain rare, with a maximum of 3 deaths reported in June 2017; many months recorded zero fatalities, indicating improved disease management. <br/>
- Seasonal fluctuations are observed, with cases peaking during summer months (June to August), but the peaks are less pronounced in recent years. <br/>
- The consistent decrease in cases, especially post-2020, suggests enhanced public health interventions and better control over the spread of these diseases. <br/>

Analysis of typhoid and paratyphoid fever data from the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 reveals a clear seasonal pattern and a general trend of declining case numbers. The data shows peaks typically occurring mid-year, around June to August, with lower cases in winter months. This seasonality may be linked to environmental factors such as increased rainfall and temperature, which can affect the transmission of these waterborne and foodborne illnesses. Notably, the number of cases was highest in 2014, with a marked decrease over subsequent years, reaching lower figures by 2024.

Mortality rates remained consistently low across the years, with only sporadic deaths reported. The data indicates that while cases have been on a declining trend, deaths have not shown a significant increase, suggesting improvements in medical treatment and access to healthcare may have played a role in managing the disease more effectively. Despite the reduction in cases, continuous public health efforts and surveillance remain essential in maintaining control over typhoid and paratyphoid fever, especially given their potential for outbreaks in susceptible populations.

The analysis of deaths related to typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever in mainland China from 2014 to September 2024 reveals a generally low mortality rate, with sporadic fatal cases reported. Across the observed period, the number of deaths remains very low in relation to the number of cases. The dataset shows only 24 deaths out of 110,846 total cases reported over 10 years, highlighting an average case fatality rate (CFR) of approximately 0.02%. The deaths are scattered and do not follow a consistent seasonal or annual pattern, though isolated clusters (such as June 2017 with three deaths) appear, suggesting random fluctuations in mortality events.

Notably, despite fluctuations in the number of cases, there are many months with no recorded deaths, especially from 2015 through 2023, where several months show higher case counts (e.g., June 2018 with 1,466 cases and one death). This suggests effective case management or interventions in the healthcare system that have kept the mortality rate extremely low. There is no clear increasing or decreasing trend in both cases and deaths, with mortality peaking in certain months. These patterns could reflect localized outbreaks or variations in healthcare access, disease strain, or population immunity, but overall, typhoid and paratyphoid remain under control in terms of death toll.
