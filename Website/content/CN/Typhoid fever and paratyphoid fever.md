# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: October 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by the bacteria Salmonella Typhi and Salmonella Paratyphi, respectively. These diseases are primarily transmitted through contaminated food and water, predominantly affecting regions with poor sanitation and hygiene practices. Symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, and diarrhea or constipation. If untreated, these infections can lead to severe complications or death. Diagnosis is typically confirmed through blood, bone marrow, or stool cultures. While vaccines are available for typhoid fever, there is no vaccine for paratyphoid fever, emphasizing the importance of clean water, sanitation, and hygiene in prevention.

## Highlights

- There is a clear declining trend in the number of cases from 2015 through 2025, with a significant drop seen in recent years. <br/>
- Seasonal patterns are noticeable, with peaks generally occurring during the summer months of June to August. <br/>
- Deaths remain rare, with sporadic occurrences over the years, and the case-fatality rate has been consistently low. <br/>
- As of October 2025, the disease situation shows a reduction in both cases and deaths, indicating effective control and public health measures. <br/>

Analyzing typhoid and paratyphoid fever data from the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals several trends. Cases exhibit a seasonal pattern, peaking usually between May and September. For instance, cases exceeded 1,300 in July 2016 and August 2017, correlating with warmer months that likely increase transmission due to environmental factors facilitating bacteria replication or heightened exposure through contaminated food and water sources. Conversely, colder months show a decline, with numbers dropping to as low as 234 cases in January 2024, hinting at reduced transmission risks during these periods.

Overall, the data highlights a gradual downward trend in case numbers. After peaking in 2018 with several months exceeding a thousand cases, a decline is evident by 2025, as no month surpasses 700 cases. This reduction may indicate effective public health interventions, improved sanitation, and increased awareness, contributing to controlling the disease's spread. Deaths remain sporadic and generally low throughout the years, reflecting treatment effectiveness and authorities' success in managing severe cases. Notably, some months, such as June 2017 and June 2019, saw marginal increases in mortality, suggesting areas for potential enhancement in prevention and treatment protocols. Despite these fluctuations, the overall low death rate points to advancements in healthcare handling of typhoid complications.

Most months report zero fatalities, indicating a good level of control over these infections, potentially stemming from effective healthcare responses and public health strategies.

Despite fluctuations in the number of cases, there is no apparent direct correlation with deaths, suggesting that while outbreaks occur, they don't necessarily lead to increased fatalities. This pattern can be attributed to effective prevention, timely medical intervention, and high levels of awareness among the public and healthcare providers. The presence of isolated deaths, occurring every few months without a consistent pattern, may signify individual cases of complications or delayed treatment. Overall, the reported data indicate that while typhoid and paratyphoid continue to pose a public health challenge in terms of transmission, the impact in terms of mortality is kept largely in check, reflective of China's healthcare capabilities in managing these infectious diseases.
