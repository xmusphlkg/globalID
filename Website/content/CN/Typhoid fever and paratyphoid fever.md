# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: August 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are bacterial infections primarily caused by Salmonella typhi and Salmonella paratyphi, respectively. These illnesses are typically transmitted through contaminated food and water, leading to symptoms like high fever, fatigue, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and a rash. Common in areas with inadequate sanitation, both diseases can be life-threatening if untreated. Diagnosis is usually confirmed by blood, stool, or urine cultures. Treatment involves antibiotics, and preventive measures include improved sanitation, proper hygiene, and vaccination, particularly for travelers to endemic regions.

## Highlights

- The incidence of typhoid and paratyphoid fever has shown a general decline from 2014 to August 2024, with a significant drop in cases, particularly since 2019. <br/>
- Mortality remains low, with only occasional deaths recorded, suggesting improved disease management or early detection efforts. <br/>
- Seasonal trends indicate peaks in summer months, particularly in June to August, although these have become less pronounced in recent years. <br/>
- By August 2024, the number of cases stands at 550, representing continued efforts in controlling the spread of these diseases in the Chinese mainland. <br/>

The data on typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 demonstrates a steady decline in both the number of reported cases and fatalities. Peaks are observed seasonally, with higher incidences typically occurring during the summer months, especially in June through September. In 2014, the number of cases fluctuated between 918 and 1787 per month, while by 2024, case numbers had significantly decreased, with August recording 550 cases. This pattern suggests an overall reduction in transmission, likely attributable to improved public health interventions, water sanitation, and vaccination programs.

Mortality rates, although low across the entire period, show occasional spikes, with notable deaths occurring in 2014, 2016, 2017, 2018, and 2020. Most months reported zero deaths, but during certain months—such as June 2017 and February 2020—there were sudden increases in fatalities. These instances of mortality could be tied to localized outbreaks or co-infections, which require further epidemiological investigation. The overall trend points towards effective disease control, with significantly reduced case counts and fatalities over the decade.
