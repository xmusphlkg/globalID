# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by Salmonella enterica serotypes Typhi and Paratyphi, respectively. These diseases are transmitted via contaminated food and water, prevalent in regions with inadequate sanitation. Symptoms include prolonged fever, headache, nausea, loss of appetite, and abdominal pain. In severe cases, complications like intestinal perforation may occur. Diagnosing these fevers requires blood, stool, or bone marrow culture, and treatment involves antibiotics. Vaccines are available for typhoid fever but not for paratyphoid. Prevention focuses on improved sanitation, access to clean water, and vaccination in high-risk areas.

## Highlights

- **Downward Trend in Incidence**: Since 2015, the number of cases has consistently declined, particularly from an average of over 1000 monthly cases in 2015-2019 to less than 650 cases per month by September 2025.<br/>
- **Stable Mortality**: Despite fluctuations in case numbers, the mortality rate has remained low, with only sporadic deaths each year, indicating successful disease management.<br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: Incidence tends to rise during the warmer months, suggesting seasonal factors may play a role in disease transmission.<br/>
- **Recent Stabilization**: The data from 2025 shows relative stabilization in case numbers, reflecting effective control measures and public health interventions.<br/>

The data on Typhoid and Paratyphoid fever cases on the Chinese mainland from September 2015 to September 2025 indicates a general pattern of seasonality, with higher case numbers in the mid-year months, typically peaking around July and August. These months consistently reflect increased transmission possibly due to climatic or social factors such as monsoon season and increased movement of people. The trend over the years shows fluctuations, but with significant decreases in the number of cases starting from 2021 onwards. This decline could suggest potential improvements in public health interventions, sanitation, or perhaps the impact of vaccine deployment that might have intensified post-2020.

When focusing on mortality, the data shows a low death count compared to the number of cases, indicating perhaps an overall effective healthcare response that prevents mortality in affected individuals. The sporadic nature of deaths throughout the years, with no more than three deaths recorded in a single month, might suggest that while the system is capable of managing the disease, critical cases occasionally arise that lead to fatalities. The notable exception in deaths during more recent years, despite a lower case count, might reflect episodic challenges or vulnerabilities in specific health system capacities, highlighting areas needing attention for comprehensive disease management.
