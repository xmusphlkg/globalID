# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: May 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are serious infections caused by Salmonella enterica serotypes Typhi and Paratyphi A, B, and C, respectively. They are transmitted through ingestion of contaminated food or water. Common symptoms include prolonged fever, headache, nausea, loss of appetite, and abdominal discomfort. Without timely treatment, they can lead to severe complications such as intestinal perforation and septicemia. Diagnosis often involves blood, stool, or bone marrow cultures. Vaccination and improved sanitation are key preventive measures. Treatment typically includes antibiotics like ciprofloxacin or azithromycin.

## Highlights

- **Decline in Cases:** There has been a significant reduction in Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases, from 1109 in May 2015 to 418 in May 2024. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends:** Higher case counts typically appeared during the summer months (June to September), indicating a seasonal pattern. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** The mortality rate has remained consistently low, with sporadic deaths mainly around one or zero per month. <br/>
- **Recent Trends:** From 2021 to 2024, both cases and deaths have consistently declined, indicating effective public health strategies and disease management. <br/>

An analysis of the reported cases data for Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever in the Chinese mainland from May 2014 to May 2024 reveals noticeable seasonal and annual fluctuations. Initially, in 2014, the number of cases peaked notably in the summer months, reaching as high as 1787 cases in September before declining towards the year's end. This summertime peak pattern repeats annually, indicating a possible seasonal influence, likely linked to factors such as increased travel, consumption of contaminated food or water, and environmental conditions conducive to the proliferation of the causative bacteria.

The data also show a gradual yet significant reduction in the number of cases from a high of 1787 in September 2014 to only 418 in May 2024. This decline points towards possibly effective public health interventions, improvements in sanitation, and perhaps heightened awareness and preventive measures over the years. However, the death count, while relatively low, shows some sporadic incidences, with notable spikes such as in June 2017 (3 deaths) and November 2020 (2 deaths), which might warrant targeted investigation into the causes or specific outbreaks in these periods. Overall, while the incidence of typhoid and paratyphoid fever demonstrates a downward trend, continuous monitoring and public health efforts remain essential to mitigate any potential resurgence and to strive for continuous overall improvement in controlling these infectious diseases.
