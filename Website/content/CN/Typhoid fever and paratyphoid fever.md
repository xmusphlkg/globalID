# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: November 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are bacterial infections caused by *Salmonella* species: *S. typhi* for typhoid and *S. paratyphi* for paratyphoid. Both are transmitted through contaminated food and water. Symptoms include fever, abdominal pain, weakness, and digestive disturbances. Typhoid fever is generally more severe, while paratyphoid tends to be milder. Diagnosis is based on blood cultures, and treatment involves antibiotics. Preventive measures include proper sanitation, vaccination, and safe food practices. These diseases are more common in areas with inadequate hygiene and water treatment.

## Highlights

- **Decline in cases**: Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases have steadily decreased since 2014, with monthly cases typically under 1,000 since 2020. <br/>

- **Low mortality**: Deaths remain rare, with only a few reported annually, highlighting the effectiveness of treatment and control measures. <br/>

- **Seasonal peaks**: Case numbers tend to rise in summer and fall, likely linked to warmer temperatures and increased travel. <br/>

- **Ongoing control**: By 2024, the disease remains under control, with low case numbers and no significant outbreaks, indicating successful prevention strategies. <br/>

Deaths remain extremely rare, with only a few recorded fatalities, suggesting effective control measures like improved sanitation and access to treatment.

Seasonality is evident, with higher case numbers typically occurring from June to August, possibly due to increased travel, food contamination, or environmental factors that favor the spread of Salmonella. Despite these seasonal spikes, the disease appears under control, evidenced by the low death rate and declining case counts in recent years. The sharp drop in cases after 2021 may reflect the success of vaccination programs and other public health interventions.

While the disease continues to circulate in the population, the relatively low number of deaths and consistent decrease in cases point to the effectiveness of ongoing preventive measures, which likely contribute to the overall reduction in the disease burden in China.
