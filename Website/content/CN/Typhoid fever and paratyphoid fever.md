# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: June 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are bacterial infections caused by *Salmonella Typhi* and *Salmonella Paratyphi*, respectively. These diseases are transmitted through contaminated food and water. Symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, and constipation or diarrhea. Without treatment, typhoid and paratyphoid fevers can be fatal due to complications like intestinal perforation. Diagnosis is confirmed via blood, stool, or urine cultures. Prevention includes improved sanitation, vaccination, and safe food practices. Treatment involves antibiotics, although resistance is a growing concern.

## Highlights

- **Steady Decline in Cases**: From 2014 to 2024, there has been a notable decrease in the monthly cases of typhoid and paratyphoid fever, indicating improved control and prevention measures. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rates**: Deaths have remained extremely low throughout the period, with only occasional fatalities, suggesting effective treatment protocols. <br/>
- **Seasonal Trends**: Higher numbers of cases are generally observed during summer months (June to September), highlighting a possible seasonal pattern in the disease's incidence. <br/>
- **Recent Stability**: Since 2021, the number of monthly cases has stabilized, maintaining lower levels compared to previous years, reflecting sustained public health efforts. <br/>

The data for Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever in mainland China from June 2014 to June 2024 shows a fluctuating trend with a seasonal pattern. Cases generally peak in summer months and decline in winter, likely due to environmental and behavioral factors. For example, cases peaked at 1787 in September 2014, with consistently higher numbers in summer months like June, July, and August, and lower numbers in winter months such as January and February.

Despite fluctuating case numbers, the mortality rate remained low. The highest number of deaths in a single month was three in June 2017, with most months reporting zero deaths. This indicates effective medical interventions and public health measures. However, there were sporadic deaths, such as in October 2014, June 2015, and February 2016, suggesting that while the overall risk of death is low, fatalities can occur due to complications or delayed treatment.

The data highlights the importance of continuous monitoring and targeted public health interventions, especially during peak seasons. The effective management of these diseases is crucial to reduce both case numbers and fatalities, ensuring that the risk remains minimal even during times of increased incidence.
