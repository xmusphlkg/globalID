# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: July 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are enteric illnesses caused by the bacteria Salmonella Typhi and Salmonella Paratyphi, respectively. These diseases spread primarily through contaminated food and water, particularly in regions with inadequate sanitation. Symptoms often include prolonged fever, headache, nausea, loss of appetite, and abdominal discomfort. In severe cases, complications such as intestinal hemorrhage or perforation may occur. Both diseases are preventable with improved sanitation, access to clean water, and vaccination. Diagnosis typically involves blood cultures, and treatment consists of antibiotics, though drug resistance is an emerging concern. Left untreated, these illnesses can be potentially fatal.

## Highlights

- Steady Decline in Cases: Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases have declined significantly from 1358 in July 2015 to 672 in July 2025, indicating improved control measures. <br/>
- Decreasing Mortality: Deaths related to these fevers have been rare with a maximum of 3 deaths in June 2017. Recent months observe minimal to no deaths, reflecting better treatment and awareness. <br/>
- Seasonal Patterns: A predictable seasonal pattern is evident with peaks in summer months (June-August), suggesting potential factors like weather and water quality. <br/>
- Recent Uptick: A slight increase in cases is noted recently, from 215 in February 2025 to 672 in July 2025, warranting continuous monitoring to prevent a resurgence. <br/>

The analysis of typhoid and paratyphoid fever data in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals a dynamic seasonal pattern in case numbers. Peaks in cases consistently occur during the summer months—June to August—suggesting that warmer temperatures and environmental conditions may contribute to increased transmission rates via contaminated food and water sources. Conversely, the winter and spring months consistently show lower case numbers. 

Over the years, there's a gradual overall decline in case numbers, which could be indicative of effective public health interventions. This could include vaccination campaigns, improved sanitation, and public awareness efforts aimed at reducing transmission. The heightened case incidence during summer months might suggest opportunities for targeted intervention and resource allocation during these critical periods.

Interestingly, the death count remains low throughout the period, which suggests successful clinical management and treatment strategies. The ability to maintain low mortality rates, even with periodic peaks in case incidence, indicates effective healthcare delivery and access to necessary medical interventions. The consistent decline in cases towards the later years further hints at advancements in public health efforts, possibly highlighting technological progress or increased adherence to preventive measures. Overall, the data suggests both a seasonal influence on case incidence and positive trends in controlling the disease over time.

The dataset presents a history of cases and deaths attributed to typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever in mainland China from mid-2015 to mid-2025. Throughout this period, the number of cases consistently fluctuates, but a general declining trend can be noted from the higher numbers in 2015–2018 towards lower counts in later years up to 2025. Interestingly, the data reveals that deaths associated with these diseases are relatively rare, with several months reporting zero fatalities. This observation underscores the effectiveness of public health interventions, accessible medical treatments, or naturally low mortality rates for these diseases within China's healthcare system.

Examining the fatalities over the decade, we notice sporadic deaths occurring across different months, with no clear pattern of seasonality. For instance, years such as 2017, 2020, and 2021 exhibit isolated months recording unexpected fatalities amidst relatively stable case numbers. These isolated death occurrences might relate to specific complications in patients or underscore gaps in healthcare accessibility or timely interventions. The analysis indicates that while cases remain a persistent public health issue, the mortality aspect has been largely managed, possibly through robust disease surveillance, effective antibiotics, improvements in sanitation, and widespread vaccination efforts, contributing to the long-term decline in both cases and deaths.
