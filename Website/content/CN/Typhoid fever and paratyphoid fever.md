# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are infectious diseases caused by Salmonella enterica serotypes Typhi and Paratyphi, respectively. These illnesses spread through fecal-oral transmission, commonly via consumption of contaminated food or water. Symptoms include prolonged fever, abdominal pain, weakness, headaches, and sometimes rash. Untreated cases may lead to severe complications, such as intestinal perforation or sepsis. Both diseases are prevalent in areas with poor sanitation and limited access to clean water. Prevention includes improved hygiene, vaccination, and ensuring safe drinking water. Prompt diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics are essential to reduce morbidity and prevent transmission.

## Highlights

- A significant decline in cases has been observed, dropping from over 1,000 per month in 2015-2018 to under 500 per month post-2020. <br/>
- Deaths have decreased over time, with the highest number (3) in June 2017 and sporadic deaths in subsequent years. <br/>
- Cases typically peak between May and October, indicating the need for targeted interventions during these months. <br/>
- As of March 2025, the situation shows a stable trend with 270 cases and no deaths, continuing the low incidence pattern of recent years. <br/>

The reported data for Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever in Chinese mainland from March 2015 to March 2025 shows significant monthly and yearly fluctuations in the number of cases. From 2015 to 2017, there were higher incidences, particularly from June to August, with June 2017 peaking at 1242 cases and three deaths, suggesting seasonal outbreaks during warmer months.

From 2020 onwards, there is a clear downward trend in cases, likely due to improved hygiene practices and health interventions during the COVID-19 pandemic. Notably, cases in February and March 2020 were among the lowest, with 364 and 360 respectively. By 2022, case numbers further declined, peaking at 741 in July. This trend continued into 2024 and 2025, with most months recording fewer than 600 cases and no deaths for extended periods.

The death rate throughout the period is relatively low, with occasional spikes such as in November 2020 with two deaths. The data indicates effective disease management and improved public health measures. Seasonal patterns are evident, with higher cases during warmer months, emphasizing the need for continuous surveillance and preventive measures to maintain the declining trend and reduce the health burden of Typhoid and paratyphoid fever in the Chinese mainland.

However, even during peak case months, fatalities remain sparse, indicating effective treatment or disease management strategies. From 2015 onward, deaths occurred infrequently, with isolated instances in months like June 2015, February 2016, and June 2017. As case numbers declined over the years, notably in the latter half of the timeline, mortality did not correspondingly lower, evident in October 2023's one death among only 480 cases. This disparity suggests variability in disease complications or patient outcomes, underscoring the need for continued attention to treatment practices.
