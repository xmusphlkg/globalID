# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are systemic infections caused by the bacteria *Salmonella enterica* serotypes Typhi and Paratyphi A, B, and C, respectively. These illnesses are transmitted through contaminated food and water, often in regions with inadequate sanitation and hygiene. Symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, abdominal pain, and either constipation or diarrhea. Without treatment, these fevers can lead to severe complications, such as intestinal perforation. Diagnosis is typically confirmed via blood culture, and treatment involves antibiotics. Preventative measures include vaccination and improved sanitation efforts. Both conditions collectively account for significant global disease burden.

## Highlights

- **Significant Decrease in Cases:** From 2014 to July 2024, typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in the Chinese mainland have decreased significantly, from 1662 in July 2014 to 557 in July 2024. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate:** The number of deaths has remained relatively low throughout the years, with intermittent spikes but generally fewer than 5 deaths annually. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations:** Cases consistently rise during the summer months and drop during winter, reflecting a potential seasonal trend in disease transmission. <br/>
- **Continuous Decline:** Recent data since 2023 shows a steady decline in cases, suggesting improved control measures and public health interventions. <br/>

The analysis of Typhoid and Paratyphoid fever cases in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to July 2024 shows distinct trends. Seasonally, cases peak from June to August, likely due to climatic conditions, increased consumption of contaminated food or water, or other environmental factors. There is a general decline in annual cases starting from 2015, reflecting improved public health interventions such as vaccination and better sanitation. The sharp decrease since 2020 might also relate to COVID-19 pandemic measures like enhanced hygiene and travel restrictions, indirectly reducing disease transmission.

Overall, mortality rates are low, with sporadic deaths reported, indicating effective clinical management despite disease occurrence. Notably, periods like June 2017 and November 2020 saw isolated mortality spikes, possibly due to localized outbreaks or brief lapses in disease management. From 2020 onward, cases fluctuate less, despite pandemic disruptions. By July 2024, the number of cases has significantly decreased, showcasing the effectiveness of public health efforts in managing the disease.

While trends indicate progress in disease control, the sporadic mortality highlights the need for continued vigilance in outbreak management and treatment efficacy. The data points to a successful and ongoing public health response, yet underscores the necessity of maintaining robust strategies to handle occasional outbreaks and prevent fatalities related to Typhoid and Paratyphoid fever.

This indicates that while typhoid and paratyphoid cases present regularly, the severity in terms of deaths remains limited or well-controlled, suggesting effective clinical management, public health interventions, or both.
