# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi and Salmonella enterica serotype Paratyphi, respectively. These infections primarily affect the gastrointestinal tract and are transmitted through contaminated food and water. Common symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, abdominal pain, and diarrhea or constipation. While typhoid fever is more severe, both illnesses pose significant public health challenges, especially in regions with poor sanitation. Vaccination, improved sanitation, and access to clean water are crucial for prevention. Antibiotic treatment is effective, but rising resistance is a growing concern.

## Highlights

- Between 2015 and 2025, a substantial decline in typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases is evident, dropping from 1,260 cases in August 2015 to 647 cases in August 2025. <br/>
- Seasonality remains noticeable, with case peaks persistently occurring during summer months, particularly from May to August. <br/>
- The death toll from these diseases has generally remained low, with only 16 reported deaths over the decade, indicating effective management despite occasional spikes. <br/>
- Public health interventions have likely contributed to the decline, showcasing improved disease control measures and awareness campaigns in the region. <br/>

Initially, there is a decline in cases from late 2015, with numbers decreasing from 1,260 in August 2015 to 671 by January 2016. Cases then rise again, peaking in the summer months annually, possibly due to environmental factors influencing disease transmission, such as contaminated water sources during warmer weather.

Mortality remains low, with occasional spikes like in June 2017. However, from late 2019, there is a noticeable decline in cases, reaching a low in early 2021. This period aligns with global public health interventions during the COVID-19 pandemic, likely affecting transmission through measures like lockdowns and increased hygiene practices. The reduction in cases during this time mirrors trends in other communicable diseases, reflecting broad preventive health measures.

From mid-2021, case numbers stabilize but remain lower than pre-2020 levels, potentially indicating lasting effects of public health strategies or changes in surveillance methods. Throughout the period, deaths remain sparse, possibly reflecting effective medical treatments or underreporting. The trends suggest a complex interplay of environmental, public health, and possibly reporting factors influencing typhoid and paratyphoid fever dynamics in China over this decade.
