# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are acute illnesses caused by the bacteria Salmonella Typhi and Salmonella Paratyphi, respectively. These illnesses are transmitted through the ingestion of contaminated food or water. Common symptoms include prolonged fever, fatigue, headache, nausea, abdominal pain, and diarrhea or constipation. Without treatment, the conditions can lead to severe complications such as intestinal hemorrhage or perforation. They are most prevalent in regions with poor sanitation and limited access to clean water. Vaccination and improvements in sanitation, hygiene, and water quality are essential preventive measures. Antibiotics are the main treatment, although resistance is an emerging concern.

## Highlights

- Typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in mainland China have experienced a general downward trend from 2014 to 2024, with a significant decline in monthly cases, particularly evident from mid-2020 onwards.<br/>
- Deaths from the diseases have remained relatively low throughout the years, with periodic occurrences but no significant increase in mortality rates.<br/>
- Monthly case numbers show seasonal variation, peaking in warmer months like July and August, but the peaks have become less pronounced in recent years.<br/>
- The most recent data for October 2024 indicates continued low case numbers (456 cases) compared to earlier years, suggesting control measures and public health interventions may be effective.<br/>

Analyzing typhoid and paratyphoid fever cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 highlights some significant patterns. The data shows higher cases during warmer months, particularly from June to September, suggesting a seasonal trend typical of enteric diseases. This might be linked to temperatures affecting food and water safety and seasonal human activities. Initially, monthly cases average was higher from 2014 to 2016, peaking mid-year. However, from 2017 onwards, there's a notable decline, potentially indicating effective public health measures, better sanitation, or socio-environmental changes.

In terms of mortality, deaths remain consistently low, illustrating a low case-fatality rate despite the volume of cases. This suggests efficient healthcare management and access to medical treatment for these diseases. Certain months, like June 2017 and November 2020, showed slightly increased mortality, but without a sustained rise, implying that these could be isolated incidents or localized outbreaks rapidly controlled. The overall downward trend in cases and maintained low death rates point to ongoing success in managing and controlling typhoid and paratyphoid fever in mainland China.

The analysis of typhoid and paratyphoid fever deaths in the Chinese mainland from 2014 to 2024 indicates a consistently low mortality rate, despite fluctuations in the number of reported cases. Over the decade, a total of 19 deaths have been recorded across over 91,000 cases, which underscores a significantly low case-fatality ratio. This mortality trend remains stable with sporadic peaks occurring occasionally, such as in June 2017 with three deaths and November 2020 with two deaths. The incidence of fatalities does not show any strong correlation with any particular month or season annually, suggesting that external factors or isolated outbreaks could be responsible for these sporadic fatalities rather than seasonal trends.

Moreover, throughout this period, there is an observable decline in the overall number of cases, with a notable drop in cases reported in the later years of the dataset, particularly starting from early 2022. This pattern could reflect improved public health interventions, enhanced access to clean water and sanitation, or advancements in medical treatment for managing infections. Despite this decline in cases, the single death reported in October 2023 highlights that typhoid still poses a risk, albeit to a limited extent. The data also illustrates resilience in controlling the spread and severity of the disease on the Chinese mainland, though ongoing vigilance remains essential to maintain and potentially further reduce both case numbers and fatalities.
