# Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever

Version: January 2025

## Introduction

Typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever are systemic infections caused by Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi and Salmonella enterica serotype Paratyphi, respectively. Both diseases are transmitted through contaminated food and water, primarily in areas with poor sanitation. Symptoms include prolonged fever, abdominal pain, headache, and gastrointestinal disturbances. Typhoid fever is generally more severe than paratyphoid fever. Diagnosis is typically confirmed through blood cultures, and treatment involves antibiotics. Vaccines are available for typhoid fever, but prevention relies heavily on improved hygiene and sanitation practices. Early detection and treatment are crucial for reducing morbidity and mortality associated with these infections.

## Highlights

- **Declining Trend in Cases**: From 2015 to 2025, reported cases of typhoid and paratyphoid fever in mainland China have significantly decreased, dropping from over 1,000 per month in 2015 to below 300 by January 2025. <br/>

- **Low Mortality Rate**: The mortality rate remains low, with few deaths reported annually, indicating effective disease management. <br/>

- **Seasonal Variations**: Higher case numbers are typically seen in summer months, suggesting a seasonal transmission pattern. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: The last few years show a consistent decrease in cases, reflecting successful public health interventions and improved sanitation. <br/>

The data on Typhoid and paratyphoid fever in the Chinese mainland from 2015 to 2025 reveals significant fluctuations in reported cases, with a notable peak in mid-2015. The highest number of cases was recorded in May 2015, with 1,109 cases, followed by a gradual decline towards the end of the year. Interestingly, the mortality rate remained low throughout this period, with only a few deaths reported, indicating effective management and treatment protocols in place. The data suggests a seasonal pattern, with higher incidence rates during the warmer months, which may correlate with increased travel and outdoor activities, potentially leading to higher exposure to contaminated sources.

In subsequent years, the overall trend appears to show a gradual decline in cases, particularly from 2020 onwards, where the numbers consistently dropped, reaching a low of 184 cases in January 2023. This decline could be attributed to improved public health measures, vaccination efforts, and increased awareness regarding hygiene and sanitation practices. However, the sporadic spikes in cases, such as the one in July 2017 and the slight increase in 2024, highlight the need for ongoing surveillance and intervention strategies to prevent outbreaks. The data underscores the importance of continuous public health efforts to maintain low incidence rates and address any emerging threats effectively.
