# Typhus

Version: March 2025

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted through arthropod vectors like lice, fleas, and mites. The disease manifests with symptoms such as fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain, often appearing suddenly. Historically significant due to large outbreaks, particularly in wartime conditions or crowded environments, typhus can be categorized primarily into epidemic typhus, murine typhus, and scrub typhus. Treatment typically involves antibiotics such as doxycycline or azithromycin. Prevention is primarily through controlling vector populations and improving sanitation standards, especially in vulnerable regions or populations.

## Highlights

- There is a noticeable seasonal trend, with cases peaking during mid-year months (May to October) and declining during winter months (December to February). <br/>
- The data shows a decreasing overall trend in cases during peak months from 2023 to 2024, suggesting possible improvements in prevention or surveillance measures. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations in case numbers, deaths from Typhus have remained minimal, indicating effective disease management and treatment strategies. <br/>
- As of March 2025, the number of reported cases has notably declined, showing improved control compared to previous years. Continued monitoring and intervention are crucial to sustaining this reduction. <br/>

This trend may be linked to increased outdoor activities or higher vector proliferation during warmer months. Over the years, an upward trend in case numbers is seen, particularly from 2019 onward, possibly indicating environmental changes, increased vector presence, or improvements in reporting accuracy.

Despite a rise in cases, mortality remains extremely low, with only a few deaths reported sporadically throughout the period, reflecting effective treatment or access to healthcare. The significant spike in cases during 2023, peaking notably in September, highlights the need for ongoing monitoring and potential intervention strategies to curb the spread of Typhus. Subsequent years show similar seasonal patterns and case fluctuations, emphasizing the importance of epidemiological surveillance in understanding and managing Typhus transmission dynamics.

Continuous public health efforts are crucial, considering the socio-ecological factors driving seasonal and annual variations in Typhus incidence. Investigating environmental, demographic, and healthcare factors further can aid in developing robust strategies to prevent outbreaks and control the disease.
