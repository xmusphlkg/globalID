# Typhus

Version: May 2025

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, primarily transmitted to humans through arthropod vectors like lice, fleas, or mites. The main forms include epidemic typhus, endemic (murine) typhus, and scrub typhus. Symptoms typically begin suddenly and include high fever, headache, rash, and muscle aches. Epidemic typhus, caused by *Rickettsia prowazekii*, is associated with crowded, unhygienic conditions. Endemic typhus is milder and caused by *Rickettsia typhi*. Early diagnosis and prompt antibiotic treatment, usually with doxycycline, are crucial for recovery and reducing fatality rates. Prevention involves vector control and hygiene measures.

## Highlights

- Typhus incidence in mainland China shows consistent seasonality, with case peaks between July and October each year, and minima during January through March. <br/>
- The case count in May 2025 (218) is the highest recorded for that month since 2015, reflecting a possible rising trend after previous increases in 2023 and 2024. <br/>
- Despite fluctuations in case numbers, mortality remains extremely low—only two deaths were reported from 2015 to 2025, with no fatalities since September 2016. <br/>
- Overall, while typhus remains endemic with seasonal surges, consistent absence of deaths suggests effective clinical management and relatively low disease severity in recent years. <br/>

Between mid-2015 and mid-2025, the reporting of Typhus cases in mainland China shows clear seasonal fluctuation, with peaks typically occurring from July to October. For instance, September has recurrently high counts: 2015 (142), 2016 (118), 2017 (88), 2018 (114), 2019 (143), 2020 (184), 2021 (211), 2022 (174), 2023 (227), and 2024 (188). These patterns suggest environmental or vector-related factors affecting transmission dynamics, likely tied to warmer months conducive to louse or flea activity.

From a trend perspective, annual variation shows relatively stable lows during winters (e.g., February consistently under 50 cases) and incremental growth in peak season totals post-2020. While 2015–2019 peaks rarely surpassed 150 cases, peaks from 2021 onward often exceed 200 (e.g., 217 in August 2023, 218 in May 2025). Notably, reported deaths were extremely rare, with only two fatalities over the 10-year period (August 2015 and September 2016), underscoring low lethality.

These fatalities occurred early in the dataset and were isolated, with no additional deaths reported in the nearly nine subsequent years. This stark reduction—combined with consistent reporting of cases over time—indicates significant progress in disease management, early diagnosis, treatment access, or public health processes. Despite fluctuations in case numbers, particularly predictable seasonal increases in warmer months, no further deaths have occurred, pointing toward an effective healthcare response in preventing complications from the disease.

Both fatal months exhibited typical seasonal incidence with case numbers (143 in August 2015 and 118 in September 2016) falling within expected ranges for late summer and early autumn when typhus activity rises. However, similarly high or even higher monthly caseloads in subsequent years—such as 227 cases in September 2023—did not result in fatalities, reinforcing the likelihood of improved medical interventions or surveillance systems. The long-term absence of reported deaths, even amid rising case counts in certain years, underscores the overall success in mortality reduction from typhus and may reflect either lower virulence strains or enhanced patient outcomes from more systematic treatment protocols.
