# Typhus

Version: September 2024

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans through lice, fleas, and mites. Notable types include epidemic typhus, murine typhus, and scrub typhus. Symptoms typically include fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain. Epidemic typhus, historically significant during wars and natural disasters, is primarily spread by body lice. Murine typhus is associated with rat fleas, while scrub typhus is linked to chiggers in rural Asia-Pacific regions. Prompt diagnosis and treatment with antibiotics, such as doxycycline, are crucial to prevent severe complications. Public health measures focus on vector control and improved sanitation.

## Highlights

- **Increasing Trends:** Typhus cases have shown an upward trend since 2018, with notable peaks in September and October each year. The highest number of monthly cases recorded was 227 in September 2023.<br/>

- **Seasonal Variation:** Cases generally rise in the summer months from May to October, indicating potential seasonal factors influencing transmission.<br/>

- **Mortality:** Mortality rates remain extremely low, with only two deaths reported in the entire dataset, indicating effective management or low virulence.<br/>

- **Current Situation:** As of September 2024, the number of cases slightly decreased to 188, highlighting continued vigilance is necessary despite the absence of fatalities.<br/>

The reported data for typhus cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 show a distinctive seasonal pattern. Cases increase during the warmer months, peaking typically from May to October, and decrease in the cooler months. This trend aligns with the typical epidemiological characteristics of typhus, as vector activity (such as lice or fleas) often increases in warmer climates, leading to a rise in disease transmission. There are notable yearly fluctuations in case numbers, with some years showing higher peaks than others. For example, in 2020, a significant peak occurred in October with 192 cases, while in subsequent years, 2023 and 2024, peaks reached 227 and 207 cases respectively.

Death rates across all reported years remained remarkably low, with only two deaths recorded in the entire period — one in August 2015 and another in September 2016. This consistently low mortality rate suggests effective clinical management or a predominantly milder strain of the disease prevalent in the region. The increasing trend in case numbers towards 2024 may warrant closer observation and investigation into potential factors such as changes in vector populations, human demographics, or environmental conditions. Additionally, health policies and prevention strategies need to be adaptive to address the potential rise in cases during the peak transmission periods.
