# Typhus

Version: September 2025

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans through arthropod vectors like lice, fleas, and mites. The three main types are epidemic typhus, endemic typhus, and scrub typhus. Symptoms typically include high fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain, often occurring a few days after exposure. Historically, typhus has been associated with crowded and unhygienic living conditions, such as in times of war or natural disasters. Diagnosis is through clinical evaluation and laboratory testing, while treatment commonly involves antibiotics such as doxycycline. Preventative measures focus on improving sanitation and controlling vector populations.

## Highlights

- **Increase in Cases:** There has been a notable increase in typhus cases from 2015 to 2025, with case numbers peaking in September 2023 at 227 and maintaining high levels in subsequent years. <br/>  
- **Consistent Seasonality:** The disease exhibits a seasonal trend, with higher case numbers typically observed in late summer and autumn months, particularly from August to October. <br/>  
- **Low Mortality Rate:** There has been only one reported death since 2015, indicating a very low mortality rate for typhus in this region. <br/>  
- **Recent Surge in 2025:** The data for 2025 shows a significant surge in cases during May and September, suggesting growing public health concerns. <br/>

The reported data for Typhus cases in mainland China from 2015 to 2025 indicates a seasonal trend with peaks generally occurring in the warmer months from May to October. Initial observations reveal a significant annual fluctuation, with higher numbers of cases reported in late summer and autumn months compared to the winter and early spring periods. This pattern is likely connected to environmental factors favorable for the vector population that transmits the disease, such as lice or fleas, which tend to proliferate in warmer climates.

Additionally, a gradual increase in reported cases is noted from 2015 to 2025. While 2015 saw monthly peaks reaching the low hundreds, the later years, such as 2023 and 2024, show peak monthly cases surpassing 200. Despite this increase in cases, the number of deaths reported remains remarkably low, with only a single death reported in September 2016. This suggests that while the incidence rate may be climbing, improvements in medical interventions, public health measures, or perhaps the pathogen's virulence might be contributing to low mortality. The long-term data suggest a stable but rising burden of Typhus, necessitating continued public health vigilance and potentially more proactive vector control strategies.
