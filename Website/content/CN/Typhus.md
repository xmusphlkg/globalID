# Typhus

Version: April 2025

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans through arthropod vectors such as lice, fleas, and mites. The main types include epidemic typhus, endemic (murine) typhus, and scrub typhus. Symptoms typically include sudden fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain. Epidemic typhus, spread by body lice, has caused significant outbreaks during war and famine. Endemic typhus is usually milder and transmitted by fleas on rodents. Scrub typhus occurs in rural areas of Asia-Pacific regions and is spread by chigger bites. Prompt antibiotic treatment is essential to reduce complications and mortality.

## Highlights

- Typhus incidence in the Chinese mainland has shown a seasonal pattern, with higher case numbers typically from May to October each year. <br/>
- The overall trend since 2015 suggests gradual increases in annual peak case numbers, peaking in September 2023 (227 cases) and August 2024 (207 cases). <br/>
- Mortality remains extremely low, with only two deaths reported (in August 2015 and September 2016), indicating excellent disease management and treatment outcomes. <br/>
- As of April 2025, 89 cases have been reported, reflecting a seasonal rise but showing a decrease compared to April 2024 (153 cases), suggesting no unusual outbreak activity. <br/>

The reported typhus data from 2015 to early 2025 in mainland China reveals a consistent seasonal pattern with a clear increase in cases during the summer and autumn months (June to October). For most years, the number of reported cases peaks during these warmer months, which aligns with the epidemiological behavior of typhus, a vector-borne disease often associated with increased vector activity in warmer climates. Between 2015 and 2024, the annual low generally occurs from January through March. Notably, 2023 and 2024 both observed sharp increases in cases during peak months, with September 2023 recording 227 cases—the highest monthly figure in the entire series.

Mortality remained extremely low across the dataset, with only two deaths reported, one in August 2015 and another in September 2016, despite fluctuations in case counts over the years. This suggests effective case management and public health interventions. While earlier years (2015–2017) show more modest seasonal peaks, the data from 2020 onward displays more pronounced case surges during peak seasons, particularly in the late summer and early fall. This shift could be due to improved detection, increased vector exposure, or environmental and behavioral changes, warranting closer examination of contributing ecological and social factors in recent years.

These isolated fatalities occurred during months with moderate to elevated case counts (143 and 118, respectively), yet similar or higher incidence levels in subsequent years did not result in additional deaths. This pattern strongly implies that fatal outcomes are rare and may depend more on individual risk factors or healthcare access than on case volume alone. The sustained absence of mortality, even through multiple annual peaks, suggests effective clinical management and early detection protocols.

Despite seasonal surges, particularly from May to October, the lack of corresponding increases in deaths further underscores the benign case outcomes and possibly indicates that most infections remain within mild to moderate clinical severity. This likely reflects robust public health infrastructure, appropriate antimicrobial treatment availability, and possibly improved disease awareness among both clinicians and the public. The constancy of zero deaths over more than eight years, despite fluctuating incidence, highlights typhus as a vanishingly rare cause of mortality in China’s current epidemiological landscape.
