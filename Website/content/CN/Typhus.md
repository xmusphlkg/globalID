## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans primarily through lice, fleas, and ticks. Historically significant in times of war and natural disasters, typhus manifests with symptoms such as high fever, headache, rash, and malaise. There are different types, including epidemic typhus (spread by lice), endemic typhus (from fleas), and scrub typhus (via mites). Left untreated, the disease can lead to severe complications or death, making it critical to address conditions favoring its spread, like overcrowding and poor sanitation. Diagnosis often involves serological tests, and treatment includes antibiotics like doxycycline.

## Highlights

- **Seasonal Pattern**: Typhus cases in the Chinese mainland show a clear seasonal pattern, peaking during summer and autumn, particularly in August and September. <br/>

- **Increasing Trend**: There is a significant increase in cases from 2015 to 2025, with a peak of 227 cases in September 2023, possibly due to factors like population growth or better reporting. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Despite rising cases, the mortality rate is consistently low, indicating effective treatment or the mild nature of current strains. <br/>

- **Recent Surge**: July 2025 reported 164 cases, reflecting a continued high disease burden compared to previous years, necessitating ongoing public health efforts. <br/>

The pattern suggests potential environmental influences and historical trends affecting vector-human interaction, which could be explored further.
