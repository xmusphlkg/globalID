## Introduction

Typhus is a bacterial infection caused by *Rickettsia* species, transmitted by lice, fleas, or mites. There are several types, including epidemic typhus (caused by *Rickettsia prowazekii*) and endemic typhus (caused by *Rickettsia typhi*). Symptoms include fever, headache, rash, and muscle aches. Epidemic typhus is often associated with poor hygiene and overcrowded conditions, while endemic typhus is more common in areas with fleas. Treatment typically involves antibiotics, and prevention focuses on controlling the vectors and improving sanitation. Without treatment, typhus can be fatal.

## Highlights

1. **Stable Case Numbers**: Typhus cases in China have remained relatively steady, with monthly counts ranging from 40-227 cases between 2014 and November 2024. <br/>

2. **No Fatalities**: There have been no reported deaths from Typhus during this period, suggesting effective disease management. <br/>

3. **Seasonal Peaks**: Typhus cases typically peak during the warmer months, especially from May to September. <br/>

4. **Recent Decrease**: Cases have generally decreased in 2023 and 2024, potentially reflecting improved control measures or reporting changes. <br/>

The data on typhus cases in mainland China from 2014 to 2024 shows fluctuating case numbers, with a noticeable peak during the summer months, particularly from May to August. These seasonal spikes likely reflect favorable conditions for the spread of lice and fleas, the primary vectors. Despite these fluctuations, there were no deaths in the majority of the cases, suggesting effective treatment and public health interventions. 

From 2019 to 2022, there was a general decline in reported cases, which could be attributed to improved vector control, enhanced surveillance, and better diagnostic practices. However, case numbers began to rise again from mid-2022 to 2024. This resurgence may indicate localized outbreaks or changes in environmental or public health factors. Despite the increase in cases, no fatalities were reported, reinforcing the effectiveness of ongoing medical management and prevention strategies in reducing the severity of typhus infections.
