# Typhus

Version: August 2025

## Introduction

Typhus is an infectious disease caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans through arthropod vectors like lice, fleas, and mites. The most common forms include epidemic typhus, spread by body lice; endemic typhus, spread by fleas; and scrub typhus, transmitted by chigger mites. Symptoms generally appear within 1-2 weeks of infection and include high fever, severe headache, rash, and muscle pain. It was historically significant in times of war and overcrowding. Modern advances in sanitation and antibiotics, like doxycycline, have reduced its prevalence and mortality, yet it remains a concern in certain parts of the world.

## Highlights

- **Rising Trend in Typhus Cases**: From 2015 to August 2025, there is a notable increase in reported cases of typhus, with peak values reaching 227 cases in September 2023. <br/>
- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: The data indicate a recurring pattern with higher cases during late summer and early autumn months, reflecting seasonal variability. <br/>
- **Low Mortality Rate**: Despite the increase in cases, the mortality rate remains exceptionally low, with only two reported deaths across the entire dataset. <br/>
- **Recent Fluctuations**: Recent data show fluctuations with a significant rise to 218 cases in May 2025 and slight decline to 145 in August 2025, indicating variable disease activity. <br/>

Typically, case numbers rise in late summer to early autumn (August to October) and drop during winter and early spring (December to March). This pattern aligns with the disease's transmission dynamics, often influenced by environmental conditions affecting flea activity. Highest case counts are seen in September and October, often varying year to year. An overall upward trend in incidence is visible, especially with substantial peaks from August to October in recent years.

The data highlights an extremely low death rate, with only two fatalities reported over this period, indicating effective medical interventions and awareness strategies. Despite high incidences, the disease's fatality rate remains near zero, suggesting strong healthcare responses. Notably, certain years, such as 2021, 2022, and 2023, experience pronounced spikes, indicating potential outbreaks or changes in reporting. These fluctuations could be linked to public health shifts, environmental factors, or vector population dynamics, necessitating further research. The consistent seasonal peaks suggest opportunities to reinforce preventive measures before peak months. While the data provides insights into trends, additional context from health records and environmental studies would help understand causes behind the variations and strategize better surveillance and control efforts.

Given that typhus, caused by various species of Rickettsia, can potentially lead to severe outcomes if untreated, the negligible death rate suggests effective public health measures and medical interventions.
