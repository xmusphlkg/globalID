# Typhus

Version: February 2025

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans through vectors like lice, fleas, and mites. The most common forms include epidemic typhus, spread by body lice, and murine typhus, spread by fleas. Symptoms typically include high fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain. Historically, typhus has been associated with crowded and unsanitary conditions, such as those found in war zones and refugee camps. Treatment usually involves antibiotics like doxycycline. Preventive measures focus on improving hygiene and controlling vector populations to reduce transmission risk.

## Highlights

- **Decreasing Early Year Cases**: Typhus cases in early months show a decline, with February 2025 reporting 37 cases, down from 68 in February 2015. <br/>

- **Seasonal Peaks**: Cases peak during summer, especially from July to October, often exceeding 150 cases. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: Mortality remains low, with only two deaths reported from 2015 to 2025. <br/>

- **Recent Stability**: Recent years show stable case numbers, with slight increases in summer but no major outbreaks. <br/>

The data on Typhus cases in mainland China from 2015 to early 2025 reveals several trends and patterns. Initially, the number of cases fluctuated moderately, with a noticeable peak in October 2015 at 189 cases. This was followed by a general decline in cases through 2016 and 2017, with the lowest point in February 2018 at just 20 cases. However, from 2018 onwards, there was a gradual increase in the number of cases, peaking in September 2023 with 227 cases. This suggests a potential resurgence or increased reporting of Typhus cases in recent years.

Throughout the dataset, the number of deaths reported is remarkably low, with only two deaths recorded over the entire period, one in August 2015 and another in September 2016. This low mortality rate could indicate effective treatment protocols or a less virulent strain of the disease. The seasonal pattern is also evident, with higher case numbers typically occurring in the warmer months, which aligns with the known epidemiology of Typhus, as it is often associated with increased vector activity during these periods. The data suggests a need for continued surveillance and possibly enhanced public health interventions to manage and reduce the incidence of Typhus in the region.
