# Typhus

Version: July 2024

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted primarily through lice, fleas, and mites. The primary forms include epidemic typhus, spread by body lice; endemic typhus, carried by fleas on rats; and scrub typhus, transmitted by chiggers. Symptoms typically begin within one to two weeks post-exposure and include high fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain. Severe cases can lead to life-threatening complications such as pneumonia and kidney failure. Historically significant as a wartime disease, typhus is now relatively rare with improved personal hygiene and vector control, though still present in regions with poor sanitation.

## Highlights

- **Rising Case Trend**: Since 2018, Typhus cases have generally increased, with the highest recent counts in 2023 and July 2024 (179 cases). <br/>
  
- **Seasonal Peaks**: Cases consistently peak between July and October, indicating a strong seasonal pattern linked to environmental factors. <br/>
  
- **Low Fatality Rate**: Despite rising cases, the fatality rate remains very low, with only two deaths recorded from 2014 to 2024. <br/>
  
- **Current Situation**: As of July 2024, cases are high, underscoring the need for ongoing surveillance and preventive actions during peak periods. <br/>

The reported Typhus data in the Chinese mainland from July 2014 to July 2024 shows notable seasonal patterns, with cases generally peaking between August and October each year. This seasonal trend suggests environmental or climatic factors influencing the disease's transmission. For example, cases peaked at 257 in October 2014, followed by a decrease during the colder months.

From 2015 to 2020, the number of cases remained relatively stable, mostly below 200 per month, with some fluctuations. However, starting in 2021, there is a noticeable increase, particularly with a peak of 227 cases in September 2023. This rise could indicate increased exposure, better reporting, or improved detection practices.

Despite fluctuations in case numbers, the mortality rate remains extremely low, with only two deaths reported over the entire period. This suggests that while Typhus is present, its impact in terms of mortality is minimal, likely due to effective clinical management or the possibility that the cases are of a less severe form. Overall, the data reflects a persistent presence of Typhus with seasonal peaks but low fatality, indicating a controlled public health situation.
