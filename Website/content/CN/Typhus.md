## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, which are spread primarily through vectors such as lice, fleas, and ticks. The three main types are epidemic typhus, murine typhus, and scrub typhus. Epidemic typhus, transmitted by body lice, often occurs in crowded and unsanitary conditions; murine typhus, spread by fleas, is primarily associated with rodents; while scrub typhus, transmitted by chiggers, is prevalent in rural Asia-Pacific regions. Symptoms include fever, headache, rash, and muscle aches. Historically significant, typhus has been curtailed by improved hygiene and vector control methods, though outbreaks can still occur.

## Highlights

- **Steady Increase**: From 2015, typhus cases in the Chinese mainland demonstrate a rising trend, peaking in late summer and early autumn. June 2025 recorded 135 cases. <br/>
- **Low Mortality**: Despite rising cases, deaths remain rare, indicating effective treatment and healthcare improvements. <br/>
- **Seasonal Peaks**: Cases peak in warmer months, mainly June to October, reflecting a persistent seasonal pattern. <br/>
- **Recent Surge**: In 2023, cases spiked significantly from August to October, with high numbers persisting into 2025, emphasizing the need for ongoing vigilance and response measures. <br/>

The typhus case data for mainland China from 2015 through mid-2025 reflects a noticeable seasonal variation, which peaks generally in late summer to early autumn. For instance, the highest number of cases tend to occur around August through October. This pattern could be influenced by climatic factors that provide optimal conditions for the vectors responsible for spreading typhus. Over the years, there is a recurrent trend of lower infection rates during winter months, such as January and February, which may be attributed to the colder temperatures that elevate barriers against transmission.

Furthermore, an observable subtle upward trend in yearly peak case numbers can be identified, especially from the year 2021 onwards. The case counts in years like 2023 and 2024 exceeded those of prior years, reaching upwards of 227 cases in September 2023. Despite these rising numbers, the mortality associated with typhus remains notably low, with very few reported deaths since 2015, suggesting effective control and management measures in place. These insights can be pivotal for public health strategies to target preventive measures more efficiently during high-risk periods and locations within the region.
