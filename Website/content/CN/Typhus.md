## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, typically transmitted to humans through fleas, lice, and mites. There are three main types: epidemic typhus, caused by Rickettsia prowazekii and spread by lice; endemic or murine typhus, caused by Rickettsia typhi and spread by fleas; and scrub typhus, caused by Orientia tsutsugamushi and transmitted by chiggers. Symptoms often include high fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain. Historically, typhus outbreaks have been associated with poor living conditions, such as overcrowding, poverty, and war, emphasizing the need for improved sanitation and pest control for prevention.

## Highlights

- Cases of typhus in mainland China demonstrate significant seasonal variability, often peaking between August and October before declining in the colder months. This trend is consistent across the years. <br/>
- Over the years, the highest number of cases was observed in September 2023 with 227 cases, indicating a potential increase in transmission or reporting improvements. <br/>
- Mortality appears to be extremely low with only one death reported throughout the entire dataset, which underscores high survival rates and possibly effective treatment protocols. <br/>
- The data reflects a gradual upward trend in the number of cases after 2020, potentially indicating increased spread or improved surveillance and reporting. <br/>

The incidence of typhus cases in mainland China shows a recurring seasonal pattern with a marked increase in cases during the warmer months of May through October. This variation is consistent with the known transmission dynamics of typhus, which is often correlated with environmental factors. From 2015 to 2020, the number of cases generally peaked from July to October, inherently suggesting the seasonal and potentially vector-mediated nature of typhus transmission. The year 2020 exhibits an unusual spike in September and October, possibly influenced by extraneous factors such as changes in surveillance, reporting practices, or ecological events affecting vector populations.

Post-2020 reveals a rise in recorded cases, with peaks consistently appearing in the second half of the year, particularly in August and September from 2022 to 2025. The absence of reported deaths across these years, with the exceptional case in September 2016, could indicate improvements in clinical management or the relative non-severity of reported cases. Interestingly, the spring months (February to April) consistently show lower reported cases across most years analyzed. This pattern, coupled with the sudden drop in cases every January following peak months, suggests concerted control measures and the natural decline in vector activity during cooler months might influence these variations.

Examining the typhus cases and mortality data from 2015 to 2025 in mainland China reveals a consistently low death rate despite fluctuations in reported cases. Out of hundreds of cases recorded monthly over the period, the data shows only a single death in September 2016. This suggests a very low mortality rate attributable to typhus, indicating effective management and treatment strategies for the disease in the region. The substantial number of cases without corresponding deaths implies a combination of timely medical intervention, possible advances in medical treatments, effective public health measures, and perhaps a strain of the disease that is less lethal.

The trend in reported cases also exhibits significant seasonal and annual variability. Notably, higher case numbers tend to occur in the mid to late months of each year, particularly from May to October, which might correlate with environmental conditions conducive to the vectors of typhus, such as lice or fleas. Despite this variability in case numbers, the data reinforces the success of public health efforts as fatalities are largely absent, which highlights the robustness of the healthcare system in addressing typhus. Further exploration into prevention measures and healthcare responses could provide valuable insights into the factors ensuring this low mortality rate.
