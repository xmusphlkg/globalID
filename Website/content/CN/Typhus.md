# Typhus

Version: October 2024

## Introduction

Typhus is a group of infectious diseases caused by Rickettsia bacteria, transmitted to humans through lice, fleas, and mites. It manifests as endemic typhus, transmitted by fleas, or epidemic typhus, spread by body lice. Symptoms include fever, headache, rash, and muscle pain, appearing within one to two weeks after exposure. Historically associated with poor sanitation and crowded living conditions, typhus can become severe if untreated, potentially leading to life-threatening complications. Effective treatments include antibiotics like doxycycline. Preventative measures focus on maintaining hygiene and controlling vector populations to reduce the risk of outbreaks.

## Highlights

- **Rising Trend**: Typhus cases in mainland China have been gradually increasing since 2014, with notable peaks from 2022 onwards. The year 2023 saw high counts, peaking in September at 227 cases. <br/>

- **Seasonal Fluctuations**: There is a clear seasonal pattern, with cases peaking in August and September each year, suggesting seasonal factors influence transmission. <br/>

- **Low Mortality**: The mortality rate remains very low, with deaths being extremely rare, such as in August 2015 and September 2016. <br/>

- **Current Situation**: As of October 2024, cases remain high at 186, reflecting a continuing public health challenge that aligns with past trends. <br/>

The typhus data for the Chinese mainland reveals notable trends and fluctuations over the years from 2014 to 2024. Initially, between October 2014 and December 2016, there is a general decline in reported cases from 257 down to 93, with a brief increase during the warmer months of each year. Noteworthy is a seasonal pattern where cases tend to rise from April to October, which could correlate with environmental factors conducive to the transmission of the disease. Within this period, the mortality rate remains remarkably low with only two deaths reported, suggesting either effective treatment protocols or a relatively benign strain of the disease circulating at the time.

From 2017 onwards, the data suggests a stabilization and slight increase in case numbers with typical monthly fluctuations; for instance, higher case counts consistently recorded from June to October. Particularly striking is the upward trend post-2020, peaking sharply in September 2023 at 227 reported cases. This uptrend could be attributed to numerous factors, including possible changes in public health reporting, population movement, environmental changes, or the emergence of a more virulent strain. However, despite this increase in cases, the absence of reported deaths from 2017 onwards signifies ongoing efficacy in managing the disease, whether through medical interventions or public health measures. This consistent zero-death rate might point to improvements in healthcare access or disease management strategies being implemented effectively throughout these years.
